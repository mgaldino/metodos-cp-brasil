Sailing on Waves beyond National Sovereign Land Borders: On the Crossroads between International Ocean Relations and the Blue Economy

Navegando em ondas além das fronteiras terrestres nacionais soberanas: Na encruzilhada entre as Relações Internacionais Oceânicas e a economia azul

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The last decades have witnessed the increasing weight of sustainability on the global agenda and the consequent entry of seas and ocean into this realm. Hence, IR theories have been progressively pressured to address such concerns, although it is well known and recognized that many depend on dichotomous policies and sovereignty (land) boundaries. This article then outlines the need to overcome this bias, shedding new light on the need to review the relationship between land and sea in the international agenda. In the IR literature, the ocean is rarely a particular object of analysis; in practice, contemporary global politics mostly addresses the issue in the 2030 Agenda (in general, only through SDG 14) and BBNJ. The results support the idea that the cross-cutting nature of this agenda calls for a change of attitude from different stakeholders and a new understanding from academia, particularly since 2015. To deal with these limitations, we propose international ocean relations (IOR) approach, reinforcing the co-constitution between land and sea and deeply relying on the blue economy concept. The blue economy perspective can help shape bold and novel ways of thinking and deal with global emerging cha(lle)nges, which range from illegal fishing to modern piracy and ocean-climate nexus.

    Key words
    sea; blue economy; ocean governance; international ocean relations; sustainable development; 2030 Agenda; Ocean Decade

    Resumo

    As últimas décadas testemunharam o aumento do peso da sustentabilidade na agenda global e a consequente entrada dos mares e oceanos nessa esfera. Assim, as teorias de RI têm sido progressivamente pressionadas a abordar tais preocupações, embora seja bem conhecido e reconhecido que muitas delas dependem de políticas dicotômicas e fronteiras de soberania (terra). Este artigo descreve a necessidade de superar esse viés, lançando uma nova luz sobre a necessidade de rever a relação entre terra e mar na agenda internacional. Na literatura e nas políticas recentes, o oceano frequentemente se refere à Agenda 2030, quase exclusivamente relacionada ao ODS 14. Os resultados apoiam a ideia de que a natureza transversal dessa agenda exige uma mudança de atitude de diferentes partes interessadas e um novo entendimento do meio acadêmico, especialmente desde 2015. Desde então, foram criadas as relações internacionais oceânicas (IOR), que reforçam a co-constituição entre terra e mar e se baseiam profundamente no conceito de economia azul. Como o “novo El Dorado do século XXI”, a economia azul ajudará a moldar novas formas de pensar e a lidar com os desafios emergentes globais, que vão desde a pesca ilegal até a pirataria moderna e o nexo oceano-clima.

    Palavras-chave
    mar; economia azul; governança oceânica; relações oceânicas internacionais; desenvolvimento sustentável; Agenda 2030; Ocean Decade

    Introduction

    Intrinsically related to the creation and consolidation of the discipline on international relations (IR), state sovereignty, fixed borders, and national security are concepts that shape the scope of IR in ontological and epistemological terms, making it a discipline still very much influenced by the state-centric view and guided by national land borders (Santos and Colgan 2023). These borders often fuel and emphasize the discourse of dichotomies between internal order versus disorder beyond national land borders.

    ‘Movements across oceans […] essentially shaped the modern world we know today, yet, the maritime sphere is virtually absent from IR theory’ (Blachford 2024: 1). Contrary to this paradigm, the relevance of the seas and the ocean in the contemporary global agenda has grown in recent times, particularly within the sustainable development framework. This brings a series of theoretical and conceptual challenges to the discipline of IR, once again dichotomizing the debate between what happens on land and what happens at sea. Seeking to revise and critique these principles, this article stresses the co-constitution between sea and land, tying the statist and static ontology on land to the boundaryless and fluid one of the seas.

    The research question guiding the analysis is: can the blue economy change the mindset of international theory and practice toward the ocean? Despite covering around 75% of the Earth’s surface, seas and ocean are still marginal and often overlooked in global affairs, especially regarding IR research and teaching around the world. Hence the relevance of this research, which aims to highlight their weight and potential contribution to addressing contemporary global challenges. The main justification for this research is due to the growing relevance of the seas and oceans in the recent global agenda, whose proper and full understanding will enable new theoretical approaches and international policies. The methodology has a qualitative approach, an applied nature, descriptive and explanatory objectives, and combines bibliographic procedures and documentary analysis.

    To this end, this article is organized as follows: the first section briefly reviews the mainstream literature on IR theories, highlighting their heavy dependence on dichotomies and sovereignty (land) boundaries. Next, the second section gives a descriptive presentation of how slow and gradual the insertion of the ocean into the global agenda was, which originally started as an environmental concern in the 1950s and 1960s and broadened its scope over the decades. 2015 is a paradigmatic year for the global sustainable development agenda, which is why the following section analyzes the evolution of this timeline from this year on, stressing some milestones that were critical for the consolidation of the current international ocean relations (IOR). This will focus on the two global agendas underway in the United Nations (UN) umbrella – the UN 2030 Agenda (2016-2030) and the UN Ocean Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development (2021-2030), which calls for considering the three United Nations Ocean Conferences (UNOCs) – in New York (2017), Lisbon (2022), and Nice (2025) – and the Ocean Decade Conference (Barcelona, 2024). Then, another theory-based section constitutes the conceptual contribution of this article in insofar as it proposes the blue economy as a key piece of the puzzle. ‘Shaking up the foundations of IR theories’ to ‘setting up international ocean relations (IOR)’ is urgently needed to address land-sea issues in an integrated manner.

    Although it is not the primary goal of this research to provide a novel and worldwide theoretical-analytical framework for RIs, identifying and highlighting theoretic restrictions that result in practical constraints is crucial to our argument. These recent mentioned events emphasize the need to reshape theoretical and political discussion. In this framework, the blue economy emerges as capable of remaking and redesigning the analytical bases and practices of different actors and institutional foundations, rebalancing the role of the ocean and its relationship with what happens on land. It will shed light on the new human, cultural, diplomatic, economic, and governance relations that have fashioned what can be understood as IOR. We, therefore, propose a reconfiguration of the geographical and spatial reading and relation as potentially impacting the modus operandi of international politics in the coming years. Being a reality in different regions, blue growth policies have been indeed helping to redesign national and regional cooperation schemes towards sustainable development.

    Shaking up the foundations of IR theory

    To understand and analyze the role of the seas and the ocean in international politics, it is necessary to turn first to the foundations and theories of international relations (IR) that constitute the analytical basis of the discipline. Without making a detailed analysis of these theories and focusing substantially on more mainstream approaches, this section will cite the main ones to highlight their limitations in dealing with the challenges present in the marine and ocean environment. More recent approaches by contemporary scholars indeed seek to embrace these limitations, but they still have little impact on the teaching and practice of IR around the world. ‘Traditional IR theories can generally be categorized by their focus either on humans, states, or on the state system as the primary source of conflict’ (Cristol 2019). It is clear from the definition of the discipline that it is closely related to states. This causes its ontology and epistemology to be circumscribed and delimited by this theoretical boundary that ends up reflecting and reproducing narrow analytical capacities and shallow understandings of certain events in international politics. Its very definition as a discipline comes from the delimitation of what is inside the state and what is outside, defining the framework of analysis between political science and IR.

    As a discipline, IR is very recent since it is assumed to be born in the interwar (1919) in Aberystwyth (UK). Depending on the region (Pereira and Rocha 2014), it was more influenced by the empiricism or positivism of political science (US) or more autonomously with other areas, such as law, philosophy, and history (Europe). However, there is no consensus on its main theoretical lenses, and it is often stated that there are three major branches of IR theory (Snyder 2004): realism, liberalism, and constructivism – which will be the focus of the brief analysis of IR theories in this section.

    English School, critical IR theory, feminist IR theory, Marxism, poststructuralism, postcolonialism, post-Cold War IR theory, and foreign policy are sometimes associated with branches of these basic theories, sometimes as an alternative and therefore more authentic readings. Some perspectives add other theoretical branches, such as international society, and international political economy (IPE). They are sometimes divided into the classical traditions and the mainstream contesting theories or even divided into the major debates. Although it is not the aim of this section or this article, at the end of this section we will briefly present some approaches by contemporary scholars that have great potential to contribute to an innovative and more integrated approach between land and sea in IR.

    Classical realism and its developments still shape much of contemporary international relations, even though academically many of its main axioms have been widely questioned and often annihilated. Influenced by Thucydides, Machiavelli, and Hobbes, it is based on assumptions and ideas such as rationalism, international anarchy (zero-sum game), state-centrism, power, (uneven) distribution/balance of power, change within the system rather than in the system itself, pessimistic view of human nature, state of nature (permanent state of war), survival and self-help. In this fashion, international politics ended up being summarized as power politics, with a strong tendency toward conflict. National (land) borders therefore play a key role in these approaches, limiting any possibility of looking at the ocean as anything other than a means of expanding power and/or a potential space for new conflicts.

    Edward Carr (1946) criticizes idealism and the failure of the League of Nations and highlights the relevance of political, military, economic, and opinion power as instruments of power at the international level. Hans Morgenthau (1948) is another author who stands out in classical realism, who reaffirms the need to analyze the state as a unitary and rational actor, emphasizes that the national interest is defined in terms of power, and typifies foreign policies (status quo, imperialism, and prestige). The Hobbesian conception is clear, which associates international politics with the state of war, and the move away from a universalistic Kantian vision, which emphasizes transnational social ties and cooperation. Despite their nuances, Castro (2012) argues that in the case of classical, neoclassical realism and neorealism, we have the recurrent approach of security, survival, and the logic of power for such ends in the human interactive sphere.

    In contrast to realists, liberals see international relations as a potential realm of progress and purposive change. They value individual freedom above all else, and they believe that the state ought to be constrained from acting in ways that undermine that freedom. Domestically, the power of the liberal constitutional state is limited by its democratic accountability to its citizens, the need to respect the demands of the economic marketplace and the rule of law. (…). Among the key thinkers (…), there are differences of emphasis between the priority to be given to democracy, economic interdependence and the international legal regulation of security and economic issue-areas. (Griffiths 1999: 51)

    Often associated with the term ‘idealists’ and presenting different branches, liberalism is strongly influenced by John Locke, Immanuel Kant, Woodrow Wilson, and Adam Smith; unlike realism, it has an essentially positive view of human nature. Thus, they highlight the possibility and relevance of democratic peace, international cooperation, free trade, and human rights. By not limiting their analysis of international politics to states, they make room for international organizations, and transnational companies – which is why they are also known as ‘pluralists.’ Some highlight interdependence (Keohane 1984), ‘domestic actors’ interests/preferences (Moravcsik 1997), legalization and international law (Kahler 2000), and international cooperation (Axelrod 1984).

    It is possible to identify in this branch some key conceptual bases that are crucial to the current debate on ocean governance and to our proposed concept of International Ocean Relations (IOR). Thinking about cooperation and public goods requires a move towards a cross-border approach, understanding that there are common themes that require global efforts. It is along these lines that the blue economy is likely to fill certain gaps in the business mindset.

    Corresponding to the third major branch of IR theory and the third major debate of IR, constructivism is a much more recent body of theory and arises from the critique of neorealism and neoliberalism, which matched the dominant theoretical frameworks of the late 1980s study of IR in the US. Although they have their particularities, the prefix ‘neo’ generally refers to what both have in common (the commitment to ontological atomism and epistemological positivism). With sociological influence, especially from Anthony Giddens, Peter Berger, and Thomas Luckmann, constructivism highlights mutual relations of construction and co-construction, the relevance of intersubjective beliefs (such as thought, ideas, and values), and the weight of identities in the formation of ideas and ideals.

    Despite the questioning that he does, Alexander Wendt maintains the state-centric approach, revises the agent-structure relation, and states that ‘social structures have an inherently discursive dimension in the sense that they are inseparable from the reasons and self-understandings that agents bring to their actions’ (Wendt 1987: 359). Relativizing several pillars of the IR mainstream at the moment, the author claims in the title and his reference article that ‘anarchy is what states make it’ (Wendt 1992), shedding light on the need to question – even if sparingly – the theoretical status quo. In line with some liberal authors, the role of norms, rules, and law in international politics becomes weighty (Kratochwil 2000). Onuf (2013) points to the influence of social relations on the agency of civil society and global governance. According to this interpretation, the relativization of some core IR theory foundations makes room for fresh analyses and problematizations, which appear essential in elevating seas and ocean to the top of the global agenda for the twenty first century.

    Curiously, authors who were influential in classical IR theories had already stressed the role of the sea and the ocean, but their disciples paid little attention to these issues. In the case of realism, despite the relevance of this approach in the analysis of American and British sea power, the guarantee of the global commons would be hindered since states acted based on self-interested ways. The same occurs with Adam Smith, whose focus on international trade – made possible by maritime transportation – was central to his thesis on the theory of absolute advantages and the wealth of nations as early as 1776; following the same line of argumentation, it also occurs with Hugo Grotius who, despite having a broad influence over different authors of IR theories for being one of the pioneers in the study of international law, had little in terms of results of the classical IR theories about the sea; Grotius indeed advocates the principle of freedom of navigation of the seas – known as ‘mare liberum’ (Castro 2012). Regarding constructivism, due to its difficulty in predicting future behavior given the possibility of changing the identity of actors, there is ‘[solely] “norm” to have agreements concerning the global commons but not preemptive agreements’ (Covey 2020: 8).

    It is worth mentioning that different authors of classical geopolitical theory underlined the role played by the seas in the architecture and dynamics of international politics. Although they focused mainly on land territory, some pointed to the ocean as a means of defense or trade. Analyzing the Maritime Strategy, Mearsheimer (1986) argues that it was fundamentally flawed and a misstep for not increasing deterrence posture in Europe and for being too costly for the American Navy in the context of the war against the Soviet Union. More recently, Mearsheimer (2001: 84) suggests the concept of the ‘stopping power of water,’ arguing that the expanse of the ocean ‘makes it impossible for any state to achieve global hegemony.’ Despite the nuances related to the theoretical contributions of Alfred T. Mahan (1890), Halford Mackinder (1904), Nicholas J. Spykman (1944) and Julian Corbett (1911), Mearsheimer focuses on the analysis of the ocean/water, focusing on sea power, the history of blockades and the (limited) potential for island countries to extend their power.

    Authors from the international society and English School, such as Hedley Bull and Barry Buzan, review the dynamics of world politics/society, questioning anarchy and globalization, respectively. As post-structuralism authors, Chris Brown (1974) doubts the theoretical basis of IR by proposing a reading from critical theory, while Richard Ashley problematizes state sovereignty. Although the other schools of thought on IR question several ontological, epistemological, and methodological bases of the classical IR theories and their derivations, they sin in their criticism for being embedded in the baseline spectrum of international politics until the end of the twentieth century. Even in the twenty first century, they rarely address issues such as sustainable development, environment, climate, and ocean. Although in some cases there are subnational layers (such as the case of paradiplomacy), at the level of analysis of the individual (such as the case of gender and race studies), or even regional (such as dependency theories and IPE), they quite often focus on interstate relations, limited to national land borders.

    Even though the land-sea dichotomy is quite clear in much of the literature presented, there has been considerable progress in the IR literature in its engagement with blue issues. However, it still relies inside the box and is grounded in the national sovereign land border, often understanding the ocean merely as a zone to be protected, an access route to new markets, and an interface space with external insecurity, without fully integrating it into foundational reflections on the nature of the state itself.

    Consequently, it is difficult to sail on waves beyond national sovereign land borders, which is why this article stresses the co-constitution between sea and land, proposing to link the statist and static ontology and epistemology on land to the boundaryless and fluid one of the seas. To do so, it will be necessary to doubt the dichotomies that are protagonists in the theoretical debates in IR, reproduced and witnessed by the most diverse theoretical analyses previously presented. It is precisely for this reason that the title of this article places the theme in question on a ‘crossroad,’ since such dichotomies are no longer clear and boundaries are no longer obvious, but fluid and diffuse.

    According to Cruz (1990: 221), ‘dichotomies are fundamental in practically every philosophical and religious system, from Manichaeism to Christianity, and from Confucianism to Existentialism.’ Figure 1 shows that many existing dichotomies play relevant roles in IR theories, such as material/ideational terms (Wight 2006), theory/practice (Carr 1946), inside/outside (Walker 1993), agent/structure (Brown 2012), male/female (Tickner 1992), conflict/cooperation (Nitoiu and Pasatoiu 2022), realism/utopism (Bull 1977), West/Non-West (Hurrell 2016), domestic/international politics (Milner 1991), rational/irrational, state/market, public/private, war/peace, micro/macro, subject/object, individual/society, ideational/material, progress/repetition, civil society/state of nature, tradition/modernity, North/South, developed/developing countries, progress/repetition, and sovereignty/anarchy. Based on these more than twenty dichotomies systematically present in IR theories, it is evident how the analytical basis of this discipline relies heavily on it.

    The dichotomy between political theory versus international relations defines the possible contours of IR and the disciplinary boundaries as a discipline. Indeed, Bueger and Gadinger (2014: 3) point out that ‘practice theorists argue that most of our traditionally learned dichotomies are more a hindrance than an aid to better understanding the world.’ Jackson (2011: 158) reaffirms that it is ‘a distinction all too familiar to us in IR as the old hoary “explanation” versus “understanding” dichotomy.’ In the scope of this article, such a dichotomy becomes even less clear to consider especially thinking about international relations on the ocean and high seas. We thus argue in favor of abandoning these dialectical analyses, which are unduly linked to the study and development of global policies.

    Hence, questioning and relativizing these conceptual foundations of IR requires new theoretical contours, analytical designs, discursive practices, collaborative policies, and interdisciplinary contributions. The number of issues has led to questioning the very end of IR theories (Dunne, Hansen, and Wight 2013), sometimes advocating for justice beyond borders (Caney 2006) with domestic institutions beyond national states (Farrell and Newman 2014).

    According to Blachford (2024), shifting focus from bounded territory can enhance our understanding of IR by incorporating the influence of ocean and the maritime sphere. The author also argues that traditional IR theory is focused on territorial assumptions, which shape our understanding of the state system and the concept of the international itself. By examining early modern maritime empires, such as the British Empire, for example, we can see how maritime power played a crucial role in shaping the modern world. Therefore, we should expand the traditional Westphalian narrative to include maritime domains. Although it remains a marginalized approach, major concerns such as maritime terrorism, modern piracy, maritime crimes, blue economy, and environmental protection have contributed to rising interest in maritime security in recent years (Buerger and Edmunds 2017).

    In ‘International Relations in Uncommon Places,’ Marshall Beier (2005) suggests broadening the scope of IR, its methods, and the voice(s) we use to discuss it. Sensitive to his privileges, the author draws our attention to provide a valuable counterbalance to the ‘hegemonologue’ of dominant Western voices. In this vein, it might be claimed that reading the international theories deeply embedded in national land borders narrows the feasible horizons of the maritime and ocean agenda. Consequently, its unique characteristics, as well as the knowledge of fishermen and indigenous people, are frequently overlooked in the debate. Along with Richard Ashley, the author critically reviews the hegemonic epistemic systems and demonstrates that exclusions have been constantly perpetuated by the business-as-usual-doing IR theory.

    Kimberly Peters and Philip Steinberg are authors who have become references in IR theory, looking at the ocean and pointing out the limitations of mainstream IR thinking. Steinberg and Peters (2015), Peters, Steinberg and Stratford (2018), Peters and Steinberg (2019) and Peters et al. (2023) examine a more-than-wet ontology to shed light on how ocean dynamics both influence and are influenced by human activities They and argue for an IR that considers ‘Territory Beyond Terra.’ Relativizing the central foundations of IR theories, the authors propose a more comprehensive approach that recognizes the ocean as a dynamic and complex space that challenges territorial boundaries and traditional conceptions of sovereignty. They emphasize the need to consider the ocean as a fundamental element in discussions about environmental justice, international trade, maritime security, and global interconnections.

    In line with the central argument of this article, Khalili (2022) reflects on the politics of ocean knowledge production. Emphasizing the role of maritime transport and the commoditization of goods, Khalili (2023) challenged conventional wisdom by arguing that shipping is fundamental to the fabric of global capitalism. Following Beier’s concerns, the Iranian American scholar incorporates issues of regions frequently overlooked in the literature, such as the Arabian Peninsula, Middle East, and Lebanon, as well as her personal experiences traveling on cargo ships, into her critical analysis.

    Regardless of the directions given to IR theoretical contributions to current international policy challenges, it is clear that methodological pluralism is already widely accepted by the discipline (Jervis, 1985, Walt, 1999), with authors highlighting the role of philosophy (Chernoff, 2009), psychology, and behavioral economics (Stein 2017), race and theology (Blamire 2022), poetry (Odysseos 2019), grammar (Considine 2015), and arts (Sylvester 2001). Drawing on this openness to new contributions and perspectives, the following section will therefore highlight how the global agenda has adapted to accommodate the particularities and needs of the global ocean agenda since their theoretical foundations were certainly very focused on issues on land and consequently lacked accurate and adequate inputs to challenges at sea.The literature review enables reflection on the research question, which is whether the blue economy has the potential to shift the mindset of international theories and practices toward the ocean. Only approaches that aim to disrupt the status quo, such as the blue economy, will allow for a rethinking of global ocean governance and effective International Ocean Relations (IOR) not limited to specific topics and does not perpetuate the mindset of policies designed for land-based activities. To accomplish this, it is vital to overcome these dichotomies and think in an integrated manner about these themes, which will be addressed in the following section. As will be obvious, this more inclusive approach is unique in the twenty first century.

    Drawing up a global ocean agenda

    Unlike the global relevance of the Great Navigations and their centuries-long impacts on all continents, little attention has been paid to marine and ocean environments in IR. This is mildly curious, given the relevance of the seas and the ocean in expanding what was understood by the world’s borders. Marginally and increasingly, especially since the late 1960s, themes such as oil spills, maritime security, naval power, maritime transportation, migration, and refugees were gaining the front pages of newspapers and the daily life of the population, and soon new contributions in terms of theoretical evaluation and public policy proposition became necessary.

    From this period on, there was a growing need for a more active role of international organizations (IOs) at sea and in the ocean (Cheever 1968). Soon after the publication of the Tragedy of the Commons (Hardin 1968) some academics already pointed out the need to embrace sea and ocean in this debate (Wijkman 1982), since the ‘use of the oceans or seabed should be used for internationally agreed purposes’ (Cooper 1975: 370).

    In 1967, Edward W. Seabrook Hull, an American journalist, and marine science expert, emphasized the ocean’s role in providing food and recreation, arguing that ‘it has been mostly a two-dimensional environment […]. But now man is extending its reach into the third dimension, and the traditional concepts of freedom of the high seas and territorial waters are confounded by unprecedented situations’ (Hull 1967: 493). Less than a decade later, Brown and Fabian (1974) stated that current diplomatic fashion tempts us to label 1974 the Year of the Sea.

    In the twenty first century, new, more complex, and more challenging issues emerge as threats to the ocean in the Anthropocene era (Harrington 2016), such as outlaw ocean (Urbina 2019), carcerality at sea (Khalili 2021), modern piracy (Samatar, Lindberg, and Mahayni 2010), overfishing (IPCC 2019), marine plastic pollution (Fava 2022), seabed mining exploitation (Miller et al. 2018), ocean renewable energies (IRENA 2020), Arctic and Antarctic seas (Burke et al 2016), ocean acidification (Kroeker et al 2013), marine biodiversity (Galaz et al 2011), ocean ecosystem services (Worm et al 2006), sea level rise and migration (Hinkel et al 2018), and ocean-climate nexus (Stow 2017). Recent IR studies have paid considerable attention to China and its regional surroundings, such as Southeast Asia and the South China Sea (Hendler and Motta 2021) and East China (Oliveira 2021).

    What exists in practice in the marine environment is a great overlap of actors, norms, laws, and agencies, which makes governance overseas and the ocean fuzzy and trapping. From agendas that are securitized in IR theories to contributions from the legal field (Sanger 1986), such as the law of the sea (public approach) and maritime law (private approach), to advocates of cooperation at sea, it is now a global consensus that the Amazon is no longer the lung of the world, with its role being replaced by the ocean (UN 2022).

    Figure 2 briefly presents how the ocean gradually takes up space on the global agenda, albeit in a timid, slow, and limited way in the first decades. Although there were important movements as early as the 1950s and 1960s, it was particularly after the 1990s that the ocean effectively acquired real relevance. Based on Figure 2, the 1950s and 1960s were a time of major oil pollution concerns, not least because of the growth of this industry in those decades. The International Convention for the Prevention of Pollution of the Sea by Oil (OILPOL) and the International Convention on Civil Liability for Oil Pollution Damage (CLC) are just a few examples. In 1972, the London Convention forbade waste dumping in the ocean and entered into force in 1975, followed by the MARPOL Convention (1973) which prevents ships from throwing garbage in the ocean. The 1980s brought the issue of fishing to the agenda, through the Code of Conduct for Responsible Fisheries (1982). In the following year, the Common Fisheries Policy in the European Union (EU) consolidated the concern of the theme in the regional scope – which would be reproduced by other continents in the following decades.

    Nonetheless, the ocean effectively becomes very relevant on the global agenda from the 1990s on. With the growing importance of sustainable development, 1992 stands out as a strategic year with The United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED) – and the adoption of Agenda 21 – and the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD). Chapter 17 of Agenda 21 deals specifically with the provision for the protection of the marine environment, entitled ‘Protection of the Oceans, All Kinds of Seas, Including Enclosed and Semi-Enclosed Seas, and Coastal Areas and the Protection, Rational Use and Development of Their Living Resources.’ Regarding the CBD, although Article 2 clarifies that the definition of biological diversity includes ‘marine and other aquatic ecosystems and the ecological complexes of which they are part,’ there is little focus on the marine issue. Also, as a spin-off of UNCED (known as the ‘Earth Summit’ or ‘Eco 92’), Oceans Day was declared at the Global Forum on June 8th of the same year.

    Among other relevant milestones, 1994 is the year of the effective entry into force of the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), signed on December 10, 1982. In 1995, the UN Fish Stocks Agreement and the UN Global Programme of Action for the Protection of the Marine Environment from Land-based Activities (UN GPA) highlighted the UN’s engagement in the protection of the marine environment and life, including by relating it to land-based activities. This movement is fully in line with the central argument of this article, which emphasizes the need to reduce the dichotomy between land- and sea-based activities. In 1996, the London Protocol was adopted to update the London Convention bringing a precautionary approach, and entered into force ten years later, in 2006.

    The first decade of the twenty first century witnesses continued regional and international efforts to bring the seas and ocean onto the global agenda. In 2003, the UN created the UN-Oceans. According to the official website of this agency,

    UN Oceans is an inter-agency mechanism that seeks to enhance the coordination, coherence and effectiveness of competent organizations of the United Nations system and the International Seabed Authority, in conformity with the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, the respective competences of each of its participating organizations and the mandates and priorities approved by their respective governing bodies.1

    In October 2007, the European Commission adopted, and the European Council approved the EU Blue Paper, which outlines a strategy for integrated maritime policy for the European Union. The EU proposed a Marine/Maritime Spatial Planning (MSP) Roadmap in June 2008, and a Marine Strategy Directive in November of the same year. Since then, the EU’s leading role in this issue has become clear.

    It is undoubtedly in the 2010s that the sea and the ocean grow in relevance on a global scale, which will justify the defense of the term ‘international ocean relations (IOR)’ in the next section. In 2011, the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) raised for the very first time plastics in the ocean as an emerging issue and soon afterward there was the Global Plastic Alliance Declaration for Solutions on Marine Litter. In the same year, Honolulu Strategy points out a framework for a global collaborative effort to reduce the impacts of marine debris worldwide. In 2012, the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development (UNCSD), better known as Rio+20, brought in the outcome document ‘The Future We Want’ discussions about oceans and seas (§158-177) and the Member States stressed the relevance of ‘the conservation and sustainable use of the oceans and seas and of their resources for sustainable development.’

    Besides, the UN launched in the context of Rio+20 the ‘blue economy concept paper,’ which will be discussed later in this article. This should be interpreted as a seed of influence on how the international community perceives the ocean as a key to the current global agenda, paving the way for blue policies that consider coastal, marine, maritime, and ocean resources, activities, industries, and services without distancing them from what happens on land.

    In 2014, the SIDS Accelerated Modalities Of Action (S.A.M.O.A.) Pathway reaffirms the relevance of small island developing states (SIDS) in the movement to bring the sea and ocean to the global agenda. 2015 is a paradigmatic year in this discussion, which is why the next section will analyze in detail the 2030 Agenda (2015), The Ocean Conference (2017), the UN Ocean Decade (2021), the Lisbon Ocean Conference (2022), and the Ocean Decade Conference (2024). The rationale is that they effectively bring this discussion to a role never seen before on the global agenda.

    In any case, other related events in the 2010s that keep reinforcing this relevance, such as the UNEP Clean Sea Campaign (2017), the Commonwealth Clean Oceans Alliance (2018), and the European Plastics Pact (2020), all focused on pollution, as well as the creation of the High Level Panel for a Sustainable Ocean Economy, better known as the ‘Ocean Panel’ (2018). Regarding the climate issue, the Paris Agreement – which was adopted in December 2015 and entered into force in November 2016 – does not underline the role of the ocean. Yet, Gallo, Victor, and Levin (2017) analyze 161 nationally determined contributions (NDCs) and conclude that 70% include marine issues. Considering these international milestones, it is only in 2019 that the ocean-climate nexus becomes more evident with COP25, known as the ‘Blue COP’, held in Madrid (Spain).

    2022 has been named ‘the Super Year of the Ocean’ owing to different events. In March, the UN Environment Assembly (UNEA-5) began negotiations for an internationally binding agreement to end plastic pollution by 2024. In June, the UN Ocean Conference took place in Lisbon (Portugal) driving governments to collectively scale up science-based and innovative actions to face ocean needs. Two more recent events show interesting but less-than-expected results. In August, after fifteen years of discussions and four previous formal sessions, there was a conference in New York (USA) on a treaty on Areas Beyond National Jurisdiction (ABNJ) and its marine biodiversity (BBNJ) – also known as the High Seas Treaty. In November, in Sharm el-Sheikh (Egypt), although the ocean produces over 70% of the Earth’s oxygen and absorbs over 30% of global CO2 emissions and over 90% of the heat from global warming, at COP27 there was little progress towards intensifying real ocean-climate actions.

    In June 2023, nearly 200 countries adopted the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea on the Conservation and Sustainable Use of Marine Biological Diversity of Areas beyond National Jurisdiction (BBNJ Agreement). More than ever, this recent event emphasizes the importance of the research question and the justification for this article in overcoming the dichotomy between sovereignty on land and anarchy on the sea when it comes to defining and agreeing on rules for managing areas outside of national jurisdiction. On the one hand, it represents the continuation of the status quo in the ocean environment; on the other, it demonstrates that demand, flows, and dynamics on land are increasingly embedded and dependent on what is exploited in the ocean.

    In 2024, various events highlight the importance of communicating, cooperating, and sharing ocean-related knowledge. The Ocean Decade Conference (Barcelona, Spain) in April was the decade’s first formal worldwide in-person event, consisting of a week of sharing experiences and commitments for the future. In July, the Global Dialogue on Sustainable Ocean Development (Bali, Indonesia) emphasized the importance of ocean accounting and developing consistent methodologies for economically evaluating the ocean’s resources, activities, and services.

    2025 marks a pivotal moment for the global blue economy agenda and international commitments around sustainable ocean economic development, with both the first Blue Economy and Finance Forum (BEFF) in Monaco and the third UN Ocean Conference (UNOC 3) in Nice shaping the landscape for ocean-related action in June. On these two occasions, blue economy stakeholders gained prominence, both for their intersection with broader ocean-related agendas and for renewed commitments to increased financial investments in this sector. By August 7, 2025, 52 countries had ratified the BBNJ Agreement, approaching the threshold of 60 ratifications required for the agreement to enter into force 120 days after this milestone.

    As time goes by, the ocean has been increasingly thought of along the lines of the Roman Empire’s Mare Clausum as a global common. Other issues have been added to the agenda, including marine spatial planning (MSP), mitigation of emissions from maritime transport, gender issues, and the ocean-climate nexus. While the agenda effectively initially focused on the environmental perspective of oil and plastics pollution, it is now much wider, more complex, and multi-stakeholder. This new nature will require moves beyond national and regional boundaries, now bringing these issues to the center of the sustainable development agenda at the UN.

    Thus, it is possible to state that there are currently international ocean relations (IOR) being developed on the global agenda – particularly after 2015. Although a relevant part of the literature ends up focusing on the legal and regulatory issues (McDorman 2009), here we propose a broader and bolder approach. Unlike the dichotomies presented and the hierarchies between policies and actors in international politics, the particularity of this agenda is that the IOR stresses the co-constitution between land and sea. It ties the statist and static ontology on land to the boundaryless and fluid one of the seas.

    In line with the criticism of the dichotomy in the previous section, this section has adopted a descriptive approach to institutional evolution to demonstrate that the treatment of ocean governance is still very rigid. In fact, regional institutions, international organizations and sectoral initiatives deal with the different challenges of the complex blue agendas autonomously and do not consider it in a transversal way, connected to each other and belonging to the land-sea nexus. Although the sea and the ocean have invisible borders (internal waters, territorial sea, exclusive economic zone, continental shelf, and high seas, for example), in practice its policies require a collective and global effort beyond national sovereign towards the productive and sustainable use of marine resources, maritime activities, and ocean services questioning (more than ever) the pillars of traditional IR theories.

    Setting up international ocean relations

    Although there has been a significant advance in the global agenda in dealing with maritime and oceanic challenges, debates have nearly always been centered on themes, threats, or regions, as indicated in Figure 2 and the preceding section. They were dealing with issues such as marine debris, oil spills, and an increase in illicit fishing in an isolated fashion. It is only with the 2030 Agenda that ocean-oriented international policies begin to take a broader and more integrated view of the challenges and opportunities associated with sustainable development. That is why this section’s timeframe is set for 2015.

    In 2015, the 2030 Agenda was approved, leading to the establishment of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which came into force on January 1, 2016. With 17 interlinked goals, 169 targets, and 232 associated indicators, this ambitious agenda was adopted by the UN to face the great challenges of the twenty first century. The set of goals, targets, and indicators focus on people, the planet, prosperity, peace, and partnerships (known as ‘the 5Ps’) and can be understood as a more holistic approach than the Millennial Development Goals (MDGs).

    This is a broad and multi-themed agenda, including poverty (SDG 1); hunger (SDG 2); health (SDG 3); education (SDG 4); gender (SDG 5); sanitation (SDG 6); clean energy (SDG 7); economic growth and employment (SDG 8); industry, innovation, and infrastructure (SDG 9); inequalities (SDG 10); sustainable cities (SDG 11); responsible consumption and production (SDG 12); climate change (SDG 13); marine life (SDG 14); life on land (SDG 15); strong institutions (SDG 16); and partnerships for the goals (SDG 17). SDG 14 addresses marine pollution; healthy ocean; ocean acidification; sustainable fisheries – overfishing and illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing; marine protected areas; fishing subsidies; and benefits for small island developing states (SIDS) and least developed countries (LDCs) (UN 2016).

    Therefore, SDG 14 focuses on living marine resources and stresses the biological and environmental perspective of life in marine spaces. Consequently, most of the analyses that deal with the relevance of the seas and the ocean in the 2030 Agenda tend to focus exclusively on it – which is limited, especially considering the transversality of the 2030 Agenda (Santos 2023). In this vein, and considered alone, ‘SDG 14 has received the least amount of funding compared to other SDGs and ocean science makes up just a small part of this funding’ (UNESCO-IOC 2022a: 10).

    In practice, there are different interconnections and trade-offs between the 17 goals (Breuer et al. 2019, Scherer et al. 2018). Addressing the current challenges of the international system become even more complex due to their multi-level governance and cross-cutting nature. In these cases, it is necessary to consider different levels of hierarchies, bringing together trade-offs between domestic/international coordination and land/maritime activities. This is precisely the case of seas and ocean in the 2030 Agenda.

    This global-scale sight is critical because, although ‘the protection of ocean areas under national jurisdiction is increasing, […] more than 60% of the oceans are still outside national jurisdiction, and only about 1% of the area is covered’ (UN 2019: 28). Le Blanc (2015) emphasizes the close relationship of SDG 14 to SDG 8 and SDG 12, concluding that the SDGs are more complete and more interconnected than the MDGs. ICSU (2017) highlights that SDG 14 is among those that interact the most with others, having strong relationships with SDGs 1, 2, 8, 11, 12, and 13. Hence, dealing with seas and ocean in the context of the 2030 Agenda requires a change of perspective, mainly because it is a cross-cutting issue. It is not reasonable to be limited to the boxes presented in the visual identity of the 2030 Agenda.     The number of targets for each SDG varies widely, but they are all related to the blue economy. Seas and ocean have then a much broader and transversal spectrum in the 2030 Agenda than the SDG 14 itself and, given this proposed methodological paradigm, all the SDGs have an impact on marine space and maritime/ocean activities. This argument is in line with the view of the UN, which claims that ‘issues related to oceans and seas are addressed in the 10 targets under SDG 14, as well as many other related SDGs, under the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development.’2

    After this great move to place the ocean at the forefront of the global sustainable development agenda, the first World Ocean Assessment, by the UN in 2016, reinforced the urgency of controlling activities in the ocean with sustainability. In 2017, the first UN Ocean Conference, held in New York (USA), allowed the global community to rethink the ocean health and seas hoping for concrete solutions. The Governments of Fiji and Sweden co-hosted responsibilities of this high-level UN Conference to Support the Implementation of SDG 14, whose expected outcomes were:

    Identify ways and means to support the implementation of Sustainable Development Goal 14; Build on existing successful partnerships and stimulate innovative and concrete new partnerships to advance the implementation of Goal 14; Involve all relevant stakeholders (…) to assess challenges and opportunities relating to, as well as actions taken towards, the implementation of Goal 14; Share the experiences gained at the national, regional and international levels in the implementation of Goal 14; Contribute to the follow-up and review process of the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development by providing an input to the high-level political forum on sustainable development (…).3

    In contrast to the cross-cutting nature of the ocean in the 2030 Agenda, the conference focus was exclusive to SDG 14. However, the most important point of the event was the launch of the United Nations Decade of Ocean Science for Sustainable Development, also known as the ‘(UN) Ocean Decade.’ To be implemented between 2021 and 2030, it seeks to fulfill the commitments of the 2030 Agenda with a focus on SDG 14 – and other correlates.

    The Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission (IOC) of the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO) is responsible for the design and preparation of the (UN Ocean) Decade Implementation Plan, which defines a set of scientific and technological activities to achieve seven desired outcomes. Unlike what had been done so far, the discourse and publications of the decade insist on using the term ‘ocean’ in the singular, highlighting its unique nature, which requires the unification of collective action in favor of this common good.

    The Ocean Decade addresses ocean pollution; understanding, protection, restoration, and management of ocean ecosystems; productive and sustainable food supply and sustainable ocean economy; predictability in the face of changes; safety of life and livelihood from ocean-related hazards; open and equitable access to data, information, technology, and innovation; and appreciation and involvement with the ocean by the society. Its slogan ‘the science we need for the ocean we want’ stresses the paramount role of a cooperative, systemic, and integrated strategy considering 3 objectives, and 7 expected outcomes in 10 years, with 10 challenges to address key issues having 5 pillars.

    Summing up, it has 3 main objectives (to identify required knowledge for sustainable development; to generate comprehensive knowledge and understanding of the ocean; and to increase the use of ocean knowledge), 10 challenges (to understand and beat marine pollution; to protect and restore ecosystems and biodiversity; to sustainably feed the global population; to develop a sustainable and equitable ocean economy; to unlock ocean-based solutions to climate change; to increase community resilience to ocean hazards; to expand the global ocean observing system; to create a digital representation of the ocean; skills, knowledge, and technology for all; and to change humanity’s relationship with the ocean) and 7 expected outcomes (a clean ocean; a healthy and resilient ocean; a productive ocean; a predicted ocean; a safe ocean; an accessible ocean; and an inspiring and engaging ocean) (UNESCO-IOC 2020). There is a close alignment between its objectives and the priority ocean knowledge needs for a sustainable ocean economy, also known as the blue economy (UNESCO-IOC 2021a).

    Regarding the 2030 Agenda, although the Ocean Decade is more closely related to SDG 14 it is not limited to it (UNESCO-IOC 2022). Therefore, ‘the ocean we want’ (7 expected outcomes) and the challenges are related, for example, to SDG 1 (knowledge and solutions for a sustainable ocean economy), SDG 2 (knowledge and solutions for sustainable fisheries and aquaculture), SDG 3 (knowledge and innovation for multi-hazard early warning systems), SDG 4 (formal and informal education on the importance of the ocean for sustainable development), SDG 5 (increasing gender equity in ocean science), SDG 7 (knowledge and solutions for low-impact ocean energy), SDG 8 (knowledge and solutions for a sustainable ocean economy), SDG 10 (involvement of SIDS and LDCs, including greater access to data, information, capabilities, and technologies), SDG 11 (knowledge and solutions for community resilience) and SDG 13 (greater understanding of the ocean-climate nexus) (UNESCO-IOC 2021b, Silverwood-Cope and Santos 2022).

    Faced with this new complex framework, the global agenda no longer addresses individual issues directly or indirectly related to the ocean in an isolated and disconnected manner. On the contrary, what is evident is a tendency to tie such issues together, even with events that take place on land. That is precisely the theoretical proposal of this article, which aims to no longer dichotomize land-sea relations given their co-dependence and co-constitution. As stated in the introduction, this article does not have the boldness to propose a single, general, global analytical model that can do that, but rather to highlight the weaknesses of global ocean policies over the past seven decades and to propose a blue economy-based approach as an alternative to business mindset and the governance status quo.

    The next section will show the relationship of the Ocean Decade with the blue economy, especially considering its third expected outcome, third challenge, the third pillar. Furthermore, the dialogue between the expected outcomes and the different SDGs of the 2030 Agenda is clear, as well as the role of different institutions (global policy frameworks) with ocean governance (UNFCCC, CBD, SENDAI Framework, SAMOA Pathway, and BBNJ). Regarding the actors, the weight of scientists, governments, businesses, philanthropists, and NGOs stands out, emphasizing one more time the need for a solid, robust, and collective global agenda in favor of the seas and ocean. Once again, given that there are many relationships with different SDGs to achieve the expected results and the challenges of the UN Ocean Decade, overcoming the false land-sea boundary ensures actor engagement in the promotion of local, regional, and global public policy.

    Blue economy as a key piece of the puzzle

    Seas and ocean have progressively gained space on the global agenda as the relevance of sustainable development has increased. Initially related to environmental concerns, it is a fact that today it is a much broader and more complex agenda. The same happens with the blue economy. Following Ripsman (2005: 25), there is a ‘false dichotomy between economics and high politics that dominates the security studies literature’; as the agenda has progressed since 2015, the strategic weight of economics in high politics decisions is becoming increasingly clear. ‘As demand for resources continues to grow and land-based sources decline, expectations for the ocean as an engine of human development are increasing’ (Jouffray et al. 2020: 43). The authors point out the current existence of different ocean claims, with the ocean as the center for securing food (seafood, feeds, and nutraceuticals), materials (hydrocarbons, minerals, desalinated water, ornamental resources, genetic resources, and scientific information), and space (shipping, pipelines and cables, tourism and recreation, land reclamation, renewable energies, geoengineering, waste disposal, conservation, territorial boundaries, and military activities).

    Although a few disciplines have already placed the ocean at the center of their analysis – such as oceanography, marine biology and naval engineering -, economics (along the same lines as IR) has only recently done so. As already mentioned, it was only in 2012, in the context of Rio+20, that the UN launched the ‘Blue economy concept paper,’ highlighting the relevance of the discussion and effectively allowing the agenda to become global.

    The Blue Economy is a developing world initiative pioneered by SIDS but relevant to all coastal states and countries with an interest in waters beyond national jurisdiction. (…) The Blue Economy conceptualises oceans as “Development Spaces” where spatial planning integrates conservation, sustainable use, oil and mineral wealth extraction, bioprospecting, sustainable energy production and marine transport. The Blue Economy breaks the mould of the business as usual “brown” development model where the oceans have been perceived as a means of free resource extraction and waste dumping (…) The Blue Economy will incorporate ocean values and services into economic modelling and decision-making processes. (UN 2012: 3)

    The document highlights the following issues: sustainable use of biodiversity; food security; unsustainable fisheries; climate change and managing carbon budgets; marine and coastal tourism; pollution and marine debris, and governance and international cooperation. Among the main opportunities, it stresses shipping and port facilities; fisheries; tourism; aquaculture; energy; biotechnology; and submarine mining. The relevance of the sectors related to the living and non-living resources of the sea, of the activities carried out in the sea that do not use marine resources, and even the role of international policy in promoting this agenda is quite clear, requesting ocean governance and international cooperation.

    This document served as the basis for standardizing the use of the term globally since until then there was a great deal of confusion in the literature about the available concepts (Santos 2021). Each term first appeared in a different decade and referred to a distinct region: marine economy referring to Poland (1979), maritime economy to Canada (1992), ocean and coastal economy to the USA (2004), and blue economy to the EU (2011) (Martínez-Vázquez, Milán-García and Pablo Valenciano 2021). Following the same bibliometric methodology approach, Madeira (2020) and Santos (2022) reveal that such terms are often used interchangeably, although in practice they do not deal with the same issue.

    Given that it is the broadest term and takes into consideration ocean governance, which is why it has been adopted in the global agenda and international politics, the focus of this article is effectively on the blue economy concept as the key to the new sustainable development agenda in the twenty first century. A concept of this nature was established in a UN meeting that discussed sustainable development (Rio+20), which is why it is intrinsically and necessarily related to this global ongoing agenda.

    Another reference report is ‘The Ocean Economy in 2030,’ published by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) in 2016. According to it, ‘the ocean economy is essential to the future welfare and prosperity of humankind. It is a key source of food, energy, minerals, health, leisure and transport upon which hundreds of millions of people depend’ and new ‘ocean economy is driven by a combination of population growth, rising incomes, dwindling natural resources, responses to climate change and pioneering technologies’ (OECD 2016: 3). Stating that the ocean is a new economic frontier, the document conceptualizes that the ocean economy encompasses ocean-based industries, natural assets, and ecosystem services that the ocean provides and maps that:

    Preliminary calculations (…) value the ocean economy’s contribution in 2010 very conservatively at USD 1.5 trillion, or approximately 2.5% of world gross value added (GVA). Offshore oil and gas accounted for one-third of total value added of the ocean-based industries, followed by maritime and coastal tourism, maritime equipment and ports. Direct full-time employment in the ocean economy amounted to around 31 million jobs in 2010. The largest employers were industrial capture fisheries with over one-third of the total, and maritime and coastal tourism with almost one-quarter. (OECD 2016: 13)

    Before the COVID-19 pandemic, this study projected a doubling of the ocean economy from 2010 to 2030, to reach US$ 3 trillion and employ 40 million people. Following the same methodological line of creating prospective scenarios and attempting to estimate the global net benefit over a 30-year time horizon (2020–50) from implementing sustainable, ocean-based interventions, Konar and Ding (2020: 34) found that ‘over 30 years, investing $2.0–$3.7 trillion globally across the four areas would generate a net benefit of $8.2–$22.8 trillion.’ They consider four ocean-based policy interventions, namely: conserving and restoring mangrove habitats; scaling up offshore wind production; decarbonizing the international shipping sector; and increasing the production of sustainably sourced ocean-based proteins. According to the authors, the ocean-based investments yield benefits at least five times greater than the costs, given that the benefit-cost ratios for the four ocean-based policy interventions in 2050 are 3:1, 12:1, 4:1, and 10:1, respectively.

    The Ocean Panel defines sustainable ocean economy as ‘the use of ocean resources for economic growth, improved livelihoods and jobs while preserving the health of ocean ecosystems and associated services.’4 Again, the definition encompasses ocean services and ocean health, revealing that the blue economy is far more than the total of the ocean-related industries or presents only an economic perspective. Besides, this definition reinforces a trend in the literature, which uses the term ‘sustainable ocean economy’ to refer to the ‘blue economy.’

    To date, there is no consensus on the meaning of the blue economy; in practice, it is a political flag that reflects the interests and particularities of certain nations and areas. What is well-known and consensual is that it is much more than just economics. Directly related to sustainability, it addresses environmental, climate, social, and inclusive economic variables. In addition to the emphatic use of the term ‘sustainable’ before ‘blue economy,’ some authors use the ‘equitable’ before it (Bennet et al 2019), highlighting the role of blue justice in addressing risks and proposing solutions, particularly the Global South (Barros Platiau, Gonçalves, and Oliveira 2021). As such, the blue economy dialogues directly with the global agenda of sustainable development and ocean governance, whose ‘changes are driven by an array of actors with different objectives and varying degrees of power’ (Blythe et al. 2021: 1).

    Therefore, this article proposes the perspective on the blue economy in supporting the global sustainable development agenda and particular attention to the asymmetry between the actors in this agenda, nationally and globally. In line with the theoretical discussion on land-sea dichotomies, considering coastal zones and land demands for offshore resources and services at sea narrows the gap between these two environments and reinforces the need to think of them in an integrated manner.

    Among the examples of possible interactions with the existing global ocean governance arrangement, the following stand out: UNFCCC (greater understanding of the ocean-climate nexus), CBD (knowledge for the implementation of the post-2020 Global Biodiversity Framework), SENDAI Framework (knowledge and solutions for community resilience), SAMOA Pathway (capacity development and transfer of marine technology to SIDS) and BBNJ (knowledge and solutions for the conservation and sustainable use of marine biodiversity outside national jurisdiction) (IOC-UNESCO, 2021b). There is no need to recreate the wheel, but rather to take advantage of the tangle of institutions, actors, and policies that already exist to align them and make them speak the same language in favor of the common good.

    As a result, it is evident that ‘shaking up the foundations of IR theories’ is required to comprehend how various players and organizations ‘draw up a global ocean agenda.’ From there, ‘setting up international ocean relations (IOR)’ framework that can effectively examine topics, sectors, needs, and land-to-sea connections in an integrated manner is crucial to a paradigm shift. Building new political approaches to address the twenty first century’s global concerns calls for changing paradigms and rethinking how policies and strategies have been established. To address this, we propose the ‘blue economy as a key piece of the puzzle,’ which is broad and incorporates variables and themes of various types, not just those related to economics.

    The blue economy connects sea and land by considering maritime activities (ocean-based industries), (living and non-living) marine resources, ocean ecosystem services, and, consequently, ocean governance. Furthermore, it proposes a fresh analytical and policy framework for dealing with difficulties that neither traditional economics nor mainstream IR theories alone often address. Climate change, social justice, profit-sharing, and the participation of underrepresented players stand out as issues that gain relevance, leadership, and voice in the debate. Small fishermen, seafood, and SIDS have already been mentioned in several worldwide debates about the blue agenda. Thus, the blue economy not only aims to challenge dominant thinking, but also to give analytical tools for effectively promoting an equitable, sustainable, resilient, and inclusive economy.

    Conclusions

    Sailing on waves beyond national sovereign land borders is not an easy task. This article has pointed out that the theoretical basis of IR is fundamentally rooted in the concept of national sovereignty, which in turn is intrinsically tied to the notion of national land borders. Very much present in the different IR theories, dichotomies should not be reproduced regarding the agenda and policies either on land or at sea. Thus, our baseline approach proposes a crossroads between international ocean relations (IOR) and the blue economy, a perspective that broadens perception, overcomes ontological (and sectorial) constraints, and gives due relevance to the seas and ocean in supporting the ongoing global sustainable development framework. Proposing policies and governance arrangements for the resources and activities of the sea in the light of what is done on land will only reproduce the status quo without enabling any systemic transformation.

    However, this is not a simple proposition. The theme entered the international agenda in the 1950s and focused on environmental issues, such as plastic and oil pollution, but its complexity has increased over the decades. Because it currently includes different economic sectors that depend on living and non-living marine resources and other economic activities carried out at sea, it is critical to explore the theoretical and policy foundations that can address this issue. Given that the blue economy involves different actors, such as small-scale fishers, extractive industries, civil society organizations, national governments, regional institutions, private investors and creditors, NGOs, and academics, it appears to be an option capable of accomplishing it.

    Notwithstanding, when it comes to blue policies, an initial diagnosis of the Ocean Decade shows that programs (51%), projects (51%), and activities (50%) have been concentrated in Europe in terms of the location of the lead partner institution, followed by North America – 33%, 29%, and 18%, respectively (UNESCO-IOC 2022b). This reveals the need to make this agenda effectively global and less geographically concentrated in the richest countries, which already have better financial and technological capabilities. There is an urgent need for greater engagement of the Global South in this agenda, particularly due to the richness and regional challenges, which range from illegal fishing to modern piracy.

    Given that it is not a consensus concept and ends up being suitable for different situations and contexts, the blue economy is currently a political discourse. As such, it requires political will, public and private funding, international cooperation, and stakeholder engagement. Without it, the idea becomes merely theoretical without the political will to sway and alter the economic mindset and the IOR’s governance, as this article suggests.

    Some fear privatizing ocean spaces, especially with the possibility of exploitation in areas beyond national jurisdiction (ABJN), including new activities with high carbon intensity and negative environmental consequences, such as the emerging seabed mining industry. Moreover, threats directly related to climate change, such as rising sea levels, highlight the need to promote a common and collective effort in favor of the global economy and governance in this new frontier. In this realm, the blue economy is a strategic piece of the puzzle to foster a change in the mindset of business and humanity concerning marine and ocean resources, being key in ensuring the objectives and goals of the 2030 Agenda, the UN Ocean Decade, and, ultimately, the viability of life and sustainable exploitation on planet Earth, which should perhaps be known as ‘planet Ocean.’ More than ever, coping with the existing difficulties on land necessitates the contribution and critical role of what occurs at sea, which is why the blue agenda has grown in importance in recent decades.

    The particularity of this agenda is that the IOR stresses the co-constitution between sea and land, tying the statist and static ontology on land to the boundaryless and fluid one of the seas, overcoming the false land-sea boundary. As the new maritime and ocean frontier, the blue economy can help to shape new ways of thinking and dealing with emerging changes, based on existing ocean governance very much rooted in policies to promote sustainable development.
