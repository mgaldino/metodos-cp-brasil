The Global Politics of Dis/Abled Students Production: Cultivating Fortified Autistic Self-Love Through Autoethnography and Poetry

A Política Global da Produção de Estudantes com/sem Deficiência: Cultivando um Amor-Próprio Autista Fortificado por Meio da Autoetnografia e da Poesia

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    How does neurotypical education manage neurodiversity? This article combines slam poetry with autoethnography to show how neurotypical International Relations (IR) classrooms manage neurodiversity by shaping students into ‘abled’ or ‘disabled’ identities. It also examines how Autistic students resist ableist pressures to conform through practices of fortified self-love. The article highlights how IR classrooms in Mexico and the United States of America construct dis/abled students through requirements of masking, ableist evaluation methods, care labour to comfort neurotypical peers and professors, and inadequate or non-existent university accommodations. It delves into the tension between students’ lived experiences and the policies created by universities, governments, and global activist networks—with and without input from Autistic people. Emphasising the need for concrete tools by and for Autistic people, this article offers survival tools to students for transforming their social context, creating communities of mutual aid, and cultivating fortified self-love. In doing so, it argues that managing neurodiversity within and beyond a neurotypical educational system could be more ethical if students and professors cultivate a praxis of listening to the bodies and experiences of Autistic people from different backgrounds and are attentive to the communal processes of nurturing survival and love according to the possibilities and limitations of local social ecosystems.

    Key words
    autism; autoethnography; pedagogy; reflexivity; sociology of knowledge

    Resumo

    Como a educação neurotípica lida com a neurodiversidade? Este artigo combina poesia slam com autoetnografia para mostrar como as salas de aula de Relações Internacionais (RI) neurotípicas gerenciam a neurodiversidade por meio da produção de estudantes com/sem deficiência, e como estudantes autistas podem resistir a essas exigências de conformidade com o capacitismo por meio de um amor-próprio fortificado. O texto narra como salas de aula de RI no México e nos Estados Unidos constroem estudantes com/sem deficiência por meio de exigências de camuflagem (masking), métodos avaliativos capacitistas, trabalho de cuidado voltado ao conforto de estudantes e professores “capazes”, e políticas universitárias de acomodação ineficazes ou inexistentes. Demonstra as tensões entre as experiências estudantis na indústria universitária e as políticas e práticas diferenciadas produzidas por instituições, pelo Estado e por redes ativistas globais com ou sem a participação de pessoas autistas. Como alternativa à situação atual, e enfatizando ferramentas concretas criadas por e para pessoas autistas, o artigo também oferece instrumentos de sobrevivência para estudantes transformarem seu contexto social, criarem comunidades de ajuda mútua e cultivarem um amor-próprio fortificado. Ao fazê-lo, argumenta que o gerenciamento da neurodiversidade dentro e fora de um sistema educacional neurotípico poderia ser mais ético se estudantes e professores cultivassem uma práxis de escuta dos corpos e das experiências de pessoas autistas de diferentes origens, atentos aos processos comunitários de nutrição da sobrevivência e do amor, de acordo com as possibilidades e limitações dos ecossistemas sociais locais.

    Palavras-chave
    autismo; autoetnografia; pedagogia; reflexividade; sociologia do conhecimento

    Introduction

    I’m sorry

    To Jeanann Verlee

    I’m sorry I keep failing mom.

    I’m sorry I’m smart but I can’t get out of bed.

    I’m sorry I fantasize about killing myself instead of doing homework.

    I’m sorry I don’t go out.

    I’m sorry I don’t understand people.

    I’m sorry I don’t have any “one day long” good moments.

    I’m sorry I lie and say I am okay.

    I’m sorry I’m so afraid of trying because I know I won’t be able to do it.

    I’m sorry she killed herself.

    I’m sorry she has been my only friend.

    I’m sorry he died when I started to love him.

    I’m sorry he wasn’t a girl.

    I’m sorry I didn’t feel anything when my father died.

    I’m sorry I hated you because you couldn’t protect me.

    I’m sorry I smiled and ran away.

    I’m sorry I stopped saying what I truly think.

    I’m sorry I don’t trust you anymore.

    I’m sorry I’m sick.

    I’m sorry there’s nothing you can do.

    I’m sorry I can’t reach my potential.

    I’m sorry I don’t care anymore.

    I’m sorry I can’t be a role model for Autistic kids.

    I’m sorry this is my life.

    I’m sorry, this is my life.

    I wrote this poem years ago, when I was an International Relations (IR) undergraduate at a prestigious private Mexican University. Back then, I was in tears, debating whether I should just give up. Today, it stands as a testament to survival.

    The poem is not strictly autoethnographic, but it is inspired by slam poetry and the stories of my Autistic friends. Slam poetry is a form of resistance: I felt it for the first time in English through YouTube videos, especially the Button Poetry channel. Later, I understood through practice how these videos are part of a global Spoken Words movement, organising national and international performances, workshops, labs, conferences, and more.

    A slam poem blends free verse, activism, theatre, community, therapy, and recreation. It is both an art form and a performance, typically shared in public spaces, in communion, making every delivery a theatrical enactment.1 The main inspirations for this poem were Jeanann Verlee’s Genetics of Regret, Denice Frohman’s Dear Straight People, Sabrina Benaim’s Explaining My Depression to My Mother, and Doc Luben’s 14 Lines from Love Letters or Suicide Notes.

    It took me years to perform the poem in public. The first time I shared it was in my best friend’s car. During my undergraduate years, she often came to pick me up at night when I was in crisis and needed company to resist suicidal desires. She was also an IR undergraduate student at the time. Later, as a Ph.D. student, I started reading the poem at IR conferences, colloquiums, and talk series. It became the introduction to my ideas about the violence we, teachers and students, perpetuate against students in universities in Mexico and the USA, and how we, Autistic teachers and students, resist this violence by envisioning Autistic reparations (see Díaz Calderón 2024).

    Each time I performed the poem, it evoked strong affective resonance and inspired the audience and discussants to tell me about their relations to autism, disabilities, or suicide while staying in IR. Many expressed how hearing the poem validated their experiences and helped them recognise the importance of addressing their pain and creating spaces for collective healing in IR classrooms. Starting at the 2022 International Studies Association Northeast Conference in Baltimore, USA, I began inviting someone from the audience to read the poem instead of reading it myself. Another set of politics started to emerge from there.

    The Autistic revolution cannot be taught…

    The difference between poetry and rhetoric

    is being ready to kill

    yourself

    instead of your children

    (Lorde 1997: 215).

    As you can gather from my first-person writing in the poem, that night, I stopped crying and went home. I managed to finish my degree. Nobody knew of the existence of the poem for many years. I began a Political Science Ph.D. program in the USA. I kept reading about IR scholarship, yet found a contradiction where the scholarship of the field interested me, but the workings of the field excluded me. Now, I am burned out and angry. I got tired of people lecturing me about autism in IR every time I had to drop one class. My frustration grew as I spoke publicly about the challenges Autistic students face in universities, only to encounter half-hearted solutions or outright hostility.

    Growing up, I was more aware of my suicidal tendencies than my autistic traits. Maybe it is because, as a child, I focused more on external things—like not having friends or the strange looks I would get for doing (or not doing) certain things. I knew it was odd how every birthday party ended with me crying under a table because something had gone wrong. Now, both tendencies complement each other. But being suicidal is easier to grasp; it has a material component and a clear focus. For years, autism was a label I wanted to escape. I ran from it over and over, only to find myself becoming it every time. I ran until my body started to shut down and I began to cry… until autism was all I was.

    I grew up in Oaxaca, Mexico, during the 1990s. Then, people around me did not talk about autism. Years later, I came to understand that, as I was growing up, dis/abled people and those with disabilities were being progressively included—though often co-opted—into conversations about politics, public policy, and human rights in Mexico and around the world. I witnessed the gradual creation and popularisation of new vocabularies, strategies, demands, and both lived and academic knowledge about dis/abilities and autism. However, these changes largely unfolded outside the ‘traditional’ educational spaces, where people like me, who could pass as ‘normal’ or ‘abled,’ were allowed to study. So, it is understandable that, at the time, most people, including myself, did not know much, if any, about diagnosis/dis-identifications, special education/segregation, or accommodations/‘how to fit in without structural changes,’ nor was there enough visibility about the social advocacy about dis/ability rights.

    In school, neither my parents nor my teachers knew how to react, nor how to deal, much less how to live, with Autistic people or those with autistic behaviour. They decided, and decided for me, to look away. Aside from obsessively reading everything about autism in public libraries and bookshops during secondary school, I spent most of my life teaching myself how to survive. I discovered many behavioural treatments by trying to survive in the peripheries. I learned that headphones, strict schedules, sports, small groups, and being considered the ‘smart kid’ in class were all useful ways to navigate life when your behaviour aligns with autism.

    Things changed when I moved to Mexico City for my undergraduate studies. As the second-largest city in the Americas after São Paulo and the capital of a highly centralised state, the megacity exposed me to new layers of medicalisation and ableism. At the same time, it introduced me to activism and gave me access to powerful actors shaping public policy for the entire country.

    Over the years, my younger sister became the first specialist in Oaxaca focusing on the diagnosis, treatment, and public awareness of autism, influencing institutional and governmental policies on the subject. Meanwhile, through prizes and the cultivation of words and communities, I became an active participant in Mexico’s Autistic movement, caring about the lives of Autistic comrades and resisting the State and institutional responses ‘about us, without us.’2

    The concept of ableism became an important tool I encountered through the Autistic movement. Ableism is a ‘network of beliefs, processes and practices that produces a particular kind of self and body (the corporeal standard) that is projected as the perfect, species-typical and therefore essential and fully human. Disability then is cast as a diminished state of being human.’ (Campbell 2001: 44). Once you begin recognising ableism in the world, you can start identifying and working to change harmful patterns of oppression. For instance, Stephen Michael Christian analysed the uses of autistic metaphors in IR scholarship. He showed how IR scholars, often unconsciously, sustain ‘an [ableist] ideology... that dehumanizes Autistic people through the use of metaphors that allude to negative stereotypes.’ (2018: 465).

    Medicalisation is another recurring theme in conversations about autism. It refers to the global political economies that shape, limit, and regulate social relations using medical knowledge to define and treat what is considered an ‘average,’ ‘normal,’ or ‘neurotypical’ body. This medical knowledge encompasses fields like eugenics, biomedical sciences, psy-sciences (including psychology, psychiatry, psychotherapy, psychoanalysis, and neuroscience), and legal medicine.

    By emphasising—or even inventing—specific body ontologies, parts, and processes, as well as techniques and procedures for diagnosis and treatment, these economies also influence the emergence and the agendas of resistance and social movements. Scholars have demonstrated how medicalisation in the Americas is deeply tied to colonisation, US imperial governance, international security, human rights and international development regimes, and transnational networks of medical knowledge. These systems produce and perpetuate class-based, Christian, homophobic, moral-based, nationality-based, racist, and sexist body hierarchies to the detriment of those labelled as ‘autistic’ (McGuire 2016), ‘disabled’ (Mitchell and Snyder 2015), ‘diseased’ (Amador 2015), ‘mentally ill’ (Howell 2011), ‘homosexual’ (McManus 2014), or ‘perverse’ (Díaz Calderón 2019).

    I understand neurotypical classrooms and education as those requiring an ‘average’/‘normal’/‘neurotypical’ behaviour due to the structural ableism and medicalisation imposed through formal and informal education norms. Apart from learning to navigate neurotypical education, I cultivated other more subtle and affective tools for survival. As many of my bosses, therapists, and teachers have noticed, one of my Autistic ‘superpowers’ is that I have got used to dealing with failure and loving my defeated self. I learned to face people telling me I was not worthy by cultivating fortified self-love. That process was not easy; on many days, I was still the undergrad crying in an empty room, feeling sorry for their existence. I had to cultivate a kind of love strong enough to face structural injustices every day. I still do it during morning, afternoon, or evening walks.

    Other survival tools include learning how to cry to release endorphins, picking myself up again, and pretending it never happened. I came to terms with the reality that most people—including those who love me—will not help me, and I have learned that I will be okay regardless. I have gotten moderately good at faking control and handling being called names or antisocial. The hardest part, though, has been learning to endure moments of distress, the stares, and the pity.

    Both the ‘poetic’ and ‘optimistic’ fortified self-love and the more ‘practical’ and ‘cruel’ tools of survival are key to facing ableism and medicalisation. When hearing the stories of people marked by autism, I often focus on finding practical tips and paths toward self-love. However, emotions derive their meaning from situated experiences. Fortified self-love can mean many things—even becoming an oppressive force—if it is not understood as a response to ableism in IR classrooms, rooted in particular times and places, and connected to diverse stories of resistance against medicalisation.

    Neurotypical classrooms can be spaces where some Autistic individuals thrive. Patrick Thaddeus Jackson, a successful IR professor, noted in the foundational book about Autobiographical IR that ‘the proverbial absent-minded professor was probably on the spectrum, and the sciences and the priesthood both feature a surplus of structured behaviors and interactions that can be very comforting to an autistic’ (Jackson 2011: 168-169). Yet, these same neurotypical classrooms can be harmful to other Autistic individuals. This is why IR scholars of colour who work with autoethnography and poetry emphasise the importance of listening to intersectional, situated knowledge (Calderón and Munhazim 2023; Norwood 2017).

    To clarify, I build through autoethnography and poetry an affective ecosystem between the reader and the poet, enabling the reader to better understand the diverse and open-ended forms of violence experienced by people of colour marked by autism and the urgent need to act ourselves to transform these violent social structures. My goal is not to provide universal tools that work for everyone, everywhere—each body and place has its unique diversities, needs, and constraints.

    My disinterest in the seduction theoretical debates, in favour of focusing on anti-violence ethics and relations useful for people in marginalised positions (to transform violent settings), has been previously advocated by IR feminist storytelling (Disch 2003). A similar approach, emphasising connection-making through IR autoethnography, was later advanced by Doty (2010).

    Behind those lessons, life keeps unfolding. Sometimes, I try to ignore the people who stare. I tell myself that everyone has a little autism and depression in them. Popular culture makes this idea easier to believe. We are now comfortable talking about introverted personalities and joking about how everyone born in the 1990s is depressed. There are plenty of shows and movies with Autistic characters who seem to be doing just fine. Stories of people overcoming depression have become so prevalent that they feel almost formulaic, offering recipes for recovery: running, exercising, going to therapy, and building a support system—ideally with longtime friends.

    When I mentioned that my body wasn’t functioning—that all I could do that weekend was cry and sleep, cry to sleep—, an IR professor responded that it was normal, that many students face similar struggles at the end of the semester. This ‘comfort’ in commonality makes a compelling argument for why taking an interest in the lives of Autistic students and those with Autistic traits benefits everyone, not just some supposedly ‘burdensome’ others.

    Those narratives of commonality erase the unique differences I felt growing up and still feel now. Before I cultivated my Autistic ‘superpowers,’ I wanted to be something else. I wished I could distance myself from the ‘Autistic’ label—an aspiration that became harder as time passed, disciplinary demands intensified, and the ‘Autistic’ label began to offer a seductive epistemic and policy framework for action.

    In my journey of cultivating Autistic self-love, I have made a variety of mind-shifts to both recognise my many layers of privilege and critically resist systemic injustices. One privilege I have come to recognise is that I was a ‘passing-abled’ kid in the classroom. Most of my teachers never noticed the redness of my eyes from crying all night. They did not question why I failed to turn in homework, even though I excelled on the assignments I did complete. My parents did not question it either.

    Over time, I grew tired of explaining myself and hearing the same responses: ‘You need to toughen up. We’re preparing you for the real world, where accommodations don’t exist. Eventually, you’ll understand that this system works because it has to generalise for students with varying strengths and interests. And after a few more years of education, you’ll be free and happy to pursue the research you truly care about.’ Lies.

    Dear abled people:

    I am already resilient. Look at how many of us are here, how I—and those of us who remain in universities—no longer cry out in public. I don’t want, even if I sometimes need, accommodations just to survive in an institution hostile to my existence, one that ties my chance to move forward to a degree. I don’t want to preserve this world as it is. Instead, I dream of building a world where differences are celebrated and solidarities are nurtured.

    I’m not even convinced the ‘inclusive’ world you envision exists—or ever truly did. I understand you feel bound to uphold certain norms, but if we are challenging them, it’s because they fail many, if not most, students. You might not see this because those norms make you feel good. It’s strange how much pleasure some people derive from being labelled ‘special.’ It seems being ‘gifted’ because we’re Autistic doesn’t hold the same weight as being ‘gifted’ for conforming to the racist and sexist ideals of IR.3

    What I can’t stand is how Professors’ sense of doing ‘good’ often translates into wielding their authority to erase us or brand us as failures. We are labelled as failures even while the same ableist system thrives on our ability to ‘think outside the box,’ our quick memorisation, or our talent for grasping complex, abstract concepts with ease.

    I know that ‘we’ here really means ‘I,’ but many IR scholars speak in universal terms, cloaking their “professional” opinions as theories. So, I think it’s only fair to do the same.4

    I used to believe the narratives about how I needed to discipline my body and feel shame when my emotions disrupted the norms. During graduate school, I realised that disciplining my body often meant suppressing my possibilities of existence. ‘Possibilities of existence’ can serve as a metaphor for the different ways we survive and live in the world—ways we often study as political, even though some of us do not want to be political all the time. While some choose to make their personal political, others are made to be political. ‘Possibilities of existence’ also reflects the tangible decline of my physical body: hair falling out, skin rashes spreading, uncontrollable trembling while crying at night, or the sheer inability to move. It can also evoke an awareness of the consequences of suicidal tendencies in environments that consistently trigger them.

    My body became one of my greatest teachers, showing me my limits, the limitations of formal education, and the importance of stopping the futile desire to be valued by those who refuse—or are unable—to truly listen. It also revealed the necessity of transforming structures to celebrate (and enable) (neuro)-diversity in education—not merely treating it as an optional, ‘nice-to-have’ addition to existing frameworks.

    The possibilities of existence place me in a paradox: I simultaneously desire the education that is killing me and refuse it through fortified self-love. I adapt to remain within the system—to gain enough power and financial stability to support my communities—while also yearning to dismantle it and cultivate territories for autonomous pedagogies to grow.

    Yet in an IR classroom

    I was only too happy to be accepted by the school.

    There was the swing, there was the jungle jim and plenty of space to play and be a free bird.

    But a school was a school.

    And the school had to be with some rules.

    I was as undisciplined as ever. Being a free bird was not expected from any student. I got addicted to the swing, with the breeze which I felt on my face along with the to and fro movements.

    (Mukhopadhyay 2000: 69).

    I wrote I’m sorry in the provisional office at the Mexican university where I completed my IR undergraduate degree. I had begun my undergraduate studies two years earlier at the largest university in Latin America, pursuing a degree in Mathematics. My decision to switch schools was partly due to my parents’ ceasing financial support. As an internal migrant, I faced a significant hurdle: the university where I was studying Mathematics lacked a strong financial aid program, which was a common issue among public universities in Mexico.

    There was also a profound shift in my life. I developed severe depression, spurred by the suicide of a close friend, my grandmother’s passing, and a ligament injury that forced me to stop practising martial arts—which I did more than half my life. During my fourth semester, my perfect Grade Point Average (GPA) slipped; I received a 9 out of 10 in one class and had to drop others. After losing my parents’ support, my education in Mathematics was sustained by excellence prizes from external institutions, which demanded either a flawless GPA or an impressive academic record without course withdrawals. I lost those options with my depression, and I began looking for alternatives.

    Years after doing mathematics at an intensely demanding level—competing in national mathematical contests since secondary school—I felt the need for a change. I wanted to pursue something that might provide me with tools to understand how the oppression I observed around me became normalised in society. The IR curricula I reviewed at Mexican universities stood out for their impressive interdisciplinarity, offering what seemed like a promising avenue to start understanding the world differently. I applied to several programs. One of Mexico’s most prestigious private universities offered me the only full-tuition scholarship for their IR program, awarded to the top-performing applicant based on the admission exam. This scholarship was further supplemented by additional financial aid to address my economic needs. I accepted.

    I wrote the poem in response to a pivotal moment in one of my classes. Until then, I had been excelling: I achieved the highest possible grades on every test and assignment, except for one. The issue arose when I became ‘useless’ just days before the deadline for the final paper. Although I had completed most of the work, my body shut down, and I could not transform my partial draft into a finished submission.

    I told the lecturer about my situation, that I could not concentrate or write. The professor extended the deadline to coincide with the final exam date. While I managed to complete the final exam and present my paper in class, I still could not write at home. The structure and urgency of an exam, with the assurance that it would not be insurmountably challenging, tend to calm me. The control of having a presentation on a subject I have studied carefully, and my capacity to speak non-stop about the topics I am researching, make delivering a presentation enjoyable.

    I attempted to dictate the paper to my computer, but the technology available to me was not advanced enough to accurately recognise my voice. Each night, I went to bed in tears, crying myself to sleep. One of those nights, my body gave out entirely, and I ended up in the emergency room. There, I was medicated intravenously, leaving my arms covered in bruises. I bore the physical marks of the struggle that everyone seemed to need as ‘proof’ of my illness when I said I was not well. Yet, those visible wounds meant little—to me or to the professor.

    On the day of the final exam, I explained to the professor that I had not been able to write at home, but I understood the material, and that I was hoping for an extension. My visibly wounded body was there for everyone to see. But it was already too late. The professor, focused on advancing within the Department’s ranks and maintaining a ‘professional’ image, dismissed my request. I understood such reasoning; after all, I had often been expected to look the other way and endure—or conceal—my pain so that others (abled, white, heterosexual, more powerful...) would not have their feelings (and pockets) hurt. In the end, I received none of the 40% of points allocated to the final paper. I wrote the poem when I saw my final grade: 60%. Just enough to pass, but a disaster for my scholarship.

    In Mexico, IR exerts control over your body when your survival depends on scholarships. The same is true when you lack health insurance outside of the overburdened free government healthcare system—where my psychiatrist could only spare a few minutes once a month to prescribe medication—and when you are forced to take informal jobs to make ends meet while simultaneously signing declarations for scholarships stating that you are not employed.

    Fortunately, my performance in Mathematics, Statistics, Law, and Economics classes saved me. Those subjects kept my overall average high enough. Those disciplines focused solely on the written and oral exam results. That grade held significant weight, as the top-performing student in the class received an invitation to become the teacher’s research assistant. At the time, the professor was working on and teaching topics I found deeply compelling. Instead of being offered the assistantship, the professor stopped speaking to me entirely. I felt an overwhelming sense of embarrassment, especially when the professor remarked, ‘I am disappointed in you.’ Ultimately, I had to seek out another precarious job—or, perhaps more aptly, another so-called ‘learning opportunity’ to survive.

    Since my last semester in undergraduate education, I have been wondering why a lot of people in the classroom found me annoying. Maybe it is because I forced my classmates to act as if they should have cared about the education they were receiving and, by doing so, I displaced the attention from how they are forced by the capitalistic system and the illusion of meritocracy to get higher education to enter an already saturated, exploitative, and ever devaluating market-job. I came to that conclusion after one of my friends tried to explain to me why people at our university hated me. The conversation started with me asking her: ‘Do you think people in that class hate me?’. I explained how some people openly ignored me when I tried to speak to them. My friend promptly replied: ‘Yes. Why? Was it not obvious?’. I was stunned—it had not been obvious to me at all.

    I can only guess, but perhaps my classmates disliked me because I was the one who always excelled in public, consistently achieving the highest grades regardless of the volume of reading or how unappealing the assignments were. Because I could decide what was the hard stuff in the classroom with my speech act: ‘Professor, I think this time the reading was difficult.’ That the time I got bad grades (which were usually zero as I was in the hospital, in my bed, or just could not work for a weekend), it was not by my incompetence, but because something weird happened, outliers we needed to overlook to make the model work. They hated me because they did not understand me. Look at him, he is fine now, look at him: he seems fine. Ask him questions so you get that he is fine.

    My speech and survival mechanisms were technologies legitimising a neurotypical system, which, ironically, was disabling me. I was the example that justified silencing my classmates’ complaints. While I stood alone, I earned the professors’ respect. They knew that whenever they sought reassurance, they could turn to me, and I would provide comforting words. After all, I relied on my scholarship to afford food, medication, and rent, enabling me to endure the system. I performed emotional labour for them because I needed their support, hoping they would choose to overlook my ‘anomalies.’

    I wrote that poem because I was convinced, I could not earn a degree under those same conditions. The only reason I succeeded was that, on other occasions, teachers in positions of power chose not to look the other way and extended their help. Understanding the alternatives available to each student is challenging: it requires teachers to actively ask and truly listen to the stories of students’ wounded bodies, all while navigating the complexities of their own experiences. For me, listening entails a humility that allows for hesitation, the ability to suspend the impulse to assume you know better than Autistic individuals what works for them.

    Humility is equally important in conversations with other Autistic people. The autism that inspires me to identify as Autistic is different from what motivates another person to do the same, and my needs and strategies often differ from or even conflict with those of others. Listening with an openness to tensions and contradictions enables teachers, medical professionals, parents, and policymakers to find meaningful answers in our histories and bodies.

    In the face of everyday and structural ableism, I discovered some strategies for survival.5 Asking Autistic individuals provided me with options I was not ready—or equipped—to fully understand or implement. Yet, rather than dismissing those options, I learned to struggle with them and imagine an academia otherwise. Living in ableist environments is, inherently, a creative endeavour.

    Institutionally, there are extensive legal and empirical resources available to advocate for and highlight the benefits of engaging with autism (Baker 2011). Additionally, IR students and scholars have contributed their recommendations for addressing autism both within and beyond the classroom (e.g. Harmon 2017; Szendrő 2017). For me, the failure to act when everything seemed fine often led to even greater consequences when things inevitably went awry. Autism Studies consistently reminded me that the price of willful neglect is far too high—our lives. Many works by Autistic activists begin with stark reminders of the Autistic individuals whose lives were taken by ableism (see The Autistic Self Advocacy Network 2012).

    While I survived, I understood that listening and fostering dialogue in neurotypical classrooms, shaped by the remnants of neo-colonialism, imperialism, patriarchy, and capitalism, is profoundly challenging. I was among the fortunate ones. My discourse—here and elsewhere—inevitably silences others. This dynamic is beyond my control; it is structural. I am one of those perceived as ‘normal,’ someone who received significant help and special treatment. I am easily cast as a ‘success story,’ with my critiques of neurotypical education often overshadowed by a focus on my achievements and ‘resilience.’ This is why I resonate with post-colonial/communist/conflict and trans/feminist/queer IR scholars writing about the banality of critique in environments where one is repeatedly asked to propose alternatives, only for those alternatives to be ignored or dismissed (Hozić 2016; Díaz Calderón 2024). Instead, the conversation often pivots to well-packaged, simple-to-consume, explain, and implement ‘solutions’ that fail to address the structural changes being demanded.

    Paradoxically, the privileged conditions of my survival acted on me. I no longer prioritise my survival. Being suicidal has granted me a detachment from the fear of my own death, whether physical or academic. Yet, after years of resistance within oppressive systems, I am acutely aware that my existence—and even my death—can be weaponised to invalidate other experiences. I also recognise the profound pain my absence could cause to those I love and those I have conflicted relationships with. This includes advisers, students, teachers, colleagues, coauthors, funders, administrative staff, cleaning workers, and fellow activists and artivists.

    So, what now? When I began sharing this narrative publicly, I was often asked: ‘Why do you remain in IR? It sounds like such a terrible environment.’ My answer has always been that IR, despite its flaws, contains cracks—moments of possibility and brilliance. There is extraordinary scholarship here that I struggle to find elsewhere: works that weave stories of connection across borders, oppressions, and expressions of love.6 And really, where else could we go? In my journey across borders, I have been welcomed into rooms for a day or two in various houses (and disciplines), but I have yet to find a decolonial home where I could truly stay. Materially, without savings and having given too much, I’ve been left with too little—almost no space for (academic) movement.

    I end this section in hesitation. Maybe I am staying in IR precisely because I am suicidal. After all, staying in IR is just another way of dying. I’m not sorry, this is my death.

    Autistic global political economies... About us with us

    It’s never too late for someone else to have your happy childhood.

    (Chen 2018: 25).

    After everything I have shared in this text, an internationalist reader might ask: What does autism mean to you? Why do you identify yourself and your life story with autism? Why should you re-search the academic and disciplinary significance of autism? These are my best answers for now: Autism is what we collectively make of it, for what, and with whom. For someone like me, with the privilege of passing as abled through masking, identifying as Autistic is deeply tied to the politics and legacies that I can invoke by naming aspects of my life as Autistic.7 Autism is increasingly present in the key arenas of IR through international and local legislation as well as transnational and grassroots activism. Engaging with and for Autistic people is critical to interrogate whether international relations regarding autism are being constructed and enacted without Autistic individuals, to assess the impact of their exclusion, and to imagine how IR might transform if activists, scholars, and policymakers were to listen and collaborate in solidarity with emerging Autistic-led movements at both local and transnational scales. In what follows, I unpack each response through the conocimiento—a decolonial orientation toward embodied knowledge—that I had acquired from my experience as an Autistic person navigating ableist borders and boundaries.8

    My hesitation to define autism stems from recognising the immense power that states, markets, and individuals wield to regulate it. As John J. Pitney, Jr. observed,

    Autism is ‘political’ in a broader sense… Just about everything concerning autism is subject to dispute. What is it? What causes it? How many different kinds of it are there? Who has it? What can we do about it? Is it even the right [political] problem to be thinking about? […] The stakes are high: […] the national cost of supporting people with autism adds up to $236 billion per year (Pitney 2015: 1).

    Amid these political debates, Autistic people are forced to navigate and grapple with definitions and perceptions of autism just to survive in ableist societies. As a result, I choose to focus my energy and resources on exploring autism through the lived knowledge and needs of specific Autistic individuals. Additionally, my experiences traversing linguistic, geographic, and sexual borders and boundaries highlight how English concepts, such as autism, are both insufficient for fully capturing global politics and products of transnational political economies that shape bodies, minds, and emotions in unexpected ways.

    What I absorbed from libraries and bookshops about autism as a child and later as an adolescent gave me the impression that to claim autism, I would need a formal diagnosis from a psychologist or psychiatrist. This process would require emphasising the body-mind traits that make me ‘Autistic.’9 The diagnostician, in turn, would be expected to adhere to standards set by institutions in the USA and Europe, applying them as though I, the Autistic subject, existed in isolation from the broader social and political forces shaping my behaviour.10 Following a diagnosis, I would either be offered—or perhaps resigned to—the prospect of living an ‘Autistic’ life, while simultaneously grappling with what that life might mean: for whom, with whom, for what, and where.

    As Melanie Yergeau eloquently describes within the English-language discourse surrounding autism:

    My hand and full-body movements became self-stimulatory behaviors; my years-long obsession with maps and the Electric Light Orchestra became perseverations; my repetition of lines from the movie Airplane! became echolalia…. This, my body, this was autism—and suddenly, with the neuropsychologist’s signature on my diagnostic papers, I was no longer my body’s author (Yergeau 2018: 1).

    While Melanie Yergeau’s account reflects the medical disciplining of ‘Autistic’ lives and bodies through diagnosis, Autistic individuals who are also Brown, homosexual, or economically disadvantaged in the USA but hold Mexican citizenship must contend with additional layers. They navigate how the contours of autism are largely constructed through the social experiences and imaginations of white, heterosexual, and affluent individuals, often in countries like the USA, where those creating these narratives possess the privileges of citizenship in the place they reside.

    According to popular opinion… Autism = (white male-presenting) toddler wearing a Thomas the Train t-shirt; autism = (white male-presenting) quirky teen gamer; autism = (white male-presenting) geeky computer programmer; autism = (white male-presenting) adult rocking and staring off into space… a ready scapegoat for all of their caregiver’s life disappointments; autism = Temple Gradin, puzzle pieces, ABA therapy, and Autism Speaks (Onaiwu 2017: xv).

    During my adolescence, I experienced body-mind crises tied to the meanings of autism and their intersections with class, gender, nationality, race, and sexuality.11 Yet, I had no formal medical diagnosis because I lived in a state in Mexico without autism specialists. I understood myself as autistic according to psychiatric standards. I completed numerous checklists that highlighted autistic traits, and the challenges they described for Autistic people resembled my daily interactions.

    The forms of domination tied to psy-sciences—those I would later endure and sometimes strategically use while living in Mexico City—did not shape me during my upbringing.12 In a way, the neuropsychologist’s signature on my diagnosis was my own. The cultural-media ecosystems defining autism for me were primarily those I consumed on the internet in English. Thus, one could say that the Empire reached me through libraries, bookstores, and cyber cafes. While I faced oppression from the lack of interest or outright refusal of my social environment in Oaxaca to engage with autism, the primary form of discrimination I encountered, especially through my early years to the beginning of my higher education, stemmed from the assumption of homosexuality due to my feminine ways of being.

    In Mexico City, each attempt to inhabit autism, its imaginaries ran through my brain and body in a way that created and reflected realities negating my radical alternatives, my alternative worlds built through transnational solidarities. During my years as an IR undergraduate, as I attended consultations with psychologists and psychiatrists and began medication, I came to view the autistic label primarily as an instrument of power.

    The autistic label served various pragmatic purposes: justifying my absences from class, explaining visible body differences, obtaining medication to meet high academic standards, challenging institutional failures to accommodate bodies beyond the imagined ‘normal’ or ‘average,’ finding strength after severing ties with my family, and gaining access to public debates on autism laws and regulations. Yet, outside the confines of the label, I found ways to transform my bodily immobility into unanticipated forms of mobility. I explored the limits of my body, forged communities with other outcasts, walked across bridges that had not been burned, escaped from self-proclaimed allies who caused harm, and imagined worlds where violent behaviours against people with body-mind differences were not normalised.

    It took years of reading, listening, and sharing space with Autistic activists, scholars, and poets during my undergraduate and graduate education to begin recognising the flexibility and possibilities behind all the medical noise. Since 2015, I have witnessed the emergence and consolidation of activist communities in Mexico City led by Autistic and Neurodivergent individuals for Autistic and Neurodivergent individuals. These include organisations such as Asociación Autistas de México, Mad in México, Orgullo Loco México, SinColectivo México, and Trans Pride World (Gutiérrez 2019a, 2019b; Ordorika Sacristán and Gómez-Aguilar 2021; Rodríguez Everaert 2019). Autism had become synonymous with resistance against ableism.

    Let me provide an example of contemporary Autism politics in Mexico to illustrate this context: during 2014 and 2015, while I was an IR undergraduate student, Federal Deputy Paloma Villaseñor Vargas, who is the mother of an autistic child, organised a public debate that culminated in the approval of Mexico’s most significant autism-related legislation—the General Law for the Attention and Protection of People with Autistic Spectrum Condition. However, she notably excluded Autistic individuals from the debate. Consequently, the resulting law infringed upon the human rights of Autistic people.13

    In contrast, as I transitioned from undergraduate to graduate education, I observed a shift in resistance dynamics. In 2017, during Federal Deputy Leticia Amparano Gámez’s proposal of the General Law on Mental Health in the House of Deputies, and again in 2019 with Federal Senator Geovanna del Carmen Bañuelos de la Torre’s similar proposal in the Senate, Autistic, Disabled, Mad, and Neurodivergent people took a leading role in opposing these legislative initiatives.

    During the legislative process for both proposals of the General Law on Mental Health, activists were systematically excluded from shaping the law. This exclusion violated the principle of public consultation and ignored calls from Autistic, Neurodiverse, and Mad activists for open debate (Montesinos 2020). However, transnational pressure enabled activists to block both proposals successfully. In response to these legislative shortcomings, the Colectivo Salud Mental con Derechos (Mental Health with Rights Collective) was formed, comprising over 120 activists and more than 30 non-governmental organisations, some led by Autistic and Neurodivergent individuals.

    Under the leadership of the Collective, activists, academics, physicians, and politicians collaborated to create a more inclusive legislative framework. Their most significant achievement was the reform of the General Health Law on mental health and addictions, which took effect in May 2022 (Yo También 2020; Jiménez 2022).14 This reform marked a paradigm shift in mental health public policy, moving from a model rooted in isolation, forced treatment, and institutionalisation to one focused on human rights, community-based care, and informed consent.

    When academic writing becomes an unattainable task, I turn to creative writing and reflexive thinking to create breathing spaces. Departing from the linear paths of disciplinary traditions allows me to explore sideway alternatives, such as artistic roads. This shift allows me to move away from IR theories’ rejection of the lived body to an excess of communitarian life.15 It reimagines inter-body-mind relations, transitioning from the paradigm of autonomous, rational subjects to one of inter-dependence and affective communion.

    In my loca times and places, I embrace being nothing and let the void be filled with creative forms.16 Of course, I recognise that lived experiences are never shaped in a vacuum. Acts of violence, desires, habits, histories, institutional constraints, material limitations, meanings, scars, orientations, and traumas all shape and constrain my body’s movements. Yet, listening to the empty spaces within my body-mind has proven useful to subvert its boundaries.

    As seen in neurodiverse legal activism, breathing spaces and inter-dependent, affective communions can foster autistic pedagogies rooted in communal responses and informed consent. Can we imagine working collectively toward the progressive dismantling of neurotypical education?

    Terror, hope, and love as Autistic (auto-ethnographic) IR methodologies

    … I’ve noticed tons of abled activists will happily add ‘ableism’ to the list of stuff they’re against (you know, like that big sign in front of the club in my town that says ‘No racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, ableism’) or throw around the word ‘disability justice’ in the list of ‘justices’ in their manifesto. But then nothing else changes: all their organizing is still run the exact same inaccessible way, with the ten-mile-long marches, workshops that urge people to ‘get out of your seats and move!’ and lack of inclusion of any disabled issues or organizing strategies. And of course none of them think they’re ableist… [Yet, t]hey still silently believe that they’d rather die than be us… (Piepzna-Samarasinha 2018: 123).

    Laura Shepherd revealed being Autistic on Twitter a year after being elected President of the International Studies Association. In her thread, she reflected on the challenges of navigating the ‘“quirky hyperlexic kid to anxious and depressed yet overachieving adult to late-diagnosed #ActuallyAutistic” pipeline.’ Her intersectional autobiographical narrative (yet still cisgender, heterosexual, middle-class, and white) offered a nuanced counterpoint to Patrick Thaddeus Jackson’s more optimistic (cisgender, heterosexual, masculine, middle-class, and white) narrative.

    Shepherd’s announcement added complexity to the Autistic academic narrative landscape, particularly regarding the interplay of autism, depression, suicide, and IR. This dynamic was starkly exemplified in Will H. Moore’s public suicide letter (2017) and the responses it garnered from Autistic (and primarily white) students, such as Harmon (2017) and Szendrő (2017).17

    Building on these dialogues, this article amplifies voices and experiences from social locations that diverge from whiteness, heterosexuality, cisnormativity, middle-class privilege, and the Anglosphere. It also reflects on the affects—terror, hope, and love—that both follow and unsettle existing autobiographical narratives about autism within IR.18 Terror and hope marked my writing in this article, as well as my broader research on autism and suicide. To fully grasp this, it is essential to share my positionality as both an academic and a student during the development of this work. By doing so, I aim to make transparent the possibilities, limitations, and conditions for the reproducibility of the ideas presented here.

    K. Melchor Quick Hall proposed the principle of radically transparent positionality as being articulated through autoethnography to offer ‘the level of transparency required for our analysis to reflect our partial perspectives and motivations’ (2020: 23). In her work with poor Garifuna women in rural Honduras, she underscores the importance of acknowledging how her ‘(social and economic) position is linked to (imperialistic) systems that undermine the struggles of Black and indigenous people’ to make transparent how ‘[t]here have been some exchanges of resources and time, but none that meet my highest standard for solidarity between communities with a shared struggle against patriarchy, racism, and capitalism.’ (Hall 2020: 171). Similarly, my research and transnational solidarity with Autistic people of colour in IR academic spaces demand reflection on my own privileges. These include English language proficiency, access to fellowship funds and academic awards, the benefit of free time from communal care labour facilitated by networks of solidarity, and the support of academic communities led by influential tenured Professors in the Anglosphere who back my work on violence against Autistic students as an Autistic researcher myself.

    The following paragraphs expand on the concepts of centring researchers’ affects and embracing radically transparent positionalities to demonstrate the paradoxes inherent in writing IR scholarship with and for Autistic people while navigating one’s identity as (or as not) an Autistic person. This exploration moves beyond a simplistic cataloguing of privileges or oppressions or the binary framing of a researcher’s identity as either victim or oppressor. Instead, this autoethnographic conclusion seeks to open a dialogue about incorporating fear, hope, and love as methodologies in scholarly practice.19

    My aspiration is for IR scholars to adopt the principle of radically transparent author positionality, critically reflecting on whether they are capable of ‘meeting their highest standard for solidarity.’ This reflection is particularly pressing for scholars who are not part of the same communities of Autistic researchers ‘struggling against ableism, patriarchy, racism, and capitalism.’ By applying this principle and engaging with their own power and capacity for harm, IR scholars can better understand the importance of building critical transnational solidarities with Autistic researchers and how to do it.

    In conducting this research, I encountered terror from three sources. First, I was inundated with narratives—many autobiographical and (made) close to me—about the challenges faced by (post)/(de)colonial feminist and queer scholars of colour in pursuing academic careers—if also their tools for fighting back—, particularly when they dared to critique the very institutions that fed them. I felt immense fear about writing this article, compounded by my lack of a Ph.D. and the vulnerability of my position within the academic hierarchy. At the time, I still envisioned a future rooted in IR research, and I did not want to make my life harder considering my health and the people I help(ed) financially.

    Another source of fear stemmed from implying to folks marked by autism that IR could ever be a welcoming space for them. By that time, I was acutely aware of how problematic—and potentially deadly—it could be to offer generalised advice without carefully considering the specific positionality of the individual within intersecting systems of oppression. I understood the critical importance of grounding any recommendations in a robust conocimiento that accounted for the workings in praxis of those pieces of advice for people in similar locations. For instance, it could be disastrous to direct a person of colour at a predominantly white university in the USA to a suicide emergency hotline that involves police intervention without their consent (see Díaz Calderón 2024). Similarly, it would be misguided to assume that poor people of colour without US citizenship could find meaningful solidarity in predominantly white, middle-to-upper-class activist groups of Autistic people and those with autistic traits who hold US citizenship (see Brown, Ashkenazy and Onaiwu 2017).

    Finally, I was overwhelmed by a sense of not having a choice and urgency. There were no volunteers stepping forward to share the burden of my daily autistic care work within my communities. Many IR scholars, including some who self-identified as Autistic, seemed eager to write about autism and disabilities without creating material spaces for all individuals identified with autism (see Christian 2018). As if autism was only accommodating to/for (critiquing) already acceptable IR. Now, as I find myself on medical leave, uncertain about whether I can or even want to return to academia, I can say that my body, once again, was right.

    Hope—I am not entirely sure where it came from, but I was truly optimistic about the potential for IR scholars to transform our field into one that fosters the flourishing of differences. Perhaps it stemmed from my immersion in writing workshops and activism spaces led by (Autistic) people of colour for people of colour marked by autism. Maybe it was inspired by my readings of (post)/(de)colonial trans/feminist/queer IR scholars, whose works seemed largely unknown in my academic program. It might have also been rooted in witnessing my own body endure relentless humiliation, institutional violence, and social exclusions—yet somehow still manage to perform, though increasingly less so, in classroom settings. Or perhaps it was in the testimonies of friends and colleagues, revealing how systems of oppression and rigid institutions seemed to bend, even slightly, when their limits were tested. Now, as I find myself on medical leave, uncertain about whether I can or even want to return to academia, I can say that my body can be wrong.

    Rather than presenting an argument about how emotions, purpose, and subjectivity challenge the principles of distance, value-neutrality, and objectivity in IR scholarship (on autism)20, this conclusion sought to demonstrate how terror, hope, and love can be considered as methodologies. Reflecting on how ‘Trans scholars are likely either to be suicidal or to have suicidal friends,’ Ruth Pearce (2020: 808) proposed ‘to treat challenges associated with structures of oppression as a methodological problem.’ In doing so, she emphasised how aspects like ‘ethical responsibility towards the self, and a supportive community of scholarship’ could be integrated to methodology discussions in academia with the intention of ‘support[ing] the survival of scholars who are themselves marginalised, especially if they are also studying marginalised communities; for if the researcher cannot survive the research process, then something has gone horribly wrong with the methodological design of their study.’ (Pearce 2020: 809). She further argued that ‘[f]or researchers without access to particular forms of privilege, exercising an ethical responsibility towards the self and being positioned within a supportive community of scholarship are not simply methods for coping, but necessary components of knowledge production.’ (Pearce 2020: 818).21

    In this article, terror and hope emerged as methodological tools, essential for both surviving and writing. These affects also drove my need to articulate a fortified self-love. Terror underscored the necessity of fortifying self-love, while hope sustained the possibility of communal love. Viewing terror, hope, and love as methodologies encourages scholars to consider both the epistemologies needed to understand the conditions of being Autistic in academia and the specific needs of Autistic individuals conducting research projects. This perspective also highlights the limitations of neurotypical methods training, which often fails to account for these needs. As typically taught, IR methods can lead Autistic scholars to miscalculate the feasibility of their research, particularly because they overlook the resources required to ensure they survive their research.

    While various neurotypical classrooms and neoliberal (affective) responses aim to mitigate violence, these affective methodologies offer a situated set of tools for survival within, but not beyond, neoliberal education. Some comrades have advanced ‘abolitionist’ frameworks to transcend neoliberal education (Motta et al. 2024). However, this article assumes that the level of solidarity required to fully achieve neurodiversity is not yet attainable, and that the costs of quitting, refusing, or leaving such education are prohibitive for those with care responsibilities.

    Additionally, this text does not propose alternative teaching practices for regions where Autism Studies and psy-sciences lack significant normative (imperial) influence, as was true for Oaxaca during my upbringing and perhaps remains true today. In such contexts, one might inquire whether it is possible to implement pedagogies beyond neurotypical classrooms without first developing fortified self-love to endure the inevitable humiliations and gaslighting involved in the process.22 Today, I question if I can ‘kill myself instead of my students’ or whether it is already ‘too late for another Autistic student to have my happy IR education.’

    The point of departure of this article was to imagine what to do when the communities you expected to understand and celebrate your differences instead ask you to conform or, as in the case of the white, middle-class scholars referenced earlier, offer survival strategies that fail to account for your intersectional positionalities. It assumed a context in which you lack the power or supportive communities necessary to resist and envision (m)otherwise, to birth alternative worlds. Hopefully, as students and scholars of colour cultivate autistic self-love and build transnational networks of neurodiverse care to secure their survival (and share the costs of failure), we can continue advancing conversations on abolition and refusal.

    This concluding section illustrated how terror, hope, and love illuminated my miscalculations—both regarding the labour required to remain in academia as an Autistic scholar of colour and the magnitude and forms of power sustaining the ableist systems creating those burdens. These systems include the power that upholds social and methodological barriers to understanding violence against Autistic people and the pathways to addressing it. This article advocates for listening to our bodies and cultivating theories and scholarship informed by the material conditions of each Autistic scholar of colour.

    By considering terror, hope, and love as methodologies, we can broaden the scope of our research approaches. For instance, we might argue for incorporating time and resources to address emotions into research design and grant proposals. Alternatively, we could work to cultivate communities with other Autistic scholars of colour to share the burdens and privileges of conducting research—understanding that the tools we have may not suffice for everyone and that we may need to build new ones collaboratively rather than replicate existing frameworks.

    Fortified self-love was essential in reflecting on the magnitude and forms of power already attained by marginalised IR scholars and in sustaining resistance against the violence inflicted on marginalised individuals within neurotypical IR classrooms and texts. Could we make fortresses unnecessary?
