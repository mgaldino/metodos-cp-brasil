Peeking Behind the Arches: An Examination of Diplomats’ Party Affiliations and Candidacies in Brazil

Espreitando por trás dos arcos: um exame das filiações partidárias e candidaturas de diplomatas no Brasil

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article explores the intricate dynamics between Brazilian diplomats and political parties within the realm of foreign policy. Our central research problem is to uncover how the interplay of the party system’s structure, the attributes and ideologies of political parties, and the aspirations of bureaucrats collectively influence the extent of diplomats’ involvement in formal party-political activities. Despite the growing attention to partisanship in Brazilian foreign policy debate, empirical research and methodological approaches to measure the concept are limited. The construct validity of the term is often confused with ideologization and politicization. Moreover, the involvement of Brazilian bureaucrats in party politics lacks comprehensive investigation. To address these gaps, this study examines the relationship between diplomats and political parties, uncovering its multifaceted dimensions and implications. Using a novel data source, we explore diplomats’ affiliations and candidacies, shedding light on interaction patterns during the Nova República period. We present a set of hypotheses investigating the association between diplomats’ party affiliations and their interactions with a) party ideologies (H1) and b) professional ambitions, delineated into i) intra-bureaucratic advantages (H2.1) and ii) political career aspirations (H2.2). By employing a mixed methods approach, incorporating thematic content analysis, descriptive statistics, and hypothesis testing, we aggregated evidence that substantiates hypotheses H1 and H2.2. However, in the case of H2.1, the findings proved inconclusive, prompting the need for further exploration into the topic.

    Keywords
    affiliation; candidacies; parties; partisanship; foreign policy; bureaucracy

    Resumo

    Este artigo investiga a intrincada dinâmica entre os diplomatas brasileiros e os partidos políticos no contexto da política externa. Nosso problema central de pesquisa busca desvendar como a interação entre a estrutura do sistema partidário, os atributos e ideologias dos partidos políticos e as aspirações dos burocratas moldam coletivamente a extensão do envolvimento dos diplomatas em atividades político-partidárias formais. Apesar da crescente atenção ao partidarismo no debate sobre a política externa brasileira, a pesquisa empírica e as abordagens metodológicas para medir o conceito são limitadas. A validade do construto do termo é frequentemente confundida com ideologização e politização. Além disso, o envolvimento dos burocratas brasileiros na política partidária carece de uma investigação abrangente. Para abordar essas lacunas, este estudo examina a relação entre diplomatas e partidos políticos, revelando suas dimensões e implicações multifacetadas. Usando uma nova fonte de dados, exploramos as filiações e candidaturas dos diplomatas, lançando luz sobre os padrões de interação durante o período da Nova República. Apresentamos um conjunto de hipóteses que investigam a associação entre as filiações partidárias dos diplomatas e suas interações com a) ideologias partidárias (H1) e b) ambições profissionais, delineadas em i) vantagens intraburocráticas (H2.1) e ii) aspirações de carreira política (H2.2). Ao empregar uma abordagem de métodos mistos, incorporando a análise de conteúdo temático, estatísticas descritivas e testes de hipóteses, agregamos evidências que fundamentam as hipóteses H1 e H2.2. No entanto, no caso da H2.1, as descobertas se mostraram inconclusivas, o que suscitou a necessidade de uma maior exploração do tópico.

    Introduction

    An illuminating facet of the discourse surrounding partisanship and Brazilian foreign policy is embodied in the viewpoint articulated by Luiz Felipe Lampreia. He expressed that ‘the ideologization of foreign policy, the adoption of partisan criteria, should not be admitted’ (Lampreia, cited in Belém Lopes 2013: 305). Lampreia further underscored the distinctiveness of the association between the administration and Itamaraty during the Lula administration, observing that party affiliation had not been obligatory at Itamaraty before (Lampreia, cited in Belém Lopes 2013: 305).

    Conversely, Celso Amorim, who served as the Foreign Minister during Lula da Silva’s tenure, presented a counterargument to critics of foreign policy during his leadership. He asserted that ‘when people say that the PT’s foreign policy was partisan, it’s a lie. The party does hold influence, much like the Democratic or Republican Party in the USA’ (Uol 2017). Amorim’s statement carries a certain ambivalence and reservation concerning the term ‘partisanship.’ While he rejects the party’s direct role in foreign policy, he implicitly acknowledges its impact.

    ‘By remaining silent, the perception was created that diplomats were devoid of party affiliations or ideological inclinations, unaffected by the shifts in political dynamics, and solely dedicated to safeguarding the State’s interests’ (Sequeira 2007, emphasis added). In 1902, upon assuming the role of Foreign Affairs, Baron Rio Branco, a respected figure in Brazilian diplomacy, stated: ‘I am not here to serve a political party; I am here to serve our beloved Brazil, which we all aspire to see united, principled, robust, and respected’ (Ministério das Relações Exteriores 2012).

    This sentiment set the stage for the perception that engagement with ‘party politics’ by Brazilian foreign policy actors was undesirable, as evidenced by derogatory terms like ‘politicization,’ ‘partisanship,’ and ‘ideologization’ (Lafer 2009; Barbosa 2010). Itamaraty, the Brazilian Ministry of Foreign Affairs, has maintained a ‘unique pattern of engagement in Brazilian domestic political controversies: despite considering itself impartial, it has fashioned itself into a sort of bureaucratic faction, with its programmatic objectives intertwined with the proverbial “national interest”‘ (Belém Lopes and Carvalho 2016).

    This context raises the paradox of how to distance oneself from politics while remaining embedded within its realm. The focus on ‘partisanship’ has gained prominence in the evolving discourse of Brazilian foreign policy. In light of Baron Rio Branco’s statement and the discussions presented, exploring the concept of ‘Depoliticization’ becomes pertinent. This concept refers to the comprehensive process by which a situation, entity, or topic is perceived as detached from politics and positioned as apolitical (Louis and Maertens 2021).

    Despite the attention given to the impact of political parties on foreign policy, especially during the Workers’ Party federal administrations (Almeida 1992), comprehensive studies on this matter (partisanship, politicization, and depoliticization) remain insufficient. Given this, it becomes crucial to examine the possible inhibitions and challenges that might hinder diplomats’ participation in party-political endeavours, stemming from: i) internal regulations and ethical limitations; ii) professional motivations; and iii) impediments to entering the realm of party politics.

    Diplomats could encounter obstacles when participating in partisan political life due to concerns that such engagement might lead to i) conflicts of interest, ii) conflicts arising from time constraints stemming from their professional duties, or iii) potential negative repercussions on the reputation of their country’s diplomatic efforts. Consequently, diplomats may exhibit cautious behaviour concerning their affiliation with political parties.

    Partisanship’s significance in Brazilian foreign policy discourse is evident, but empirical research and methodology gaps persist in measuring it. Construct validity of the term remains unclear, often conflated with ideologization and politicization. Moreover, Brazilian foreign policy bureaucrats’ involvement in party politics lacks investigation. This paper confronts these dual challenges, offering fresh insights into Brazilian diplomats’ interactions with political parties.

    To tackle the first challenge, we collected and cross-referenced Superior Electoral Court (TSE1, in Portuguese) data on party affiliations and candidacies, aiming to analyse the interaction patterns between Brazilian bureaucrats and political parties during the Nova República era. Notably, party affiliations and candidacies constitute just a subset of formal expressions of citizen-party commitment. The second challenge stems from the term ‘partisanship’ being pejoratively associated with political interference in Brazilian foreign policy, often used interchangeably with ideologization and politicization. This necessitates a clearer conceptual and empirical grasp of the phenomenon. This study addresses this by investigating diplomats’ formal ties to political parties, utilizing a distinct data source from earlier research that predominantly examined party programs in the context of political-party relationships with foreign policy.

    Our focus centres on addressing the research question: considering the assumed disincentives and the proposed hypotheses related to ideology and career ambitions, what is the extent of diplomats’ involvement in party politics, and how do these factors collectively influence their decision to participate in party-political activities? This study aims to explore the multifaceted relationship between diplomats, political parties, and foreign policy, shedding light on the nuanced motivations and mechanisms that shape diplomats’ interactions with political parties.

    We advance hypotheses to investigate the connection between diplomats’ party affiliations and: i) party ideologies, and ii) professional ambition, categorized into a) intra-bureaucratic advantages and b) political career aspirations. Our argument posits that these hypotheses are influenced by the party system, driven by the assumption of disincentives for diplomats to engage in party political activities.

    By incorporating these diverse dimensions, our study seeks to comprehensively elucidate the intricate interactions between diplomats and political parties, revealing factors that shape diplomats’ involvement in formal party-political activities. This endeavour contributes to a deeper understanding of the dynamic relationship between political parties and diplomats, impacting various stages of foreign policy.

    The article is organized as follows: the first section discusses the debate around the relationship between political parties and foreign policy, highlighting different perspectives and potential divergences on the influence of parties. The second section provides an overview of the different ways in which the relationship between political parties and citizens is expressed and discusses the data and methods. Finally, the article concludes with a discussion of the findings.

    The party-foreign policy nexus: agenda setting, political support, and electoral gains

    The scholarly literature acknowledges the insufficient attention given to the relationship between political parties and foreign policy, despite several attempts to address this matter. This gap may result from the division of labour between Political Science and International Relations, although efforts have been made to bridge this divide (Kaarbo 2015; Coelho and Santos 2017; Wagner et al. 2018; Raunio and Wagner 2020; Santos and Belém Lopes 2021).

    The ‘domestic policy turn’ within Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) aimed to broaden the understanding of foreign policy beyond the traditional focus on the state and its elites (Kaarbo 2015). However, party politics and the role of political parties in foreign policy decision-making still lack sufficient attention in the field. This oversight is partly due to the continued dominance of the realist paradigm, which asserts that foreign policy is determined by states’ interests and the pursuit of power in the international system (Alden and Aran 2016).

    Some literature suggests that the connection between political parties and foreign policy is weak (Epstein 1967; Schneider 1976; Merle 1981). This belief arises from the notion that one obstacle to parties’ international actions is the limited electoral return on international issues (Epstein 1967). However, others argue that political parties do hold clear foreign policy positions and possess mechanisms to influence their country’s foreign behaviour (Onuki, Ribeiro and Oliveira 2009; Joly and Dandoy 2018; Raunio and Wagner 2020; Wenzelburger and Böller 2020; Hofmann and Martill 2021; Haesebrouck and Joly 2021). This questioning of the notion that party politics ‘stop at the water’s edge’ in foreign policy is gaining traction (Joly and Dandoy 2018; Wagner et al. 2018; Raunio and Wagner 2020; Wenzelburger and Böller 2020).

    In terms of security, the shift from territorial defence to ‘wars of choice’ has notably affected domestic policies on military interventions, with right-wing parties generally supporting such missions more than their left-wing counterparts. Differences also emerge in decision-making procedures, as left-wing parties tend to favour stronger parliamentary control, while right-wing parties prefer a less restricted executive (Rathbun 2004; Wagner 2018a, 2018b; Raunio and Wagner 2020).

    Contrary to the notion that ‘politics stops at water,’ studies reveal that political parties in Europe contest military interventions. This contestation primarily aligns with the left/right axis rather than new divides and correlates with the government or opposition status of the parties. This dynamic is influenced by bureaucratic and international pressures on ruling parties and presents political opportunities for opposition parties (Rathbun 2004; Wagner 2018a, 2018b).

    Moreover, the role of political parties in shaping foreign policy extends beyond security concerns to encompass foreign aid provision. Research on foreign aid indicates that social democratic parties impact a country’s development assistance levels, mediated by welfare state institutions and social spending (Thérien and Noel 2000).

    The influence of political parties on foreign policy formulation is not confined to European nations. In Ukraine, political parties’ foreign policy agendas significantly sway voter decisions, underscoring the relevance of foreign policy in overall campaign strategies (Makhorkina 2005). Empirical literature provides other examples of the nexus between political parties and foreign policy, such as advocating for or against closer bilateral relations with China (Campello and Urdinez 2021), addressing environmental issues (Smith 2009), views on international migration (Stefancik, Némethová and Seresová 2021), perspectives on regional integration (Mesquita 2011), and expressing the party’s political agenda (Almeida 2004; Belém Lopes and Carvalho 2022). Therefore, considering potential differences among parties, the notion of a constant ‘national interest’ over time is challenged, as ideological disparities influence how parties interpret the international landscape (Hofmann and Martill 2021).

    Bureaucrats as catalysts for the nexus: untangling the unseen ties

    Up to this point, we have suggested engaging in a dialogue with the literature on parties and foreign policy. The subsequent challenge is to underscore the potential mediation between bureaucrats and the connection between party organizations and a nation’s international actions. According to Morin (2018: 12) ‘the relationships between bureaucrats and political leaders are not always clear-cut.’ In the same direction, Schnose (2017: 3), argues that ‘comparative politics often assume that political parties are the primary instruments for translating citizens’ preferences into specific policy outcomes. However, the crucial but often forgotten link between preferences, parties, and outcomes is the bureaucracy.’

    In this context, examining the Chilean case, Ferraro (2008) highlights a noticeable gap in research regarding the influence of institutions such as the Chilean Congress in overseeing bureaucratic activities. In his study, the author delves into the informal mechanisms through which congressmen can wield influence in shaping and implementing public policies. As a result, the influence exerted by the parties on the bureaucracy, particularly on policy change, is crossed by the system of appointments responsible for selecting bureaucrats (Schnose 2017). In this context, the debate on the political control of the bureaucracy (Moe 2012) is gaining ground, as it is argued that the bureaucracy plays a key role in formulation and implementation, even in the case of foreign policy.

    Given this assumption, the actions of diplomats often reflect the decisions and orientations of the government bureaucracy, which find in the mechanisms of political control the way in which the preferences of political actors are expressed. Confronted with this context, a pivotal question emerged, ‘Who controls the bureaucracy?’, and evolved into a prominent topic of debate within academic literature (Hammond and Knott 1996). Consequently, various perspectives have surfaced, highlighting the unique influence of Congress, the President, legal constraints, and bureaucratic autonomy. Within this framework, bureaucrats take centre stage as crucial mediators in translating party preferences into foreign policy decisions (Arel-Bundock, Atkinson and Potter 2015; Wood and Lewis 2017; Santos 2022). Considering the relationship between presidents and bureaucrats, the possibility of appointing aligned agents facilitates the direct implementation of party preferences (Bendor, Glazer and Hammond 2001). As a result, the identification of the cadres available to exercise control becomes central, an exercise that we now propose with this research.

    The complex relationship between party objectives, strategies, and modalities of influencing foreign policy becomes evident through the intermediation of political actors, particularly diplomats, who engage with or align themselves closely to party-political spheres. Consequently, ‘ideas have the potential to mould the identity and preferences of Brazilian foreign policy, influenced by the passage of individuals and groups who eventually gain access to the resources for its formulation’ (Mendes 2020).

    Amid this intricate interrelation, it becomes imperative to explore the influence of diplomats’ affiliations with political parties on the chain of party-driven influence in foreign policy decisions. This exploration encompasses the delineation of strategic options and execution of the nation’s international engagement. This pursuit is crucial, given the potential for parties to wield impact on foreign policy through bureaucratic channels. Although our immediate objective does not encompass a comprehensive analysis of the party-bureaucracy-foreign policy nexus, we present a concise overview encapsulating the various stages, mechanisms, empirical manifestations, evidential avenues, and hypotheses underlying this complex interaction in this future research agenda.

    In essence, while not currently our central focus, this endeavour underscores the importance of untangling the intricate web connecting parties, bureaucrats, and foreign policy. It provides a foundational framework for understanding how diplomatic affiliations with political parties contribute to shaping foreign policy and prompts us to embark on further investigations into the multifaceted relationships within the realm of international relations.

    Up to this point, we have delineated the interrelationship among bureaucrats, political parties, and foreign policy. In the following section, we will transition from this overview to a focused exploration of the subject at the heart of this article: the pattern of membership and candidacies.

    Political parties, diplomats and party affiliations

    Diplomats’ ideological bonds: how party affiliations reflect alignment with foreign policy visions

    Political parties play a pivotal role in representative democracies by organizing electoral competition, consolidating interests, and facilitating legislative processes (Amaral 2013). Similar to other public policies, political parties hold distinct positions on foreign policy (Almeida 2004; Onuki and Oliveira 2010; Mesquita 2011; Hebling 2015; Contrera and Hebling 2017; Casarões 2019; Graça 2020; Mendes 2020). Consequently, the enduring consistency of ideological stances provides voters with informative shortcuts, diminishing uncertainty regarding the party’s forthcoming positions and lowering the informational burden associated with unfamiliar subjects. In light of this, voters gauge the anticipated utility of each party when determining their support, thereby emphasizing the distinctive nature of ideological perspectives in shaping foreign policy agendas (Amaral 2013).

    Examining political manifestos provides an avenue to understand the stances of political parties on diverse matters. These programs serve as a means for expressing their preferences and suggestions, which, upon receiving approval through the ballot box, transform into guiding principles for their agenda. They form a horizon that influences the decision-making process (Mendes and Graça 2020) Drawing from the theoretical framework presented in the previous section and the insights gained in this discussion on diplomats’ motivations for formal party affiliation, we formulate the following hypothesis:

    H.1: Party Ideology Hypothesis: The affiliation of diplomats with specific political parties is associated with party ideology. Diplomats who align with the ideology of a particular party may be more likely to join that party

    With that being acknowledged, given that Brazilian political parties do indeed exhibit distinct stances on foreign policy (Almeida 2004; Mesquita 2011; Contrera and Hebling 2017; Mendes 2020; Graça 2020), and recognizing the influential role party institutions play in shaping preferences and guiding the conduct of political agents, it is plausible that party affiliations could exert an impact on the perspectives and positions of bureaucrats concerning foreign policy matters.

    From diplomacy to bureaucracy: how party engagement shapes diplomats’ trajectories and positions

    Career advancement within the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) hinges on colleague support through horizontal and vertical voting, prompting an analysis of factors that influence the distribution of this intra-bureaucratic resource (Farias and Ramazini Júnior 2015). Decree No. 6.559 of September 8, 2008, regulates MFA promotions, overseen by the Minister of State for Foreign Affairs. Seniority determines promotions to Third Secretary, while other levels of progression coexist with merit-based methods. Promotions by seniority follow a biannual ‘seniority list’ from the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Merit-based promotions involve an access list, utilizing horizontal/vertical voting, I/II Evaluation Chamber voting, and Promotions Committee deliberation.

    The Ministry of Foreign Affairs’ list and voting outcomes shape Assessment Board II, guiding Assessment Board I selection and influencing Promotions Committee decisions. This intricate interplay, involving various chambers and voting mechanisms, shapes the MFA’s promotion process2 (Balbino 2011; Gobo 2018; Lima and Oliveira 2018; Lima 2021). The implications are significant, emphasizing the need for advocates to safeguard and advance careers. While capable individuals may ascend the ranks, the speed and challenges may vary. The prevailing model values skills in political manoeuvring, crucial in the realm of diplomacy (Schreiber 2017). Farias and Ramazini Júnior (2015) highlight that during periods characterized by ‘intra-bureaucratic conflicts,’ it is reasonable to infer that diplomats are less likely to challenge a superior colleague. This phenomenon can be attributed to the hierarchical structure of the institution and the regulations governing career advancement, which necessitate garnering support from colleagues.

    Promotions, as well as removals, are highly sought-after assets within the bureaucratic realm. In addressing this matter, Paulo Roberto de Almeida (2020) underscores the existence of a ‘detrimental framework’ within Itamaraty, where an underlying apprehension lingers regarding possible transfers or promotions. This unsettling cycle frequently compels individuals to adopt a cautious stance, unintentionally curbing the unrestricted expression of ideas (Schreiber 2017). Other studies have explored the correlation between the decrease in the average time required for promotions to the position of Second Minister and appointments to positions of trust (UN or Presidency of the Republic) within the Secretariat of State (Lima 2018, 2019). Party affiliations would constitute an additional factor influencing the interaction with removals.

    H.2.1 Trajectories Hypothesis: Diplomats aiming for career advancement within the bureaucratic hierarchy may strategically involve themselves in party politics to enhance their professional prospects.

    We need to consider that direct party influence on foreign policy can occur through the occupation of key decision-making positions, with political appointments of party-affiliated figures playing a crucial role (Almeida 1992; Onuki and Oliveira 2010).

    Party connections and political goals: diplomats’ journey from foreign affairs to political arenas

    In the analysis of political recruitment, a comprehensive evaluation goes beyond the assessment of potential candidates for elective roles. Equally important is understanding the individuals responsible for the selection process within political parties. This spotlight on party leadership and the intricate power dynamics within the party machinery holds pivotal significance in shaping the roster of eligible candidates. It is within this context that the act of pursuing elected office gains prominence, serving as an indicator of the depth of the association between citizens and political parties. Notably, when viewed in conjunction with one’s party affiliations, running for office assumes a role of pivotal importance (Amaral 2013).

    This endeavour transcends a mere political pursuit, representing a tangible means of not only underscoring one’s commitment to the party but also actively immersing oneself in the realm of party-political life (Norris 2013; Amaral 2013). Moreover, delving into political party activities and internal disputes becomes essential. The engagement of individuals in such activities is closely intertwined with the time and effort they invest in understanding and influencing the party’s policies and direction. It’s a manifestation of political identity, reflective of a robust dedication to the party’s values and objectives (Amaral 2013).

    Furthermore, this engagement factor intersects with the broader dynamics of political career aspirations. Beyond exemplifying loyalty, the decision to run for office reflects a deeper understanding of the party’s inner workings and its organizational fabric. In this context, navigating internal disputes and demonstrating a proactive role in shaping party discourse become vital components of the political landscape. These facets collectively underscore the intricate interplay between individuals, political parties, and the political processes that shape representative governance (Amaral 2013).

    H.2.2: Political Ambitions Hypothesis: Diplomats with aspirations for political careers may leverage their roles within the diplomatic service as a stepping stone, utilizing party connections to enhance their political visibility and secure support for potential runs for elected office or appointments in government positions.

    Additionally, once elected, these officials can wield direct and indirect forms of influence on international relations and potentially foreign policy. In this regard, we acknowledge that monitoring diplomats who become involved in politics through candidacies and party/congressional activities aligns with the argument that these officials contribute to the parties’ political agenda by sharing their expertise (Santos 2022).

    Data and methods

    We focus on two main forms of interaction – affiliations and candidacies – to comprehend partisanship within the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Despite party affiliation’s underrepresentation in Brazilian Political Science, it holds significance for research (Speck 2013; Speck 2014b; Speck, Braga and Costa 2015; Cervi, Terron and Soares 2020). While some have questioned its importance as a variable, Speck, Braga and Costa (2015) argue that affiliation is not arbitrary but reflects an entity’s societal connections (Speck 2013; Speck, Braga and Costa 2015; Cervi, Terron and Soares 2020). Affiliation thus deserves consideration as a vital variable in political science research.

    Navigating diplomatic partisanship: affiliations and candidacies

    We gathered data on political party affiliation in Brazil from the Superior Electoral Court (TSE). We downloaded each political party database and consolidated them into a single one with more than 24 million observations and 19 variables. The variables included, for example, the affiliation date and subtitle.

    We obtained data on both municipal and federal elections between 1998 and 2022 using the R language package ElectionBR. The databases were divided into federal (1998, 2002, 2006, 2010, 2014, 2018, and 2022) and municipal elections. After merging the databases, we found a total of 159 789 candidacies for federal office, with 63 associated variables. For municipal elections, we found a total of 2 708 571 observations and 63 variables. When combining the two sets, we obtained 2 868 360 observations and 63 variables.

    We obtained the list of all diplomats in the Brazilian foreign service by consulting the Yearbooks of the Rio Branco Institute. To enrich this list with attributive information such as origin, filiation (father and mother), and state of origin, career promotions, we consulted the Personnel Yearbooks of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, the Official Gazette of the Union, and the minutes of the Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defence (CRE-SENADO). By combining these sources, we identified 2322 diplomats between 1946 and 2019.

    To pair the information about affiliates and diplomats, we used the names present in the TSE database and that of the diplomats as primary keys. This transformed the 24 million observations into 1605 records. However, the challenge of dealing with homonyms, as mapped by other researchers (Lopez and Silva 2019; Praça, Freitas and Hoepers 2011), still remains. To address this issue, we chose to use additional variables in conjunction with the names: ‘municipality name’ and ‘UF.’

    We filtered electoral domiciles in Brasilia, cities where there are regional offices of the MRE, and cities abroad for the ‘municipality name’ variable. For ‘UF,’ the affiliation database adopts the acronym ‘ZZ’ for all cities outside the country. Following this protocol resulted in a database of 224 observations and the original 19 variables. However, we excluded the records with double entries. Therefore, the universe we will deal with in the data analysis section concerns 101 entries and 19 variables between 1979-2020.

    For the candidacy data, we stacked the databases of both federal and municipal elections and filtered the column ‘occupation description’ for observations where this variable takes on the value ‘diplomat.’ This resulted in 30 candidacies and 63 variables.

    The data analysis phase of this study involves the integration of various methodologies, leading to a comprehensive understanding of the research question. This encompasses i) historical analysis of party stability, ii) party manifestos, and iii) ideological alignment, along with iv) memoirs, v) diplomat interviews, and vi) career progression analysis. These components were harmonized with I) content analysis and II) descriptive statistics and hypothesis testing. This approach culminates in presenting results that combine A) narrative policy analysis with B) absolute frequency and t-test.

    The potential criticism that might question the appropriateness of the methodology based on the sample size can be effectively addressed by recognizing that we are not dealing with a sample but with the entire population of interest. In the specific context mentioned, where a group of 77 (if we consider only the interval of the Nova República) individuals representing all affiliated diplomats is under analysis, the methodological approach can be more accurately characterized as an examination of the entire population. By considering all affiliated diplomats as the population under study rather than a sample, some of the challenges typically associated with small samples can be mitigated. The main distinction between a sample and a population lies in the fact that in a complete population no statistical inference is necessary as all members are included in the analysis.

    Therefore, criticisms questioning the validity and reliability of empirical conclusions based on a small sample may be relevant in contexts where generalization is crucial. However, when the analysis covers the entire population of interest, as in the case of all affiliated diplomats, the validity of empirical conclusions is not based on statistical inference, but on the representativeness and relevance of the data collected.

    This does not mean that there are no other critical methodological considerations. It is essential to guarantee the quality of the data, the accuracy of the measurements, and the rigorous application of the analysis methods. Furthermore, when dealing with the entire population, the emphasis can be directed towards a more comprehensive and detailed analysis, seeking to understand the patterns and nuances within this specific group of diplomats.

    Thus, rather than fixating on the absolute number of individuals in the ‘sample,’ it is more relevant to assess the representativeness, variability, and suitability of the methodology to the specific nature of the population under analysis. The methodological approach should be adapted to maximize understanding of the phenomena within the group studied, taking into account the unique characteristics of the entire population of affiliated diplomats.

    Results and discussions

    Laying the groundwork: political engagement hurdles

    In the early years of the Nova República, only a few diplomats were linked to political parties, with some years showing no affiliations. Chart 1 displays varied patterns of party affiliations among diplomats. Notably, the first year of Fernando Henrique Cardoso’s presidency in 1995 and Lula da Silva’s administration between 2003 and 2009 witnessed increased affiliations. In 2018, the highest number of affiliations was recorded. Therefore, 2018 (10), 1995 (9), and 2004 (6) marked the peak of diplomats’ party affiliations. Observing party affiliations by year, we find: three during the military regime, four under Sarney, two with Collor, four during Franco’s interregnum, 19 in the Fernando Henrique Cardoso era, 30 under Lula da Silva, 18 during Dilma Rousseff’s term, 13 during Temer, and seven in Bolsonaro’s first year. Notably, PSDB holds the most affiliations (30), followed by NOVO (10), while PT and PDT each have nine, and MDB boasts eight.

    Affiliations persist despite disincentives in various forms. Amaury Banhos Porto Oliveira (2014: 23) exemplifies this through personal experience, openly engaging in party activities and political activism. Oliveira explains: ‘My stance was known to everyone at Itamaraty; there was no attempt to conceal my involvement.’ He adds that his political positions impacted his career trajectory, leading to slow advancement and constraints on progression. He observes: ‘While others were promoted on merit, I remained in my position, witnessing their advancement.’

    Given the preceding observations, a new perspective prompts a revaluation of the inquiry into affiliation frequency, suggesting that these connections endure despite disincentives. This shift in view leads to the following deductions: firstly, while formal regulations don’t explicitly bar diplomats from party affiliations, evidence suggests a reluctance to engage in broader political activities. Involvement in partisan affairs might undermine the presumed impartiality, neutrality, and integrity of diplomacy, echoing terms like ‘ideologization of foreign policy’ (Lafer 2009; Barbosa 2010). This calls for a deeper exploration of informal guidelines advocating detachment from party-political matters, due to potential conflicts of interest and the impact such involvement could have on the nation’s diplomatic standing.

    Secondly, we must consider the professional incentives that can complicate involvement in party-related activities. Diplomats often spend a significant part of their careers abroad, which might hinder such engagement. Thus, diplomats might choose to focus solely on their diplomatic responsibilities if party involvement doesn’t align with their values or career goals. Notably, experiences like the Workers’ Party Nuclei abroad (Partido dos Trabalhadores 2023) can help mitigate this challenge. Balancing party activities with representing the nation abroad can also add complexity to pursuing political engagement.

    Thirdly, existing literature underscores the competitiveness and exclusivity of party structures. Regulations within these political contexts can be challenging for ‘non-politicians,’ particularly those aiming for elective positions. The commitment needed for selection processes and networking, crucial for gaining prominence in the party apparatus, adds another layer of complexity for diplomats.

    Anchored parties and flocking diplomats: navigating the institutional seas

    In Brazil, the New Republic marked a significant shift from the bipartisanship mandated by Institutional Act 2 (AI2) during the military dictatorship. This change happened in 1980, following the 1979 party reform (Law no. 6767), which broke the dominance of Aliança Renovadora Nacional (ARENA) and Movimento Democrático Brasileiro (MDB) as the sole political associations. This shift to multi-partisanship led to the emergence of new parties, resulting in a more diverse and potentially competitive political landscape. This change necessitated the establishment of programmatic identities, electoral platforms, and the recognition of ideological distinctions by voters. Despite the fragmentation characterizing the Brazilian party system, research highlights that its structure started to gain clearer contours in the 1990s (Kinzo 2004; Braga and Kinzo 2007).

    The party system underwent a significant shift after the 2018 elections, breaking away from the long-standing polarization between the Workers’ Party and the Social Democracy Party, which had characterized politics since 1994. This polarization had provided some predictability to national elections (Braga and Borges 2019). As highlighted by Abranches (2019), the end of the PT-PSDB era in the presidency, combined with increased fragmentation of political factions, brought about a notable disruption to the political landscape. This disruption had far-reaching consequences, destabilizing the political-party dynamics that had previously maintained democratic stability and facilitated the operation of coalition presidentialism for about twenty-five years.

    The findings suggest that the party stability hypothesis can explain the fluctuating proportions of diplomats affiliating with different parties, even when considering the emergence of PSOL and NOVO. This indicates that aligning with distinct foreign policy positions of political parties becomes a driving force for membership (H.4., next section). However, as the political landscape matures, a lack of resonance with existing parties could lead to membership saturation, unless new parties emerge (H.2. growth of the party system) to offer fresh options for party alignment, thereby leading to a renewed increase in affiliations. In the case of the PSB, the upswing in affiliations corresponds with the period following the mensalão scandal. This episode acted as a catalyst for changes in party dynamics, as exemplified by André Luís Costa de Sousa’s transition from PT to PDT (CPDOC n.d.).

    Shared beliefs, party ties: igniting diplomats’ affiliations

    To analyse Brazilian political parties’ foreign policy approaches, we’ve chosen four key ones: PSOL, Partido Novo, Partido dos Trabalhadores (PT), and Partido da Social Democracia Brasileira (PSDB). These parties exhibit distinct perspectives, reflecting the two hypotheses about the Brazilian party system (Mendes 2020). PT and PSDB represent the establishment shaping the Nova República, while PSOL and NOVO symbolize its expansion.

    In a concise comparison, PSOL criticizes the capitalist system and imperialism. The Partido Novo emphasizes sustainability and technological advancement. The PT prioritizes sovereignty, regional integration, and global cooperation. The PSDB focuses on flexibility, collaboration, and sustainable development. Regarding regional integration, PSOL emphasizes resistance and South-South cooperation. Partido Novo strengthens Mercosur and forms global partnerships. The PT pursues Latin American integration with an emphasis on Africa and multilateralism. The PSDB enhances Mercosur with adaptable partnership strategies (Mesquita 2011; Almeida 2004; Onuki and Oliveira 2010)

    In international cooperation, PSOL supports socialist and international movements. The Partido Novo engages in global forums and security discussions, while the PT seeks South-South cooperation and conflict resolution. The PSDB contributes to peace, trade, and energy talks. For relations with the United States, PSOL criticizes alignments, the Partido Novo promotes dialogue, the PT pursues equitable and multilateral relations, and the PSDB strengthens ties with developed nations. In defence and technology, the Partido Novo focuses on technology and national sovereignty. The PT modernizes the Armed Forces and enhances defence, while the PSDB emphasizes technology and international trade (Almeida 2004; Onuki and Oliveira 2010; Mesquita 2011).     We can explore the distinct impact of two key entities within the Nova República party system: PT and PSDB, which significantly influenced the era’s political dynamics. Within the PT realm, a prominent instance of alleged partisanship centres on Celso Amorim. Initially linked to MDB, Amorim became Foreign Minister during Lula’s administration and joined the Workers’ Party in 2009, a move often cited as evidence of foreign policy partisanship during Lula and Dilma’s presidencies (Faria 2012).

    Amorim (2005) explains that his affiliation with PMDB was a ‘personal choice’ when Ulisses Guimarães led the party. He downplays his party involvement, mentioning, ‘I joined, but never disaffiliated from PMDB. But I also never received a monthly or annual newsletter’ (Amorim, cited in Chade 2005). In 2009, he joined PT, and Berzoini, the party’s president, indicated the decision wasn’t linked to specific political projects, though a potential candidate was considered.     In the context of PSDB, another notable contributor is Rubens Barbosa, whose party involvement dates back to the party’s inception. Like Celso Amorim, Barbosa’s engagement in political affairs carries significant historical context. He played an active role, even participating in the party’s formation. He stated:

    My connection was very apparent with the Brazilian Democratic Movement (MDB), then with (...) (PMDB), and I even took part in initial discussions for the creation of the Brazilian Social Democracy Party (PSDB) (...) I was, as they say in England, a loyal opposition. (Barbosa 2018)

    If certain disincentives pertain to the allocation of time, which may compete with other professional commitments, and the necessity to establish close ties with political dynamics, Barbosa’s account provides insightful nuances regarding the influence of networking in one’s career trajectory. The diplomat highlights, ‘My assignment in Brasilia granted me access to the entire legislative body, particularly its leadership.’ In this context, as conveyed by the same bureaucrat, a direct outcome materialized: ‘During that period, I crossed paths with André Franco Montoro, and our acquaintance blossomed into a strong bond with his entire family. Consequently, I became deeply associated with Christian democracy’ (Barbosa 2018, emphasis added).

    Barbosa’s memoirs recount instances of his party-political involvement, including collaborative efforts like supporting Franco Montoro’s governorship campaign in São Paulo. He was even invited to advise at the Bandeirantes Palace but declined the position (CPDOC n.d.; Barbosa 2018). As a member of PSDB, he contributed to the presidential campaigns of politicians like Serra, FHC, and Aécio. He noted, ‘I had many conversations and provided various suggestions for the government program.’ He also coordinated work for foreign trade and foreign policy areas during Alckmin’s campaign (Barbosa 2018). It’s worth noting that Barbosa’s party involvement differs from other bureaucrats in this study, representing a distinct relationship between citizens and parties, involving active participation and even party formation (CPDOC n.d.; Barbosa 2018).

    From diplomatic missions to political positions: party engagement as a stepping stone

    Diplomatic involvement in political campaigns traces back to 1998, with José Carlos Fonseca and André Costa running for federal deputy positions. Ana Lélia Beltrame joined them in 2002. Changes occurred in 2006, as Fonseca left, and Bezerra de Menezes Neto entered the list. The years 2010 and 2014 saw the peak of diplomats as candidates, with four in each election, including Arthur Virgílio, Rômulo Neves, Marcelo Calero, Jean Marcel, and Betha Rocha. In sub-national elections, Fernando Figueira ran for a city council seat in 2000. In 2004, Ricardo Araújo and Ana Lelia Beltrame joined the list. The 2008 elections featured candidates for municipal executive positions, including Dr. Jadiel and Sóstenes Arruda. Subsequent elections saw Arthur Virgílio, Claudete, and Carlos Oliveira joining. The Brazilian Social Democracy Party (PSDB) had the most candidates with 10, followed by PSB with six and PT with four. Geographically, Rio de Janeiro had the most candidates with nine, followed by Goiás and Amazonas with four each.

    Diplomat Marcelo Calero’s political journey reflects his early affinity for politics, even though he initially pursued a diplomatic career due to family tradition (Kaplan 2019). His involvement surged when he founded the ‘Rio Decides Movement’4 and was appointed deputy coordinator of International Relations by Rio de Janeiro’s mayor, Eduardo Paes. He later became president of the Rio450 Committee and held the position of Rio de Janeiro’s Municipal Secretary for Culture. Calero’s political trajectory intensified as he became the Minister of Culture under the Temer government in 2016. Faced with pressures from Geddel Vieira Lima to expedite a license, he resigned, joining the Political Action Network for Sustainability (Weterman 2018). He contested elections as a PSDB member in 2010, emphasizing social-democratic values (Kannenberg 2016). He later joined the PPS and was elected to parliament in 2018. In the 2022 elections, he ran as a PSD member but was not re-elected.

    The Virgílio family’s political legacy spans generations, exemplified by Arthur Virgílio Neto, who followed his father’s political path. Virgílio Filho’s memoir reveals Neto’s desire to return to Amazonas for a political journey despite a diplomatic career. Balancing diplomacy and politics proved challenging, with the professional calling often taking him abroad. Consequently, Virgílio Neto left his career for Amazonas (Virgílio Filho and Adolfo 2011). Arthur Virgílio Neto’s political journey encompasses his initial tenure as a federal deputy for Amazonas in 1982, representing the PMDB. He subsequently aligned himself with the PSDB and assumed the role of Chief of Staff during the administration of Fernando Henrique Cardoso. His leadership within the PSDB extended to the Senate, where he held the position of party leader from 2003 to 2010.

    Concluding remarks

    Hofmann and Martill (2021) emphasize that merely acknowledging the relevance of political parties to foreign policy is insufficient; understanding the specific contexts in which this relevance becomes significant is essential. To grasp the implications of political engagement, it is crucial to explore diverse forms of political activism within bureaucratic studies and examine the impact of extra-partisan networks. Building on our initial exploration, our research agenda aims to uncover how different interactions among diplomats, citizens, and parties influence political appointments.

    The blurred line between politics, parties, and bureaucrats within the Brazilian federal administration, as highlighted by Lopez and Silva (2019), underscores the need to comprehend the precise role occupied by party-affiliated nominees in the ministerial bureaucracy. Examining this relationship provides a systematic approach to comprehending partisanship and its manifestations in foreign policy. While anecdotes exist regarding bureaucrats with partisan leanings, comprehensive research on the ‘politics of bureaucracy,’ particularly the connection between political parties and civil service in Brazil, remains limited. To enhance our understanding, we should explore diverse citizen/party interactions, including financial support for candidacies and identifying individuals who have run for office. Analysing diplomats’ career trajectories unveils career patterns and opens up new avenues for research.

    This paper underscores the necessity to move beyond assertions about the relevance of political parties in foreign policy and instead delve into the intricate contextual nuances and mechanisms at play. Engaging with bureaucratic studies enables us to comprehend the diverse forms of political activism and their impact on foreign policy. Additionally, investigating other forms of citizen/party engagement, such as financial support and candidacy, is crucial.

    Our findings shed light on the relationship between Brazilian diplomats and political parties. Contrary to popular belief, the PSDB boasts the highest prevalence of affiliations among diplomats. Notable years for affiliations include 2018, 1995, and 2004. While affiliations and candidacies capture certain aspects, we acknowledge the potential existence of other, less formalized forms of partisanship. Further exploration is necessary to comprehensively understand the intricate connection between political parties and the bureaucracy in Brazil.

    The study’s conclusions indicate that political parties with greater stability and a longer history in the political landscape tend to attract diplomats’ concentrated membership and candidacies. Furthermore, the expansion of the party system, representing diverse ideas through party affiliations, can facilitate formal associations with these parties. The research also highlights various disincentives that discourage diplomats from engaging in party political activities.

    Overall, the article underscores the importance of exploring the nuanced context and mechanisms in diplomats’ interactions with political parties in Brazil. From an individual perspective, it emphasizes the role of ‘Party Ideology’ in shaping party affiliations. Diplomats are more likely to align themselves with a party’s ideology upon joining. The study also explores ‘Bureaucratic Progression,’ a scenario in which diplomats engage in party activities to enhance their career prospects within the bureaucracy. However, the results in this aspect lack conclusiveness, possibly stemming from the limited number of affiliate cases that impact the feasibility of statistical tests, such as the t-test in this instance. Lastly, it addresses ‘Political Career Aspirations,’ where diplomats aim to increase visibility and garner support for future candidacies.
