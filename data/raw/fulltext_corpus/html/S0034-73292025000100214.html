Beyond Ambitious Goals: Unveiling the True Drivers of Countries’ Sustainable Development Performance

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article investigates the structural, institutional, and financial determinants of national performance in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). Using cross-sectional data for 193 countries and Ordinary Least Squares regression models, it compares three explanatory dimensions: governance quality, foreign direct investment (FDI), and productive sophistication measured by the Economic Complexity Index (ECI). Results show that economic complexity is the strongest and most consistent predictor of SDG performance, while institutional indicators and FDI exhibit limited statistical significance. The analysis explains 64% of cross-national variance in SDG scores, confirming that productive capabilities, rather than governance or financial inflows, drive sustainable development outcomes. Conceptually, the paper situates these findings within the political economy debate on the global financial architecture, arguing that current frameworks reproduce structural asymmetries and fail to support long-term transformation. The study concludes that mission-oriented industrial and innovation policies — aligned with green and inclusive objectives — are essential to combine economic diversification with sustainable and equitable growth.

    Keywords
    Sustainable Development Goals; comparative analysis; political economy; global financial architecture; economic complexity

    Introduction

    The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development represents one of the most ambitious global governance initiatives of the twenty-first century. Signed by 193 member states in 2015 (United Nations General Assembly 2015), it seeks to eradicate poverty, promote social inclusion, and foster sustainable economic growth while safeguarding the planet. Yet, at the midpoint of implementation, the results remain disappointing. According to the latest UN DESA report (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs 2024), only 17% of the targets are on track, while most countries face major or significant challenges to achieving the goals by 2030.

    This persistent underperformance underscores a deeper question: what truly drives countries’ progress toward the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)? Despite the widespread consensus on their importance, the global financial and institutional frameworks appear insufficient to sustain long-term and inclusive development. The debate has increasingly shifted from the availability of resources to the structural capacity of economies to transform — emphasizing productive diversification, technological learning, and innovation as critical enablers of sustainable growth (Reinert 2008; Hidalgo 2021; Mazzucato 2023).

    This study investigates the determinants of countries’ performance in achieving the SDGs by comparing three broad explanatory perspectives: (i) the institutional quality of governance, (ii) external financial inflows through foreign direct investment (FDI), and (iii) the structural sophistication of economies, measured by the Economic Complexity Index (ECI). It empirically tests which of these dimensions better explains cross-national variations in SDG performance.

    While the historical and political processes that shaped the SDG framework have been extensively documented, this paper shifts the focus toward its measurable outcomes—examining how progress, setbacks, and structural drivers influence countries’ actual performance. By doing so, it bridges the gap between the political construction of global goals and the material conditions that determine their implementation.

    The contribution of this research is twofold. Empirically, it offers one of the few cross-country analyses linking economic complexity directly to SDG outcomes, providing quantitative evidence that productive structures play a more decisive role than institutional or financial variables. Conceptually, it advances the sustainable development debate by connecting the political economy of structural change with the SDG agenda—arguing that lasting progress depends less on external financing or governance quality and more on endogenous productive capabilities.

    Besides this introduction, the paper is structured as follows. The next part provides an overview of countries’ progress toward the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) since 2015, combining a descriptive assessment of global performance with an analysis of the international financial architecture that underpins the 2030 Agenda. The following section develops the theoretical and empirical framework, reviewing the main determinants of sustainable development and testing their explanatory power across countries through quantitative analysis. The paper concludes by summarizing the findings and outlining policy implications for advancing productive and sustainable transformation.

    The Sustainable Development Goals

    Countries’ Performance

    In 2015, under the leadership of the United Nations (UN), world governments adopted the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, establishing a collaborative global framework to enhance quality of life worldwide. This agenda represents an evolution of earlier international efforts, particularly the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) launched in 2000, which set eight universal targets addressing poverty, education, gender equality, environmental sustainability, and human rights.

    Although the MDGs achieved partial progress, mainly in political awareness and global coordination—many objectives remained unmet due to persistent socioeconomic disparities. Issues such as hunger, inequality, malnutrition, and deforestation continued to challenge both developed and developing nations.

    To address these ongoing gaps, the Rio+20 Conference held in 2012 laid the foundation for a broader and more inclusive framework. The resulting Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), adopted in 2015, expanded the agenda to 17 goals and 169 targets, incorporating new priorities such as climate action, innovation, sustainable production, and economic equality. The SDGs thus mark a transition from a sectoral approach to a comprehensive governance paradigm, one that integrates economic, social, and environmental dimensions of development while emphasizing collective global responsibility.

    What has been the performance of the Sustainable Development Goals thus far? With only six years remaining to achieve the SDGs, the overall outlook is not promising. Most nations have yet to attain any of the 17 goals, with two goals falling behind and the rest facing significant or major challenges that hinder progress toward a successful trajectory by 2030 (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs 2024). Since 2015, regarding target indicators, only 17% have achieved success or are on track, while 66% have made limited or no progress, and 17% have experienced a reversal in progress.

    To facilitate the analysis of countries’ performances, this inquiry disaggregated the 193 nations into four groups based on UNTAC’s classification of economic development1. Firstly, a developed economy (DE), also known as an industrialized country or advanced economy, exhibits a Gross National Income (GNI) of more than US$12,615. Conversely, developing economies face numerous challenges, including high poverty rates, economic vulnerability, and human resource deficiencies. They are categorized into three types: i) Low-Income Developing Economy (LIDE) encompasses countries with GNI less than US$1,035; ii) Middle-Income Developing Economy (MIDE) includes those with GNI per capita between US$1,036 and US$4,085; and iii) High-Income Developing Economy (HIDE), or upper-middle-income countries, fall within the range of US$4,086 to US$12,615.

    The following graphs draw on the UN’s SDG Dashboard taxonomy to evaluate whether countries are on or off track to meet the 2030 targets. Rather than covering all 17 goals, this analysis focuses on seven key SDGs that capture the core dimensions of development, in line with the World Bank’s (2017) framework of growth, equity, sustainability, and security, as well as Sen’s (2001) Human Development Index (HDI), which integrates health, education, and living standards. Accordingly, the study addresses:

    • Economic prosperity: Goals 1 (No Poverty) and 10 (Reduced Inequalities);

    • Social development: Goals 3 (Good Health and Well-being) and 4 (Quality Education);

    • Environmental sustainability: Goals 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation) and 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy);

    • Institutional resilience: Goal 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions).

High-income countries have largely achieved or are close to achieving most targets, while progress declines sharply among middle- and low-income economies. In the poorest nations, structural inequality continues to constrain income growth, often benefiting only narrow population segments—reinforcing the uneven trajectory of the 2030 Agenda.

    Undoubtedly, it is imperative to acknowledge that external disruptions — such as the pandemic and the conflict in Ukraine — have severely affected the global economy and societies, consequently impeding progress towards the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). While advances were visible during the first five years of implementation, progress markedly decelerated after 2020. What is more concerning is that the projected disparities in SDG Index scores between Developed Economies (DEs) and Low-Income Developing Economies (LIDEs) are expected to widen by 2030 compared to the onset of this global policy agenda in 2015.

    Persistent challenges such as hunger, inequality, malnutrition, and deforestation remain critical, as reported in the United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs (2024) Sustainable Development Goals Report, which highlights limited or even reversed progress in most developing regions. Consequently, economic and environmental crises continue to disproportionately burden the poorest and most marginalized individuals and nations, deepening global inequalities.

    SDG’s Global Financial Architecture (GFA)

    To overcome these structural constraints, a resilient policy implementation process requires new and complementary financial mechanisms capable of mobilizing both public and private resources. Despite the disappointing results, United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs (2024) argues that most SDGs remain achievable, with only one target considered fully out of reach. The key question, therefore, is whether the existing financing arrangements are adequate to sustain this agenda.

    While the 4th International Conference on Financing for Development (United Nations, 2025) and the global rise of ESG frameworks have expanded the scope of this debate, this study focuses on examining how the prevailing financial architecture — largely inherited from the MDG era — continues to shape resource allocation and countries’ capacity to achieve the SDGs.

    The MDG financing model was replicated under the 2030 Agenda, with the United Nations and its member states assuming primary responsibility for implementation. At the same time, a comprehensive framework for investment mobilization was established, involving National Development Banks (NDBs), Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs), and private actors increasingly driven by ESG-oriented incentives. However, this global financial architecture has been repeatedly disrupted by successive crises — notably the pandemic and recent international conflicts — which have triggered widespread fiscal tightening and constrained the already limited budgets allocated to sustainable development. These shocks have further aggravated fiscal space limitations, particularly in low- and middle-income developing economies (Sachs et al. 2023), thereby threatening the very foundations of global cooperation envisioned by the SDG framework.

    The GFA is riddled with inequities, gaps, and inefficiencies deeply entrenched within the system, encompassing: i) higher borrowing costs for developing countries in financial markets; ii) variation in countries’ access to liquidity in times of crisis; iii) dramatic underinvestment in global public goods and; iv) volatile financial markets and capital flows with consequences for sustainable development (United Nations 2023). In this sense, the current framework fails to allocate global savings at the necessary pace and scale to support the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). According to (Sachs et al. 2023), the problems involve:

    1. Persistent under-investment undermines the most low and middle-income countries;

    2. The majority of them lack the credit ratings needed to borrow on favorable terms;

    3. These poorest nations are highly susceptible to liquidity and balance of payments crises, impeding their ability to pursue sustainable long-term investment strategies;

    4. Developed nations can swiftly mobilize vast financial resources during crises but are reluctant to do so for global sustainable development despite prior commitments;

    5. Private capital markets often direct funds towards unsustainable technologies and practices, delaying the transition to a decarbonized energy system and contributing to environmental degradation;

    6. Bureaucratic institutional frameworks hinder international cooperation, slowing the SDG funding process and failing to provide the necessary framework for large-scale SDG financing.

    As a result, this directly impacts the asymmetries of how economies access resources. For instance, according to the IMF World Economic Outlook, the Developed countries with 16% of the global population hold 50% of the global investments. In comparison, LIDEs and MIDEs together make up 51% of the world population but are the destiny of only 12,4% of investments (Sachs et al. 2023). The list of problems with SDG financing seems to make it difficult to reach the desired targets, which led to efforts from scholars and multilateral organizations to provide recommendations that could shift the world’s investment patterns to increase the overall investment flow in order to build the future that SDGs are achieving.

    To accelerate progress toward the SDGs, Sachs et al. (2023) emphasize the urgent need to scale up development finance and reform the global financial architecture. Their proposals include expanding funding to national and subnational governments and private enterprises, revising credit rating systems and debt sustainability metrics, and improving liquidity mechanisms—particularly for the poorest countries. They also call for new instruments to enhance the quality and agility of international cooperation, among other ambitious initiatives.

    Echoing these concerns, Mazzucato (2023) argues for channeling long-term, risk-tolerant finance toward sustainable development, with National Development Banks (NDBs) and Multilateral Development Banks (MDBs) playing a central, mission-oriented role. She envisions a global financial system that is inclusive, innovation-driven, and aligned with transformative public purposes. Achieving this requires three major shifts:

    1. Aligning NDBs and MDBs around specific missions to translate broad SDG challenges into concrete investment pathways and foster coordinated action;

    2. Leveraging missions to mobilize large-scale public and private capital, establishing clear objectives and expectations that catalyze an SDG “multiplier effect”;

    3. Imposing reciprocity conditions on finance flows to ensure responsible corporate conduct — including living wages, improved working conditions, and greener supply chains — replacing traditional conditionalities that restricted fiscal autonomy in developing economies.

    Complementing these perspectives, the UNCTAD Investment Policy Framework for Sustainable Development (IPFSD) provides a comprehensive roadmap to guide and mobilize investments aligned with the 2030 Agenda, especially in developing countries. The IPFSD outlines four guiding principles for private sector engagement in SDG financing: (i) balancing liberalization with effective regulation; (ii) ensuring affordability and accessibility while maintaining attractive risk–return ratios; (iii) coordinating public and private investment coherently; and (iv) prioritizing support for least-developed and vulnerable economies through context-specific national and international measures (Zhan and Santos-Paulino 2021).

    Despite commitments from 193 nations and the efforts of governmental bodies, multilateral organizations, development banks, and the business sector to address funding for policies related to SDG targets, the diagnosis of the global financial architecture falls short of providing the robust and comprehensive funding needed for green and inclusive development worldwide. Furthermore, while recommendations have been ambitious in discourse, they have yet to be implemented or taken seriously in international debate arenas. Consequently, despite some improvements in SDG index scores, as indicated by the latest UN DESA report, disparities among the economic groups persist, painting a picture of a segregated planet, as depicted in Graph 5.

    The reference to the Global Framework Agreement (GFA) pertains to its institutional role within the UN-led financing agenda rather than its geopolitical discourse. While the GFA embodies a consensus largely shaped by OECD and multilateral institutions, countries from the Global South—particularly within the G77 and BRICS—have consistently called for greater autonomy in defining their development priorities and for reforms in the governance of international financial institutions, as reaffirmed in the 2025 Seville Conference outcomes. In this context, the present study does not take the UN DESA or Sachs frameworks as prescriptive truths but as analytical baselines to empirically test whether their assumptions about institutional and financial determinants of SDG performance are held when compared with structural economic factors.

    Determinants of the Sustainable Development

    Palliative or Productive approach?

    Considering the persistent inability of the current global financial architecture to effectively address the challenges regarding world asymmetries and the lack of progress in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), is there an alternative path available to developing countries to foster prosperity, equality, and environmental improvements?

    Erik Reinert (2008), in his seminal book How Rich Countries Got Rich and Why Poor Countries Stay Poor, criticizes the approach focused on Millennium Development Goals, which had the same logic as the SDG, for him, so-called the palliative economy. According to the scholar, these goals initially appear as noble objectives to tackle urgent global social issues, such as reducing poverty and hunger, combating disease, and lowering mortality rates. However, the critical problem comes from the emphasis on foreign financing rather than internal resources for development. This reliance on international aid raises concerns about perpetuating dependency and a potential “welfare colonialism” dynamic. Moreover, the emphasis on alleviating symptoms rather than the root causes of these significant public issues suggests we avoid effectively facing the real problems. Reinert (2008, pg. 374) states:

    “Today, as we deal with Third World problems through the Millennium Development Goals, we have made permanent the temporary solutions of the 1930s – humanitarian aid, soup distributions and shelter for the homeless.”

    The shift towards a palliative economy has largely replaced developmental economics. Striking a balance between developmental initiatives aimed at fundamentally transforming the productive structures of the poorest nations and palliative measures aimed at alleviating economic suffering has been a persistent and unsolved dilemma for global governance. In this sense, Sustainable Development Goals are founded on novel principles with long-term ramifications that need to be more adequately strategized and comprehended.

    Therefore, the country’s emphasis should shift from tackling temporary issues to addressing the enduring facets of economic and productive frameworks. This trajectory has garnered growing support for promoting structural transformations and facilitating a green transition through strategic initiatives such as economic diversification or complexity. So, the question turns to the best approach for nations’ strategies to reach the desired social and sustainable development.

    In developing nations, economic diversification entails adopting products or industries that have already proven successful elsewhere to propel development forward. This process, called emulation, has played a crucial role in advancing less developed economies by introducing new sectors, reshaping consumption patterns, and influencing the dynamics of global competition. When new sectors introduce innovative goods, they generate income and employment opportunities. However, if they compete directly with existing global products, they may displace jobs in other countries. The connection between diversification and economic outcomes is common, but discernible patterns emerge from the international interactions among economies.

    One way of measuring this process is by the Economic Complexity Index (ECI), a measure of a society’s knowledge as reflected in its exported products (Hausmann et al. 2014). ECI considers both the diversity of export products and their ubiquity, i.e., the quality of products in the export basket or the number of countries that export that product2. Nations endowed with a broad spectrum of productive capabilities encompassing sophisticated expertise typically manufacture diverse and intricate goods. The complexity of a country’s exports is a robust predictor of its present income levels and future growth prospects, serving as a valuable metric for gauging economic development. Based on it, a fertile research strand started to compare the effects of economic complexity on various economic factors such as income levels, growth, inequality, and carbon emissions.

    From this premise, scholars have delved into the correlation between the Economic Complexity Index (ECI) and sustainable development from various angles. Initially, a series of studies centered on the nexus between economic complexity and growth. Notably, Hausmann et al. (2014) illustrate those economies with higher economic complexity, relative to GDP per capita, experienced accelerated growth. On average, researchers ascertain that an increase of one standard deviation in economic complexity while holding GDP per capita constant correlates with an annualized growth increase ranging from 4% to 7%.

    Similarly, Poncet and Waldemar (2013) analyzed the case of China by adapting the ECI to a city-level measure and comparing it with GDP per capita growth. The results are impressive as they found that the more complex a city’s productive structure, the faster its subsequent economic growth. Similarly, Chávez et al. (2017) focus on the relationship between economic complexity and regional growth performance in Mexico. The results suggest that the country’s states differ markedly in terms of the activities they specialize in and their economic complexity. The authors also assert that the dispersion of productive knowledge—measured according to the specialization of the states or the location of the various economic activities performed there—contributes significantly to explaining their different growth patterns. Thus, the findings indicate significant disparities among the country’s states regarding their specialized activities and economic complexity, playing a pivotal role in elucidating their distinct growth trajectories.

    More recently, scholars studying economic complexity have redirected their focus toward other pertinent topics, including income inequality and environmental sustainability. This shift stems from the increasing popularity of a comparative approach in economic geography, international development, and innovation studies. Hidalgo (2021) highlights two main streams of literature. The first emphasizes the evolution of specialization patterns, while the other delves into metrics of economic complexity, utilizing dimensionality reduction techniques to devise metrics of economic sophistication that can predict variations in income, economic growth, emissions, and income inequality.

    Building upon the latter stream, Zhu et al. (2020) explored the effects of export structures on income inequality in China’s urban-rural divide, revealing that export upgrading only contributes to reducing income inequality in the country’s urban areas. Similarly, Hartmann et al. (2017) utilized multivariate regression analysis to demonstrate that economic complexity is a significant and negative predictor of income inequality. This relationship remains robust even after controlling for aggregate income, institutions, export concentration, and human capital measures. The authors contend that economic complexity encapsulates information about an economy’s developmental level, which is pertinent to understanding how it generates and distributes income and suggests that a country’s productive structure may impose limitations on its income inequality.

    Continuing within the social dimension, Zhu and Li (2017) utilize a comparative analysis of cross-country panel data to examine the correlation between economic complexity and levels of human capital. Their findings suggest positive effects on both long- and short-term growth, as there exists a positive interaction effect on economic growth between economic complexity and human capital. Furthermore, secondary education, serving as a proxy for human capital, exhibits a notably more significant positive direct effect and a much stronger interactive effect with complexity on economic growth.

    Introducing a sustainability lens into the equation, Stojkosket al. (2023) examined how complexity may elucidate future economic growth, income inequality, and lower emission intensities in countries. They discussed a significant discovery: the amalgamation of trade geography, technology, and research can elucidate inclusive green growth, indicating the interdependence of development and the environment. Moreover, they suggested that countries’ economic growth can only be sustained if inclusive and green.

    Examining this relationship empirically, Neagu (2019) applied the Environmental Kuznets Curve (EKC) framework to European Union countries between 1995 and 2017 to assess the connection between the Economic Complexity Index (ECI) and carbon emissions. Results indicate an inverted U-shaped pattern: in early stages of industrial upgrading, emissions rise with increased product complexity, but beyond a certain threshold, higher complexity leads to cleaner and more efficient production. Similarly, Can and Gozgor (2017) confirmed this relationship for France, finding that income, energy consumption, and economic complexity are the main determinants of CO₂ emissions. Their long-run analysis showed that income is the most influential variable, producing an inverted-U relationship in which emissions grow with income until reaching a stabilization point, after which they begin to decline.

    In summary, regardless of whether studies focus on growth, human capital, or inclusive and sustainable outcomes, economic complexity consistently appears to play a significant role in shaping countries’ performance. This raises an important question: what can we infer about the Economic Complexity Index’s (ECI) impact on the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) so far?

    Data and Methods

    To examine the determinants of countries’ performance in achieving the SDGs, this study employs a cross-sectional quantitative design combining descriptive analysis and regression modeling. The dataset comprises 193 countries, following the United Nations classification. The dependent variable — SDG Score (2024) — was obtained from the UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs (United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs 2024).

    To provide a thorough analysis of the ECI’s effect on SDG performance—measured by countries’ projected 2024 scores, this study uses a multiple regression model with ECI as the primary independent variable3. The ECI ranking was normalized on a scale from 0 to 100, where Switzerland, ranked first, was assigned a score of 100, and Nigeria, ranked last, was assigned a score of 0. To account for the palliative approach explanation, the analysis uses the nations’ foreign direct investment (net inflows as a percentage of GDP) and, as for the economic institutional perspective, the model incorporates three indices from the Worldwide Governance Indicators (WGI) project (Kaufmann et al. 2010). The underlying assumption is that strong institutions would foster political and economic environments conducive to development. The variables are defined as follows:

    1. Regulatory Quality: reflects perceptions of the government’s ability to design and implement effective policies and regulations that support private sector growth.

    2. Rule of Law: captures perceptions of how much confidence people have in societal rules and the extent to which they adhere to them, focusing on areas like contract enforcement, property rights, law enforcement, judicial systems, and the prevalence of crime and violence.

    3. Control of Corruption: assesses the perceptions of the extent to which public power is misused for private gain, encompassing both minor and large-scale corruption, as well as the undue influence of elites and private interests on state policies.

    All variables were standardized on a 0–100 scale to facilitate interpretation and comparability across countries. Descriptive statistics and correlation matrices are reported in the Annex (Table 2).

    So, the multivariate regression model used to test these correlations is defined by the formula below. The dependent variable, the left-hand side of the equation, is the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) score in 2024. On the right side are the independent variables described as follows:

    SDG Score i = β 0 + β 1 Economic Complexity Index i + β 2 Foreign Direct Investment i + β 3 Rule of Law Lom i + β 4 Control of Corruption i + β 5 Regulatory Quality i + u i

    Empirical Analysis

    Two models were estimated. Model A includes all independent variables; Model B excludes Rule of Law due to multicollinearity, ensuring more stable coefficients. The analysis assesses both statistical significance and substantive explanatory power (R2) to determine which dimension most strongly predicts SDG outcomes. Both models were estimated using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression on cross-sectional data.

    A cross-sectional approach was adopted given the limited temporal coverage of consistent SDG indicators and governance measures across countries. While panel data would enable dynamic inference, the present design allows for broader coverage and comparability. This strategy aligns with prior studies examining the structural determinants of economic and social development (Hausmann et al. 2014; Hartmann et al. 2017; Stojkoski et al. 2023). All estimations were performed using Stata 18. Robust standard errors were computed to mitigate heteroskedasticity.

    The findings are notable in several respects. First, the coefficients of determination (R2) for both models indicate that the independent variables explain 64% of the variation in countries’ SDG performance. This represents a reasonable level of explanatory power, suggesting the models capture important factors influencing SDG scores.

    The regression results in Table 1 confirm that economic complexity has a significant positive impact on a nation’s SDG performance. The Economic Complexity Index (ECI), the primary independent variable, shows a highly significant effect in both models. Specifically, a one-unit increase in ECI is associated with a 0.15-point increase in SDG scores, holding all other variables constant.

    In contrast, the remaining explanatory variables do not demonstrate statistically significant relationships with SDG scores in either model, as their p-values exceed the 0.05 threshold. Among these variables, regulatory quality comes closest to significance, with a p-value of 0.112. While not statistically significant, this suggests that a one-unit increase in regulatory quality could correspond to a 0.08-point increase in SDG scores, though this result should be interpreted with caution.

    These findings support the hypothesis that the primary driver of sustainable development outcomes is the complexity of a nation’s economy. On the other hand, alternative explanations rooted in institutional factors, such as corruption control or regulatory quality, fail to account for the observed variation in SDG performance across nations. This suggests that a focus on economic complexity, rather than institutional or palliative approaches, is key to understanding and improving sustainable development globally.

    In addition, an alternative way to analyse this relationship involves comparing countries’ success and failure cases across both dimensions. For instance, examining the nation’s performance from distinct regions such as Latin America and Central/South Asia can shed light on how economic diversification, particularly by deliberately bolstering industrial and technological sectors, tends to foster actual sustainable and inclusive development.

    Whereas Latin Americans have undergone a significant process of deindustrialization and reprimarization, resulting in profound social, economic, and political implications, Asian nations are experiencing the opposite trend, characterized by consistent growth in industrial and technological production over recent decades (Kim and Lee 2014; Ocampo 2017). These contrasting trajectories are evident in several countries from these regions, as depicted in Graph 6 below. The blue bars illustrate changes in each nation’s economic complexity rank from 1995 to 2021, while the orange bars indicate modifications in their positions in the SDG index rank. The longer time frames chosen for the ICE are intentional, recognizing that transformations in productive structures may take time to manifest in socio-economic and environmental indicators.

    In Asia, the past decades have been characterized by vigorous industrialization and economic diversification, initially led by the Asian Tigers (South Korea, Hong Kong, Taiwan, and Singapore) and, more recently, by China, with significant spill-over effects on smaller economies in the region. These examples vividly demonstrate the correlation between economic complexity and progress towards achieving the SDGs. For instance, since 1995, China has ascended 28 positions in the ICE rank, propelled by a unique development trajectory that shifted its predominantly agricultural economy into an industrial global powerhouse. This transformation enabled the country to elevate its share of world exports from 2% to 12.5% during this period. Conversely, two major Latin American economies, Brazil and Argentina, are undergoing a reverse process of simplification, manifested in the stagnation and regression of their shares in global exports. Brazil’s share declined marginally from 0.9% to 1.1%, while Argentina’s decreased from 0.4% to 0.3%.

    These distinct patterns in both dimensions underscore the arguments of developmental economics rather than the palliative perspective (Reinert, 2008) and illuminate the need for global governance stakeholders to initiate discussions on structural changes to advance the 2030 agenda effectively.

    Final Remarks

    This study sets out to identify the key drivers of countries’ performance in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), comparing institutional, financial, and structural explanations. The analysis demonstrated that economic complexity proxies productive and technological capabilities—emerges as the strongest and most consistent predictor of SDG performance across nations. By contrast, traditional institutional indicators, such as regulatory quality and corruption control, and financial variables like foreign direct investment (FDI), show limited explanatory power.

    These findings reinforce a central argument of the political economy of development: achieving inclusive and sustainable progress requires transforming the structure of production rather than merely improving governance indicators or increasing financial inflows. Economic complexity captures the embedded knowledge and coordination capacity of societies, their ability to diversify production, upgrade technologies, and generate high-value activities. In this sense, it offers a more powerful lens for understanding long-term development than institutional or palliative approaches centered on external aid or compliance metrics.

    The results also highlight the limitations of the current global financial architecture, which continues to reproduce structural asymmetries between high- and low-income countries. While developed economies can mobilize resources rapidly during crises, developing nations face higher borrowing costs, volatile capital flows, and reduced fiscal space. Reforming this architecture is necessary but insufficient: financing without productive transformation risks perpetuating dependency rather than building resilience.

    In policy terms, the findings support a renewed emphasis on industrial and innovation policies (IIP) as engines of sustainable development. Governments should adopt mission-oriented strategies that align technological upgrading and diversification with social and environmental objectives—particularly in green, digital, and knowledge-intensive sectors. This agenda resonates with the new wave of global institutions promoting active industrial policy, such as the IMF’s New Industrial Policy Observatory (2024) and the World Bank’s 2024 Development Report.

    Ultimately, the evidence points to a broader conclusion: the path to achieving the SDGs is not only financial or institutional, but structural. Nations that cultivate productive capabilities and innovation ecosystems are better equipped to combine growth with equity and sustainability. Future research should extend this analysis through panel data and causal inference techniques to explore how economic complexity interacts with governance and environmental outcomes over time.
