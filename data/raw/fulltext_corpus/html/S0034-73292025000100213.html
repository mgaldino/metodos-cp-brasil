Rivalry without War? The WTO and Institutional Change Beyond the Thucydides Trap

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    How do international institutions adapt to structural changes in global power? We examine the WTO amid escalating US-China tensions. The WTO, central to the post-Cold War order, faces a multidimensional crisis exacerbated by the trade tensions between these nations. We assess the Thucydides Trap crisis within institutional change literature through four pieces of evidence: (i) the case of non-recognition of China as a market economy; (ii) USA-China relations among 636 complaints in the DSB; (iii) data on the Appellate Body representation; and(iv) ‘trade war’ crisis and unilateral trade strategies. Findings reveal that US diplomacy undermines the institutional body from within, compelling institutional change by preferring discretion rather than rule-making, through conversion, drift, layering, and displacement, disrupting dispute resolution, blocking the Appellate Body, and implementing authoritative rule interpretation. As a result, the US increasingly bypassed WTO mechanisms, relying on unilateral strategies. This dysfunction weakens the multilateral trade regime, raising concerns over the WTO’s ability to regulate international trade effectively.

    Keywords
    WTO; international trade; trade war; DSB; Thucydides Trap; institutional change; US–China Rivalry

    Introduction

    Donald Trump’s tariff hikes, first deployed during his initial mandate and dramatically expanded in his second, have embodied a new era of economic coercion in international trade. What began as targeted measures against China quickly evolved into sweeping tariffs on steel, aluminum, and other strategic goods that affected long-standing US allies such as the European Union (EU), Canada, and Japan. These measures were not merely protective trade policies but instruments of pressure and bargaining, often leveraged to extract political concessions or renegotiate trade terms outside the multilateral framework of World Trade Organization (WTO). The tariff shocks stressed Washington’s willingness to bypass rules-based dispute mechanisms in favor of unilateral power-based strategies, reshaping international trade dynamics and intensifying perception of US unreliability as a guardian of the liberal trade order.

    Who could imagine those events only three decades after the foundation of the greatest international organization on trade? This contrast is even more striking when placed against the optimism of the 1990s. At that time, there was a strong, although contested, conviction that after the dismantlement of the Soviet Union, free markets and liberal democracies would become the dominant model (Fukuyama 1992). The WTO’s creation in 1994 embodied this vision, since its core purpose of trade liberalization made it the epitome of liberal order.

    Since then, however, the American economy is no longer as predominant, and emerging economies have reshaped the global economy (Han and Paul 2020). The main rivalry today is between China and the US, but unlike earlier geopolitical disputes, both powers now have comparable technological capacities, with China even leading in some areas (Kaska et al. 2019).

    Such a rivalry has revived classical debates about hegemonic transition and conflict, particularly Graham Allison’s Thucydides Trap analysis. In his project, structural stress caused by a rising power’s ambition and a ruling power’s fear of decline often leads to conflict. Historically, such transitions frequently result in war (Allison 2017). His central insight is that it is not merely the material shift in power that provokes conflict, but also perceptions, miscalculations, and security dilemmas generated by that shift. Observing sixteen recorded hegemonic transitions, or threat alternation, he shows the risk of a Trap, considering that twelve cases of power alternation in minimally consolidated international systems have occurred through violent wars, starting with Athens-Sparta rivalry and Peloponnesian War. Only four avoided full-scale confrontation.

    Whether US–China rivalry follows this conflictual logic or primarily unfolds through institutional and economic arenas is the central question guiding this paper, and makes us pose two guiding questions:

    1. How do the shifts in the international system materialize in institutional transformations within the WTO? How do macro changes impact meso arrangements?

    2. Are these transformations an outcome of structural shifts in international order, or are they phenomena intrinsically tied to policies of Trump’s administration?

    We base our analysis on two theoretical levels as we are interested in how hegemonic rivalry and shifting power relations manifest in institutional transformations. At macro level, we combine hegemonic stability theory and offensive neorealism to capture the structural logic of power competition, while Allison’s Thucydides Trap provides a historical-structural lens to read the recurring drama of rising and ruling powers. At meso level, theories of gradual institutional change allow us to trace how these tectonic shifts ripple through corridors of WTO, reshaping its rules and routines from within1.

    Our methodology is mainly descriptive, based on empirical data from primary sources publicly available at the WTO website. We do not seek to establish causal effects, but to generate valid descriptive inferences about how systemic rivalry materializes in institutional practices, linking macro theories of hegemonic transition with meso-level institutional change through three lenses: rule reinterpretations, weakening of dispute settlement, and rise of alternatives to WTO mechanisms. We illustrate these dynamics through evidence drawn from four areas: dispute over China’s market economy status, decline in formal complaints at Dispute Settlement Body (DSB), paralysis of the Appellate Tribunal, and escalation of trade tensions and unilateral measures. Together, these cases highlight how systemic rivalry translates into institutional practices.

    The Chinese rise within the WTO shows how hegemonic transition unfolds within a dense institutional regime. The WTO is the only international organization combining robust rules with a binding dispute system, reinforcing state commitments with sanctioning power embodied by Pax Americana and the vision of an integrated global market when US hegemony faced few obstacles. Despite extensive debates on hegemonic transitions and crisis of multilateralism, there is still a lack of systematic empirical analysis on how macro-level geopolitical shifts translate into institutional dynamics within WTO2.

    By bridging systemic theories with evidence of institutional change, we contribute to understanding how great power rivalry reshapes multilateral governance. Specifically, we show how rivalry between the United States and China transforms the WTO from within, redefining its role in the international trade order.

    This paper is divided into six sections, including this introduction. Section 2 outlines the theoretical concepts of hegemonic stability theory (TSH) and institutional change. Section 3 presents empirical data, examining US performance within the WTO, in four different arenas, and discusses how those strategies set up a scenario configuring several layers of institutional change presented in Section 4. Section 5 concludes with final remarks.

    From the Thucydides trap, hegemony stabilization to institutional change theoretical concepts: linking structures to institutions

    Hegemonic Stability Theory (HST) posits that international economic order is sustainable only under the leadership of a single dominant state (Kindleberger 1973; Gilpin 1981; Keohane 1984). Kindleberger’s conclusion that the international system requires one stabilizer to prevent collective action failures became central to post-war interpretations crediting US hegemony with the Bretton Woods era stability. Yet, HST has faced increasing scrutiny. Critics emphasize that hegemony can also generate instability, as illustrated by the 2008 financial crisis (Gavris 2021). Realist perspectives contend that the rise of China and signs of US decline reveal limits of assuming that a single hegemon can indefinitely sustain liberal economic order (Layne 2012).

    The multilateral trade regime centered on the WTO has faced growing strains that expose institutional fragility, which was boosted by the 2008 financial crisis. The prolonged impasse of Doha Round and WTO’s difficulties updating rules have indicated institutional paralysis (Baldwin 2016). Last decade, the US shifted toward open unilateralism and protectionism (Hopewell 2020), imposing wide-ranging tariffs, invoking national-security exceptions, and paralyzing the Appellate Body (AB) by blocking appointments (Pauwelyn 2019). Such actions not only eroded WTO’s credibility but also promoted a crisis of legitimacy (Chow 2020).

    Building on these institutional and systemic debates, scholars underscore that hegemonic transitions are rarely peaceful and often hinge on geopolitical conflict. Allison’s notion of “Thucydides Trap” warns that when a rising power threatens to displace an established hegemon, structural pressures make war a likely outcome, as historical cases repeatedly illustrate. In parallel, Mearsheimer’s analysis in The Great Delusion highlights inherent limits of liberal internationalism, stressing that great powers inevitably revert to competition when security and survival are at stake. These perspectives shift attention from institutional fragility to the possibility that hegemonic change may be driven less by cooperation and rule-making than by disruptive dynamics of rivalry and conflict (Mearsheimer 2018).

    Yet important differences set current US-China rivalry apart from these historical precedents. Unlike Allison’s archetypal Thucydidean scenario, China’s rise may generate less strain on international security institutions and greater potential for peaceful adjustment. Its nuclear status and permanent seat at the UN Security Council, alongside its embeddedness in global markets, provide stabilizing factors (Fernandes 2023). Han and Paul (2020) emphasize that this rivalry differs from past hegemonic struggles in one crucial respect: absence of opposing military alliances. Unlike the Cold War, where balancing was organized through rival blocs, US-China competition is shaped by economic interdependence and China’s strategic emphasis on peaceful economic expansion. These features suggest an alternative trajectory of hegemonic transition that does not necessarily culminate in military confrontation.

    Thelen (2004) develops a robust analysis of institutional building, resilience, and transformation. For her, institutions cannot be understood as ‘immutable residues of critical junctures’ or even that they would be conditioned to follow only the direction assigned to them at the time of their conception (2004, p. 293). The approach advances a perspective in which institutions are not static outcomes of critical junctures but dynamic arenas of ongoing dispute. They are objects of ‘constant political contestation’, susceptible to changes in the political coalitions upon which they are founded (2004, p. 290). Thus, exogenous and endogenous factors interfere within the dynamics of institutions.

    The literature identifies four mechanisms of gradual institutional change that help understand the ongoing tensions within the WTO (Mahoney and Thelen 2010). The first, conversion, refers to a strategic reinterpretation and redeployment of existing rules, altering the enactment of existing rules to serve new ends. The second, drift, occurs when external changes alter the impact of existing rules without formal modification. It emerges when external shifts—economic, political, or technological—alter the functioning and consequences of institutional rules without formal change. The third, layering, involves introducing new rules alongside, or on top of, existing ones, creating parallel mechanisms that gradually alter institutional trajectories. Finally, institutional displacement occurs when existing rules are removed and replaced with new ones.

    These mechanisms demonstrate that institutional change is not solely a matter of punctuated equilibrium or external shocks. Rather, it emerges through incremental but cumulative processes of contestation, reinterpretation, and selective reinforcement. This framework allows us to understand WTO not as a victim of a sudden breakdown, but as an organization whose very rules and practices are being reworked from within under pressures of hegemonic rivalry.

    From an institutional point of view, a more acute American response to WTO challenges with China in the international trade regime refers to the latent questioning of working inside WTO, which is one of the topics of next sections.

    Piecing together fragments of evidence

    Our empirical strategy is twofold. We conduct an institutional-historical analysis to trace the escalation of geopolitical and economic tensions, and we provide an analysis of WTO disputes. Each case works as evidence representing a critical lens through which broader structural rivalries become visible at the institutional level (Bennett and Elman 2007). Combining these three domains – rule reinterpretation, institutional paralysis, and unilateral measures – we capture how macro-level power rivalry translates into meso-level institutional transformation. Our dataset covers 636 complaints in WTO’s DSB from 1995 to 2021 and 23 map participation and the AB national composition to produce systematic descriptive insights connecting hegemonic transition theories with the gradual but cumulative processes of institutional change within WTO.

    Walking in through the front door? China’s non-recognition as a market economy by the US

    Despite the initial liberalization under the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), the 1980s resulted in protectionist pressures on the Tokyo Round’s “Enabling Clause,” which introduced differential treatment for developing countries (Thorstensen and Jank 2005). Nevertheless, by then the US demanded deeper liberalization, culminating in the creation of WTO (Cepaluni and Fernandes 2022), which became a benchmark of the liberal economic and institutionalist scenario of the 1990s with the consolidation of rules and a binding DSS with an AB becoming one of the busiest international courts in the world (Reich 2018). The failed Doha Development Agenda (2001) symbolized an ambitious attempt to include new issues and integrate developing countries.

    China’s story with the multilateral trading regime has been complex within this context. After the Communist Revolution (1949), Taiwan formally left GATT, obtaining observer status (1965), while Beijing only applied to join as a contracting party in 1986, finally entering WTO in 2002. Hong Kong, a GATT member since 1986 under the UK, became an original WTO member in 1994. After returning to China in 1997 as a Special Administrative Region, Hong Kong retained separate customs territory status, allowing it autonomy within WTO. This arrangement facilitated trade flows and positioned China as a center for foreign investment (Chen 2009).

    The term of the ‘Accession Protocol of the People’s Republic of China’ was concluded only in 2001 to protect former WTO members. Nevertheless, whether China should be recognized as a Market Economy (MES) within the WTO framework represents the core clash between legal interpretation and geopolitical rivalry. The Accession Protocol addresses a fundamental problem in anti-dumping cases against a “Non-Market Economy” (NME). In such cases, investigating countries could set aside China’s own prices and costs—often considered distorted by state intervention—and instead rely on figures from a ‘surrogate’ economy to determine fair value. This practice made it easier to demonstrate dumping and justify higher anti-dumping duties (Urdinez and Masiero 2015).

    The heart of dispute lies in an expiration clause: Article 15(a)(ii) established that NME provision would lapse 15 years after China’s accession in December 2016. As that date approached, two opposing views emerged (Puccio 2015). China argued that the clause expired automatically and unconditionally. The US and EU, however, claimed the expiration applied only to one sub-clause, allowing them to continue using alternative methodologies under other WTO rules and their domestic laws.

    This debate is a proxy for a larger struggle, and it became the dispute for institutional conversion: China interpreted it as an unconditional guarantee of MES, whereas the US and EU strategically reinterpreted the same clause to preserve flexibility.

    The demise of the Dispute Settlement System (DSS)

    The Dispute Settlement System (DSS) is a cornerstone of WTO, embodying one of its fundamental principles: predictability. WTO agreements are binding and transparent, and violations can be formally addressed through a structured dispute resolution process. Without an effective dispute settlement mechanism, WTO’s legal framework would risk becoming a set of unenforceable norms, undermining the stability of the international trade regime. In contrast to the weaker legal structure of GATT, the DSS represents a major institutional advancement, ensuring compliance and providing legal certainty in trade relations through a Dispute Settlement Body (DSB) and an AB (Busch and Reinhardt 2002).

    The DSB operates based on four core principles: fairness, speed, effectiveness, and mutual acceptance. Its design reinforces procedural efficiency through strict resolution deadlines, reverse consensus rule, and possibility of appellation, making it significantly more robust than other dispute settlement instruments.

    The DSS follows a structured timeline, with deadlines set for each phase of dispute resolution3. The reverse consensus principle ensures that panel reports are automatically adopted unless all WTO members unanimously oppose them. Given the diversity of interests within the WTO, panel findings are highly unlikely to be rejected, reinforcing the regime’s authority and minimizing political interference.

    Based on WTO’s dispute database, available on the institution’s website in November 2021, we reorganized the information to establish dyads that identify a complaining and complained-about country. When several countries established a case against a specific one, we divided that dispute into the number of complaining parties. In total, we obtained 636 disputes between 1995 and November 2021. The dataset allows us to understand the inside dynamics of the DSS.

    Table 1 indicates the percentage of DSB usage in five-year periods since 1995. The US share dropped from 29% in the first period to about 15% between 2015 and 2019, while the share of the rest of the world grew from 48% to 64%, even after China entered the DSS, which accounts for about 8% in the last two full counted quinquennia. If we only count that period after 2002, when China joined the WTO, the US accounts for 15.1% of the complaints and China only 6.3% on average. After its entry, China became the third-largest user of the system. Besides, data indicate that the last five-year period does present a small increase in DSB usage. Details by a country participating in the DSB can be seen in Table 2.

    We note that, notwithstanding general trends, the US is still the leading user of the system, with 124 complaints out of 636 identified. In addition, the US has been the most active complainant against China, with twenty-three out of forty-three total cases and eight out of nineteen adjudicated cases. The country has been very successful in its disputes, with a victory in every adjudicated case.

    Mavroidis and Sapir (2019) indicate that since it acceded to WTO until 2019, China has been a defendant in 43 cases, six of which have been settled or terminated, and 11 are in consultations, some for a long time. Of remaining 26 cases, 19 have been adjudicated, while seven are pending at our writing4. China lost in nineteen cases and has complied with DSB recommendations in all of these cases.

    Figure 1 shows interesting trends regarding the number of complaints in DSB from 1995 to 2021. We identify a drop in absolute participation not only of the US but also of the EU since China joined the WTO. On one hand, this movement signals a loss of dynamism in the DSS for the main Western poles. On other hand, a general drop by the other countries was not observed. China’s participation has also come closer to that of the US.

    The declining use of the DSS illustrates institutional drift, as although the rules remain formally intact, their effectiveness has eroded as key members strategically abstain from initiating or pursuing disputes, weakening the system’s centrality without changing its legal framework.

    The jewel of the crown undervalued?

    The DSS also contemplates a fixed body to review the DSB’s decisions. Within the logic of any mature dispute settlement system, recourse to another decision center is possible to guarantee full defense and adversary proceedings. This is the role of the AB, which reviews the questions of law presented in the DSB’s decision.

    The AB comprises seven members who serve a four-year term and are re-electable for another four. Its permanence counters the ad hoc character of the panels and contributes to the formation of ‘case law,’ such as a set of uniform and cohesive interpretations within the WTO regulatory system. The relevance of the AB’s role has led some members of the WTO GC to coin it ‘the crown jewel’5.

    The exercise of the DBS’s powers has been hampered since 2016 when the US refused to endorse the reappointment process of AB members. Figure 2 indicates the functionality of the AB dramatically decreased in 2016. Until the end of 2023, there was no active designation of members from the AB, blocking any institutional innovation independent of the country members’ agreement. The first case was the non-reappointment of Seung Wha Chang, from South Korea. Since the Obama administration (2008-2016), US has adopted a strategy to prevent the election of panelists to the body. Faced with vacant positions at the end of their terms of office, the most relevant body in the institution was emptied.

    As a result, its legitimacy was questioned (Farrely 2019), preventing its decision-making and substantive effects on the cosmopolitan consolidation of a multilateral trade regime. The AB would have no members joining it as the terms of the still-active members came to an end, resulting in complete paralysis of the institution.

    We also investigated the members’ nationality. Table 3 shows the length of the mandates by country. We observe that the US is the only WTO member with representatives on the AB from its inception until 2019. India had representatives from 2000 until 2019, and Europe with members from Germany, Italy, and Belgium until 2017. China, in turn, has had a representative since 2008, whose last term ended in 2020.

    Notwithstanding its technical character, the AB trend to empower itself by seeking binding interpretation to third parties, thus making it closer to a judge-made law system, has transformed the multilateral trade regime from within (Steinberg 2004). Recognizing this autonomy as a manifestation of institutional power is fundamental because it inserts the functional exercise of the DSB, especially via the AB.

    In the realm of endogenous institutional determinants of WTO transformations, there has been a general discomfort with the DSS, especially the AB, regarding an apparent exacerbation of its jurisdiction with broad interpretations that draw near to an illegitimate quasi-legislative body (Read 2007). Despite the quasi-legislative rites for resolving disputes in the AB, the decision to accept the results of negotiations remains at the national level, so the political dimension prevails (Guzman and Simmons 2002; Zhang and Li 2014).

    The AB’s excesses in exercising its powers, labeled in discussions at WTO GC as overreach, would take place (i) to promote its decisions as if they had precedent effect hence past decisions could bind future cases without members explicit agreement; (ii) to consider the internal law of the members as applicable law and not as a matter of fact, thereby interpreting and applying it, and (iii) as a quasi-judicial body would have been transformed into a quasi-legislative body.

    On 12 December 2018, WTO members agreed to launch an informal process to deal with the AB situation, led by the New Zealand ambassador, David Walker. The so-called ‘Walker Process’ resulted in three reports presented in 20196. Two key points were considered: the overreach via interpretation and precedent approach through the ‘cogent reason’ rationale made up by the AB. A draft resolution for a GC decision was prepared by Mr. Walker and introduced for adoption for the meeting held on 9-10 December 2019. However, members did not reach a consensus to adopt it (WT/GC/M/181) (World Trade Organization 2020).

    Alongside those efforts, EU-led Multi-Party Interim Appeal Arbitration Arrangement (MPIA) emerged in 2020 as an interim response to the paralysis of the AB. It has preserved the essence of the two-tier adjudication system by replacing appeals to the AB with binding arbitration among participating members. The MPIA functions as an interim mechanism for procedural reforms and highlights the potential to redesign the DSS, demonstrating how institutional layering can sustain core functions even in the face of deadlock. Yet, the limited membership prevents it from fully replacing the universality of the AB, and questions remain about its long-term legitimacy as a solution to the crisis. In this sense, the MPIA embodies the resilience and fragility of the multilateral trading system under stress (Pauwelyn 2023; Starshinova 2021).

    The paralysis of the AB, in this sense, triggered institutional layering, as in the absence of its functioning, alternative mechanisms were created alongside the WTO framework. Rather than formally replacing the DSS, these parallel arrangements add a new layer that gradually reshapes authority and reconfigures the centrality of the multilateral system.

    Bringing the war to the trade floor and a red carpet to unilateralism

    The commercial relationship between China and the US reveals a strong economic interdependence under stress. The US became China’s main trade partner in 2014, but since then, interdependence has evolved into rivalry, and disputes have increasingly bypassed multilateral venues. The expression ‘trade war’, referring to rising trading dispute between US and China, was incorporated in WTO’s daily affairs in 2018 when it appeared for the first time in official records7. Beyond rhetoric, this moment signaled a shift from a rule-based system to unilateral action, illustrating a process of displacement where multilateral adjudication was sidelined. Fulfilling campaign promises under the slogan “Make America Great Again”, Trump escalated tensions over intellectual property, subsidies, and national security, privileging unilateral trade policy over multilateral solutions (Mattoo and Staiger 2019). From the US perspective, these measures addressed longstanding concerns with technology transfer and subsidies to state-owned enterprises that threatened the US economy and national security interests (Sachs 2019).

    The GATT legal text allows general (Article XX) and special (Article XXI) exceptions applicable to its main principles. The special exceptions concern security issues, which could be applied in a loose situation: if action was taken when considered necessary to protect a member’s essential security interests (Article XXI, b). In specifying the context of such essential security issues, the subparagraphs narrow down to the following: (i) fissionable materials, (ii) traffic in arms, and (iii) time of war or emergency in international relations. The Trump administration invoked this clause broadly to justify unilateral tariffs, stretching its original meaning.

    Despite that, DSB had previously challenged a strict self-judgment approach by a country trying to justify its trade policy on such grounds. On 5 April 2019, a DSB panel ruled against Russia’s unilateral trade restrictions raised on Ukraine in the context of the 2014 annexation of Crimea by Russia (Russia—Measures Concerning Traffic in Transit) (World Trade Organization 2019). The DSB panel found that it had jurisdiction over the case and could assess the legality of the security issues by verifying whether there was a genuine and close relationship of ends and means as demanded in previous cases by the AB. This is one example of the DSS altering the international trade regime from within the WTO.

    The DSB narrowed down the conditions for what could be considered an ‘emergency situation in international relations.’ It stated that they ‘can therefore be interpreted as ‘armed conflict,’ ‘latent armed conflict,’ ‘heightened tension or crisis,’ or ‘general instability engulfing or surrounding a state,’ and such situations were to be taken as an ‘objective reality’ that must also be in connection with temporal issues8. Thus, it could be the case that for the DBS, the so-called ‘trade war’ could be unfit to justify deploying Article XXI for unilateral trade policies, but that remains to be seen by future litigation. This precedent suggested that ‘trade war’ rhetoric might not suffice to justify unilateral tariffs. Still, US invocation of national security marked a reinterpretation of rules and a crucial step toward bypassing WTO adjudication in favor of unilateral action.

    By then, US had deployed several unilateral and not always WTO-consistent trade enforcement tools, such as Section 301 (‘Relief from Unfair Trade Practices’) of US Trade Act of 1974, the ‘Special 301’ also of US Trade Act of 1974, and Section 232 of US Expansion Trade Act of 1962.

    Against the backdrop of unilateralisation of trade responses, the US strategy of undermining the AB became effective since Section 301 could be used to enforce US rights sponsored by trade agreements or to respond to unreasonable, unjustifiable, or discriminatory foreign practices against the US on a unilateral basis. The trade tool was applied very infrequently against China between 1975 and 2020, with the Asian country facing only six Section 301 investigations, representing 4.6% of all investigations during this period. The 2017 investigations, on the contrary, resulted in 73.1% of US imports from China being placed under tariff revision between 2018 and 2019, with additional rates going from 10% up to 25% ad valorem (Schwarzenberg 2020). The Chinese government took a hammering approach with four revised lists of tariff countermeasures, with increases from 2.5% to 25% (US Trade Representative 2023). Section 301 thus displaced WTO dispute settlement with unilateral sanctions and bilateral retaliation.

    The ‘Special 301’ refers to the extension to property rights after the amendment made by the Omnibus Trade and Competitiveness Act of 1988 and the 1994 Uruguay Round Agreements Act. This procedure allows the country to monitor the TRIPs Agreement’s compliance, and it catalogs countries in different categories (‘priority foreign country,’ ‘priority watch list,’ ‘watch list,’ ‘306 monitoring’, and ‘special mention’). Once a country is identified as a Priority Foreign Country (PFC), a Section 301 investigation is open, leading to some trade sanctions. After China’s membership in WTO, the country was put under ‘monitoring’ only for the first four years (2001-2004). Since then, it has been considered a PFC. In August 2017, the US administration started investigating China’s practices related to forced technology transfer, unfair licensing, and intellectual property policies (US Trade Representative 2017). This procedure institutionalized unilateral oversight of TRIPS obligations, bypassing, again, WTO multilateral monitoring.

    The last one, Section 232, allows the investigation to ascertain if a specific import threatens US national security. The US government seldom used this procedure; from 1963 to 2017, only thirty-five investigations were opened on such grounds. Before Trump, the last time a President imposed sanctions based on Section 232 was in 1986 (Fefer 2022). In March 2018, the average added fare ranged between 10% and 25% on certain steel and aluminum imports (US Trade Representative 2023).

    Disputes such as those initiated by US President Donald Trump represent a great dilemma in the international multilateral trade regime. Seven lawsuits to adjudicate trade barriers created by the Trump administration have been opened at the DBS. This intensified US movement to question the legitimacy and viability of WTO, including its rules and outcomes, with a consequent drop in demand filings at the DBS, connecting the expansion of unilateral tariffs with the decline of the DBS. The quest for unilateral instruments, especially against China, and the shift in US approach can be perceived as a signal of frustration of the US towards WTO (Bown 2019).

    Consequently, the country has searched for alternative ways to handle its complaints about China. The combination of greater demand for adjudication and disabling the system’s most important body exemplifies the effective substitution of WTO adjudication with domestic instruments and retaliatory tariffs.

    Fearful disputes and tit-for-tat retaliation illustrate how displacement operates: multilateral adjudication is not eliminated but bypassed, replaced by unilateral, bilateral, and regional instruments. The so-called “trade war” thus represents more than a rhetorical shift — it reflects the erosion of WTO’s role as anchor of global trade governance and its substitution by fragmented and competing venues.

    Using national security-based tariffs revived by President Trump illustrates the limits of WTO. The imposition of unilateral tariffs, combined with the paralysis of AB, reveals displacement in practice: WTO dispute system is not inherently unable to address conflict, but systemic rivalry has led major powers to substitute it with unilateral, bilateral, and regional solutions. This trend underscores the erosion of multilateral trade governance and sets the stage for how hegemonic rivalry reshapes institutional orders.

    Endogenous and exogenous mechanisms of change: conversion, drift, layering, displacement

    Institutional change in WTO cannot be reduced to moments of rupture or simple decline due to inevitable conflict among great powers. Rather, it reflects cumulative and contested adjustments as states reinterpret rules, exploit ambiguities, and shift governance venues. Mahoney and Thelen’s typology provides a useful lens to unpack these processes, revealing how institutional resilience often conceals profound transformations beneath the surface. In the case of WTO, these transformations are not merely technical but reveal political struggles over authority and legitimacy that jeopardize trade governance.

    We identify two intertwined trajectories of transformation: endogenous change through reinterpretation of existing rules, and exogenous pressures that displace governance outside WTO. The first involves endogenous change through conversion, drift, and layering, driven by reinterpretation of existing rules without formal reform. They demonstrate how existing rules are subtly bent or hollowed out under new conditions. The second trajectory involves explicit change through displacement, which became visible when calls for WTO reform were officially introduced.

    Conversion is evident in how provisions designed to protect exceptional interests, such as national security clauses, have been strategically reinterpreted to justify sweeping unilateral trade restrictions. Since entering WTO, China has recorded relatively high compliance with adverse DSS decisions (Zhang and Li 2014), corroborating the strategy of peaceful ascension (Glaser and Medeiros 2007). Still, on 12 December 2016, the country requested a consultation with the EU regarding its anti-dumping methodology (DS516) (World Trade Organization 2025). China’s MES dispute illustrates how existing rules were redirected toward competing interpretations rather than being discarded or rewritten (USTR 2025). Such moves reframe the meaning of established rules without formally altering them, shifting WTO’s enforcement logic toward geopolitical confrontation and selective regional realignments. Drift, in turn, arises when exogenous transformations change the context in which WTO agreements operate, while the rules themselves remain static. The absence of institutional adaptation generates structural gaps, as the same provisions produce diminishing effects as members strategically reduce their engagement with the system. This erosion exemplifies how DSS has become increasingly unable to perform its original function of guaranteeing predictability and compliance within WTO legal order.

    Layering, in turn, is visible when AB shapes institutional evolution through legal interpretation and establishes trade jurisprudence. The AB of DSB largely carried out this endogenous transformation, which gradually expanded its jurisdictional role, assuming a quasi-judicial review function not originally envisioned by WTO’s founding members (Read 2007). The layering effect expanded jurisprudential authority, often criticized as an illegitimate quasi-legislative move, without corresponding political consent, fueling legitimacy crises that reverberated into AB’s collapse. In response to this vacuum, new mechanisms were layered onto the multilateral system, most notably EU-led MPIA (2020) and backed by 24 members and EU. Rather than signaling the persistence of WTO in its original design, MPIA illustrates the coexistence of parallel dispute settlement frameworks that partially replicate and transform the multilateral regime.

    Displacement, by contrast, stems from unilateral practices and the migration of governance outside WTO, as regional and plurilateral agreements accumulate and generate parallel legal frameworks that compete with the multilateral system. These arrangements dilute the organization’s centrality while gradually redefining trade rules. The dynamic is visible in the collapse of AB, where US blockage of judicial appointments effectively suspended the institution’s authority. It is most evident, however, in US–China trade tensions, where unilateral tariffs and retaliatory measures displaced DSS with bilateral confrontation. The rhetoric of a “trade war” reflects more than political discourse: it embodies bypassing of WTO rules and the substitution of multilateral adjudication by fragmented and competing venues of governance.     To sum up, the controversy over China’s market economy status exemplifies conversion, as existing rules were strategically reinterpreted to serve geopolitical goals. The declining use of DSS and the paralysis of its AB illustrate drift and layering, as rules remain formally intact but lose effectiveness while parallel mechanisms gradually reshape dispute settlement authority. Finally, escalation of trade wars and recourse to unilateral measures illustrate displacement, as states bypass WTO’s rules and replace them with direct power-based retaliation and tit-for-tat strategies, fostering a conflict-driven trade regime.

    Viewed collectively, these mechanisms reveal WTO’s evolution less as decline than as transformation: its architecture is continually reshaped by conversion, drift, layering, and displacement. Internal contestation and external pressures have constantly reconfigured its institutional foundation. This hybrid pattern unsettles classical theories of institutional change as interplay between layering, conversion, and drift represents an incremental, yet cumulative, form of institutional transformation that does not neatly fit within dichotomies of stability or collapse.

    Instead of a complete rupture, international trade governance is undergoing a gradual reconfiguration and possible fragmentation, where old rules are reinterpreted, and new institutional arrangements emerge amid growing global uncertainty. The reform process could lead to either more layering—through incremental modifications within existing framework—or institutional displacement, removing and replacing foundational rules. Progress on this reform has been limited so far.

    Concluding remarks: Thucydides trap and the WTO

    Since its origins, WTO institutional-normative structure has allowed the expansion of the number of topics and members, increasing the degree of complexity of the negotiation process of the multilateral trade regime.

    The WTO moved away from its initial model, as it had to deal with the tensions of the Thucydides Trap. Its great promoter was no longer the economic hegemon with competitive potential superior to all international peers. The US stance reflects its broader effort to resist shifting global power dynamics. The country that promoted the diffusion and consolidation of the rules and principles of economic openness for the liberalization of the international trade regime no longer feels contemplated by the established structure, which has allowed competitors to rise. Our main conclusion is that there is evidence that WTO is no longer the primary locus of trade liberalization and that its main institutions and functions have been sidelined.

    Although the US had faced strong international competition in the 1970s, threats came from smaller economies and politically ideologically aligned countries like Japan and the European Community. The world has witnessed China emerging in the technological, financial, political, and, albeit more cautiously, military and monetary arenas. The political model proposed by the new competitor offers an alternative to the hegemonic liberal order of the West.

    The spotlight on the so-called trade war overshadows an incremental and ongoing multidimensional competition between the two powers. The ramifications of this dispute are not limited to possible rearrangements in the multilateral trade regime but also spill over to other spheres of international governance and an already consistent Chinese presence worldwide.

    Our data show WTO is no longer the primary forum for trade liberalization. Major trade powerhouses have increasingly sidelined DSB, and unilateral strategies, not always WTO-consistent, have been heavily used (Hopewell 2021). We found evidence that supports the hypothesis that WTO is undergoing a process of institutional readjustment, even in the absence of formal changes to its regulatory framework, resulting from changes in the macro structure of the system. The center of the dispute, concentrated in the trade sphere, added to other challenges intensified by the less accommodating postures of the US, threatens the survival of the multilateral trade regime itself. With the collapse of the Doha Round, the rule-making function has lost scope, and WTO no longer provides a useful forum for negotiations. The actions of Section 301 not only brought trade frictions with China to the fore, but this strategy also represented a possible breakdown of the multilateral trade regime. Secondly, dismantling the DBS undermines the other major function of WTO, creating a quasi-judicial process to deal with disagreements and violations of trade agreements, moving closer to promoting cosmopolitan trade law, a chimera of the optimistic expectations of the 1990s.

    It is still not possible to predict the future of the WTO. Currently, no actor has hegemonic potential to sustain the stability of the international free trade regime. The US strategy of bypassing WTO mechanisms promotes dysfunctions within the system and reduces its ability to regulate international trade effectively. Even though it is highly institutionalized, changes in the international system have impacted its functioning precisely because its main promoter is now its main tormentor. The findings illustrate the role of the hegemon in guaranteeing the institutionality of international regimes, even in scenarios with high institutional density and quasi-judicial processes. The processes of gradual institutional change endogenous to the institution have given its de facto operation a new format.

    We started this work asking: (i) how the shifts in the international system affect WTO and its institutional framework? How do macro changes impact meso arrangements? Our research points out that the International System altered institutional balance, with the weakening of North American leadership and the reduction of the relative institutional autonomy of the DSS. Regarding our second question: would it happen anyway, or is this phenomenon intrinsically related to Trump’s office?

    Unlike the League of Nations, WTO has achieved a solid institutional status that will probably not disappear, but will pass through institutional change. Furthermore, we observed those trajectories before Trump’s first and second cabinets. These changes are not the result of the specific actions of a political leader or his ideological stance. The fracture in WTO is the result of changes in the structures of the international system and not of a supposedly more offensive and intransigent stance by the current US president.

    In the short or medium term, tensions arising from China-US rivalry will not be expected to result in military tensions. However, the effects on the WTO are already clear. The changing dynamics of the global economy affect its ability to remain the largest economic forum for international trade.

    On the other hand, current challenges and dysfunctions of WTO do not mean its complete nullification. The existence of endogenous institutional adaptation mechanisms allows us to envision their survival over time and the reorganization of two activities. The mechanisms indicate that even in crisis, there are attempts at rehabilitation through the collective provision of public goods for its institutional renewal, such as the still incipient case of the MPIA. The institution is still alive, but breathing with enormous difficulties and unable to fulfill its mandate or even close the long negotiations on the Doha development agenda. Furthermore, the main institutional representation of the euphoria of the 1990s is completely paralyzed by the actions of its own mentor. The reform negotiations will be decisive for the future of the organization and the multilateral trade regime.
