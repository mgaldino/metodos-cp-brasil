Beyond Science Fiction: Magical Realism as an Approach to Literature in International Relations

Além da ficção científica: o realismo mágico como uma abordagem da literatura em Relações Internacionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The importance of works of science fiction to scholars of International Relations has already been established by discussions pertaining to its pedagogical value, its value as a study object and even its value as a constitutive aspect of world politics. In this article, we argue that it is equally important to give attention to works of magical realism in IR, especially because magical realism blurs the boundaries between literary and genre fiction, posing a constructive challenge to the different ways literature is either considered art or mere entertainment. By relaying the deep connection between magical realism and its birthplace, Latin America, we use One Hundred Years of Solitude, by Gabriel García Márquez, and The House of Spirits, by Isabel Allende, to illustrate how magical realist pieces can serve as reflections of Latin American sensibilities and therefore provide relevant insights for the scholar of IR.

    Keywords
    magical realism; science fiction; Latin American literature; decolonialism; imperialism

    Resumo

    A importância das obras de ficção científica para os acadêmicos de Relações Internacionais já foi estabelecida por meio de discussões relativas ao seu valor pedagógico, seu valor como objeto de estudo e até mesmo seu valor como um aspecto constitutivo da política mundial. Neste artigo, argumentamos que é igualmente importante dar atenção às obras de realismo mágico nas RI, especialmente porque o realismo mágico borra as fronteiras entre a ficção literária e a ficção de gênero, representando um desafio construtivo para as diferentes maneiras pelas quais a literatura é considerada arte ou mero entretenimento. Ao relatar a profunda conexão entre o realismo mágico e seu local de nascimento, a América Latina, usamos Cem Anos de Solidão, de Gabriel García Márquez, e A Casa dos Espíritos, de Isabel Allende, para ilustrar como as obras do realismo mágico podem servir como reflexos das sensibilidades latino-americanas e, portanto, fornecer percepções relevantes para o estudioso de RI.

    Palavras-chave
    realismo mágico; ficção científica; literatura latino-americana; decolonialismo; imperialismo

    Introduction

    No genre of literature can be easily defined. The very need to classify works by genre doesn’t exist in a vacuum and can be motivated by a variety of reasons, from the organisation of a particular artistic movement to market definitions that help sell certain books better than others. The genres themselves, then, appear into the literary scene within a context, with a reason for its establishment – again, be it the historical events surrounding its emergence, a particular artistic manifesto, or marketability of a certain type of book.

    Science fiction, as the subject of this volume, is just as elusive as any other genre. It is widely understood as having its origins in English gothic literature, with Frankenstein by Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley being the founding stone of the genre. In fact, Frankenstein serves as a foundation for two genres at the same time, being also considered the first horror story to be published. In it, science trumps nature in the creation of an artificial life, the wonders of science walk hand in hand with the horrors humans are capable of, and it questions the morality of using knowledge to move the natural direction of life and death. In its origins, science fiction has much of the supernatural and fantastical in it, and Brantlinger (1980) argues that it is, in fact, a form of fantasy, one that focuses on a fantasy of the future, and that definitions of the genre that try to pin it as a more rational form of fiction due to the recurrent theme of science as a catalyst for plot events tend to ignore the intense influence of Gothic romance in the origins and baseline structures of the genre.

    As elusive to define as science fiction might be, however, magical realism, the subject of this article, proves itself to be even more elusive. In fact, it could be argued that its elusiveness is a key characteristic of magical realism. Just as much as science fiction, magical realism has its roots in a specific context and is influenced by genres that came before it – namely, as Bennett (1999) points out, in realism itself, in the writings of the colonisers of the Americas, and in the European tales of chivalry from the 15th century. Magical realism has One Hundred Years of Solitude, by Gabriel García Márquez, as its widely recognized foundational text, a work that marks the uniquely Latin American flavour that marks the genre. Generally, magical realism is defined by narratives that mix mundane episodes, historical events of immense significance, and incidents that might be understood, especially in the West, as preternatural or supernatural.

    This definition, however, does not encompass much of what makes magical realism unique – and, if any definition could do that, Bennett argues, much of the genre’s power and impact would be lost. Beyond the weirdness of mundane events existing side by side with levitating priests and vengeful ghosts, magical realism is a product of its geography, and has colonialism and political conflict as central themes of its narratives. The genre tends to mix fiction with historical narrative, both as a political statement and influenced by European writings from the period of the conquest of the Americas, where a certain naiveté could be felt from the reports of the Spanish, relating the history they were active participants in with a mixture of cold description and fanciful invention (Bennett 1999: 45). Miguel de Cervantes’s work is also cited by Bennett as an influence, also emphasizing the mix between fiction and fact, and between literature and history:

    Devices taken from earlier romantic literature include the pretended historicity of the text. The author claims to be relating a story told by another author, Cide Hamete Benengeli. Many features in Don Quixote come from the chivalresque, such as the interruptions for interpolated stories which add to the suspense, the ridiculous names of the characters, and certain words and phrases, especially from Amadis. The issue of the relationship between language and life continuously threads through the novel, and Don Quixote is constantly confused about what is real and what is not, over fact and fiction. (Bennett 1999: 61)

    While Spanish conquest literature and the satire of chivalrous stories of Cervantes are cited as historical influences for the development of magical realism, modern Latin American authors are cited as the other side of influences for the genre. Authors mentioned as influential include: Machado de Assis, with his scathing sarcasm and unreliable narrators; Mario de Andrade, who tried to develop a uniquely Brazilian style of literature, humour, and surrealism; and Jorge Luis Borges, a writer who emphasized the blurring of the lines between the psychological and the factual, and who had a ‘professed affection for the traditionally lowly genre of detective fiction’ (Bennett 1999: 72). A very clear line can be drawn among all these influences that lead directly to magical realism, where the focus is always on dealing with the contradictions between what is perceived as real or imaginary, between the fantastical and the concrete, between history and fiction. Magical realism dances across this binary, working within it and denying it at the same time – and, above all things, doing so in a way that reflects the historical and social environment in which it came to be: Latin America.

    Much like science fiction and fantasy, a lot of magical realism deals with the surreal, the impossible and the supernatural. However, unlike the previously mentioned genres, magical realism is imbued by the literary community with a sense of prestige, mainly signified by the recognition of authors such as Gabriel García Márquez, winner of the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1982. That is not to say, however, that magical realism is supposedly superior to science fiction and fantasy in anyway – on the contrary, we aim to show how they have much more in common than these classifications make it seem. Our task here is not to pit magical realism, fantasy, and science fiction against each other. Rather, our goal is to reflect upon genre distinctions and what they mean politically, and how blurring the lines between marginalized literary genres can broaden our analytical horizons when connecting literature, art, and IR.

    To achieve this goal, we divide our work in three parts. In the first section, we discuss science fiction and its presence in IR, to locate this article within a broader context of discussing the contributions of literature, and speculative literature more broadly, to the field. Next, we discuss how speculative fiction and science fiction are perceived as a less respected form of literature within literary circles. Mainly, the editorial and academic worlds of literature mark a distinction between genre fiction (where science fiction and fantasy are included) – a commercially accessible, supposedly more simplistic and less artistically respected form of literature – and literary fiction – considered by many to be a style of novel that transcends genre, and that has more artistic value than its counterpart; an artistry that is decided by arbitrary rules, defined in a very specific, White, and Western space.

    Finally, in the third section, we take a deeper dive into magical realism and its intricacies. As a form of literature that is so uniquely Latin American, it becomes a window to the lived experiences and to the bloody history of the region. As such, magical realism can become a tool for knowledge production in IR, a way of looking and interpreting the world that escapes the confines of Western International Relations scholarship and opens the doors to a deeper connection between IR and literature in the Global South. With this, we hope to demonstrate that engagement with magical realism is just as productive and important to IR as the engagement with science fiction.

    International Relations and science fiction: a productive partnership

    In IR, science fiction has been garnering more interest since the end of the 20th and beginning of the 21st century. It is productive to think of Der Derian and Shapiro’s (1989) book on how IR is made up of a deep and widespread intertextuality – that is, that our field is made up of relationalities, since we derive meaning from concurrent and sometimes contradictory interpretations of texts of most different natures. Helpfully, Shapiro himself sheds light into the fact that ‘literary texts have a mediated relationship with the social reality they represent’ (1989: 11), arguing that social reality oftentimes is built upon and is skilfully reflected in all sorts of literary works. For Shapiro and Der Derian, it is important to consider intertextuality in the studies of IR because the production of meaning is an issue that is central to the field, and literature and other cultural productions are places in which meanings are made and contested. Therefore, there is much to gain in examining the relationship between how texts are produced and politics – and IR scholars are well equipped to deal with such issues.

    Furthermore, it can be argued that Joseph Nye’s (2005) work on soft power has also paved the way for studies of such cultural objects within IR, because of the emphasis it placed on the importance of cultural power in the international politics sphere was deeply influential in the ways through which cultural objects were understood as political tools. However, the study of cultural artefacts within IR has incorporated other arguments other than the power of building an attractive set of values through different works of representation.

    Science fiction as a genre has always had deep political roots, because it is, at its core, made up of stories about the evolution of society – and, especially, how to deal with anxieties relating to technological advances, and how technology can shape humanity and human relations. Works of science fiction are also well known to serve as inspiration for scholars throughout the social sciences – such as Paul Krugman (2012) and his relationship with Isaac Asimov’s Foundation trilogy, which propelled him to pursue a career in Economics.1

    Jutta Weldes (2003) points out that science fiction’s role in the study of IR amounts to more than analyses of different cultural objects that are of interest because of its cult status or box-office results (Dodds 2006). For Weldes, it is relevant to consider the fact that science fiction and other objects associated with pop culture have the important role of not only reproducing and sustaining the conditions for world politics, but also that pop culture and science fiction can indeed produce and create such conditions. We agree with Weldes when she argues that science fiction and pop culture harbour productive intertextual relations with IR; that is especially so when we consider the astounding popularity of certain science fiction pieces in the field – such as, of course, Star Trek (Buzan 2010)and even the Foundation trilogy, as we just mentioned.

    Works of science fiction have been attracting more and more interest in the field of IR. Weldes’ own book is a fine example of this phenomenon, since it’s a volume made up of different pieces analysing different works in the science fiction genre, mainly in TV shows and movies. As previously mentioned, Star Trek is a classical case – its setting and the role of the Federation can be illustrative of the ways international politics are understood and processed by audiences.2 Most epic science fiction stories that take place in deep space and sometimes portray huge intergalactic empires – Star Wars, Dune, Foundation – can be used to helpfully enlighten a great number of IR theories and concepts (Weber 2001), and that is because they operate in the same logics of scale that we are used to when theorizing and thinking about world politics (Walker 1994). Therefore, it comes as no surprise that such material has received attention from IR scholars, either as a pedagogical tool or as understanding the texts as a crystallization of certain sets of values and dilemmas that permeated the society that produced them.

    Weldes goes on to argue that besides being an important source of analysis to IR, there’s another, deeper connection between the discipline and science fiction and pop culture in general. For her, it is possible, through the engagement with science fiction texts, to challenge the limits and borders of common sense, and even the limits of the political imaginaries that make up world politics. This is in tandem with what Grayson, Davies, and Philpott (2009) tell us when they say that world politics and pop culture are in reality a continuum: that they both interfere with and effectively produce each other, because they understand that there is an important everyday aspect to the study of world politics, and therefore it matters to take seriously the works that have importance and resonate with an increasingly greater section of the population.

    Therefore, pop culture and its science fiction pieces can be relevant and even play a constitutive role in world politics, as posed by Nexon and Neumann (2006). It is productive to consider such potential when engaging with such objects in an academic context, because, as Neumann and Nexon emphasize, pop culture is an important source of information. They demonstrate that not only heads of state rely on the engagement with pop culture to know more about the world and even make decisions regarding foreign policy and the offering of aid, but more crucial are the ways in which such works operate collectively, building and putting in place the very conditions of possibility that frame world politics.

    Hence, science fiction has a very interesting role in questioning the limits of the field of IR and even what constitutes its possible objects of study. In the following sections, we examine the intricate and contradictory relationship between science fiction and magical realism, arguing that such discussions can helpfully illustrate and pave the way for the examination of the ways in which literature can be a useful tool of analysis in IR. We begin, however, by introducing the debate over the merits and validity of genre fiction versus literary fiction, which poses interesting connections with the ways in which knowledge production is discussed and understood.

    On the categorization of literatures and knowledges

    After discussing the ways in which science fiction and IR intersect and relate to each other, it is time to turn to a nuanced discussion on the limits of what is considered to be literary art and what is considered merely ‘commercial fiction’. In doing so, we aim to question the limits of these categories and put into focus their artificiality and the fact that they exist to favour a certain worldview of what makes up worthy knowledge and literature.

    Defining genre fiction and analysing the controversies over genre and literary fiction

    We could begin by discussing Aristotle and his treatise on what makes up good poetic art. His Poetics (1996) is largely concerned with how to best go about writing epic poetry and works of tragedy, and the Greek tradition has greatly inspired and given the basis upon which Western critical thought has been built. However, it is not our aim to present a thorough history of what constitutes the field of literary criticism and genre theory.3 We are interested in examining how the debate between what constitutes literary fiction and genre fiction came about and how it influences debates on the validity and quality of literature ever since.

    Nonetheless, it is worth noticing that such discussions arguing for different tiers of literature, with one of them being inherently better, with more qualities and more worthy of praise than the others, is not anything new.4 Gupta (2015) helpfully maps how the debate around the merits of genre fiction versus the merits of literary fiction has manifested itself throughout academia and literary circles, pointing out the ways in which such discussions populate the field of literature – be it literary art or what is more commonly called ‘commercial literature’, considered to be of inferior artistic value.

    For Gupta, it is useful to think of this distinction in terms of the two different sides of the ‘literary establishment’: first, there are the cultural elites, clearly represented by the academics who build entire careers analysing and poring themselves over books that are understood as worthy of high acclaim; but there is also the side who is involved in publishing and selling books, who has a stake in its market. These distinctions, then, end up producing a dichotomy between genre and literary fiction: the latter being more heavily associated with artistic expression and ‘real’ literature, and the former being understood as intrinsically commercial and therefore somewhat lacking what constitutes actual art.

    Literary fiction is often described as not being limited by any genre – it is something so sublime that it surpasses the crude classifications that name a story a romance, murder mystery, or science fiction. It is frequently defined as being character-driven, and a work in which the act of writing and the stylistic choices that come with it are as important as the content of the plot. Literary fiction, then, is sometimes incredibly experimental, and frequently its goal is to examine human nature and human relationships, with beautifully articulated prose. That is why works of literary fiction can be considered to be too difficult to understand, sometimes boring or even inaccessible due to its language and style. However, these novels – because, as Gupta points out, traditionally only novels are considered to be literary fiction – are still appreciated by the specialized critics. Literary fiction has a high cost of entry, because there are strict requirements for its prose and the construction of the plot and its themes; therefore, it can be seen as presumptuous and even arrogant, stowed far away from the masses.

    Genre fiction, on the other hand, suffers from the opposite problems. Genre fiction does not have the requirement to be character-driven. In fact, most of its works are defined as plot-centred, with a great importance placed in the events that drive the story forward. Relying on tropes and seldom worrying about the stylistic choices that move literary fiction writers, genre fiction is produced to be consumed by a greater number of people, with somewhat easier and not incredibly sophisticated writing, that does not rely heavily in symbolism. As it has other purposes than those of literary fiction, and because it often depicts otherworldly and sometimes paranormal elements, genre fiction is considered to be produced exclusively for entertainment purposes, and not constituting works of art.

    Of course, it goes without saying, that many works of literary fiction have been met with wild and raucous acclaim, becoming best-sellers and classics – such as The Secret History, by Donna Tartt. Accordingly, there are pieces of genre fiction that have been so praised by critics that are considered to be exemplary works of art – such as 1984, by George Orwell. However, it is worth noticing that such distinctions are not as stable as they appear, even if there has been significant scholarly debate towards defining and limiting what is literary fiction (Fowler 1979).

    This confusion is so widespread that it affects even the writers themselves. In 2015, Kazuo Ishiguro, the writer of Never Let Me Go, which received great reviews and was considered one of the best English-language novels of the last century, gave an interview stating that he was afraid that readers would consider his next work, The Buried Giant, a work of fantasy and therefore would not be interested in it (Alter 2015). This propelled a fiery response from Ursula K. Le Guin herself, that felt that Ishiguro thought the moniker ‘fantasy’ to be an insult. Ishiguro later stated that he was ‘on the side of the pixies and dragons’ (Cain 2015).

    The most intriguing element of this small feud between highly acclaimed authors is that Never Let Me Go, published in 2005, was already a dystopic science fiction novel. Ishiguro states that, on the occasion of its publication, there was a highly contested debate over whether his novel was really a piece of science fiction (Cain 2015), because it was so masterly crafted, and provided such a delicate and sensitive examination of human psyche, that it could only be a pure work of art. However, as Le Guin pointed out, such genre elements, be it of fantasy or science fiction, are great devices in literature to think and question reality (Le Guin 2015).

    This only highlights the tendency of overlooking science fiction and other kinds of genre fiction, undermining it because of the use of its tropes or because it is not realist enough (Brantlinger 1980). However, as we previously pointed out, this division does not always correspond to how the works are received by audiences. As Michaels (2000) states, there is more than one case of novels that were considered genre fiction in the time of their publication but are now deemed to be works of the highest literature.

    We would like to emphasize that science fiction, in particular, is deeply inspired by real world politics, and it is an increasingly productive and creative way to engage with reality, even if it is in a markedly different setting. Michaels points out that Octavia Butler’s Xenogenesis trilogy is an insightful commentary on racism and on the myriad of ways us humans use the categories of difference (be it racial, national, or other kinds of difference) to shape the way with which we interact with each other and with the world around us. Le Guin also does this in her The Left Hand of Darkness (Le Guin 2000), in which the inhabitants of the planet Gethen do not have set biological sexes, and therefore gender as a category of difference does not exist in that society and social relations are organized in other ways.

    Hence, science fiction and other kinds of genre fiction can also meet the expectations that are set towards works of literary fiction, to the point of being awarded with scholarly recognition. Science fiction, in particular, has been receiving more and more attention from literary scholars, but also from scholars of other disciplines – as is our case and of the other scholars that make up this volume. It is equally important to have in mind that such categorizations and classifications of one kind of literature as being intrinsically superior to other kinds because of its themes and structure is something that is constructed – and therefore is not neutral. It reflects structures of power that permeate societies and markets, a reality that must not go unchecked by IR scholars.

    Genre fiction, literary fiction, and knowledge production

    The very act of proposing a connection between IR and literature is a daring one – and one that, along with many other approaches to IR that dare to go against the grain of knowledge production focused on the power politics of nation states, has been overlooked as lesser, as an incoherent research agenda (Keohane 1988). Of course, what can be considered ‘true’ science is not something that is set in stone; the idea that there can be something like a ‘true’ science starts from the assumption that science can be, and always is, objective. Objectivity, however, as Harding (2015) points out, is not a concept that is set in stone, and assuming that it is, is exactly what makes objectivity less reliable. Many things can affect the point of view of a scientist producing knowledge, from the historical context they’re inserted into, the country they are writing from, or the specificities of their own personal identity. Taking these for granted is exactly what undermines objectivity, not the effort of recognizing these contingencies and their impact in the production of knowledge.

    Reflexivity, then, according to Harding, is the key to a science that is less reliant on preconceived notions of a standard White, Western scientist. And, when we bring literature to the fore with this in mind, it is inevitable that a similar attention to subjectivity would be required. Literature is not simply a reflection of reality, but it is part of reality, it constitutes the way those who interact with it see the world. This is especially telling in the case of magical realism, as so much of it reflects the histories within which its authors existed – one does not have to look beyond the military coup in Isabel Allende’s (2017) The House of Spirits, or the corporative violence against rural peasants in Márquez’s (2006) One Hundred Years of Solitude. It is with this in mind that we wish to point to literature (especially magical realism) as a way to interact with and understand subjectivities that are specific to certain regions of the world, as ways in which IR can interact with the colonial subject and, in tandem with the way science fiction can open the way towards alternative, futuristic imaginations, magical realism can open the way towards imaginations that are not centred on a Western history and/or context.

    By looking at literature, and specifically the alternative imaginations outside of the West, we give voice and reason to those who have been historically ignored by the literary canon. Magical realism is a door to Latin American imaginations, lives, traumas, pains, and ideals. It shows to the reader how the pain of colonialism is felt through unusual or unexpected ways, and shifts focus away from an idealized objectivity and rationality to give way to how emotion can be crucial in understanding a time and a place.5 These characteristics make magical realism a valuable tool for social science, a window into a part of the world that is frequently exoticized by the West, and whose point of view about the dynamics of international politics is crucial for understanding the current international structure of power. In the next section, we will explore in further depth the role that magical realism could have in the relationship between literature and IR scholarship.

    Magical realism, science fiction and the limits of International Relations

    One Hundred Years of Solitude, by Gabriel García Márquez, as we have mentioned, is considered one of the foundational texts of magical realism. The climax of the novel, leading to its end, involves the destruction of the town of Macondo, where the entire story is told. This destruction starts with the establishment of the United Fruit Company in the town, whose abusive practices lead to workers striking and being brutally murdered in retaliation. After this massacre, the company continues its activities as normal, but with the addition of scientists in the company trying to artificially cause rain. Their attempts, however, leads to a torrential storm in Macondo that lasts for five years and destroys the place, and leads to the end of the story (Márquez 2006).

    By portraying these events, Márquez is trying to talk about many things – mainly, about economic imperialism, as what happened to Macondo’s workers is a direct reference to a massacre of banana plantation workers in Colombia in 1928, and the violent dominance of private companies such as the United Fruit Company over Latin America (Bennett 1999). This critique of imperialism and portrait of the postcolonial struggle in the Americas is very typical of magical realism, but specifically when it comes to Macondo’s destruction in the hands of scientists, there is an eerie echo of the science fiction of Mary Shelley, of the gothic horror of men breaking the limits of nature in the name of science and causing tragedy. This echo makes one wonder: where can the line truly be drawn between science fiction and other genres? How much are the themes of science fiction something that can resonate outside of its confines?

    Magical realism, as we mentioned before, is a genre that eludes sharp definitions. For clarity’s sake, we can define magical realism as a genre birthed from Latin American authors in the 20th century, with a focus on intertwining elements of realism with supernatural or surreal elements. It is a genre that, thematically, explores themes of colonialism, colonial violence, economic imperialism, authoritarianism, and political oppression (Bennett 1999). This definition, albeit vague, is commonly accepted, and we can use it as a starting point to reflect on many characteristics of the genre and observe that many of them clash directly with main features of science fiction. For example, magical realism frequently looks at the past and at history, while science fiction is notably (and almost stereotypically) a futurist genre.

    While going towards the fantastical and the imaginative, science fiction grounds itself in science and logic as far as it can, while magical realism has its roots in mysticism, mystery, superstition, and non-rational ways of experiencing the world (sometimes, also stereotypically). Science fiction, in a traditional sense, defies the limits of our reality by applying the dominant logic of science against it; in an opposite movement, magical realism puts a mirror right in front of history and the political structures of our world, making it see itself through the eyes of a logic that is completely different than the one that typically gets to portray it. Science fiction turns the wonderful into something that is just part of our daily lives in an imagined future; magical realism, on the other hand, turns the world we already have, mundane and painful, into something mystical and awesome.

    Still, science fiction and magical realism are both genres that belong to the broader realm of speculative fiction, of fiction that defies reality and dares the reader to ask questions about the structure of their reality. Despite that, magical realism is rarely grouped together with science fiction – instead, there is another important way in which magical realism blurs the lines of genre, however, and it is in its perceived prestige. Although sometimes parodied,6 magical realism is rarely classified as fantasy, and the recognition of magical realist authors by awarding institutions that are not genre-specific7 puts it much closer to what is seen as prestigious literature than to its speculative cousins in science fiction/fantasy.

    Magical realism and science fiction, then, are two sides of the same coin. On one side, magical realism is noted for its artistry, but it is still a genre that belongs to the edges of the world, portraying a colonized imagination that is relegated to the fringes of literature, and of the collective imagination of international art and politics. Science fiction, on the other side, while frequently more mainstream and accessible due to its popularity, does not receive the same level of prestige or acclaim, and is frequently seen as inferior.8 Both genres, then, stand at vastly different and similar, at the same time, positions, being in the margins in their own ways. Because of this, both give us tools to explore alternative political imaginations, when taking the literature as its own object of analysis.

    This volume of Contexto Internacional focuses on science fiction, and rightfully so, as we extensively talked about the importance it can have to political analysis. Here, however, our choice to emphasize on magical realism comes from its more obvious political character. It is practically impossible to read One Hundred Years of Solitude and not feel the uniquely Latin American, and specifically Colombian, historical experiences that inform it. The constant disputes between two opposite but markedly similar political fields, the rampant presence of military violence upon a civilian population, the hybridity between Catholic-colonizer beliefs that permeates the ways in which the citizens of Macondo interact with the world and the strange things that surround their reality, the constant coups and revolts shaking the country’s political atmosphere and the relentless violence wrought by imperialism economics – all of these themes are all too familiar for anyone that knows anything about Latin American history, and even more so for those, like us, who live it first-hand.

    Isabel Allende’s efforts on The House of Spirits echo similarly on the reader – especially as the novel progresses and Allende focuses on the political aspirations of Esteban Trueba, the villainous protagonist of the story, but even before then. Allende, just like Márquez, has beautiful prose and a poetic streak in her work that announces her Latin American sensitivities, and the portrayal of the everyday fantastical life of the Trueba family is something that resonates with most of the Latin American readers. As Allende builds her setting, focusing entirely on the Trueba and del Valle families, her world seems utterly lived in – and not despite the fantastical details that make up their rich life, but because of them.

    Allende approaches the fantastical and absurd in the daily lives of the characters with ease and a nonchalant naturality to it. Allende describes telekinesis, flying tables, monstrous pets, and political violence all at once, in a single chapter or even passage, terming all those things equally plausible and a mere feature in the lives of her characters, because, in some sense, they are. In a place in which connections across different cultures and continents is essential and primordial like in Latin America, a great number of cultures, creeds, and cosmologies come together under a shared history of colonization and violence, in an amalgamation that is manifested in the sharing of diverse stories inspired by such rich flux of influences. Take Nicolás Trueba as an example: son of a wealthy landowner and an eccentric, clairvoyant mother, Nicolás was educated in a rigorous British school, attended his mother’s spiritual sessions when older, and, by the last third of the novel, is said to be an specialist in yoga and other meditation practices that he learned in a pilgrimage across Asia, inspired by a great-uncle that once decided to travel the world and did not came back alive.

    In The House of Spirits there is no tonal shift whatsoever as Allende discusses the supernatural powers of Clara, the matriarch of the Trueba family, and the terrible earthquake that destroys their country home and almost kills Esteban, the patriarch. It is clear that, for Allende, all these elements are equally believable: the fact that Clara can perfectly foretell the day and circumstances of her own death, and the fact that, after winning fair and just elections, the left will be removed from the government by a rightist coup. In putting these different kinds of events in the same level of verisimilitude, Allende wants us to believe that all these things are either equally capable of happening in real life or equally fantastical.

    That is to say that, in Latin America, we are used to some level of fantastical, absurd, or picturesque in our everyday lives. Be it through religious syncretism, in which Indigenous and African practices are incorporated in Catholic tradition; through the folk tales and other kinds of oral stories that are passed across generations and are almost always filled with otherwise unbelievable details; or through the small details in the societal fabric that are the result of a history of exploration, violence, and creative ways to survive. Stories of magical realism set in Latin America became somewhat plausible, especially when, like Allende does, we examine the absurd political history of the continent.

    This is intrinsically connected to, we would argue, the ideas put forward by the group of scholars on the Modernity/Coloniality Research Group, especially on their discussions around coloniality of power, being, and knowledge (Ballestrin 2013). According to Quijano’s (2005) seminal chapter, coloniality is the other side of modernity’s coin: one cannot be conceived without the other. The consequences of the colonial exploration of the world by Europe can be felt even after the colonies became independent from the colonizers. Colonisation, and its oppression and exploration of the colonies on the basis of gender, race, and labour, has transformed not only political and economical aspects of the lives of the former colonized peoples. Rather, it has deeper and more complex consequences to the ways of knowledge production, of subjectivities, of the power dynamics between Europe and the places it dominated.

    Magical realism, then, can be a productive way in exploring how coloniality has profoundly affected life in Latin America, because it puts forward dynamics that were put in place during colonization, and also because it is the result of sensibilities and subjectivities that arose because of colonization. Even when works of magical realism are not centring political or social commentary, there is an uncanniness to them that marks them as the result of these complicated, visceral, and hybrid dynamics of coloniality.

    Its uncanniness crosses borders because it is both genre literature and literary fiction, simultaneously. It cannot be defined exactly, and it cannot be pinned down. Similarly, this hybrid and unstable feature puts it in a unique position, as it is both poetic literature and political manifesto, both fictional narrative and historical retelling. Magical realism puts the reader, any reader, in a uniquely colonized state of mind, and it makes it possible to understand a different way of thinking, analysing, and imagining the world we live in. It takes one away from a Westernized way of viewing both magic and realism, and sets the reader into a way of thinking that isn’t linear nor objective in a traditional sense, where surreal or supernatural things are perfectly possible. Instead, the very violence and oppression that built that reality was itself so surreal in its totality, that it makes absurdity plausible.

    Therefore, we would argue that IR has only to gain in turning to analyses of magical realism just as it has gained with its examinations of science fiction works. Of course, we believe that all engagement with literature is productive, following the argument put forward by Der Derian and Shapiro (1989), in which they defend that IR relies on intertextuality and the examination of linguistic practices in the making of the fabric of politics in general and world politics as well. They advocate for an understanding of language that is not transparent and unproblematic communication, but rather is deeply contextual and contested. As one examines language through a more critical lens, it follows that IR should also follow this approach, since the world is mediated through textual and communicative practices.

    In previous sections of this article we have discussed how science fiction has enriched and contributed to the scientific and political practices of IR, be it because to engage with science fiction texts can be useful in the teaching of the discipline or because science fiction deals, in some level, with much the same issues that interest IR. Now, we would argue that magical realism, filled with its contradictions and sensibilities, can also provide important and productive insights into issues of world politics and even political imaginaries. Magical realism is very much a product of its place of birth, being imbued with the ‘unbelievableness’ that makes up everyday life in Latin America, where the unholy marriage between colonization, local knowledges and beliefs, and international trends has affected the people as well as the arts.

    Writing this article living in Brazil in 2022 makes this surreality that constitutes Latin American living and imagining even more palpable. The type of stereotypical magical realist narrative that Bennett mentions being parodied seems like an everyday occurrence to us, absurd things being just another part of life. These events seem to happen a lot more in moments of political turmoil, and the 2022 presidential elections and its aftermath provided an almost daily influx of reports of absurd or strange behaviours or situations surrounding, especially, the extreme right and the supporters of former president Jair Bolsonaro.

    Figure 1
    Bolsonaro supporters sing the National Anthem around a truck tire

    One event that comes to mind happened in 2 November 2022, three days after the elections that declared that Bolsonaro would leave office. Dissatisfied with the results, his supporters started a movement of camping outside army barracks to request a military coup in defence of the former president and putting into question the legitimacy of the elections. In these camps, widespread conspiratorial thinking and fascist manifestations flourished, but one particular moment is illustrative of the kind of absurdist events that fill magical realist novels: a circle of Bolsonaro supporters, crowded around a loose truck tire, under heavy rain in the middle of a highway, singing the Brazilian National Anthem in a tone of almost religious reverence (Duarte 2022). It is unclear to spectators and journalists that recorded the moment what the goal was, but the seeming solemnity of the act – around, again, a loose truck tire – is striking, with the members of the act seemingly reproducing some sort of prayer circle with political overtones.

    This type of imagery is rampant in magical realism. The mundane, the absurd and the sublime interact casually, and sometimes are received as normal, other times being received with intense emotion. What is also common, and similarly echoes the current Latin American political climate, is a portrayal of a country torn apart by extreme political rivalry. The Colombia of One Hundred Years of Solitude is ravaged by constant dispute between two parties, the Liberals and the Conservatives. It is easy for any Brazilian, for example, to identify with Aureliano Buendía’s experience of listening to explanations about the difference between the parties by a Conservative person:

    On one occasion on the eve of the elections, Don Apolinar Moscote returned from one of his frequent trips worried about the political situation in the country. The Liberals were determined to go to war. Since Aureliano at that time had very confused notions about the difference between Conservatives and Liberals, his father-in-law gave him some schematic lessons. The Liberals, he said, were Freemasons, bad people, wanting to hang priests, to institute civil marriage and divorce, to recognize the rights of illegitimate children as equal to those of legitimate ones, and to cut the country up into a federal system that would take power away from the supreme authority. The Conservatives, on the other hand, who had received their power directly from God, proposed the establishment of public order and family morality. They were the defenders of the faith of Christ, of the principle of authority, and were not prepared to permit the country to be broken down into autonomous entities. (Márquez 2006: position 1387)

    This opposition between a field of politics that puts religion at the front of every other ideal, opposing a field that it accuses to be heretical, is all too familiar to contemporary politics, especially in Latin America, and more glaringly to us as Brazilians living a period of similar political tension. The same extreme right Bolsonaro supporters we mentioned pledging allegiance to a truck tire also operate within a discourse of religious fundamentalism, where the left they disagree with and protest against is completely absent, and where the right in general, and Bolsonaro specifically, is the only side of Brazilian politics that has any interest in defending good Christian citizens – in fact, they make Christianity a crucial part of their identity (Evangelista 2020: 25):

    Under Bolsonaro, the link between religion and politics has become even more explicit. His cabinet is full of proactive Christian figures, both Catholic and evangelical, who promote a neoconservative religious agenda. ‘The time has come for the church to occupy the nation’ was how Damares Alves recently described the new conjuncture. For Foreign Minister Ernesto Araújo, in turn, ‘Christian values should be back at the core of how we see the world.’

    This centrality of religious values, specifically Christian ones, echoes almost perfectly the position of the Conservatives in One Hundred Years of Solitude. The clash between religion and politics is a theme that repeats itself, both in magical realism and in Latin American history, ever since the arrival of Portuguese and Spanish colonizers and their evangelizing mission against native beliefs.

    We mentioned how genre literature is more likely to achieve commercial success and acquire popularity with the general public, despite the fact that this can also happen with literary fiction. Standing in-between these categories as a magical realism novel, as we argued, One Hundred Years of Solitude has amassed immense popularity, locally in Latin America and globally, and Bennett (1999) argues that part of the reason for such popularity, beyond the sheer quality of Márquez’s work, is in the fact that it so precisely echoes these political, social, and religious experiences that are unique to Latin America. Márquez blurs the lines between historical and fictional narrative, telling stories that are eerily similar to historical events while changing names and places, but never so much that these events stop being recognizable. His work is heavily symbolic of the history of not only his country, but of the Americas in general, and of the Western world and its foundations of violence and contradiction as a whole (Bennett 1999: 207).

    The Latin American reader of Márquez relates to his work not only due to its historicity, but also due to its social elements, that echoes so much of lived Latin American experience. The reader recognizes themselves in the book in the smaller elements, in the religious quirks, in the sweet old ladies and grandmas who cook our favourite sweets, in romantic drama, in the strength of small communities, in the aggressiveness of our men. Márquez maps out a structure of feeling and living in One Hundred Years of Solitude that reflects so much of Latin American life that, at the time of publication, and even today, it makes readers feel seen (Bennett 1999: 173).

    Márquez’s work, then, is a window into both the big movements of Latin America’s history, with its political disputes, its endless wars, and its attempts to overcome the unsurmountable impact of colonial violence, but also a window into the little lives of Latin American people, their daily interactions, their quirks, habits, and superstitions. A lot of it is very violent and pessimistic – as with how Macondo is destroyed at the end of the novel, for example –, but a lot of it is optimistic, too, and carries a sense of joy and hope and rebuilding within community that a lot of Latin Americans also carry very intensely. In Márquez’s own words, from his Nobel Prize lecture:

    [T]he immeasurable violence and pain of our history are the result of age-old inequities and untold bitterness, and not a conspiracy plotted three thousand leagues from our home. But many European leaders and thinkers have thought so, with the childishness of old-timers who have forgotten the fruitful excess of their youth as if it were impossible to find another destiny than to live at the mercy of the two great masters of the world. This, my friends, is the very scale of our solitude.

    In spite of this, to oppression, plundering and abandonment, we respond with life. Neither floods nor plagues, famines nor cataclysms, nor even the eternal wars of century upon century, have been able to subdue the persistent advantage of life over death. (Márquez 1982)

    This also happens in The House of Spirits, that is equally filled with tiny kernels and details that echo deeply inside the Latin American reader and that are easily identifiable by those who have lived in Latin America, especially through Allende’s focus on different generations of a single family, emphasizing the importance that filial bonds play into the lives of everyday people. As her focus is not restricted to political figures in the high echelon of the government, despite the fact that Esteban Trueba is elected senator, Allende portrays family life as filled with love, expectation, frustration, and dissatisfaction, and how people who live in the same house can become so different and harbour such dissonant opinions. As the novel progresses and the political commentary reaches the foreground, Allende seems to give a message of cautious optimism, urging the future to be different, urging the political and physical violence incurred by dictatorships to be really a thing of imagination and no longer of real life.

    In this section, we strived to demonstrate how magical realism can be a useful tool for analysis, pointing to its liminal position in literature, as both a genre within and without literary fiction, as a literary movement that is both marginalized due to its Latin American origins but receives prestige due to its artistic quality. We have tried to show how magical realism breaks down the barriers of genre, and, above all, we have tried to demonstrate the value of magical realism in portraying a uniquely Latin American lived experience, and how the genre, with its local and historical specificity, can be a window to understanding Latin American politics and dynamics, and a valuable lens through which we can observe the international politics of the region, and between the region and the rest of the world.

    Final remarks

    Magical realism, science fiction, and IR can have interesting connections, which we suggested should be productively explored in order to expand on current understandings of the field and of political imagination itself. We followed the contributions made firstly by Der Derian and Shapiro (1989), and later expanded by Weldes (2003) and Nexon and Neumann (2006), that tell us that science fiction, literature, and pop culture in general can be understood as playing a constitutive role in world politics, since they increasingly matter to our everyday lives and end up shaping the ways through which we interact with the world and each other. These works are important sources of information into the fabric of the societies that birthed them, exposing readers and consumers to different values and arrangements of the world.

    Crucial for us is the fact that magical realism, because of its Latin American roots and because of its ambiguous relationship with both genre fiction and literary fiction, is illustrative of what can be achieved once we push beyond the limits of categorizations that objectivist scientific practice imposes on the production of knowledge. Taking magical realism as a tool for social science, we can use it as a door or as a window into another way of looking at society and the world, a way that goes beyond the confines of Western objectivity, and that is heavily informed by the lived and inherited experiences of colonialism and imperialism.

    Our suggestions in this article are of an exploratory nature, however, taking the seminal works of Gabriel García Márquez and Isabel Allende as examples of how magical realism can be a window to Latin American history and politics. Much else could be done with this genre: the power of affects, for example, could be explored, as Bennett (1999) mentions with some frequency the important role that intense emotions play in the poetic style of magical realism. Just as well, magical realism could be an interesting tool for exploring the contingency of borders, as another example, exploring how much experiences of political conflict and imaginings described in magical realism from, say, Colombia, can be echoed to similar experiences throughout Latin America. Going beyond that, one could also explore if, and how, magical realism might have resonated with authors and readers from outside of Latin America, and how much can be translated of the colonized experience outside of the continent. These are merely suggestions of future research agendas, however, but we believe much more can be done once we open ourselves to the possibilities of working hand in hand, between IR and literature.
