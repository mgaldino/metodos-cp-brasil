SPECIAL ISSUE – TROPICAL UTOPIA, TROPICAL DYSTOPIA: GLOBAL SOUTH SCIENCE FICTION AND CRITICAL APPROACHES TO INTERNATIONAL POLITICS • Contexto int. 46 (3) • Sep/Dec 2024 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20244603e20240055 linkcopy

Tropical Utopia, Tropical Dystopia: Global South Science Fiction and Critical Approaches to International Politics

Tropical Utopia, Tropical Dystopia: Ficção Científica do Sul Global e Abordagens Críticas à Política Internacional

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    There has been a growing interest within the field of International Relations inusing popular culture, especially Sci-Fi, to think about and teach International Relations. However, what the Sci-Fi IR authors have drawn from is quite limited, consisting of movies, shows, and novels produced in the Global North. When it comes to the triptych ‘Sci-Fi/IR/Global South’ the availability of analytical material is scarce. That is the gap this Special Issue hopes to address. In this introductory essay, we would like to suggest that both theory and science fiction, despite their differences, can provide important thinking spaces for the broadening of our understanding of current issues and the conception of alternative futures. To that end, we begin by briefly describing the evolution of Sci-Fi as a genre, emphasizing its connections to historical contexts and Eurocentric perceptions. We follow by introducing some trends in Global South Science Fiction and end with an analysis of some issues at the intersection of IR and SF. We also use this last section as an opportunity to introduce the articles included in the special issue.

    Keywords
    Science Fiction; Sci-Fi; Global South; postcolonialism; futurities

    Resumo

    Tem havido um interesse crescente no campo das Relações Internacionais em usar a cultura popular, especialmente a ficção científica, para pensar e ensinar Relações Internacionais. No entanto, os autores de ficção científica de RI têm se inspirado em algo bastante limitado, consistindo em filmes, programas e romances produzidos no Norte Global. Quando se trata da tríptica “Ficção Científica/ RI/Sul Global”, a disponibilidade de material analítico é escassa. Essa é a lacuna que esta edição especial espera preencher. Neste ensaio introdutório, gostaríamos de sugerir que tanto a teoria quanto a ficção científica, apesar de suas diferenças, podem proporcionar importantes espaços de reflexão para ampliar nossa compreensão das questões atuais e a concepção de futuros alternativos. Para isso, começamos descrevendo brevemente a evolução da ficção científica como um gênero, enfatizando suas conexões com contextos históricos e percepções eurocêntricas. Em seguida, apresentamos algumas tendências da Ficção Científica do Sul Global e terminamos com uma análise de algumas questões na interseção de RI e FC. Também usamos essa última seção como uma oportunidade para apresentar os artigos incluídos na edição especial.

    Palavras-chave
    Ficção científica; ficção científica; Sul Global; pós-colonialismo; futuridades

    Introduction

    After a long history of mostly ignoring the subject, International Relations (IR) has been increasingly turning its attention to issues related to culture –be it its role in shaping politics (and vice-versa) or as a site for exploring various topics, ideas, and theories. Popular culture, in particular, has proven to be a fertile ground for this exploration. Since the 1990s, the field has been influenced by analyses from the realm of popular culture studies. Works by internationalists such as Cynthia Weber (2021), Neumann and Nexon (2006), Caso and Hamilton (2015), Reeves (2004), Davies, Grayson, Rawley, and Weldes (2010), have brought together insights from the universe of pop culture into the study of international affairs. Part of this process has been the specific interest in analysing international politics through the lens of Science Fiction (Sci-Fi or SF).

    Using Sci-Fi to think about and teach International Relations, therefore, is not a completely novel idea. There are several volumes on the subject. For instance, Dyson’s Otherworldly Politics (2015) seeks to analyse the international relations of the worlds of Star Trek, Game of Thrones, and Battlestar Galactica. The latter is also the subject of a volume entirely dedicated to the show (Kiersey and Neumann 2013), and features in a paper by Buzan (2010) alongside Star Trek, which also shows up in a volume organized by Weldes (2003). While the topics addressed in these works are quite varied (power, religion, artificial intelligence, genocide, imperialism) the Sci-Fi they draw from is quite limited, consisting of movies, shows, and novels produced in the Global North.

    This does not reflect the current diversity of science fiction. Rather, it reflects Anglocentrism, that is, ‘the problem of looking only at certain literatures and not others, utilizing only certain works and not others, constructing canons with certain texts and not others, and not supporting a more vigorous translation industry’ (Chattopadhyay 2014: 213). Being a common problem in both IR and Sci-Fi, it is no surprise that it would be present at their intersection.

    As Donna Haraway (2020: 10) reminds us, however, ‘it matters what stories we tell to tell other stories with; it matters what concepts we think to think other concepts with.’ In essence, our narratives shape our understanding, and our choice of language constructs the framework through which we perceive reality. It is imperative to acknowledge the voices from the Global South as pivotal contributors in realms like Sci-Fi, offering unique insights crucial for navigating the intricacies of our contemporary (and perhaps future) existence. Likewise, it is important to broaden our understanding of what we understand by and what belongs to ‘International Relations’, as researchers, teachers, and practitioners of ‘IR’, lest we become prisoners in conceptual cages of our own creation.

    In this introductory essay, we would like to suggest that both theory and science fiction, despite their differences, can provide important thinking spaces for the broadening of our understanding of current issues and the conception of alternative futures. This is only possible, however, by embracing a critical stance that addresses their limitations and avoids reifying historically built hierarchies of various kinds. To that end, we begin by briefly describing the evolution of Sci-Fi as a genre, emphasizing its connections to historical contexts and Eurocentric perceptions. We follow by introducing some trends in Global South Science Fiction and end with an analysis of some issues at the intersection of IR and SF. We also use this last section as an opportunity to introduce the articles included in this special issue.

    Sci-Fi: from colonial to critical

    Science fiction – much like IR – is the product of certain geographical and historical contexts. It is common to trace the origin of the genre to narratives of voyages extraordinaires, or ‘marvelous journeys’, that date back to the Ancient Greek novel, and to point to the Copernican shift to heliocentrism as a crucial turn - one which opened the possibility of the Earth as potentially just one more among many inhabited worlds (Rider 2008; Roberts 2006). Throughout its evolution, Sci-Fi has had as common threads ‘the presence and interaction of estrangement and cognition’ (Suvin 1979: 06-07), that is the encounter of that which is different and new. Darko Suvin (1979) terms this ‘the novum’, a ‘strange newness’, some imaginary device - be it material or conceptual - that removes us from our empirical environment.1

    Rieder (2008: 2) posits that the emergence of English-language science fiction is inextricably linked to colonialism and the ‘scientific discourse about culture and mankind’ that evolved in parallel to it. Indeed, one could argue that the emergence of literature that described other-worldly journeys is likely related to the fact that the ‘‘circle’ of global exploration was ‘almost circled’’ as described by poet Walt Whitman in 1860 (cited in Roberts, 2006: 107). If the development of a genre marked by the estrangement of facing alien others seems an almost natural result of the experience of encountering Others provided by colonialism and imperialism, one must not ignore that the framing of these encounters found in early Sci-Fi novels reflects structures of knowledge and power and the hierarchies between the West and the ‘rest’ that permeated those processes.

    While Rieder (2008: 2) claims that ‘the achievement of a perspective from which one’s own culture is only one of a number of possible cultures’ was a central condition for the development of Science Fiction,

    [M]ost readers will be familiar with the classic oppositional SF tropes of the grotesque bug-eyed alien bent on Earthly domination and the beautiful but empty planet, ripe for colonization or, of course, the dangerous planet whose inhabitants dare to fight back against the lantern-jawed colonial hero. (Langer 2011: 2)

    Not only that but, as a product of its context, the genre has often been complicit by ‘presenting alien races in such a way as to assuage imperialist guilt or affirm imperialist desire’ (Hoagland and Sarval 2010: 7). Even current popular dystopian fiction - such as The Walking Dead, World War Z, District 9, and The Last of Us - often reinforces the inside/outside duality (Walker 1993) which is structural to Western Political Philosophy (and, by extension, IR Theory) while opposing ‘state of nature’ to ‘state of society’ represented by the conflict between humans and other creatures (most commonly zombies or aliens).

    That being said, Sci-Fi is not homogeneous. Some earlier works struggled or even openly criticized imperialism, and more critical perspectives emerged over time. It is especially since the 1960s, however, that the genre begins ‘to peel open the ideological myth of supreme scientific competence and galactic manifest destiny’ (Broderick 2003: 78). On the cusp of this ‘New Wave’, authors as different as James Ballard, Frank Herbert, and Ursula Le Guin saw in Sci-Fi the critical potential to scrutinize deeply rooted beliefs around imperialism, capitalism, and war. These authors, and many since then, have used Sci-Fi mainly as a vehicle for exploring current issues and speculating about new ways of doing things (Sawyer 2010). As Ballard (1996: 197) himself put it ‘it is inner space, not outer, that needs to be explored.’

    It is also important to note that novels, TV shows, or movies are not inert objects. Rather, they are central elements of culture, with a role in forming attitudes, references, and experiences. As such, they can be (and have been) both an instrument of imperialism and a weapon against it, a way for colonized peoples to present counter-narratives that assert their own identities and history (Said 1993). Since the 1970s, authors from a broad range of disciplines in the Social Sciences have been developing critical approaches to Sci-Fi. One of those pioneers is Darko Suvin. According to Suvin (1979), the use of exaggeration as a narrative technique by Sci-Fi authors has the potential to provoke a critical understanding of present-day dilemmas when the projection of current problems is visualized by their displacement to ‘strange worlds’ with ‘amazing technologies’ and ‘astonishing aliens.’ Suvin emphasizes that Sci-Fi is always a set of allegories of the author’s present times, as well as a forum to express his/her political concerns and ethical perspectives.

    Following Suvin, a group of sociologists, political scientists, and literary critics has promoted the encounter between Critical Theory and Sci-Fi. Based on cultural critical works by Marxist thinkers such as Adorno and Horkheimer (2002), authors such as Suvin himself and others such as Gwyneth Jones (1999), Carl Freeman (2000), Istvan Csicsery-Ronay Jr. (2003), Fredric Jameson (2005), Haney II (2006), and China Miéville (2009) have brought Gramscian cultural criticism into Sci-Fi interpreting this genre as engaging tactics toward social-political emancipating analysis. Since then, this new subgenre within critical cultural studies – named SF Critical Studies – has been developing readings of Sci-Fi books, short stories, TV series, and movie pictures as a way to perform social-political criticism at a crossroads where pop culture, critical thinking, and philosophical reasoning can meet and perform intriguing interrogations about contemporary crucial issues such as human-machine interaction, artificial intelligence, ecology, the future of work, social exploitation, authoritarianism, revolution, and more.

    According to Suarez (2019), Sci-Fi provokes the same kind of ‘amazingness’ and ‘admiration’ that Plato or Aristotle expected from philosophy. ‘Surprise’ and ‘wonder’ are philosophical tools to promote the de-naturalization of processes and ideas, which can open the way for new and different approaches to ‘usual’ and ‘recurrent’ phenomena. In this sense, Suvin’s ‘cognitive estrangement’ can provoke critical reflection by exploring the dialectics between oddness and similarity, considering the reader’s cognitive/cultural references.

    More recently, the work on the critical potential of Sci-Fi has been pushed beyond the limits of Western critical approaches, including the SF Critical Theory. The above-mentioned interest among critical thinkers on how Sci-Fi as a genre is connected to the European and the U.S. forms of imperialism has been confronted by analyses from the Global South (Chattopadhyay 2014; Grewell 2001; Kurtz 2016). It means, simultaneously, the emergence of a new wave of Global South Sci-Fi writers as well as new Global South theorists willing to critically examine Sci-Fi from postcolonial, poststructural, feminist, and Marxist approaches (Diaz 2018; Langer 2011; Blanc-Hoang 2017).

    Therefore, though it has traditionally been seen as almost entirely made and consumed by young white males (Hoagland and Sarval 2010), ‘[m]any of the most interesting worlds of Sci-Fi have been created by women, people of colour, gay men and lesbians, who have made power visible through their estranging fictions’ (Kneale and Kitchin 1998: 7). To a limited degree, then, those who had been merely subjects of early Sci-Fi have now become voices of the genre.

    The other speaks: Global South science fiction

    Over the past few decades, the emergence of science fiction from the Global South has become increasingly prominent, challenging the dominance of Western SF narratives.2 According to O’Connell (2019), the recent surge in Global South Science Fiction during the twenty-first century is influenced by a complex interplay of cultural, formal aesthetic, and political factors. The globalization and mainstream acceptance of SF have broadened the demographic of both creators and consumers, challenging the traditional dominance of the white male community. This shift reflects a broader cultural change as SF moves from niche to mainstream cultural prominence. Additionally, the rise of Global South Science Fiction is intertwined with changes in the formal aesthetics of postcolonial literature, with SF challenging magical realism as the dominant non-mimetic form, signalling a transition from colonialism to globalization. Despite this, Global South Sci-Fi often defies strict genre categorizations, drawing on diverse ontological motivations and embracing a more flexible approach to publishing, often utilizing digital platforms and smaller presses rather than conforming to Western market-driven distinctions.

    O’Connell (2019) identifies an impressive number of writers and theorists within the Global South who have been developing narratives that go beyond the duality of ‘civilization’ and ‘barbárie’ to highlight the ‘barbarism’ within ‘civilization’ and the presence and influence of security practices and social behaviour based on futuristic technology. The past and present conflicts and troubles that convulse Global South societies, their social strife, overwhelming economic inequality, ethnic conflicts, and authoritarian political cultures have been used as raw material for fictional narratives on subjects such as race, gender, class struggle, political freedom, ecology, and original people’s rights and traditions (Kurlat Ares; De Rosso 2021; Leopori 2013; ​​Adejunmobi 2016; Bökös,2019).

    The proliferation of writers throughout the Global South has pushed forward new specialized magazines – such as the Chinese Future and the Argentinian Minotauro – and many books and short stories collections (Law & Mak 2017; Xueting 2021; Saint 2021). The wave of Global South Sci-Fi has produced sub-sub genres such as ‘Chinese SF’, ‘Black SF’, ‘AfroSF’, or ‘Afro Cyber’ (Hartmann 2012; Law & Mak 2017) as well as several ‘futurisms’, like Sinofuturism, Afrofuturism and Africanfuturism,3 Latinofuturism, etc. that challenge the notion that the North is the sole responsible for imagining the future.

    Magalí Armillas-Tiseyra (2016: 278) argues that Sci-Fi has ‘a different texture and different stakes in postcolonial contexts.’ Therefore, while we see the adoption of common SF themes and tropes by these Global South authors, they acquire new dimensions, often from the incorporation of ‘local’ motifs or literary traditions, such as magical realism. Also adding texture to these narratives is the juxtaposition between elements commonly associated with modernity, especially in the role of technological nova (to use Suvin’s expression), and contexts that are historically associated with backwardness and underdevelopment.

    Many authors play with these contrasts to offer critiques of hegemonic ideas of modernity and development. One such example is the short story Home Affairs by Sarah Lotz (2012). In this story, a future South Africa has addressed the issues of inefficiency and corruption of public officials through the installation of robots, called Konabots, that take their place. As the story unfolds, however, it becomes apparent that the new technology is not only corruptible but also a tool for the creation of a new country where ‘undesirables’ and ‘misfits’ are promptly ejected and stripped of their citizenship. Moonsamy (2016: 333) calls this a ‘sterile utopia’, a ‘techno-scientific world [...] so mechanistic that variation, improvisation, and surprise have been eliminated from daily interactions.’

    Lotz seems to present dubious feelings regarding modernization, at once subscribing to the idea that technological advancement will be central to Africa’s future (Moonsamy, 2016: 332) and reinforcing the notion that technology is not enough to overcome social and political issues that appear inherent to South Africa. In a similar vein, Deji Bryce Olukotun’s novel Nigerians in Space presents an existing technology (space travel) as a novum by placing it in an unexpected context - 1990s Nigeria. That is, the moon mission in the story ‘is only “SF” in so far as its success is presumed impossible’ (Armillas-Tiseyra 2016: 275). The very certainty of its impossibility is used by the author to express that ‘the limits that determine what is “speculative” (hypothetical or notional because of current limitations) are as much political as technological’ (Armillas-Tiseyra 2016: 275).

    An interesting and contrasting example of depictions of technologies, and particularly space travel in peripheral countries, can be found in the ‘socialist SF’ of the 1950s and 1960s, a period of particular effort by Soviet and Chinese governments to popularize science. One such example is Zheng Wenguang’s short story From Earth to Mars, a tale of space travel featuring young characters with an optimistic and playful style suitable to a young audience (Volland 2014). Zheng’s work bears more similarities to early SF from Western countries than it does to Home Affairs or Nigerians in Space. While space exploration is presented as novel and fantastic, it is also attainable - or even inevitable. Within this linear narrative of technological development, it is but the next step. One could argue that despite great differences in content and style, Cixin Liu’s Remembrance of Earth’s Past trilogy also presents this linear narrative, perhaps taking it a step further into techno-fatalist territory.4

    Contrasts between techno-fantasies and peripheral realities are often taken a step further by the production of hybrids that toe the line between mainstream conceptions of Science Fiction and literatura fanstatica. 5 Most often than not, the aim is to question the idea that ‘everything in science fiction follows or should be conceptualized through the lens of the Western scientific rationalist paradigm’ (Langer 2011: 9). Latin American SF, in particular, has porous boundaries with magical realism, but there are similar cases in different literatures, such as ‘jujutech’, a style of Sci-Fi in which African folk ‘absorbs hyper-technological paradigms’, blurring the line between the magical and the technological (MacDonald 2016: 315).

    A very straightforward example of this hybridity is the literal debate between Darwinists and creationists in Eduardo Holmberg’s Two Factions (Ferreira 2011), but it can also be found in later works such as Alfredo Véa Jr.’s The Mexican Flyboy (Frazier 2018). The latter tells the tale of Simon Vegas, a former migrant worker turned ‘Mexican superhero’, who stumbles across a time machine (the Antikythera mechanism) used by the American army and decides to repurpose it to save those he feels have been dealt an unjust death by the Western establishment. The magical element here is found in the ‘Mexican Heaven’ (located in an alternate Boca Raton, Florida), a utopian space where Vegas deposits those he saves. Véa Jr.’s work is not only an open critique of Western imperialism, Frazier (2018) proposes that it also incorporates ideas of hybridity and mestizaje, suggesting that the ‘Mexican Heaven’ mirrors Gloria Anzaldua’s (1987) Aztlán.6

    Anzaldua’s work, indeed, is a particularly fitting lens through which we can analyse works situated in these borderlands – in spaces ‘where the Third World grates against the first and bleeds’ (Anzaldua 1987: 3), but also where mestizas live, those who ‘continually walk out of one culture and into another’, who inhabit ‘all cultures at the same time’ (Ibid: 77). Interestingly, Anzaldua cites in Borderlands (1987: 11) a Navajo protection song: ‘Now among the alien gods with weapons of magic am I.’ One could argue that magic, mysticism, and folklore are indeed the weapons of SF authors in these spaces to ‘escape the gravity of its Euro-American roots’ (MacDonald, 2016: 313).

    According to Ashcroft (2017), the challenge faced by the dwellers of these borderlands is to fill these marginal spaces with affirmation and possibility, that is, to imagine futures that are built on and respond to the yearnings of postcolonial societies. Imaginings of the future, of course, can take many forms and have many purposes. In Sci-Fi, they have traditionally taken the central stage, spawning utopias, dystopias, heterotopias, and several futurisms. In postcolonial literature, these narratives ‘target the why of things: why specific images are considered futuristic, or why things, in reality, are more muddled than simple developmental paradigms’ (Chattopadhyay, 2021: 11). It is through this critique that they work to reclaim the future, after all, without critique utopias are no more than daydreams (Ashcroft 2017).

    While historically utopias have had great significance in postcolonial contexts, where struggles for liberation and nation-building have been, and are, ideologically informed by future-oriented utopian ideals - like Aztlán - SF literature has been characterized by a more heterodox futurity. While it does not negate the emancipatory power of utopia, there is a focus on the critique of those social conditions that make utopia necessary, and on the distinct possibilities for political action and social change as well as the restraints emanating from those conditions (Ashcroft 2017).

    Authors have used SF to question current paths and amplify a variety of issues: the environment, gender, globalization, migration, and religion. Two recent examples come from Latin-American cinema: the Mexican film Sleep Dealer (Alex Rivera 2008), and Brazilian Divine Love (Gabriel Mascaro 2019). The first presents an allegory of Mexican immigration through ‘node workers’, or cybraceros, who are plugged into machines run by multinational corporations so their work can be exploited around the globe. It is not only surplus value, however, that is extracted from the workers, but quite literally their life, slowly consumed by the nodes. Rivera, however, upends what could be an anti-technological narrative, and ultimately the characters ‘find liberation utilizing the very tools of their repression’ (Dollinger 2022). The tale, then, can be described as a critical utopia, combining critique with possibilities of emancipation. Mascaro’s film also does not present a Manichaean view of technology. Divine Love is set in a near-future Brazil where religion has become the politically dominant force and technology has evolved to the point of allowing near-perfect control of citizens’ bodies. The story’s main character, Joana, is an anti-heroine, a willing participant in the system of oppression that will eventually turn her into an outcast. Technology here is a tool, not a destiny, what creates the dystopia are the socio-political forces that control it.

    If in Divine Love futuristictechnology is above all a means to control the female body, in Nnedi Okorafor’s Who Fears Death it is merely a backdrop in a tale of struggle and liberation. The novel, which blends elements of Sci-Fi with Nigerian folklore, tells the story of Onyesonwu, an outcast who discovers herself to possess remarkable magical powers and sets out on a quest to find and kill her father. Okorafor also upends the hierarchy between science and the ‘mystical’ (in the form of magic rather than religion), but here it does not represent oppression – rather, it is through it that ‘Onyesonwu reasserts and maintains complete control over her body’ (Frazier 2018: 68).

    The theme of technology and the African body is also present in Tendai Huchu’s short story The Sale. The story is set in a future where China is a techno-scientific empire (CorpGov) that owns several African countries and is buying Zimbabwe piece by piece. The main character is Jimmy Munyuki, a Zimbabwean man navigating newly imposed borders in his home country, as well as encounters with deeply invasive technologies. CorpGov is interested above all in resources, which includes African labour. Therefore, public health becomes ‘a matter of enforced well-being; no one is given a choice about his or her desired state of health and mortality’ (Moonsamy, 2016: 338). For men, that means the compulsory control of testosterone levels. The story clearly evokes traumas of slavery and a history of eugenicist vilifying of the (male) African body, while at the same time presenting it as a weapon of resistance as Jimmy uses his body ‘in a final act of helplessness and defiance’ against the new colonizers (Moonsamy 2016: 340).

    While works like Who Fears Death and The Sale combine elements of the traditional Western technoscientific repertoire with postcolonial African imaginaries to develop complicated futurities that challenge the utopia-dystopia binary, the Indian movie Love Story 2050 appears to provide a more clear-cut utopia. This romantic story follows an Indian couple, Sana and Karan, in present-day Sydney, Australia. When Sana dies, Karan uses his uncle’s time-traveling machine to find her ‘next birth’ in 2050 Mumbai and bring her back to the present. The Indian city of the future is presented as an urban technoscientific utopia, clean and perfect, with androids, flying cars, automated houses, and skyscrapers. As Varughese (2017: 104) notes, however, present-day Mumbai, with its ‘sweltering weather, poverty, busy life, narrow streets, and thick population’ does not appear in the movie – neither is it reflected in the city of the future. Rather, the Indian city in 2050 exists in a continuum with present-day Sydney, representing the acceptance of a ‘universal neoliberal globality’ (Varughese 2017: 105). Therefore, while we could accept the film as a utopia, Varughese (2017) suggests that this representation is fraught with anxieties and fears regarding the future of urban spaces in the Global South. Moreover, it is remarkable that it carries an implicit acceptance of hegemonic diagnostics of the present and recipes for the future.

    Global South Science-Fiction, as can be seen, is as diverse as the GS itself. Despite differences, however, it is possible to see common themes and trends within this emerging literature, as we have attempted to show despite the limited number of examples. These works have contributed to offering counter-narratives that centres the histories and imaginaries of the South, questioning the limits of the speculative and working to de-naturalize long-held assumptions and project alternative futurities. It is clear that Global SF can offer valuable insights and spaces to think about and teach International Relations, a contribution made by the articles in this Special issue, which we introduce in the following section.

    Critical encounters: International Relations meets science fictions

    In the discipline of IR, despite the pioneering work by Olander and Greenberg (1978), and the recent efforts by Weldes (2003), Buzan (2010), Jackson and Nexon (2013), and Kierser and Neumann (2013; 2015), the debate is still residual. When it comes to the triptych ‘Sci-Fi/IR/Global South’ the availability of analytical material is even scarcer (Veracini 2011). That is the gap this Special Issue is trying to address by opening a space for researchers interested in Science Fiction to analyse their own countries, regions, or specificities in the contemporary World Order.

    As we have argued, science fiction can be used in many forms to help us think about IR. One of them is as a tool for teaching, helping students engage, visualize, and develop critical understandings of many issues and theories. In the first article in this special issue, ‘Sci-Fi as a Post-Colonial Pedagogical Tool,’ Hanna Kassab proposes the use of Sci-Fi movies to engage and help students learn Postcolonial theories. The author proposes the use of different movies to help illustrate different aspects of colonialism and postcolonialism: Planet of the Apes shows the process of colonialization through dehumanization; Invasion of the Body Snatchers illustrates the use of the ‘civilization process’ used as a justification for a predatory process of colonization; and Soylent Green is about the recovery of self, exercising cultural revitalization through ceremony as a form of anti-colonial resistance.

    Sci-Fi is also often used as a space to interrogate the past and imagine different futures. As Assis and Saint-Pierre (this issue)7 argue ‘the notion of the future manifests itself as an object of political contestation in the sphere of international relations’ as ‘imaginaries represent a means of politicizing and disputing the future.’ In ‘Memories of Future Empire,’Virginia Labiano and Juan Ignacio Percoco (this issue) use the excellent science-fiction TV series The Expanse to interrogate ‘how the imperial past and its colonialist practices have been shaping our future political horizons’ and to explore ‘the cognitive incidence of colonialism in our political creativity but also to discuss the mechanisms of its resistance and struggle, both in the confines of our present Earth and in our future beyond it.’

    Also connecting imperialist past and a critical imagining of the future, Marcio José Melo Malta and Hiago André Duarte de Albuquerque (this issue) analyse the Argentinian graphic novel that depicts an alien invasion on Earth in‘El Eternauta: Science Fiction against Imperialism.’ By introducing Argentina as a setting, more precisely Buenos Aires in the year 2001, El Eternauta creates an estrangement similar to that of Nigerians in Space: don’t alien invasions, like space exploration, belong to the developed world? Shouldn’t the aliens be landing in New York? As Malta and Albuquerque show, the volumes present a critique both of the dictatorial regimes that took over Latin America in the 1960s and 1970s and of imperialism that helped make it possible, which largely mirrors Oesterheld’s ideals and experiences.

    The role of technology has also been central in Sci-Fi’s futurities. In ‘China’s Sci-Fi Images: Constructing Alternate Technological Futures,’ Jonathan de Araujo Assis and Héctor Luis Saint-Pierre analyse Chinese imaged futures, highlighting the role of technologies as a way to ‘decentralize hegemonic technological future images through Chinese science fiction works.’ The authors focus on novels from the Chinese ‘new wave’ of SF, by authors such as Han Song, Hao Jingfang, and Liu Cixin, whose works deal primarily with themes of development, the future of technology and its human costs, usually against the backdrop of Chinese ascension. Drawing from these works, Assis and Saint-Pierre identify a series of possible contributions to technoscientific imaginaries, such as heterotemporality, and problematizations about the notions of progress and technological advancement.

    Also engaging Chinese SF, in ‘Dreams of Freedom’ Laura Pimentel Barbosa (this issue) analyses the evolution of Chinese and Cuban science fiction, tying literature with their revolutionary experiences and socialist projects. The author shows how SF from these countries has at the same time revealed rebellion against imperialism and the adoption of hegemonic subjects, in particular, views of science. Recent works, however, have revealed more critical considerations of utopian discourses and projects of the future as shown in the novels analysed in the article: Chan Koonchung’s The Fat Years and Eric Mota’s Habana Underguater. In The Fat Years, the main character spends a good part of the novel in a drug-induced state of superficial contentment that, however, only stifles but does not eliminate a deeper sense of unease. This ‘state of irony’ is quite similar to what is described by Huchu in The Sale, for example, where it is also imposed by the Chinese government. As both Barbosa and Moonsamy (2016) argue, oppression based on technology can be more insidious and efficient than violence.

    Offering an alternative view of technology, in ‘“South Fabricated”: Computing stories of GS Insecurity’ Luísa Lobato (this issue) discusses ‘how Sci-Fi tropes surrounding digital technologies help build a story that is told from the perspective of a white, male, and possibly misogynistic entrepreneur.’ Following, she offers a ‘counter-narrative’ from the vantage point of the Global South. Drawing from her fieldwork with the Fogo Cruzado app in Rio de Janeiro (an app that uses social media to crowdsource information on gunshots and armed violence), Lobato discusses ‘the potent, yet ambivalent ways in which apps help us tell stories of security, insecurity, threat, and politics in/of the Global South’ and questions Manichaean views of technology as either our doom or our salvation.

    Utopias and dystopias have also been central in offering critical views of the present, the future, and the past, particularly in the GS. In ‘Brazilian Dystopian Futures: Worlding Brazil through SF Movies,’ Gilberto Carvalho de Oliveira relies on the so-called IR aesthetic turn to analyse Brazilian movies O Quinto Poder, Os Cosmonautas, Brasil Ano 2000, and Bacurau. The author argues that these films ‘resort to inventive aesthetic solutions that are outside the formal and economic standards of the SF genre produced in the North’, offering powerful anti-colonial critiques and contributing to the project of ‘worlding’ our understanding of international politics. Carvalho de Oliveira’s analysis highlights interesting aspects of this cinematography that are particular to Brazil but also resonate with much of peripheric SF – for instance, its often satirical tone, and the contrast between tropes of modernity such as space travel, and the reality of a developing nation.

    In ‘Living in Death: The New Dystopian Reality of Israeli Settler Colonialism in Gaza,’ Reginaldo Nasser, Isabela Agostinelli dos Santos, and Bruno Huberman (this issue) analyse Palestine +100: Stories from a century after the Nakba, a collection of twelve stories in which Palestinian writers imagine Palestine in the year 2048, six of them with a specific focus on Gaza.Nasser, dos Santos and Hubermann emphasize the contrast between the creation of an Israeli ‘utopia’ and the Palestinian dystopia that emerged from it, arguing that this material reality has greatly influenced artistic production in Palestine, especially with a rise in dystopian writings. In most of the stories of Palestine +100, the Nakba remains a looming presence, even after a hundred years, a memory and a symbol to be shared or controlled or erased. Technology is present sometimes as a tool for resistance, but most often as a weapon of colonial control. As the authors show, art reflects the current reality of Gaza, and the overwhelming mood is despair, with only one of the stories being characterized as a utopia. As the authors conclude, Palestinians’ ability to dream has been ‘weaponized, captured, governmentalized, doomed, promised, restricted, and denied’ - and still, they resist.

    Offering a critical view of the future painted by the author, Veronica Korber Gonçalves, Thaís Lemos Ribeiro and Cristina Inoue (this issue), in their article ‘The Three-Body Problem and the Anthropocene,’ draw a parallel between the issues present in Cixin Liu’s Remembrance trilogy and the impacts of the Anthropocene in the International Relations field of study. Analysing each book of the trilogy in turn, the authors present their main issues, connecting those with challenges posed by the Anthropocene. They argue that ‘the narrative is based on mainstream concepts and categories, resulting in a mainstream account of the end of the world through a zero-sum game that misses current complexities.’

    Challenging boundaries through hybridization has also been one of the hallmarks of GS Sci-Fi. In ‘SF’s Boundaries and IR Approaches to Art’ Luisa Davi Oliveira de Mesquita and Karla Resende da Costa (this issue) seek to ‘to reflect upon genre distinctions and what they mean politically, and how blurring the lines between marginalized literary genres can broaden our analytical horizons when connecting literature, art, and IR.’ More specifically, the authors analyse magical realism as ‘a way to interact with and understand subjectivities that are specific to’ Latin America. By analysing the novels One Hundred Years of Solitude and The House of Spirits, the authors show how porous genre boundaries can be and, more than that, how productive cross-genre analyses can be, in particular for subjects such as colonialism and imperialism.

    Finally, Thiago Rodrigues, Bruna Toso de Alcântara, and Guilherme Ranoya take the novel Genesis (2006) by the New Zealander writer Bernard Beckett to develop a Critical Theory-inspired analysis of the tensions between ‘security’ and ‘freedom’ in a dystopian future where classical features from the Sci-Fi literature - such as the relationship among, computers, intelligent machines, and Humans, the controlling of knowledge by technocratic regimes, and the social life in post-pandemic environments - are re-signified in an original and challenging way when compared to Sci-Fi landmarks such as Ursula Le Guin’s The Dispossessed and George Orwell’s 1984. The authors claim that Beckett’s novel can be read as an anti-capitalist tale in an age when Humankind is facing the limits of Earth’s natural resources and the prospect of a structural and potential apocalyptic socioeconomic and ecological collapse.

    Concluding remarks

    Returning to a sentence we shared in the introduction: ‘it matters what stories we tell to tell other stories with; it matters what concepts we think to think other concepts with’ (Haraway 2020: 10). It is true that, despite its colonial roots, Science Fiction has been providing an important space for critical imagination for over a century, even if we focus only on anglophone SF. Authors like Ursula Le Guin and Octavia Butler have written powerful stories that touched on universal subjects, offering critical views from inside the ‘centre.’ However, it is also undeniable that GS Science Fiction has been contributing novel ideas, critiques, and hybrids that have immensely widened our perception of what Sci-Fi can be. By addressing issues of colonialism, imperialism, the role of technology, conceptions of humanity, and critical imaginings of the future, among others, it also can provide vital contributions to IR, as the articles in this Special Issue have shown.
