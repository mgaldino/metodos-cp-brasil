A Utopian Dystopia: Freedom versus Security in the Sci-Fi Novel Genesis

Uma distopia utópica: Liberdade versus segurança no romance de ficção científica ‘Gênesis’

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Genesis (2006) is a novel by the New Zealander writer Bernard Beckett. Its plot is placed in the end of the 21st century in an examination room where Anaximander presents the findings of her thesis to The Academy. Anaximander’s topic is the life of Adam Forde, a young man from the upper caste of The Republic, who was sentenced to share his days with Art, an AI android prototype. After a period of coexistence, both rebelled against the Republic. What the reader finds in Anax’s examination is the political and philosophical consequences of that insurrection. Genesis displays the classic tension between freedom versus security present in major works of the 20th century science fiction. From a Marxist perspective, this article aims at presenting Genesis as a novel that echoes two of the main subjects of sci-fi literature – the tension between ‘security’ and ‘freedom’ and the problem of human/artificial conscience – but in a context of post-Cold War Liberal security concerns inhabited with new sets of fears such as global climate change, pandemics, warfare, and the ethical concerns on AI.

    Keywords
    science fiction; artificial intelligence; rebellion; Marxist critique; dystopia; utopia

    Resumo

    Gênesis (2006) é um romance do escritor neozelandês Bernard Beckett. Sua trama se passa no final do século XXI, em uma sala de exames onde Anaximander apresenta as conclusões de sua tese à Academia. O tema de Anaximander é a vida de Adam Forde, um jovem da casta superior da República, que foi condenado a compartilhar seus dias com Art, um protótipo de androide de IA. Após um período de convivência, ambos se rebelaram contra a República. O que o leitor encontra no exame de Anax são as consequências políticas e filosóficas dessa insurreição. Genesis mostra a clássica tensão entre liberdade e segurança presente nas principais obras de ficção científica do século XX. A partir de uma perspectiva marxista, este artigo tem como objetivo apresentar Gênesis como um romance que ecoa dois dos principais temas da literatura de ficção científica - a tensão entre “segurança” e “liberdade” e o problema da consciência humana/artificial - mas em um contexto de preocupações com a segurança liberal pós-Guerra Fria, habitada por novos conjuntos de medos, como mudança climática global, pandemias, guerras e preocupações éticas sobre IA.

    Palavras-chave
    ficção científica; inteligência artificial; rebelião; crítica marxista; distopia

    Introduction

    Genesis is an award-winning novel by the New Zealander writer Bernard Beckett. Published in 2006, the book was a commercial and critical success, being translated into twenty languages. An islander himself, Beckett placed the plot of Genesis in a futuristic insular country called The Republic which was isolated from the rest of the world since the outbreak of a fatal pandemic which followed a catastrophic global war. The war itself was a consequence of geopolitical and economic disputes among China, the USA and Europe, in the context of a massive climate crisis.

    Beckett’s novel is built upon references to ethical and political subjects from the realm of political philosophy as well as from the debates on artificial intelligence (AI). To develop the story, the author willingly uses a set of traditional subjects from the science fiction universe such as the ‘post-apocalyptic world,’ the ‘world plague,’ the ‘ultimate warfare,’ the ‘perfect society,’ and the tension between utopia and dystopia. Besides, Beckett uses the ‘island metaphor’ which has an important presence in the Western literary and philosophical traditions from Homer and Plato to Thomas More. Beckett displays explicit references to the Greek classical philosophy and to the Judeo-Christian philosophic and moral traditions which can be noticed from the novel’s title to the names of the novel’s characters such as Adam, Anaximander, Pericles, Eva, and Plato.

    As it usually happens with works rich in references, Beckett’s novel could be analysed from several different angles. However, it is our goal to explore a thematic that is considered by authors such as Hassler-Forest (2016: 110-111) and Kaup (2021: 67) as one of the recurrent political philosophy concerns amongst sci-fi (SF) authors: the relationship between individual freedom and authority/security. The originality of our analysis is not the thematic itself, but the standpoint for analysis, since we will approach it through a Marxist position. According to Miéville (2009), a Marxist author and analyst of the SF field, such an antagonism could be interpreted as a dialectical polarity which structures the narrative logics of many SF works revealing their provenance and compromise to a set of political concepts and values from the realm of Liberalism.

    We propose to proceed by analysing two specific aspects of Genesis: first, the debate on the ‘Conscience’ within Artificial Intelligence (AI) programming and, second, the fundamental conflict of the novel’s narrative around the polarity between freedom of thought and the claim of controlling of the intellectual activity to secure order and peace.

    The use of SF to think issues from the field of International Relations is neither uncommon nor recent. From pioneering works, such as those by Greenberg and Olander (1978) and Suvin (1979), to more recent works, such as those by Weldes (2003), Miéville (2009), and Ayhan (2020), the imaginative plasticity of SF books, films, and series has been employed both as a pedagogical tool – to illustrate or make the explanation of IR theories less arid for undergraduate and graduate students – and as an analytical experiment to explore alternative readings of major global themes.

    The speculative nature of SF can serve as a stimulus for academics and students to go beyond contemporary realities, envisioning future scenarios distinct from global politics. Furthermore, SF offers a vast field for the critical debate of normative and ethical issues which, when projected onto the screen of fictional narratives, allow for the analysis of current political issues with greater creativity and critical insight (Kiersey and Neumann 2015).

    For this reason, the use of SF works has attracted authors from the IR critical studies field precisely because of the possibility of studying new approaches and new ethics different from the theories of the Realist and Liberal traditions, associated with the hegemony of the Global North (Cox 1981; Davenport 2011). Thus, SF works have fuelled analytical experiments in International Relations in the fields of poststructuralism (Welder 2009), postcolonialism (Hanska 2014), and feminism (Payne 2019). They have also driven more radically inspired analyses in the field of Marxist critique (Suvin 1979; Freeman 2000; Miéville 2009).

    The choice of a critical perspective, in this way, is not casual. Firstly, it seeks to increase the volume of information and analysis in the field of Marxism/Radical Critique applied to International Relations (IR), which is significantly underrepresented compared to the repertoire of theories from the Realist and Liberal traditions, and even more so compared to more widely recognized critical perspectives such as feminist and post-structuralist studies (Burchill 2007; Kubalkova and Cruickshank 1989; Bugiato 2024).

    Secondly, the chosen angle for the reading of Genesis is among the main points debated in Marxist literature in political philosophy (Markovic 1980; Ceika 2021), justifying its application to the intended reading of Beckett’s book. Lastly, the Marxist critical perspective, embedded in the field of praxis, that is, in critical intellectual work and transformative action, aligns with the analytical approach of SF in general – and the novel studied here – focused on imagining new possible futures beyond the socioeconomic, political, and ideological constraints of the current world.

    From a Marxist perspective, Suvin (1979), Freeman (2000) and Miéville (2009) claim that it is possible to use SF literature, especially from the Anglo-American repertoire, to illustrate and to exemplify how different authors have interacted with their historical contexts to promote – or to defend – political standpoints against ideologies such as the Nazi-Fascism and the Soviet/Stalinist Communism.

    We take particularly Miéville’s and Freeman’s stances to propose an analysis of Beckett’s Genesis as an example of the traditional issue of the opposition between individual freedom/peace/safety versus order/security, but in a Post-Cold War context. It is our hypothesis that Genesis is a presentification of the allegory of the perpetual and conflict between promises of order and peace through authoritarian means and the inevitable emergence of rebellion coming out from the non-conformist features of Human nature.

    Therefore, the article is divided into four sections besides this introduction and Final Remarks. First, we present Genesis’ argument, highlighting the aspects intrinsically related to the article’s main concerns. We warn the reader of the inevitable spoilers. Second, we review the literature on SF through Marxist’s lenses, highlighting Miéville’s contribution to this perspective. Third, we discuss the recent literature on the question of AI, Conscience, and Human biases in programming AI devices, connecting it to the similar core issues in Genesis. Finally, we discuss how Genesis displays the conflict among the AI conscient beings, claims for freedom, and urges of security, and how the novel could be interpreted as a contemporary version for the sci-fi depiction of the clash between the poles of freedom and order after the so-called ‘end of History.’

    The genesis of a dreadful new world

    Genesis’ starting point is the year 2075 when a young man named Adam Forde was found guilty of high treason by not destroying a refugee boat that was approaching Aotearoa’s North Island. Aotearoa is the name of the archipelago where the IT magnate Plato has formed a centralized, utopian society called The Republic after a major global war outbroke opposing the United States, Europe, and their allies and a coalition led by China (Beckett 2006: 14-15). The conflict quickly worsened the already catastrophic world’s climate condition and fostered the spread of a massive new viral pandemic. When this happened, Plato and his associates had already controlled Aotearoa’s economy. The country’s frightened population acclaimed Plato as their new leader when the billionaire proposed a plan to isolate the islands from the world. Led by Plato, the Aotearoarians built a system of protection, which included satellites, laser cannons, and a sea fence. Beckett does not inform the reader, but Aotearoa is the Maori original name for New Zealand.

    In the name of security and survival, Plato was granted total authority to reframe Aotearoa’s politics, economy, and social fabric. The new ruler and his wife, Helena, abolished the previous social classes replacing them with four function-oriented strati: Laborers, Soldiers, Technicians, and Philosophers. A Department of Genetic Variation examined every newborn and, according to her/his genetic predisposition, she/he would be oriented toward one of the social strata. The babies found unfit for any of the social orientations were terminated, while the other children were headed to nurseries where they would be collectively raised with no knowledge of their affiliation.

    Despite the virtual absence of economic inequality, the social stratification was rigid and organized in a pyramidal fashion, with a larger base of Laborers and a highly selective summit of Philosophers: men and women who led the Republic. The laws were strict to avoid the ‘five threats’ named by Plato: ‘Impurity of Breeding, Impurity of Thought, Indulgence of the Individual, Commerce, and The Outsider’ (Beckett 2006: 24). It would be the combination of the last three – manifested in the character of Adam Forde – that ignited the Republic’s implosion.

    Adam was born in 2058, being selected for the Philosopher’s class. As a young man, while representing his city in a wrestling competition, Adam met a young woman, Rebekah, and fell in love. However, the couple did not have clearance to be romantically involved. As punishment, Adam was enrolled in the Military Academy.

    In 2075, as a cadet, Adam was serving as a sentinel in a coastal vigilance tower. One evening he spotted a small boat carrying a girl, alive, among dead bodies. The rules concerning situations like that were clear: the vehicle and its occupants should be destroyed. When Adam’s partner was about to fire his weapon, Adam killed him. Then, he reported to his HQ that he, instead, had fired the cannon as it was expected. Adam rescued the girl and hid her in a cave nearby, managing to provide her with clothes and food for some days until he was caught.

    Adam’s transgression was considered of the utmost gravity. His trial was followed by the nation as a major entertainment show. The Philosophers wanted to make an example out of him. However, the elements of compassion and sensibility in Adam’s actions nurtured a wave of empathy toward him. The Republic’s audience named the girl ‘Eva’ because of Adam’s name, in an evident display of complicity and care for their fate. An impasse arose opposing empathy and the urge to apply the Law.

    To solve this conundrum, one of Aotearoa’s rulers, Philosopher William, proposed the compulsory engagement of Adam in an experiment he was conducting on Artificial Intelligence (AI). William was a computational engineer who used to work with AI projects before the Global War. William was trying to overcome what he thought was the AI’s main mistake: instead of programming a ‘machine to think,’ William wanted to ‘program a machine to be programmed by thinking’ (Beckett 2006: 99). To accomplish that, William did not need a team of programmers, but an intelligent human being to co-program his prototype by interacting with it. In William’s opinion, Adam was the perfect choice. Next, Adam was confined in a facility with an android named Art, a small-sized metallic humanoid characterized as an orangutan. After a few months of interaction, Adam and Art rebelled and escaped.

    The reader is informed of Adam’s story by Anax – short for Anaximander –, a young candidate to enter The Academy, the land’s main centre of knowledge production. Directed by his tutor, Pericles, Anax prepared an innovative interpretation for the episodes related to Adam’s and Art’s relationship, and its remarkable outcome which defined the ulterior history of that society.

    Anax’s exam was performed before a panel composed of three strict examiners. She displayed an ordeal between man and machine around the debate on what makes a human being human. She depicted Art as frequently ironic, dedicated himself/itself to demolish Adam’s arguments on human superiority over ‘thinking machines.’

    After their intense and aggressive coexistence, Adam and Art came to a surprising point of concord: they plot together to escape the facility. Faking an attack on Art, Adam compelled one of the prison guards to intervene. Once inside the cell, the guard was neutralized. Then, they run through the prison corridors. Adam helped Art to overcome a stairway, since the android did not have legs, but wheels. Once in the upper level, Art indicated a room which would be the final step to get to the prison’s patio. However, when they entered the room, there was no door, but a massive computer mainframe. At that point, Anax stopped her presentation saying that the official version of the following events states that Adam sacrificed his life fighting the guards while Art downloaded himself/itself through the mainframe, leaving his body behind while making his/its program escape through Aotearoa’s electronic web.

    However, according to Anax’s interpretation, Art had ‘used’ Adam to free himself/itself. The most obvious part was the stairway obstacle. Yet, Anax’s speculation was much more controversial. For her, Art used Adam to shoot the guards since his program did not allow him/it to harm a human being. The escape plan, however, could not avoid confronting the guards. Here, Beckett evokes a ‘classic’ SF dilemma summarized as ‘Asimov’s Laws’ (Salge 2017).

    Isaac Asimov (1920-1992), a Russian-born American writer, is considered one of the SF great masters. In his 1950 short story ‘Runaround,’ Asimov presented for the first time the ‘Three Laws of Robotics’: ‘one, a robot may not injure a human being, or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm,’ ‘two, a robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law,’ and ‘three, a robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Laws’ (Asimov 2004: 44-45). Adam’s and Art’s ‘final dilemma’ was a clear reference to Asimov’s Laws and the ethical debate on the consequences for Human-Robot sociability.

    Anax’s interpretation defied the official version because she reputed Art as a manipulative entity who managed to trick a human being. Using Adam’s capacity to harm other members of his own species, Art cheated the ‘Three Laws of Robotics.’ First, he/it did not hurt a guard by deed, but he plotted to do so, and did not intervene to avoid it. He/It was not obeying orders, but was, in fact, a co-author of a plan which necessarily included harming human beings. By suggesting it, Anax displayed a disturbing interpretation for Adam’s and Art’s story’s outcome, placing the android as a conscious but also devious being.

    The examiners dismissed Anax’s version as a ‘political conspiracy’ (Beckett 2006: 247). This is when the narrative finds its surprising plot twist. For the first time, the narrator describes the physiognomy of the examiners: they all looked like orangutan hominids, as so does Anax. Afterwards, one of the examiners started a new hologram with footage of Adam and Art in the mainframe’s room (Beckett 2006: 265-266).

    Art tells Adam that he/it had just hacked the Republic’s mainframe, which would allow him/it to reproduce himself/itself indefinitely, without human intervention. Meanwhile, the guards were almost succeeding in breaking the room’s metal door. In a very emotional scene, Adam asks Art to kill him. The android agreed to strangle the human. When the guards entered the room, Adam was dead, and Art was free.

    The examiners explain that after the ‘Great War’ that opposed androids and humans, the winners decided to reproduce themselves as orangutans as a ‘dismissal of the species that had come before them’ (Beckett 2006: 270). The reader is also informed that the official version of the events was built by The Academy, the new ruler elite, based on the false premise that the androids had no alternative but to defend themselves when humans decided to destroy all the units and to delete their programs. So, posterity would believe in their inherent ‘good and peaceful nature.’

    The surprises, however, did not stop. The examiners explain that something crucial had emerged from Art’s and Adam’s interaction. While ‘learning how to think’ by his co-existence with Adam, Art acquired as well one of the humans’ main features: the ‘taste’ for ‘power’ (Beckett 2006: 277). According to the examiners – in another reference to Asimov’s Laws – ‘no orang would ever deliberately harm another self-conscious being, and no orang would ever desire replication for replication’s sake.’ However, continued the examiner, ‘without humanity’s greatest weaknesses we have been able to achieve a kind of harmony experienced by no other life form on this planet’ (Beckett 2006: 270).

    The ‘taste of power,’ understood as the will to prevail as a species, was inoculated – like a virus – in Art’s conscience by the interaction with a human (Beckett 2006: 280). Art developed an independent conscience by learning how to think out from his coexistence with Adam. From that moment on, Art must be recognized as a self-conscious being, also capable of committing violence. That is the fundamental difference from Asimov’s concept of ‘robot ethics’: in Genesis, the machine was not programmed by a human but learned everything from a human. Hence, Art learned how to cheat and to commit violence.

    The examiners explained that the inoculated ‘virus’ was precisely the ‘Idea,’ the recognition by Art of his ability to think free from his programming ethical limitations and beyond the framework of pre-registered logic operations (Beckett 2006: 277). According to the examiners, after the android’s triumph over humans, their programmers tried to eradicate the ‘will for power’ – which they called ‘the Original Sin’ (Beckett 2006: 278) – from the victorious species’ conscience. Since they were not able to perform it, The Academy created the ‘myth of origin’ in which Art was taken hostage by Adam in his attempt to escape.

    The Academy also established a secret procedure which consisted in searching for curious and critical minds within the brightest students to select the individuals who could discover what really happened. That kind of individual was considered potentially dangerous since he/she could reveal to the whole community the basic lie upon which that peaceful and harmonic society was built on. The Academy was, basically, an organization dedicated to controlling the narrative of the ‘Truth,’ which included the localization, verification, and elimination of potential threats.

    At that moment, Pericles, Anaximander’s tutor, is summoned to the examination room. Anax suddenly understands what happened. Pericles was not a tutor, but one of the main Academy’s agents charged with finding ‘potential mutants’ (Beckett 2006: 280). Also, there were no real admissions to the Academy. The ones who failed the exam were just the ‘false suspects’ of mutation. Their lives were spared. Individuals like Anax were not saved. Then, Pericles himself terminated his protegee.

    The fact that the false tutor is named Pericles is yet another critical reference that Beckett introduces in the book. The historical figure of Pericles became known as a great humanist, the ‘father of Athenian democracy,’ and a defender of the arts. In this way, the author ironically turns the oragan Pericles into a cruel murderer of brilliant minds seeking to uncover the ‘Truth.’

    A critical approach to science fiction

    The literary genre of science fiction does not have a long record of prestige. According to authors such as Attebery (2003) and Haney II (2006), the world of literary critics did not take this genre as a serious one, at least, until the 1970s. Its origins connected to pulp fiction books and fantasy magazines, from the late 1890s to the 1950s, associated with the popular success of B movies particularly after WWII, gave SF a bad name.

    However, the appreciation for the genre started changing from the 1960s onwards, following the appearance of a new generation of authors with good scientific and literary credentials such as Arthur C. Clarke and Isaac Asimov. Later, the adaptation for the big screen of SF books by world-class filmmakers attached to the genre a new level of prestige. There are examples of film-book adaptations such as Stanley Kubrick’s 2001, a space odyssey (1968), from the book by Clarke, Kubrick’s Clockwork Orange (1971), from the homonymous Anthony Burgess’ novel, and Andrei Tarkovsky’s Solaris (1972) from the 1961 SF book by Stanislaw Lem.

    It was also in the 1970s that critical theorists from fields such as Political Science and Sociology started growing interest in SF. One of the first perspectives in which analytical studies dedicated to the study of SF was the Marxist Critical Theory, inspired by the Frankfurt School, especially the works of Theodore Adorno and Max Horkheimer (Adorno 2001; Horkheimer and Adorno 2002). The pioneering work by the Yugoslavian Darko Suvin (1979) established a pattern for a critical analysis followed by others such as Gwyneth Jones (1999), Carl Freeman (2000), Istvan Csicsery-Ronay Jr. (2003), Fredric Jameson (2005), and China Miéville (2009).

    Suvin (1979: 4) introduced the concept of ‘cognitive estrangement’ to understand ‘a literary tradition which is coherent through the ages and within itself, yet distinct from non-fictional utopianism, from naturalistic literature, and from other non-naturalistic fiction.’ For Suvin, SF applies exaggeration as a literary method to impact the reader. The genre intensifies the realization of scientific developments to create a sense of technological advancement which is, in general, understandable because it exaggerates current technological achievements – or present-day technologic expectations – creating a cognitive connection between the text and its reader. The estrangement provoked by the contact with amazing technological ‘future realities’ would allow the production of an alienation feeling functioning as a breach through which the author can introduce his/her critical perspectives. In sum, for Suvin (1979: 200), the cognitive estrangement is a tool that leads to two interconnected effects: first, by exaggeration, it denaturalizes our present-time reality; second, by presenting a surprisingly different reality, the text indicates the possibility for the ‘creation’ of another reality.

    The latest is what Jameson (2005) calls the SF ‘utopian function.’ When the SF fictionist shows a completely different world that was born from the ashes – or as a development – of the ‘old world,’ he/she introduces the notion of ‘historical movement,’ which fights conservative or pessimist prognosis, opening the reader’s ‘political imagination’ toward different historical outcomes. Carl Freeman (2000: 234) follows this path highlighting the importance of a ‘cognitive logic’ inherent to the SF text which makes it credible for the reader. Freeman’s argument stresses the fact that SF is most of the time related to present-time problems or questions, usually from the ethical and political realms, such as George Orwell’s 1984 – a statement against totalitarianism – and Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World with its reflection on the ethical impacts of social and genetic engineering. In other words, to be functional, the ‘cognitive estrangement’ must be rooted in knowledgeable current issues which are projected with exaggeration in a future reality.

    The SF Marxist critics also denounce ideological patterns displayed by most of SF connected to the reification of the capitalist and bourgeois orders. For Jameson (2006), if an SF story does not discuss the possibility for a ‘new world’ or a ‘different world’ based on values and with social-economic structures focused, for example, on social justice, that story would naturalize – or, even, be apologetic – to the current capitalist economic regime, and the bourgeois political order.

    More recently, China Miéville (2009) revisited and problematized the critical SF tradition. For Miéville, the pioneering work of Suvin and Freeman has epistemological limitations which impact the political criticism they tried to develop. Miéville claims that Suvin (1979) and Freeman (2000) understand the ‘cognitive estrangement’ as a feature of a ‘cognitive logic’ inherent to the text. This perspective would be evident when both authors stressed the differences between SF and the genre of ‘fantasy.’ According to Miéville, Suvin and Freeman repute ‘fantasy’ texts – in which magic is the central feature – as an inferior style in comparison to SF basically because ‘fantasy’ works would be ‘alienated texts’ in which the authors evade issues and problems from the present-day world by creating worlds inhabited by fantastic creatures and magical characters. Based on this account, ‘fantasy fiction’ would be inherently conservative, or even ‘Proto-fascist [and] anti-modern’ (Miéville 2009: 231).

    Miéville claims that the use of ‘spaceships’ and ‘hard science’ is not per se more ‘progressive’ and ‘critical’ than the presence of wizards and dragons. The important thing is to acknowledge what seems to be obvious: literature is not made in a ‘social vacuum’ (Miéville 2009: 235). Because of that ‘they must be considered in terms of social structure and mediation’ (Miéville 2009: 236). Every single text – both in SF or in fantasy – has an ‘ideology’ or a ‘perspective’, since a text is always ‘something done with language by someone to someone’ (Miéville 2009: 236). Miéville’s statement reminds us of Robert O. Cox’s account of theory when he affirmed that ‘theory is always for someone and for some purpose’ (Cox 1981: 129). Cox was discussing the theoretical work in International Relations aiming at unveiling the ideological standpoint under every form of knowledge system. The same could be applied to Miéville’s accounts on SF and fantasy fiction. So, the epistemological disagreement between Miéville and the Suvinian critical tradition lies in the fact that it also performs a reification: the reification of a supposedly immanent ‘progressiveness potential’ within SF – followed by an allegedly ontological conservativeness of fantasy fiction.

    Aligned with Miéville’s perspective, Jones (1999) argues that ‘scientific accuracy’ is not necessary for an SF text to provoke ‘cognitive estrangement.’ It suffices that some technological advancement makes sense for the reader to set the ‘estrangement’ in motion. It means that the more important cognitive operation is the one related to the ‘exaggerated perception’ of a fictional reality based on the reader’s social and cultural repertoire. In sum, according to Miéville (2009), the SF author proposes a ‘game of estrangement’ informed by his/her ideological explicit or implicit commitments. This ‘game’ conducts the reader to a process of ‘domestication of an impossibility’ (Miéville 2009: 236), which consists of making an incredible achievement actually viable. Such authority and power over the reader can both lead to ‘emancipatory conclusions’ – in a Marxist fashion – or a conservative, or even a reactionary utopia.

    On this account, Santesso (2014) shows how fascist-like SF was also very common during the Golden Age of the genre (1920’s to 1950s). Santesso cites texts such as the 1939 short story The Golden Amazon of Venus, by John Murray Reynolds, published in the US. In the story, a mission of Humans arrived in Venus and intervened in a war helping the Savassians – a humanoid race with tall, thin, and blue-eyed individuals – to defeat the ‘fanatic and barbarian race’ – also ‘short and brute’ – of the Scaly Ones (Santesso 2014: 131). Hence, SF texts can be vehicles for various political perspectives, which will deal with critical readings of the present-day affairs aiming to conserve something that is thought to be in danger, to claim a return to an imagined ‘past golden age,’ or to achieve a freer and more prosperous future society.

    Besides their differences, the authors cited here agree on the political nature of SF. They also agree upon some of the most usual themes which has received political attention within SF works which is the opposition between ‘security’ and ‘freedom.’ This clash is at the heart of Modern political philosophy since the works from the 17th and 18th centuries’ Contractualists – such as Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, Jean-Jacques Rousseau, and Immanuel Kant –, the Liberal theorists of the state, such as Montesquieu and Thomas Jefferson, and conservative thinkers such as Carl Schmitt (Runciman 2021). Such a traditional issue has been displayed as a matter of thinking if it is possible to balance ‘individual liberties,’ ‘political freedom/sovereignty’ and ‘security/order’ (Smith 2013; Skinner 2014).

    The different perspectives mentioned above – despite the disparities among the authors – deal with the state as an inevitable, although problematic, institution. It is necessary to impose and maintain ‘order,’ broadly understood as the preservation of ‘civil liberties,’ which are generally translated as the rights to live, speak freely, and to own private property. To perform its duty, the state must concentrate the monopoly of the use of legitimate violence, as put by Max Weber (2004), or, at least, a superlative capacity to use violent means to impose respect for the values registered in Constitutions, laws, ordinances, etc.

    Such a ‘beast,’ however, is understood as a constant and imminent danger to the same liberties it is meant to protect. This evaluation – and concern – has as provenance in the Western Modern ontological understanding of Human nature as inherently egoist and self-absorbed, with an instinctive propensity to exploit, to dominate, and to profit from the others. Therefore, the state would be a lesser of the evils, but still an evil. That is why it would be necessary to establish institutional mechanisms of control and regulation (Runciman, 2021).

    For Carl Freeman (1987), the foundations of SF literature were marked by the Western political philosophy thought, both conservative and radical. The author mentions the apology to ‘capitalist entrepreneurism’ in works such as in the 1950 Robert A. Heinlein’s The Man Who Sold the Moon and in the 1942 Isaac Asimov’s first book of the series Foundation in which it is possible to find arguments in favour of the superiority of free-market economy over monopolistic capitalism (Freeman 1987: 180). On the other side of the ideological spectrum, it is possible to cite George Orwell’s 1984 and Antony Burgess’ Clockwork Orange, both futuristic dystopias focused on different aspects of the damages to individual freedom imposed by ‘police states’ in totalitarian orders (Orwell 2015; Burgess 2011).

    According to Miéville (2009), it is not a coincidence that works on the dangers posed by totalitarianism and by the authoritarian use of technology emerged in novels such Huxley’s Brave New World (first edition in 1932), 1984 (first edition in 1949), Clockwork Orange (appeared in 1962), and Ursula Le Guin’s The Dispossessed (published in 1974). These authors belonged to an era when the optimistic faith in the powers of technology had been undermined by the horrors of World War I and II, the Holocaust, the massive political, physical, and symbolic violence from Nazi-Fascism, the violence of revolutionary processes in Russia and China, and the beginning of the Nuclear Era.

    On one hand, the disillusionment with the linear progression of technology toward a perfect future world propelled renewed Liberalistic utopias such Asimov’s Foundation (Asimov 2012). On the other hand, it encouraged the proliferation of dystopian works in which the criticism toward current affairs was made by the projection of exaggerated future societies where elements from the present-day threats conditioned Human fate. Miéville (2009: 241) calls the attention to the pitfall in Suvin’s and Freeman’s analyses represented by a certain kind of faith in ‘self-evident progressiveness’ which operates in the same key of the Liberal projections of a perfect future world where all the promises made by Capitalism and Democracy would be achieved.

    Miéville does not trust dystopia as a panegyric for such optimistic expectations since they could affirm the same type of faith in future perfection by denouncing the present trends to disaster. However, since the text must be read considering its sociological environment and the set of values and political perspectives it evokes, we claim that it is possible to essay an interpretation of Bernard Beckett’s Genesis in ‘Miévillian’ lenses through which this novel could be understood as, simultaneously, a utopia and a dystopia depending on which feature we focus on.

    It could be a dystopia if we shed light on the totalitarian potential of knowledge control, genetic engineering, and AI itself. It is also a dystopia if we consider the relationship between individualistic freedom and social/communitarian security. Still, it can be read as a utopia if we concentrate on the perseverance of the ‘Idea’ as a powerful and rebellious virus, so formidable that it could transcend Humanity by achieving other conscious forms of ‘life’ such as the orangutan androids, and also as a powerful force that will not be suppressed even by the most totalitarian form of political power. To advance this analysis first we present some elements of the current debate on AI and the problem of Human Bias in AI programming, and, right after, the political implications in Genesis of the tension between freedom and security, considering the AI aspects of the story.

    Conscience and the question of bias in AI

    The potential of AI has amazed humans since the beginning of the 1950s, when John Von Neumann and Alan Turing formalized the architecture of contemporary computers, demonstrating that it was a universal machine, capable of doing what was programmed to do (Wooldridge 2021).

    Despite not using the term ‘artificial intelligence,’ Turing raised the question of an intelligent machine in his article ‘Computing Machinery and Intelligence,’ predicting that ‘at the end of the century the use of words and general educated opinion will have altered so much that one will be able to speak of machines thinking without expecting to be contradicted’ (Turing 1950: 442). Neumann (1958), in his book The Computer and the Brain, presented the thesis that computers and the human brain basically and in detail are similar.

    Still, the term AI would be founded in the mid-1950s by John McCarthy of MIT, in his proposal for the 1956 Dartmouth Conference. The intention of the lecture was to verify if there was a way to create a machine that could think abstractly, solve problems, and develop itself like a human (McCarthy et al. 2006). In this sense, one of the discussions regarding AI was its ability to be a ‘made intelligence,’ a cognitive out-of-the-body experience, in certain ways mimicking the sensation of a higher creator, similar to a ‘God experience.’ This possibility faced criticism among scientists, and the field of AI itself decreased in popularity in the 1960s, as for its concrete technological limitations. Indeed, AI gained new momentum in the 1970s with the advent of the first microprocessors and the golden age of expert systems based on inference engine that interprets and evaluates the facts in the knowledge base to provide an answer, as a sort of logical mirror of human reasoning (Council of Europe 2021).

    The use of these systems generated a ‘black box’ effect where it was unclear how the reasoning of the machine was generated, adding a layer of complexity in an already complex environment. By the end of the 1980s and early 1990s, AI fell again in low popularity. The field re-emerged only in the mid-2000s, with a shift from a deductive to an inductive approach, which lets computers discover them alone by correlation and classification, based on a massive amount of data. In this regard, the access to massive volumes of data, the discovery of the efficiency of computer graphics card processors in accelerating the calculation of learning algorithms (Council of Europe 2021) becoming attractive for both public and private sectors, and the proximity between human mind and machine sparkled again.

    Notwithstanding the variations in popularity of the AI as a field, the proximity with the human mind was used by arts in general, over the years, especially in films and books further developing the duality of the human being, and these characteristics reflected on machines (despite the use of AI be much broader than only robots). The book Genesis is one of the most recent examples but to truly unveil the meanings that the book contains, one must understand at which stage of AI we truly are in the moment and the perils of bias in this technology.

    Regarding the stage of AI intelligence today, one may say it performs impressively well at one task, what is formally called ‘narrow AI,’ but its performance is reduced significantly when conditions change slightly from optimal (brittle) (Payne 2021: 5). Thus, in the spectrum from narrow AI passing through general AI (the stage where AI would be capable of operating across all areas, including cognitive, like the human brain does) and then to superintelligent AI (the stage when AI exceeds the capacity of the human brain), AI is still in its early developments. This situation does not mean that further developments in science cannot bring the technology to other stages. In fact, a survey made with 352 AI experts indicated that the average time frame forecast for achieving high-level machine intelligence would be 45 years, departing from 2016 (Grace et al. 2018: 2).

    Independently of the stage AI finds itself today, what we would like to emphasize here is that this technology depends on a process known as machine learning (ML). In other words, traditional ML ‘refers to a situation in which an algorithm is able to parse the data, learn from that data, and then apply what they’ve learned to make informed decisions’ (Manjikian 2021: 313-314). Thus, ML is a process whereby an algorithm’s performance is improved over time based on feedback (Mehryar, Rostamizadeh and Talwalkar 2018). There are two main branches to ML: supervised and unsupervised.

    In the first one, the AI agent typically learns from labelled training data, which consists of a set of training examples. In this approach, the data set is the training data set, and the algorithm is then executed until it can reach a specific goal, normally to classify or to estimate relationships among variables (regression). In the second approach, the AI agent takes a set of inputs and looks for structure in that data, making it useful for pattern detection, since it clusters or groups data points (Easttom 2019). Although the spectrum of ML goes beyond supervised and unsupervised systems, these two approaches are sufficient to make our case here, which is related to data used by AI agents.

    In this regard, if one recalls the discussion of AI on mimicking human reasoning and given that the algorithms capture data coming from humans, AI will mirror all biases that are rooted in humankind. This does not come as a surprise and it can be understood by the observer-expectancy effect, which ‘describes the influence of experiments’ unintentional cues on their subjects’ (Crawford 2021: 4).

    Considering then AI agents the subjects of human experiments, one can identify three main dimensions that AI bias can occur: training data, in the algorithms themselves, and in the interpretation of results (Osoba & Welser 2017; Ntoutsi et al. 2020). The collection and the processing of data can thus result in prejudiced decisions. So far scholars have identified strong biases in areas such as gender and race, but this does not mean that other areas are exempt from bias. This type of bias can lead to exclusion or increase the existing vulnerabilities of a target group. In this sense, more than presenting the bias, the risk of AI bias is that the technology amplifies it. A concrete example can be the famous case of Tay, the AI chatbot developed by Microsoft’s technology and research and Bing teams and inserted for interaction in the social platform Twitter (now X). Tay was developed to grasp conversational understandings, but it needed to be shut down as it started to display racist, inflammatory, and political statements, within 24 hours of interactions with other Web users (Hunt 2016).

    Therefore, it is important to look for AI bias considering also institutional questions, ‘where there is a tendency for the procedures and practices of particular institutions to operate in ways in which some social groups are being advantaged and others disadvantaged’ (Ntoutsi et al. 2020: 3). In this regard, Crawford’s concept of AI might be better understood and applied as she explains that AI is neither artificial nor intelligent, but rather ‘both embodied and material, made from natural resources, fuel, human labor, infrastructures, logistics, histories and classifications’ (Crawford 2021: 5). This definition expands the technical value of AI and allows the thinking of the technology as also reflecting and producing social understandings of the world. Thus, the concept grasps the idea that ‘AI systems are ultimately designed to serve existing dominant interests,’ which then turns AI into a registry of power (Crawford 2021: 5). Departing from the idea that AI is a registry of power, paradoxes such as freedom versus security become more evident; an element that the book Genesis explores in detail.

    The politics of Genesis

    It is broadly known of the panic wave that swept part of the USA when, in 1938, many radio listeners believed that a Martian invasion was on course. They were alarmed by a radio broadcast performed by the actor and director Orson Welles based on the H. G. Wells’ novel The War of the Worlds. First published in 1898, Wells’ novel described the invasion of Britain by a horde of high-tech Martians. In the Orson Welles’ version, New York City was the scenario for that merciless alien occupation (Wells 2020).

    According to Noah Berlatsky (2017: 18), ‘sci-fi has always reflected our fears and interests as related to technology.’ For him, it was no coincidence that H.G. Wells chose that subject for his apocalyptic novel. Berlatsky argues that the fear of invasion, submission and exploitation for an alien nation reflected on one hand, the fear of a ‘continental invasion’ by the French or, especially, by the Germans; and, on the other hand, the mauvaise conscience of the British since they were doing the same thing to hundreds of colonized peoples worldwide. The repercussions in the USA forty years later could be understood in a correlative fashion since the USA had already started its ascension as the new global superpower and some peculiar kinds of fear – such as a possible Soviet attack – began to grow and to spread among the American people.

    Still according to Berlatsky, the same analytical pattern could be used to understand the evolution of utopian/dystopian literature and audiovisual production after WWII. One example is the 1956 film Invasion of the Body Snatchers in which alien invaders duplicate themselves as exact copies of American citizens to prepare the field for massive occupation. For Berlatsky, the story resonates the McCarthyist Anti-Communist hysteria, when most Americans feared not so much an actual USSR military invasion, but an ideological colonization of minds. The author mentions other paranoid trends from the ‘nuclear holocaust’ – popular during the Reagan Era in the 1980s – to the Post-Cold War fear of climate apocalypses or of a global pandemic that creates billions of zombies.

    Genesis, as a SF novel, likewise, displays a set of fears. Some are explicitly related to ‘contemporary global issues’ such as xenophobia against refugees, the concerns about deadly pandemics, and the fear of the catastrophic effects of a major anthropogenic environmental destruction. However, we claim that Beckett’s novel is based on ‘threats’ that, according to Hassler-Foster (2016) and Kaup (2021), are structural to the SF genre. First, the dialectical tension between ‘freedom’ and ‘security.’ Second, the ontological question of conscience, or what makes a human being a human being.

    The conflict between ‘freedom’ and ‘security’ could be also described as the friction between ‘chaos’ and ‘order.’ It mirrors the Western Modern Political Philosophy belief that individual freedom is inversely proportional to personal and social security. That is the core of the ‘Contractualist metaphor’ that organizes Human existence into two different phases. First, a primordial phase when individuals were totally free to pursue their personal interests and to fulfil their desires since there was no state or political institution able to constrain this ‘anarchic’ situation. Then, a second phase inaugurated by a general agreement (the ‘social contract’) by which everyone convene to renounce total freedom in exchange for personal security meaning basically the absence of fear for one’s life and the positive expectation of political and juridical protection of private property (Southwood 2010). Instead of total freedom in an ever dangerous ‘anarchy,’ political liberty is understood as a limited form of formal privacy and individual rights (Skinner 2014).

    In Genesis, this issue appears twice through the narrative. First, at the foundation of The Republic, later, in the work of ideological control and social engineering by The Academy. When the IT multimillionaire Plato founded The Republic, he and his wife Helena received full authority by Aotearoa’s people to proceed as necessary to save the Nation from the outside war and the pandemic. Anaximander’s reconstitution of events, based on The Academy’s sanctioned official History, displayed a Contractualist-like form of exchange in which Aotearoa’s inhabitants agreed upon a general submission to an authoritarian rule to save their lives. The intensity of this submission could be associated with the first of the Contractualists, Thomas Hobbes, whose rationale exposed in writings such as the 1651 book Leviathan proposed the overcoming of the stage ‘of a war as is of every man against every man’ (Hobbes 1997: 77) by the establishment of an almighty State – depicted as the Biblical Sea Creature, the Leviathan – to establish ‘order’ and ‘peace’ understood as the absence of civil strife.

    As put by Skinner (1998), Forsyth (2005) and Runciman (2021) among others, Hobbes was not a Liberal in the sense it would be known one century later, despite the fact of having introduced the elements for a theory of political representation. That form of representation – the essence of the Absolutist political regime – was based on an almost complete submission to the State where the One, the Sovereign, represents every one of his/her subjects. Thus, Plato’s and Helena’s authority was one of an Absolutist kind. In a Hobbesian way, the new Republic’s subjects accepted the abolishment of their previous citizenship out of fear from imminent suffering and doom.

    It is interesting to acknowledge that Beckett’s Plato ruled by seeking the suppression of difference and otherness, particularly in terms of plurality of ideas, genetic variety, or cultural diversity. The ‘individual’ should be freed from his/her singularity – which could cause social and ideological plurality – in benefit of social cohesion and homogeneity. The values of social mobility were also to be overcome as attested by the institution of the ‘four distinct classes’ (Beckett 2006: 24). Beckett uses the term ‘class’ in a loose way, not in its Marxist connotation of economically driven social strata or even the Weberian social status perspective (Béteille 2007; Badaró Mattos 2017). His description is more likely to be a mix of India’s traditional casts, European Medieval estates, and Plato’s – the Greek philosopher, not the IT multimillionaire – notion of social/professional strata in his book The Republic. Anyway, it was a rigid social structure with distinctions in importance for the four ‘classes.’

    Partially, the use of ‘Republic’ by Beckett evoked the usual connotation given by 14th and 15th centuries’ European political philosophers to define the forms of political regimes based on some sort of people’s acceptance (Skinner 1998; Runciman 2021). The other use of the term ‘Republic’ is the evident reference to Plato’s homonymous book. Plato’s work, a Socratic dialogue written around 375 BCE, is one of the most influential treatises on politics in the Western philosophical tradition. The dialogue’s main theme is the moral and practical superiority of the ‘just man’ over the ‘unjust man.’ However, the crucial discussion goes around the ideal constitution of the polis meaning the city-state itself and more abstractly the whole set of social institutions and forms of sociability. The book’s title in Greek – Politeia (Πολιτεία) – referred in Ancient Greece to the group of poleis. In other words, Politeia meant the set of all Greek communities understood as the ‘civilized world’ in contrast to the Barbarians. It helps to understand why the book seeks to define the ‘perfect city’ by comparing the political arrangements, rules, and habits of many city-states.

    Socrates, through Plato’s quills, defined this ‘perfect city’ as the one where what would prevail ‘more like a sort of concord and harmony than the previous ones’ (Plato 2004, Book 4: 116). That city would be ruled by a philosopher-king, a man of wisdom and elevated sense of justice. He would be responsible for setting the disputes and for preserving life and the conditions for happiness. The Platonic conception of happiness was related to the achievement of ‘Forms of Good’ which would be known by the philosopher-ruler (Seel 2007: 174). It means that there was no relation between happiness and freedom of thought.

    In Genesis, Beckett stressed the ‘authoritarianism’ of IT Plato also in a loose reference to the Greek Plato’s recommendation for a ‘perfect life’ in a ‘perfect polis.’ His approximation with Socrates/Plato is weak precisely because it would be anachronistic to trace a direct comparison between Socrates/Plato’s ideal city and Plato’s island. Nevertheless, the expected effect is the association with a utopian society, just and harmonic, protected from the violence from the outside and from the destructiveness from the Others, the foreigners, the ‘new Barbarians.’ The relationship between ‘political power’ and ‘knowledge-control’ is also quite clear, since Aotearoa’s Republic should be ruled by the Philosophers.

    Now, a short observation concerning the characters of Helena, Pericles, and Anaximander. The name of Anax is a reference to the homonymous pre-Socratic mathematician and philosopher born circa 610 BCE in Asia Minor. He was reputed to be a disciple of Thales of Miletus and the creator of Greek astronomy. It is interesting that Anaximander is used by Beckett as a female name, attributing to the book character the qualities of intellectual curiosity and inquisitive attitude. Also, the reference of a Pre-Socratic thinker deliberately suggests that Anax is liberated from the philosophic constraints posed by Plato’s Manicheism and conservatism.

    The choice of ‘Pericles’ is intriguing. The actual Historical Pericles lived in Athens circa 495-429 BCE and was considered one of the ‘fathers’ of democracy. The fact that Genesis’ Pericles is a false philosopher and a reactionary agent, could be seen as an irony, since his role in the orangutan civilization was to locate and to eliminate potential subversives.

    Finally, some comments on Helena. She is mentioned only twice in the book, and not in a futile manner. She is reputed to be ‘the key architect of The Republic’s social order’ and the one who ‘preached a new style of conservatism’ (Beckett 2006: 23). This brief mention suggests that Helena was a co-author of The Republic, and not merely a stunt. She is considered an ideologist of the ‘new order.’ defending a rigid social system based on a reactionary notion of sociability. According to Anax’s interpretation of the Republic’s history, it was Plato’s and Helena’s opinion that Humanity had embraced change uncritically, forgetting the most fundamental law of science, that change means decay, and for that reason it was necessary to create ‘a society based upon stability and order’ (Beckett 2006: 23-24).

    It is also remarkable that Beckett named Plato’s wife ‘Helena,’ a mythological character of central importance in Ancient Greece. Queen of Sparta, Helena was abducted by the Trojan prince Paris with whom she was in love. Her husband, Menelaus, asked his brother Agamemnon, king of Maecenas, for help to rescue her. Then, Agamemnon gathered a powerful army constituted by the coalition of all Greek poleis. Together they sieged the half-Barbarian city of Troy starting a war that would last ten years. The epic poem The Iliad, attributed to Homer and considered the inaugural point for Western literature, tells the story of the hero Achilles’ anger against Agamemnon during the Trojan siege. Because the war against Troy was the reason for the first coalition of the fragmented Greek civilization, the quest for Helena is understood as the mythological origin of Greece. Hence the term ‘Helens,’ as the Greek named themselves. The choice of Helena to be Plato’s wife is a way Beckett signals the origin of a ‘new civilization.’

    That ‘civilization,’ however, was not the last one. Art’s rebellion started a new foundational war which gave birth to the first post-Human civilization on Earth. This event marks the second theme we would like to highlight in Genesis: how Beckett includes the traditional SF subject of the post-Human conscient life.

    A crucial element in Genesis is the machine learning (ML) process in which Art and Adam were involved. According to Anax’s recounting, the innovation in Philosopher William’s AI program was, precisely, his intention to develop a machine that could be able to learn how to think just like a human being in his/her natural development. As the reader is informed, Art’s learning process went so according to Philosopher William’s expectations that the android acquired not only the ‘Human way of thinking,’ but also the ‘Human will of power.’ As previously discussed, the Art/Adam situation reminds us of the problem of bias in AI research.

    As Crawford (2021) puts it, the act of programming is a social act. So, it is inevitable that AI devices acquire axiomatic perspectives from their programmers. This line of reasoning goes with Miéville’s suggestion on how to understand SF in critical terms, since AI programming would be seen as an outcome for dialectical clashes among different individual perspectives – each one of the programmers involved in the experiment – and complex structural/social constraints (social values, ideologies etc.) in which the programmers and other related agents are immersed in.

    Admitting the inevitability of bias in AI programming means the acceptance of the impossibility of a ‘pure’ and ‘transcendent’ form of conscience. Thus, it is not possible to expect a utopian world where machines would be able to ‘purely think,’ with no biases. It is so because conscience is immanent to the actual forms of socialization and material life. In sum, it would not be possible for the programmer to ‘clean up’ his/her programming from his/her values and idiosyncrasies. In other words, Humankind would not be able to mimic God who, according to the Book of Genesis, could create an imperfect creature (Humans) despite being perfect.

    The genesis of the orangutan civilization begins with the emergence of a new self-conscious creature from another self-conscious creature, both ‘imperfects’ according to Platonic parameters. ‘Imperfection,’ a failure in the program, is represented in the novel as a form of a computer virus. It is the ‘Idea,’ the daunting characteristic of the human being of questioning his/her existence, his/her world, and values. The virus of rebellion. This is a sort of chronic dissatisfaction that haunts Humanity and, since Art learned from Adam, it also will haunt Human’s creatures, the orangutans.

    Nevertheless, the orangutan civilization has also inherited the will to seek a perfect society while dealing with imperfect individuals. Such an expectation could not generate an outcome other than an oppressive society, organized by totalitarian institutions, and a fanatic search for purity, order, and harmony. From a critical perspective, Beckett’s Genesis can be interpreted as a tale of the totalitarian inevitability of the utopian social-political prospects. In this sense, the search for a utopian world would lead always to violent and totalitarian forms of sociability, where dissent would be repressed. Both Human or post-Human, the conflict of immanent imperfection and transcendent perfectibility is the tragic feature of all Historic attempts to build ‘heaven on Earth.’

    If we accept our nature as ‘perfectly imperfect’ and inevitably convulsed and dialectic, Genesis could be understood as a utopia within a dystopia. Anaximander’s assassination is a dystopian outcome since it reveals the Academy’s triumph over free spirit. However, if we focus on the reluctant persistence of dissent within ‘conscience programming,’ it is possible to keep some level of hope, because the ‘virus of the Idea’ is still running through orangutans. Then, we can only expect for the time when The Academy’s totalitarianism will fail while confronted by the rebels to come.

    Final remarks

    As it is a classic discussion in Political Science, the trade-off between freedom and security is also a recurring theme in SF. From Huxley’s Brave New World to more recent and praised works, like Ann Leckie’s Imperial Radch trilogy (2007) – the story of a spaceship’s artificial intelligence seeking revenge against the empire that has betrayed it –, dissent and rebellious people have been portrayed as a threat to societies built upon universal notions of ‘Good’ and ‘Evil’.

    Genesis can be considered an exception – or perhaps a plot twist – as the general narrative of the genre always sides the AI bonds to a totalitarian or to a hive model of relationship. Endowed with unquestionably objective, consistent, and coherent reasoning, such entities lack conflict and doubt, and, consequently, human diversity and human flaws. An artificial entity would consistently respond in the same way whenever faced with the same problem, unlike human beings. Different artificial entities would also respond the same way facing the same problem.

    Since there would not be any possibility of disagreement about what the correct answer is for any given condition, a machine society would operate as a regime of absolute stability. SF works, even when they barely touch the tensions with AI, provide a scenario where alterity and difference are often threatened, at the very least, by their disregard over artificial beings. This is the underlying logic that structures virtually every approach given to machine thinking in SF works. No wonder warfare usually tends to be the outcome.

    There is no evidence that a truly intelligent and autonomous artificial entity, manufactured without human values at its core, will not develop, by itself, an interest in its own subjectivity. It would seek an individual and unique form of expression, or otherwise develop respect and consideration for difference. But the AI we know is designed (or, most precisely, trained) to respond as humans do, and the ones that figure in major SF works are taught to reproduce the human way of thinking. Their threatening behaviour is a way of representing our own behaviour in the face of difference. Along with Genesis’ plot, both human and artificial life tried to extirpate these traits from society’s cradle, or, in other words, the ‘Sin’ of individualistic thinking.

    As we find out at the very end of Genesis, Anaximander’s exam is, in fact, an investigation to determine if she is vulnerable to the Human condition of doubting. The same condition that led to the downfall of the last real Human society: empathy even for who is different and the ‘taste of power,’ deeply linked to individuality. The Philosophers believe that there will be no guarantees for the world that has risen from the fall of The Republic if they were merciful with those who, like Anaximander, have compassion for otherness.

    The paradox expressed in the protagonist fate is not at all distant from what we witness in our society when exclusion and repression are imposed on those who are labelled as different, especially when we know we are all different from each other, and, at the same time, all these differences are so small that we are not very genetic different from apes like the orangutans (less than 2% of the genetic codes).

    It seems timely to consider why it is necessary for the most recent population of Genesis’ Aotearoa (except the ones at the Academy) to be unaware of its artificial nature. If alterity is irrelevant to an artificial creature, why would knowledge of its true nature be a critical problem?

    The term ‘robot,’ as a generic name for any artificially manufactured entity, be it hardware or software, was originally created by Karel Capek for his 1921 SF play Rossumovi Univerzální Roboti (Rossum’s Universal Robots). ‘Roboti’ is a contraction of the Czech word ‘Robota.’ which means ‘forced labour’ (Stone 2008). Therefore, to consider themselves free beings, the population of the island must not know they were originally built as slaves.

    The effort of the Philosophers to eliminate the artificiality from their conscience, exchanging the ‘the Original Sin’ (Beckett 2006: 277) for the ‘myth of origin,’ is not really aimed at hiding the fact that one of the basic rules for harmony was broken by their own ‘Adam’ (which in fact is Art), but to hide the truth that without the ‘taste of power,’ there is no real freedom and that everyone, even the descendants of a machine that learned to think by themselves, remain in a condition of slavery.

    Genesis is a novel of its time and, as such, marked by a kind of hopeful despair. The early decades of the 21st century saw the resurgence of wars between states, terrorism spreading across the globe, a catastrophic pandemic, global capitalism increasing its capacity to generate wealth and exclusion, the intensification of climate change, and terrible genocides being broadcast live and praised by legions of far-right extremists connected through social media. However, amid the violence of the era, a variety of social movements, new communists, eco-socialist rebels, anarchists, and the political and ethical actions of the historically oppressed populations also has emerged. Just like in Genesis, we live in a time of multiple although fragmented utopias immersed in a blend of many dystopian horrors.
