Sewing Third-Worldism: The U.S.-Brazil Textile Dispute and the Transformation of Brazilian Foreign Policy under Military Rule

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article examines the overlooked U.S.–Brazil textile dispute of the late 1960s and early 1970s and its impact on Brazilian foreign policy during the military regime (1964–1985). Drawing on recently declassified U.S. diplomatic sources, it highlights how the contradiction between the Nixon administration’s cooperative rhetoric toward Latin America and its protectionist actions against Brazilian textile exports shaped bilateral relations and Brazil’s role in the international system. Although Brasília made concessions and temporary geopolitical alignments masked tensions, the dispute’s aftermath fostered the emergence of a Third World outlook, though in a tempered and qualified form.

    Keywords:
    Textiles; Trade Disputes; US-Brazilian Relations; Brazilian Foreign Policy; Military Regime; Third World

    Introduction

    The 1964 military coup in Brazil marked a watershed in the country’s history, both domestically and in terms of its international positioning. The focus on constructing a foreign policy that emphasized the North-South divide between rich and poor countries was abruptly replaced by a firm alignment with the United States in the Cold War (Gonçalves and Miyamoto 1993, 215-220). The first of five military administrations during the 21-year dictatorship (1964-1985), that of President Marshal Castello Branco (1964–1967), strongly reinforced the emphasis on the East-West divide between capitalist and communist countries in world affairs (Hurrell 2013, 110-115).

    A key manifestation of this shift was Brazil’s altered position regarding the international economic system. Before the military takeover in 1964, the Brazilian government played a pivotal role in promoting Latin American unity around the agenda of reforming the global economic order (Krepp 2022, 93-99). This effort culminated in the launch of the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) in Geneva, in March 1964 (Toye 2014, 1760-1763). Days after the military intervention, the Castello Branco administration replaced Brazil’s delegation in Geneva with representatives that were more conservative. This shift paved the way for Brazil to abstain from key resolutions advanced by Third World countries, including those calling for preferential tariff concessions to facilitate the entry of goods from underdeveloped nations into the markets of wealthier economies (Krepp 2022, 98–99; Selcher 1970, 318–324).

    Following the Castello Branco administration, Brazil’s foreign policy under the military was marked by a gradual erosion of its alignment with the United States, coupled with a growing identification with the Third World cause (Bandeira 2011, chs 6-8; Hirst 2005, ch. 1). This shift was already evident, more in rhetoric than in practice, over the course of the Costa e Silva administration (1967–1969), became clearer under the Medici government (1969–1974), and reached a more mature stage in the fourth military presidency, that of general Ernesto Geisel (1974–1979), during which bilateral relations with Washington fell to an unprecedented low (Hurrell 2013, chs 3-7).

    Even at its height, however, this Third Worldism of Brazil’s military regime came with significant qualifications. Brazilian generals at no point expressed a fully alignment with the Third World camp, instead presenting itself as a bridge between the First and the Third Worlds (Selcher 1978, 13-15). When it endorsed Third World demands, it did so along the most moderate lines, never advocating, as some African, Latin American, and Asian countries did, for a complete overhaul of the global economic system (Selcher 1978, 15-23). Rather than pressing for structural transformations in trade and finance, the regime focused on more specific and cautious demands, particularly the stabilization of commodity prices and the opening of wealthy markets to Third World exports, especially manufactured goods with low capital intensity and high labor content (Souto 2003, 43-44). Despite these limitations, the transformation of the dictatorship’s foreign policy, compared to its early years, was truly remarkable (Vizentini 2004, 361-364).

    The extensive literature on Brazil’s foreign policy under military rule highlights several factors as driving this pronounced shift, especially with respect to the regime’s distancing from the United States. Four factors stand out: Washington’s opposition to Brazil’s nuclear program; the U.S.’s muted stance on arms sales and equipment provision; criticism of Brazil’s human rights record; and matters related to economic relations with the United States, most notably regarding Brazilian access to U.S. markets (Bandeira 2011, chs 6-8; Gonçalves and Miyamoto 1993; Hirst 2005, ch. 1; Hurrell 2013, chs 4-7; Lima 2013, chs 4-5; Martins 1974; Pecequilo 2012, ch. 1; Roriz and Loureiro 2024; Silva and Svartman 2014; Vizentini 2004). Overall, despite the Brazilian dictatorship’s staunchly anti-communist stance, the generals recognized that the close association with Washington established at the onset of the regime had not produced the expected benefits (Hurrell 2013, 109-110, 139-141).

    Of all the factors that contributed to the shifting position, we still know very little about the intense and protracted trade conflicts that Brazil had with the United States in the late 1960s and early 1970s. The Brazilian generals saw trade as crucial for legitimizing the regime, given the potential growth benefits such policies could generate (Hurrell 2013, 179-180). Apart from contributions related to soluble coffee (Loureiro 2021), and Brazil’s critique of the postwar world trade regime (Farias 2009; 2017), there are no studies focusing on other trade-related topics of the period, particularly those involving Brazil’s manufacturing exports and the multiple barriers they faced in accessing the U.S. market.

    This article seeks to address part of this gap by examining a key trade conflict between Brazil and the United States in the late 1960s and early 1970s, targeting the textile sector – here understood as crude yarns and fabrics of all types (cotton, wool, silk, etc.), excluding finished cloths and utensils. We draw on confidential and secret U.S. diplomatic records from the Department of State, the Department of Commerce, and the U.S. Embassy in Rio de Janeiro, which fully relocated to Brasília in November 1971. Several of these records have only recently been declassified and were selected because they directly dealt with economic negotiations with Brazil. We also incorporate evidence from Brazilian commercial newspapers. Together, these sources allow us, for the first time, to reconstruct a detailed chronology of the dispute, even though they reflect only how U.S. policymakers and representatives perceived and interpreted their interactions with Brazilians. We also consulted Brazilian diplomatic sources, which, unfortunately, did not provide the same level of richness as the U.S. documents for reconstructing the textile dispute.

    The paper shows that Washington’s resistance to allowing freer imports of Brazilian textile goods culminated in a serious clash at the end of the Costa e Silva administration in 1969. The conflict’s prominence reflected both the importance of the textile industry to the Brazilian economy and the growing emphasis the dictatorship placed on manufacturing exports. The dispute was contained a year later when the Medici administration chose to prioritize a more harmonious relationship with the United States, apparently in response to the deteriorating Brazilian geopolitical position in South America. We argue that the standoff, though seemingly minor and specific, generated immediate frustrations among Brazilian diplomats and officials, reinforcing the perception that Pan-Americanism – and the reliance on the United States as the main driver of Brazil’s development – was insufficient to address the country’s economic vulnerabilities.

    This does not mean that the textile dispute, by itself, was the sole factor driving Brazil’s military regime toward a more pragmatic and less ideological foreign policy, much less that it was entirely responsible for sustaining this shift over time. Nevertheless, it certainly played a role, both in the immediate and medium term. Assessing its precise weight, and how top Brazilian policymakers carried these frustrations forward, lies beyond the scope of this article. What is striking, however, is that the arguments later advanced by the Geisel administration in the late 1970s – in support of Third World demands for a restructuring of the international economic order, albeit in a more cautious tone (Spektor 2004, 201) – already appeared in the early 1970s and were closely linked to the textile conflict with the United States.

    Brazil’s Textile Manufacturing Sector and the Origins of the U.S.–Brazil Textile Trade Dispute

    Like other primary-exporting countries, Brazil’s industrialization began later than in the wealthier nations of the Global North. The textile industry, especially cotton textiles, emerged as Brazil’s first major industrial sector (Stein 1957, 1-65; Suzigan 2000, 129-130). The initial phase of textile manufacturing – excluding handicraft textile industry, which had existed since Brazil’s colonial period under Portugal (Libby 1997) – appeared around the 1840s (Suzigan 2000, 129-132). However, it was during the final decades of the 19th century, alongside the expansion of coffee production and exports, that textile factories began to multiply (Suzigan 2000, 140-151). Until the late 1930s, textiles constituted Brazil’s largest and most important manufacturing branch (Stein 1957, 191; Suzigan 2000, 129).

    Although heavier industries such as metallurgy and chemicals later gained prominence, the textile industry remained for decades one of the leading employers and producers of manufacturing goods in Brazil. As Table 1 shows, in 1959 the textile sector accounted for the largest share of Brazilian blue-collar employment and ranked second in production value. Under the military regime (1964-1985) the sector continued to lose ground to other manufacturing branches, but in 1970, when the U.S.–Brazilian textile conflict reached its peak, it still was the country’s second-largest employer and the third-ranked industrial segment in terms of output (Table 1).

    Brazilian textile producers focused almost exclusively on the domestic market. The main exception was during World War II, when wartime demands strained companies in the Global North and left poorer Latin American markets undersupplied (Stein 1957, 165-169). This situation began to change in the early 1960s, as Brazilian textile manufacturers started to establish a modest presence in neighboring markets (Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística 1965, 185-188; Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística 1966, 223-224). The 1964 military coup further pushed textile entrepreneurs to look beyond domestic borders, though for unfavorable reasons. The orthodox economic policies of the Castello Branco government led to a sharp contraction in the textile sector, causing numerous factory closures (Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística 1965, 105; 1969, 178). For some manufacturers, expanding into foreign markets became the only viable alternative to complete shutdown (“A indústria têxtil à nação.” 1966).

    The second administration of the Brazilian dictatorship, under general Costa e Silva (1967–1969), introduced a broad set of fiscal and administrative incentives to encourage Brazilian manufacturers to export (Macarini 2006). This strategy was grounded in the belief that rapid economic growth was essential to legitimize the increasingly repressive authoritarian rule (Motta 2021, ch. 9). Under general Médici (1969–1974), the third military president, export promotion became a central pillar of the regime’s economic agenda (Prado and Earp 2014, 226-227). As shown in Table 2, cotton textile exports – the most significant within the textile sector – surged, surpassing US$200 million in 1969 and accounting for nearly 9% of Brazil’s total export value by the end of the decade, while coffee, the country’s leading export product, represented 36.6% of total exports (Instituto Brasileiro de Geografia e Estatística 1969, 301).

    The pattern of Brazilian cotton textile exports to the United States showed a notable difference. The surge in foreign sales occurred not in the late 1960s, but earlier, around the middle of the decade. Between 1967 and 1969, while Brazilian yarn and fabric exports to global markets expanded rapidly, shipments to the U.S. remained sluggish (Table 2). It was only between late 1969 and mid-1970 that Brazilian textile sales to the U.S. market registered a significant increase. Not coincidently, this period saw the administration of Richard Nixon (1969-1974) push for stricter controls on the entry of Brazilian manufactured goods, sparking the trade dispute with Brazil.

    By that time, Washington had already been restricting textile imports from less developed countries for nearly a decade. Since the late 1950s, it had faced mounting pressure from domestic textile manufacturers to curb yarn and fabric imports (Bardan 1973, 10). John F. Kennedy was elected president in 1960 with a pledge to find a sustainable solution to this issue, while avoiding conflict with the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), established in the late 1940s, of which the United States was a part (Bardan 1973, 10-11). A core principle of GATT was the Most Favored Nation (MFN) clause, which required that any trade concession extended to one member be granted equally to all others (Farias 2017, 152-153). Imposing unilateral tariffs or quotas on textile imports would have openly contradicted both the letter and the spirit of the agreement.

    It was within this context that the Kennedy administration negotiated, under the auspices of GATT, the Long-Term Arrangement Regarding International Trade in Cotton Textiles (hereafter LTA) (Bardan 1973, 10-11). Signed in 1962, the LTA established two mechanisms through which net textile-importing countries could impose trade restrictions on yarn and fabric: first, unilaterally, by invoking the justification that imports were causing “market disruption” in the importing country; and second, through the conclusion of bilateral long-term agreements with net exporting countries, whereby the latter agreed to flexible, mutually established quotas (Bardan 1973, 12-14). Brazil refused to sign the LTA and rejected the principle of “market disruption”, arguing that it contradicted the fundamental basis of the non-discriminatory free trade that GATT was designed to promote (Ministério das Relações Exteriores do Brasil 1969; 1970).

    Given Brazil’s reluctance to enter into a bilateral agreement with the United States, Washington resorted to imposing unilateral textile export quotas. The first U.S. restrictions on Brazilian yarn and fabrics were introduced in October 1963, covering 500,000 square yards (418,000 square meters) of sheeting and cotton yarn (US Department of State 1967a). A second restriction followed in August 1964, limiting Brazilian yarn imports to 100,000 pounds (45 tons) (US Department of State 1967a). In late 1965, driven by the Vietnam War and the resulting surge in demand for military supplies, the Lyndon Johnson administration (1963-1969) allowed Brazil and other Third World countries, which were non-signatories of the LTA, to significantly exceed quotas for 1965 and 1966 (US Department of State 1967a). As a result, 1966 became a record year for Brazilian textile exports to the United States (Table 2). From 1967 onwards, restrictions were reimposed, though on a limited scale. Initially, Washington continued the Vietnam War–driven policy of not distinguishing between countries that adhered to the Long-Term Arrangement (LTA) and those that did not, such as Brazil (Department of State 1969b).

    Over time, however, the new Richard Nixon administration escalated U.S. protectionist and discriminatory trade measures, setting off a series of difficult and often acrimonious negotiations with the Brazilian government. Prior to this, however, there had been hopes – ultimately dashed – that Nixon’s Washington might be willing to significantly transform its economic relations with Brazil and Latin America.

    From Surprise to Stalemate: The Breakdown of U.S.-Brazil Cotton Textile Talks

    On November 10, 1969, during a ceremony in which New York Governor Nelson Rockefeller presented a long-awaited report on Latin American development, President Richard Nixon unveiled a U.S. proposal for the region. Nixon stated that if wealthy nations failed to grant generalized special entry status to goods produced by less developed countries – particularly nontraditional, higher value-added products – the United States would “open negotiations” with Latin America “to provide [it unilaterally with] preferential access to the U.S. market” (“Nixon abrirá mercado dos EUA aos subdesenvolvidos.” 1969).

    Nixon’s announcement was warmly received by the Brazilian government. Securing preferential access to the world’s largest market would have represented a major achievement for the dictatorship’s export-driven strategy. The problem, however, was that Washington had made similar pledges under the previous administration of Lyndon Johnson (1963-1967), but failed to fulfill them (Loureiro 2021, 146-147). With this record in mind, the Brazilian government sought to rally the region behind a common front to press the United States to honor its commitments.

    In February 1969, Brazil called on other Latin American governments to convene a meeting of the Special Committee for Latin American Coordination (Comissão Especial de Coordenação Latino-americana, CECLA). Originally created in November 1963 to organize the region’s position for UNCTAD I, Brazil contributed to the revival of CECLA in order to forge a common regional stance on economic issues (“O Brasil contra-ataca na conferência do CIES.” 1963; US Department of State 1969a). Three months later, with decisive backing and active involvement from Brazil’s Foreign Ministry – also known as Itamaraty – and other Latin American delegations, a CECLA meeting in Viña del Mar produced recommendations calling for improved access to the U.S. market for Latin American exports, particularly nontraditional commodities and manufactured goods (US Department of State 1969a, 2; “CECLA aprova plano que levará a Nixon.” 1969).

    Yet, once again, the U.S. government failed to live up to its lofty promises. Faced with a growing trade deficit, fiscal imbalances stemming from the protracted Vietnam War, and mounting pressure from U.S. manufacturing lobbies, Nixon ultimately reversed course. Washington not only downgraded Latin America’s economic relevance but also imposed new tariff and non-tariff barriers that further restricted the entry of Latin American goods into the U.S. market (Brands 2008, 252-253).

    The U.S. trade policy on Brazilian textiles clearly exposed the gap between Nixon’s rhetoric and actual practices. Washington pressured Brazil to tighten export controls on cotton textiles by imposing quotas on categories that had remained unrestricted since the implementation of the 1962 Long-Term Agreement on Textiles (LTA). The Brazilian Ambassador to the U.S., Mário Gibson Barbosa, strongly protested what he regarded as a betrayal of U.S. promises. In July 1969, Gibson Barbosa presented an aide-mémoire to Assistant Secretary Charles Meyer in which the Brazilian Ministry of Foreign Relations reaffirmed “its position of non-acceptance of the LTA unilateral determination of ‘market disruption,’” while also warning of “the serious economic and political consequences of such restrictions upon Brazilian exports in particular” (Ministério das Relações Exteriores do Brasil 1969).

    Despite Brazil’s protests, restrictions persisted. Confronted with Washington’s rigid approach, the Médici government opted, in early 1970, to initiate bilateral negotiations aimed at establishing mutually agreed export quotas. Itamaraty’s General Assistant Secretary for Economic Affairs, Ronaldo Costa, emphasized to a U.S. official in Rio that Brazil expected higher quotas than those granted to signatories of the Long-Term Agreement (LTA). When informed that this would not be possible, Ronaldo Costa promptly responded that such a stance conflicted with “President Nixon’s emphasis on a special relationship with Latin America and offers to assist Latin American exports.” Following this exchange, the U.S. Embassy in Rio recommended to the Department of State that the Nixon administration might consider adopting a “more generous” approach toward Brazil (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970a).

    Before formal negotiations on textiles began in Washington, Nixon’s Secretary of Commerce, Maurice Stans, toured Latin America in an effort to ease tensions. During his visit to Brazil in late April 1970, Stans met with Brazil’s Finance Minister, Delfim Netto, who urged Washington to be more flexible on the matter. Delfim argued that Brazil’s growth had often been constrained by balance of payments problems and that the government was now trying to avoid this situation by promoting exports, especially manufacturing goods such as textiles. Delfim stressed to Secretary Stans that U.S. restrictions on Brazilian yarns and fabrics were “blocking sales already contracted and nullifying recent industry investments designed to supply these new customers” (US Department of State 1970a, 1).

    Stans, however, remained unmoved. The U.S. Secretary of Commerce responded to the Brazilian Finance Minister that the majority of Brazil’s exports to the U.S. already entered duty-free and cautioned against “letting the tail wag the dog” in the case of textiles. Washington, he insisted, could not make exceptions for Brazil without setting a precedent that other countries might follow. Instead, Stans advised Delfim to focus on capital-intensive industrial exports, noting that the global market was already being flooded with cheap goods from low-wage Asian economies. The U.S. textile industry, he warned, was losing thousands of jobs each year and needed protection (US Department of State 1970a, 2-4).

    After these inconclusive negotiations, Delfim traveled to Washington in late May 1970 for a renewed effort to resolve the textile conflict. During the visit, the Brazilian Finance Minister met again with Commerce Secretary Maurice Stans and put forward, for the first time, a concrete figure for a quota on Brazil’s textile exports to the United States. Delfim explained that Brazilian textile companies had already registered, or were in the process of negotiating, sales contracts with U.S. importers totaling 250 million square yards (syds) – 209 million square meters (sqm). Considering that Brazil’s peak export volume to the U.S. had reached 96 million syds (80 million sqm) in 1966, Delfim proposed 130 million syds (108 million sqm) as a “starting point” for negotiations, with the goal of reaching “150 million syds [125 million sqm] [as] a reasonable level of restraint.” In response, the U.S. Secretary of Commerce argued that such figures were untenable, as granting them would constitute favoritism toward Brazil compared with countries participating in the Long-Term Agreement on Cotton Textiles (US Department of State 1970b, 1).

    In reality, U.S. government officials were considering figures far lower than the 150 million syds proposed by Delfim. Officials at the Department of Commerce, who had primary negotiating authority in the upcoming talks with the Brazilians (US Department of State 1969b), were contemplating a ceiling of just 60 million syds (50 million sqm) (US Department of State 1970c). Given these positions, a substantial gap in expectations was inevitable. And indeed, that is what transpired. A Brazilian delegation led by Celso Diniz, Counselor at Brazil’s Embassy in Washington, engaged in two weeks of negotiations with U.S. counterparts, from June 8 to 22, 1970. The talks ultimately collapsed when the Brazilian side walked out, frustrated by what they perceived as U.S. intransigence. The U.S. delegation refused to go beyond the 50 million syds (41 million sqm) figure as the baseline for Brazil’s quota (“Rompidas negociações com os EUA.” 1970; US Department of State 1970d).

    To make matters worse, during the negotiation process, Washington decreed additional restrictions on Brazilian textile exports, prompting Delfim Netto, Brazil’s Finance Minister, to lodge a formal protest (US Department of State 1970e). The U.S. Embassy in Rio had previously warned that such a move “would be taken in Brazil as evidence that the U.S. was using extreme pressure tactics on the Brazilian negotiators to reach an agreement” (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970b). Even so, Washington went ahead, a position that only reinforced the Brazilian delegation’s belief that a strong response was necessary to signal that the Nixon administration had overstepped the line (US Department of State 1970f).

    Following the breakdown in talks, the Brazilian government escalated the crisis by halting purchases of U.S. wheat and boosting incentives for textile exporters – openly disregarding Secretary Stans’ position that the Médici administration prioritize the foreign sales of other manufacturing goods (“Brasil suspende a compra de trigo dos EUA” 1970; US Embassy Brasília 1970a). Médici officials also met with representatives of the U.S.-Brazilian Entrepreneurship Council to argue that the collapse of negotiations was the result of U.S. intransigence, reaffirming that Brasília would continue backing textile exports (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970c). In the same vein, Brazilian diplomats reiterated to their U.S. counterparts in Rio that Brazil’s demand was “reasonable”, given Nixon’s early promises that Latin America should be prioritized in trade negotiations with the United States (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970d).

    Brazil’s retaliatory measures only strengthened the resolve of U.S. officials at the Department of Commerce. Referring to Brazil’s boycott against of U.S. wheat sales, Stanley Nehman, Deputy Assistant Secretary for Resources and one of the lead negotiators on the U.S. textile team, emphasized that “we cannot allow ourselves to be placed in the position where threats against our agricultural or other exports are used to blackmail us into higher textile import figures.” If the U.S. fell into this “trap,” he warned, “we will pay a price well beyond textiles” (US Department of State 1970g).

    In the weeks that followed, Nehman’s position prevailed: the United States would not budge. It was up to the Brazilians to give ground and swallow their pride, but not before sending a few signals to Washington. One of them came in the form of a conspicuously political editorial published by O Estado de S. Paulo – one of Brazil’s largest and most prestigious newspaper, and often used by Delfim Netto, Brazil’s Finance Minister, to indirectly send messages to both domestic and international audiences (Loureiro 2021, 151). In this provocative editorial, O Estado posed a rhetorical question: what would it take for “a big and prosperous nation” like the United States to understand that it has “the duty of, through the sacrifice of some of its partial interests,” to ensure that “rising nations” remain in the Western camp, rather than drift toward “neutralism,” which would lead them “inevitably to the Soviet orbit” (“Um reflexo da crise nos EUA.” 1970)? Even if one regards the threat of Brazil shifting its Cold War allegiance from Washington to the Soviet Union as more rhetorical than real – which it surely was –, it is striking to see O Estado advance such a warning to the United States. The unfolding U.S.-Brazil textile dispute confirmed this bitter perception, demonstrating that Washington had indeed relegated Brazil to a low-priority status in U.S. foreign policy.

    Giving Ground to Big Brother: The Wrap-Up of the U.S.-Brazil Textile Talks

    The rise of nationalist sentiment within Brazil’s military regime prompted concerned U.S. and Brazilian entrepreneurs to travel to Washington in an effort to prevent the textile crisis from escalating. Led by former Brazilian Finance Minister Octávio Gouveia de Bulhões, the delegation conveyed that Brazil might be willing to compromise, provided the Nixon administration also demonstrated some flexibility (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970e).

    The prospect of Brazil backing down had already reached the ears of the U.S. Embassy in Rio. A source close to Finance Minister Delfim Netto acknowledged that Brazil could accept a lower figure for the country’s cotton textile export quota, reducing its demand from 150 to 100 million square yards (125 to 83 million square meters) (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970f). If necessary, the source added, the 100 million syds could be phased in over three years, following a formula of 75 million for the first year, 90 for the second, and 100 for the third. But there would be no concession beyond that, the Brazilian source claimed (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970f).

    Carrying these significant concessions with them, the group of U.S. and Brazilian entrepreneurs travelled to Washington in early August 1970, fully expecting to return home bearing some good news. But to no avail. There was no willingness in Washington to increase the proposal for Brazil’s cotton textile quotas (US Department of State 1970h). With no compromises forthcoming from the Nixon administration, the U.S. Embassy in Brazil grew increasingly anxious over the ongoing boycott of U.S. wheat in the Brazilian market, particularly concerning how quickly this important market was “slipping away” (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970g). But the Department of State continued rejecting Brazil’s “75-90-100 million” proposal as unfounded, pointing out that LTA signatories should be treated better than Brazil. Considering the substantial gap in positions, Washington asked its diplomats in Rio whether it would be appropriate to continue discussions with the Brazilians, possibly sending negotiators with full authority to conduct discreet talks with Delfim (US Department of State 1970i).

    To address that question, the U.S. Embassy’s Economic Counselor met in early September with one of Brazil’s key negotiators – José Maria Vilar de Queirós, Head of the International Advisory Department at the Ministry of Finance, operating under Delfim’s authority. Vilar de Queirós agreed to receive the U.S. negotiators but presented a sobering counterproposal – the third Brazil had made to the United States (table 3): to sign a comprehensive textile agreement covering all textile fibers, not just cotton, while establishing a higher set of export limits: 130 million syds (108 million sqm) of Brazilian textiles entering the U.S. market every year (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970h, 1). The U.S. official quickly dismissed the proposal, emphasizing that even the original figure (100 million syds) was already hard for Washington to accept, let alone 130 million, regardless of the agreement’s broader scope (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1970h, 1-2). At that point, it appeared likely the U.S. negotiating team would travel to Brazil only to face another stalemate.

    And then, surprisingly, an agreement was reached days later. With Itamaraty completely sidelined from the negotiation process, Finance Minister Delfim Netto – accompanied by the same José Maria Vilar de Queirós who had insisted that Brazil had already reached the limit of its concessions – signed a deal on September 15, 1970, that set Brazil’s annual quota for cotton textile exports to the United States at 75 million square yards (62 million sqm). The deal excluded other types of textiles and included the standard LTA clause permitting a 5% annual increase over five years, eventually bringing the Brazilian quota to approximately 95 million square yards (79 million sqm) by 1975. Not only did Brazil drop the symbolic 100 million figure, but it also accepted a longer timeframe. With the matter settled, Brazil ended its unofficial embargo on U.S. wheat (US Department of State 1970j).

    Evidence indicates that Brazil’s decision to back down originated from the highest levels of power, most likely from the dictator Médici himself. Weeks after the agreement was signed, U.S. Ambassador William Rountree met with Médici and inferred, based on the tone of their conversation, that the Brazilian general had personally wanted the issue resolved. According to Rountree, the Médici administration “regrets the drifting apart of U.S. and Brazil which occurred earlier and is anxious to do what it can to improve relations.” The U.S. Ambassador also concluded that it was likely due to Médici’s intervention that the Brazilian government demonstrated a willingness “to retreat substantially in the recent cotton textile and wheat situation.” (US Embassy Brasília 1970b).

    The only significant event that occurred between Vilar de Queirós’s seemingly unwavering stance on the morning of September 5, 1970, and the signing of the U.S.-Brazil textile agreement on September 15, 1970, when Brazil’s Finance Minister Delfim Netto agreed to a major scaling back of Brazil’s demands, was the election of the first Marxist president in the history of Western democracies: Salvador Allende, of Chile’s Socialist Party (“Allende vence; Congresso decidirá.” 1970). Given the near-obsession that Allende’s Chile would come to represent for the Brazilian military in terms of national security, and considering how strongly both Médici and Nixon would seek a healthy relationship in the months and years ahead, in order to confront what they saw as a grave subversive threat in South America (Spektor 2009, Simon 2021), it is plausible to assume – although this should be empirically confirmed by future studies – that this event was one of the key factors that led Médici to intervene in September 1970, bringing the U.S.-Brazil textile dispute to a close.

    This does not mean, however, that other actors within the Brazilian government accepted the outcome without drawing significant conclusions about the long-term divergence between Brasília and Washington on economic issues. A few months after the deal was signed, the Brazilian delegation to the Organization of American States (OAS) requested the inclusion of a trade-related item on the agenda for the organization’s upcoming meeting, scheduled for April 1971 in San José, Costa Rica. The move raised concerns within Nixon’s State Department, as Washington feared that Itamaraty intended to introduce issues related to the United Nations Conference on Trade and Development (UNCTAD) in inter-American forums, particularly the goal to transform UNCTAD into a negotiating body capable of producing “binding commitments” (US Department of State 1971a).

    In the days leading up to the OAS Conference in San José, State Department officials met with Ambassador George Maciel, head of the Brazilian delegation to the OAS. A key negotiator in the U.S.–Brazil dispute over soluble coffee, Maciel had become increasingly resentful of U.S. trade protectionism toward Brazilian manufactured goods (Loureiro 2021). Maciel acknowledged Brazil’s frustration with how international organizations were addressing trade-related topics and expressed skepticism about whether trade matters should be handled primarily within the Inter-American system, noting that the most ambitious U.S. economic aid initiative for Latin America, the Alliance for Progress, “was dead, if in fact it ever was alive” (US Department of State 1971b).

    Meanwhile, in Brazil, the U.S. Embassy attempted to press Ronaldo Costa, Itamaraty’s General Assistant Secretary for Economic Affairs, for details on Brazil’s plans for the upcoming OAS conference, but Costa responded with pointed irony. He claimed that Brazil had no complaints, given “the excellent record of mutual cooperation” between Brasília and Washington. Yet, in a somber tone, Costa remarked that “recent agreements [signed by the United States] with us on textiles and soluble coffee were ‘bitter medicine’ forced upon Brazil” (US Embassy Rio de Janeiro 1971a).

    In the end, the Brazilian delegation at the OAS did, at least in part, what the United States had feared: it publicly condemned Washington for failing to support Latin America’s development efforts, although it stopped short of attempting to pass resolutions in support of turning UNCTAD decisions into binding commitments. In a forceful speech, Brazil’s chief delegate to the OAS, George Maciel, exposed a series of contradictions and inconsistencies within the inter-American system that needed to be addressed. Maciel pointed in particular to “the evident incongruity between well-intentioned statements and the selfishly petty actions of protectionist policies” (“Brasil defende união para desenvolvimento.” 1971). Brazil and other poorer countries on the continent, he warned, “have been patient, but as long as this attitude persists, the gap separating developing countries from the industrialized world will continue to grow” (“Brasil defende união para desenvolvimento.” 1971). Although the United States was not mentioned by name, the criticism was sharp enough for everyone to recognize Maciel’s intended target.

    Brazil also advanced a resolution highlighting the need to promote Latin American exports. The proposal explicitly stated that “despite efforts made in recent years, the attempts by the United States to improve its trade relations with other Latin American countries remain insufficient,” and that “the rapid expansion of Latin American exports carries strategic importance for the essential progress toward well-being and peace in the Americas” (“Conferência da OEA se encerra com elogio chileno.” 1971). Of the 23 OAS members, 22 voted in favor of the Brazilian proposal; the United States was the only one to abstain.

    Months after the OAS Conference in San José, the same top Brazilian diplomat, George Maciel, argued in a confidential report to Itamaraty that Brazil should understand from that point onward that it no longer enjoyed a “special relationship” with Washington, and that the Inter-American system had lost its “validity.” According to Maciel, while it would not be advisable for Brazil to abandon Pan-Americanism entirely – the Pan-American card should be played “tactically”, he argued –, the Brazilian government should never again succumb to the “illusion” that the system remained a priority in U.S. foreign policy (Loureiro 2021, 160)

    Even if one cannot establish a direct link between the increasingly independent foreign policy that the Brazilian dictatorship would pursue from the mid-1970s onward and the trade clashes of the early 1970s, certain patterns are evident. The public condemnation of the United States in multilateral forums, such as the OAS Conference in San José in June 1971, and the private interactions of key Brazilian diplomats at the time suggest that the outcome of the major trade disputes at the beginning of the Médici administration, including but not limited to the textile dispute, had a strong influence on some of the regime’s leading policymakers. The recurrence of these arguments later in the decade by Itamaraty indicates that these effects were by no means confined to the short term.

    Conclusion

    One of the most striking developments of the Cold War in Latin America was how ferocious anticommunist military regimes, brought to power with the assistance and diplomatic backing of the United States, saw their relationships with Washington gradually deteriorate, to the point where geopolitical and ideological alliances were effectively turned upside down by the late 1970s. Brazil provides a prime example. This shift reconnected the country’s foreign policy with the pre-1964 view that the North-South divide should serve as its primary reference point in international affairs. Although Brazil never fully embraced a Third Worldist agenda and consistently presented itself as a bridge to the wealthy, anticommunist West (Selcher 1978, ch. 1), it gradually aligned more closely with the project of the Third World than with the Inter-American system led by Washington (Hurrell 2013, 296-299; Spektor 2004, 201).

    Of course, this was neither a specifically Latin American nor, much less, a uniquely Brazilian phenomenon. The changing structures of the international system, such as the rise of postcolonial states and the disillusionment with the actual outcomes of developmentalist programs (Brands 2008), contributed to reshaping how underdeveloped and developing nations perceived their place in the world. But this does not diminish the fact that the Brazilian military regime, one of Washington’s key allies in Latin America’s Cold War during the mid-1960s, found itself criticizing how the United States waged (or failed to wage) the Cold War globally. By the late 1970s, the Brazilian government went so far as to argue that Washington was disregarding the specificity of the anticommunist struggle in less developed countries, demonstrating that Brazil’s critique of U.S. economic policies did not imply that anticommunism was unimportant to the regime (Roriz and Loureiro 2024, 15–16).

    Although scholars of Brazilian foreign policy and international history have emphasized the significance and features of the transformation in Brazil’s engagement with the international system, we still lack a clear understanding of how the individual pieces of this vast puzzle fit together over the 21 years of the military regime. This is particularly true for the first decade of the Brazilian dictatorship, when U.S.-Brazilian geopolitical and strategic perceptions and interests still aligned in many ways. The Médici administration (1969–1974) exemplifies this paradigm, as Brasília grew closer to the U.S. in pursuing Cold War objectives in Latin America, especially regarding destabilization efforts in neighboring countries like Bolivia, Uruguay, and Chile (Simon 2021). At the same time, however, significant differences emerged between the two nations, particularly on economic issues, with trade becoming a major point of contention (Bandeira 2011, ch. 7). Brazilian officials and diplomats increasingly recognized that Washington’s rhetoric supporting Brazil’s development did not match its actions, which discriminated against and restricted access to the U.S. market for Brazilian goods, specially manufactured products critical to Brazil’s development project (Hurrell 2013, 232-233).

    As this article showed, a central element in this complex puzzle of bilateral economic differences was the U.S.-Brazil textile dispute of the late 1960s and early 1970s. Contrary to Richard Nixon’s early promises to grant preferential access for Latin American manufactured goods to the U.S. market, Washington imposed trade barriers that effectively blocked Brazilian textile products from entering the United States. In response, Brazil targeted U.S. exports – particularly wheat – while simultaneously providing greater incentives for domestic textile exports, openly disregarding prior U.S. recommendations. These measures illustrate a coordinated strategy across multiple sectors to protect Brazil’s economic interests. Ultimately, however, Washington compelled Brasília to accept a lower export quota, despite the small relative significance of Brazilian textiles in the U.S. market; Brazil also rescinded sanctions on U.S. wheat exports. The case served as a wake-up call for Brazilian officials, especially high-level diplomats in Itamaraty, about the limits of advancing Brazil’s economic interests alongside Washington within the inter-American order. This opened the door for the regime to embrace a moderate reformist agenda for the global economic order – a Third Worldist agenda in all but name, even if it fell short of the more radical positions of the poorest Third World nations.

    As Ambassador George Maciel emphasized to his counterparts in Itamaraty immediately after the textile dispute, following Washington’s actions, Brazil could no longer fall prey to the illusion of Pan-Americanism. The second half of the 1970s would confirm this paradigm with particular clarity, bringing together what seemed impossible when the dictatorship came to being: a universal, pragmatic agenda in world affairs, yet without losing the regime’s strong anticommunist bias.
