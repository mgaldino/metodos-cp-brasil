Intertwined Securities: The Dilemma Between Environmental Security and Energy Security on Nuclear Power Plants Wastewater Release

Seguranças transversais: o dilema entre segurança ambiental e segurança energética no descarte de águas residuais de usinas nucleares

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The disposal of wastewater from the Fukushima Nuclear Power Plant into the Pacific Ocean, with the approval of the International Atomic Energy Agency, began in 2023 and has sparked a series of questions from some countries about ‘security.’ In International Relations, the use of nuclear energy gains greater notoriety when studied from a military perspective. However, more recently, it is possible to note that issues not directly related to military affairs have been dealt with under this scope, being described as ‘risks’ or ‘threats’; this approach is defined by the Copenhagen School (1985) as ‘securitisation’. The aim of this work is to analyse whether cases of dumping radioactive wastewater from nuclear power plants could be securitised, limiting, or preventing other countries’ access to this type of energy matrix. The research was based on the contributions of Securitisation Theory and the debates on Energy Security and Environmental Security. By presenting and problematising the possibilities for managing and disposing of nuclear waste, the article concludes that underdeveloped or inadequate disposal mechanisms can generate serious risks for the international community, as well as limiting other countries’ access to nuclear technology for generating electricity, undermining the search for a balance between environmental and energy securities.

    Key words
    nuclear energy; securitisation; energy; Japan; Fukushima; environment; wastewater; power plant

    Resumo

    O descarte de águas residuais da Usina Nuclear de Fukushima no Oceano Pacífico, iniciado em 2023 com aval da Agência Internacional de Energia Atômica, despertou uma série de questionamentos de alguns países sobre ‘segurança’. Nas Relações Internacionais, a utilização da energia nuclear ganha maior notoriedade quando estudada sob a ótica militar; contudo, mais recentemente, é possível perceber que temas não diretamente relacionados às questões militares têm sido tratados sob tal escopo, sendo desenhados como ‘riscos’ ou ‘ameaças’; essa abordagem é definida pela Escola de Copenhagen (1985) como ‘securitização’. Este trabalho tem como objetivo analisar se os casos de despejo de resíduos de água radioativa das usinas nucleares poderiam ser securitizados, limitando ou impedindo o acesso de outros países a esse tipo de matriz energética. A pesquisa apoiou-se nas contribuições da Teoria da Securitização e nos debates sobre Segurança Energética e Segurança Ambiental. Com a apresentação e problematização das possibilidades de gestão e descarte de resíduos nucleares, o trabalho conclui que mecanismos de descarte subdesenvolvidos ou inadequados podem gerar graves riscos à comunidade internacional, além de limitar o acesso de outros países à tecnologia nuclear para geração de energia elétrica, prejudicando a busca pelo equilíbrio entre segurança ambiental e energética.

    Palavras-chave
    energia nuclear; securitização; energia; Japão; Fukushima; meio ambiente

    Introduction

    In August 2023, the Japanese government announced that the country would start later that month to release Fukushima’s nuclear power plant wastewater into the Pacific Ocean. The International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA), after a detailed study, approved Japan’s plans for this release stating that it was in accordance with the Agency’s safety standards. Still, this decision was not well received by the neighbouring countries: China responded that it would ban the imports of Japanese seafood; in South Korea, protests all over the nation expressed the population’s concerns on the safety of the food consumed; the Philippines declared its trust on science and respect for IAEA’s decision but emphasised the importance of preserving and protecting marine life.

    In March 2011, an earthquake followed by a tsunami struck the city of Fukushima, north-eastern Japan, affecting the nuclear power plant Fukushima Daiichi, deactivating the energy supply and the cooling system of three nuclear reactors. Due to the high temperatures, those reactor’s nucleus melted, releasing large amounts of radiation and starting a nuclear accident classified as level 7 in the international scale.

    This situation took a long time to be controlled, since besides the need to cool down the reactors the main concern was to prevent the releasing of radioactive substances; the crisis was contained only in December that year, and, although it has caused neither diseases nor deaths, almost 100 thousand citizens had to be dislocated to other regions permanently (World Nuclear Association 2024a).

    Just over a decade after this event, the Japanese decision to release the nuclear wastewater into the Ocean raises some debates: it is estimated that almost one million tonnes of water will be dumped for 30 years and once the nuclear residues of said water are exposed it will be impossible to control its effects (Brown 2023). According to Khadka (2023), Japan justifies this action by alleging that the tanks where the wastewater is stored, once emptied, could be used to the development of other facilities envisioning the safe and complete decommissioning of Fukushima Daiichi.

    The dumping that occurred in August 2023 was the first of a total of four that were planned until March 2024. The greatest problem of this action is the impossibility of eliminating Tritium (hydrogen isotope generated during the production of nuclear energy) from those waters since there are still no technology that allows the total removal of this element; therefore, the water is diluted and gradually released into high seas since the lowest the concentration of this substance, the lowest is the environmental impact.

    In addition to concerns with the dumping of radioactive waste into the Ocean, another problem is the Japanese geological instability: in January 2024, there were several earthquakes in the country, some with considerable consequences, most notably the one that occurred in the first day of that year, off the west coast, with 7.6 magnitude, leaving at least 180 people dead and many others missing (Komae, McGill and Kageyama 2024). This is an important fact when it’s known that Japan has been planning the disposal of nuclear waste in geological repositories deeper than 300 meters (Fujiyama and Kaku 2023); however, as safely as these facilities can be developed, they are also at great risk of being affected by earthquakes and tsunamis, just as happened in 2011 (Yang 2023).

    Another factor that complexifies this situation even more is the untrust regarding the dumping of nuclear wastewater into the high seas due to the lack of transparency of the Japanese government and of the enterprise responsible for the operation of the power plant on how this release will happen. Furthermore, other regional actors have not been involved in the discussions on how to establish the manner of dumping, therefore this decision was taken without the consent of the parties (Rich and Ueno 2023). Thus, the neighbouring countries’ decision to retaliate Japan’s economic sector raised concerns on part of the Japanese people, as due to its archipelagic nature the fishing industry has always been fundamental to the economy and has been trying to recover since the nuclear crises in 2011 (Kim 2023). In 2022, the fishing sector exports totalised almost US$2.39b, a 26% increase in comparison with 2021 (Tsukada 2023); however, in 2024 the numbers of the sector dropped by 7.5% (US$2.4b) compared to the previous year mostly as a result of the Chinese ban that started in August 2023 (Chase 2025).

    The Japanese case incites an interesting debate about the comprehension on environmental and energy security concepts: in recent years, the perception on the negative effects of climate change, global warming, rising pollution rates, and rising sea levels—which harms island countries—, has led to the establishment of international compromises envisioning the achievement of certain goals to mitigate such problems, as the United Nations’ 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, set in 2015.

    Considering energy production as one of the core aspects that contributes the most to generating greenhouse gases, responsible for the rise in global temperatures, the permanent quest for sustainable energy sources points to nuclear power as an important option to meet the demand currently covered by fossil fuels. The low cost, low emission of polluting gases, and the use of highly efficient materials make nuclear sources a more accessible and possible choice. Despite that, there is a dilemma regarding its use: on the one hand, nuclear energy can possibly guarantee energy security; on the other, it can harm and endanger environmental security since the effects of releasing nuclear waste into nature, as in the Japanese case, are neither totally known nor controllable.

    So, what is the best way forward? How to completely abandon fossil fuels and take the dive with nuclear power if, by doing that, human lives could be seriously damaged by the absorption of radioactive residues? Is it possible that developed countries that already are endowed with nuclear technology and have more resources to reach for new energy sources and suppliers, could restrict access to nuclear power and securitise the issue?

    This article aims to discuss security concepts, including the environmental and energetic issues present in the use of nuclear power, the management of its residues, and the possible securitisation of this matrix. To achieve that, a bibliographical analysis was carried out based on treaties, books, articles, and news produced about each of these subjects.

    Briefly, it can be concluded that the underdevelopment or inadequacy of measures to prevent the risks of the nuclear waste disposal can produce severe damage to the international community and limit the access of other countries to the nuclear technology, restraining the production of electricity and more sustainable methods to do so, and also jeopardising progress in the search for a balance between energy production and the environment.

    Expanding the security concept: environmental security

    According to Basso (2023: 10), until the 1960s, the environment was considered as an unlimited source of resources that could be used based on the human will, an approach that originated from the Enlightenment ideas. As the author says, the first treaty proposals envisioning the usage of natural resources had three main reasons: regulating the exploration of ‘common’ use among different countries; reducing cross-border risks and damages from ‘dangerous’ activities, and, finally, aesthetic preservation.

    Debates on strategies to identify, prevent, and mitigate the risks of reckless environmental exploitation only gained prominence in the late 1960s and early 1970s, following major pollution outbreaks, shipwrecks, fires, contaminations, and the recognition of carcinogenic substances in the chemical industry. The Stockholm Conference (1972) was one of the main milestones of the progress on these issues and, as Basso (2023: 18-28) also states, broadened the comprehension about the environment, turning it the basis for the nations’ ‘development.’

    In the International Relations (IR) field, studies on the environment have been deepened, widening debates and research especially in relation to the ideas of ‘Security.’ For Baylis (2020), this is a much-contested concept, with many variations, being commonly defined on military terms by the ‘traditional’ theories. However, for the author, there are many other forms of comprehending it, ranging from the individual level, going through the national, and reaching the ‘global.’

    In recent years, as pointed out by Asif (2021), environmental issues, such as the global warming, have proved to be serious challenges for humanity: several Nobel Prize winners state that the climate change is the major threat to societies, overlapping with other problems as diseases, nuclear wars, and terrorism (Asif 2021: 2). In this regard, some IR authors search for manners of readapting the comprehension of the term ‘security,’ including new subjects besides the ones already traditionally related to the study field.

    According to Barry Buzan (1983), for example, it is necessary to enlarge the understanding of what the term covers, by considering the ever more interdependent nature of the International System. In this sense, the author affirms that it is important to have a vision that includes economic, social, and environmental affairs, besides the military ones. Baylis (2020) also highlights thinkers such as Kofi Annan and Amitav Acharya who bring the human being in the centre of the debates, presenting a new concept, the ‘human security.’ Other authors whose contributions comprise the Constructivism theoretical scope, understand IR and the structure of the International System as a result of the social relations, diverse perceptions, expectations, and various knowledges, which can be modified over time and according to the changes in social dynamics (Tickner 1992; Wendt 1992, 1999; Adler and Barnett 1996).

    For Peoples and Vaughan-Williams (2021: 133), from the 1980s onwards, ‘environmental degradation and resource scarcity have become increasingly incorporated under the rubric of security by national governments, the media and academics.’ The authors also present many debates that connect resource scarcity and problems related to the environment as focal points of possible conflicts and increased violence, such as wars over water, food, and environmental refugees.

    Asif defines ‘environmental security’ as:

    [...] [T]he state of protection of vital interests of the individual, society, and natural environment from threats resulting from anthropogenic and natural impacts on the environment [22]. Environmental security simply implies striking a balance between the dynamic human–environment interactions. The environment is one of the most transnational issues, and its security is an important dimension of sustainable development. Environmental security is also regarded to be closely linked to national security, owing to the dynamics and interconnections among humans and natural resources. [...] Ensuring environmental security, therefore, means guarding against environmental degradation to preserve or protect human, material, and natural resources at scales ranging from global to local. (Asif 2021: 5)

    Following Polyakova and Gorina (2021), the rise of the term ‘sustainable development’ has redefined the very idea of what could be considered ‘development,’ becoming a pattern. As the authors state:

    In general terms, sustainable development is a concept that is intended to combine economic, social, environmental and social development and environmental security. [...] In order to address environmental concerns, one needs to reflect on the role of the environment in the development of a sustainable society and its impact on human health, well-being and livelihoods. In this context, we consider it important to raise awareness of the need for national governments to implement a sustainable development strategy with a focus on environmental safety and protection. (Polyakova and Gorina 2021: 2)

    Asif (2021) points out that in this ‘age of sustainable development,’ the energy sector is one of the crucial factors to be debated and rethought, as—despite its contributions to society’s industrial and technological progress—it is the main driver of global warming. The energy production, distribution, and consumption, especially when fossil fuels are used (coal, crude oil, and natural gas) generate harmful substances to the environment, with emphasis on greenhouse gases. In addition, for Asif (2021: 2-3), the devastating effects of climate change will be sensed, above all, in the developing countries, which have limited financial resources to alleviate the problems and rebuild destructed areas.

    According to Klare,

    The more complex and productive a society, the greater its need for energy; without adequate supplies of basic fuels, a complex society cannot maintain a high rate of industrial output, provide a decent standard of living to its citizens or defend itself against competing powers. (Klare 2018: 498)

    Considering this reality of growing energy use and production, this theme is pertinent to discussions following the ‘energy security’ perspective.

    Expanding the security concept: energy security

    From the 1970s, energy security began to be included in academic and political debates being defined as ‘assurance of sufﬁcient energy supplies to permit the national economy to function in a politically acceptable manner.’ (Azzuni and Breyer 2018: 3).

    As Klare (2018) stated, the concept has no absolute definition and it may encompass various matters, ranging from production and delivery of the States’ needed energetic resources, through the problems of diversifying suppliers, to the quest for alternative methods of energy production, especially the ones that could meet the demand while simultaneously helping to diminish environmental pollution.

    Sovacool (2012: 52) defines ‘energy security’ as the ‘equitably providing available, affordable, reliable, efficient, environmentally benign, proactively governed, and socially acceptable energy services to end users.’ The author divides this concept in four major categories: availability, accessibility, efficiency, and management. It is worth highlighting thoughts on accessibility since Sovacool (2012) observes that the term is not exclusively related to fuel prices, but rather to the possibility to access or to have modern facilities so that the energy produced reaches the consumers easier—an opportunity that many societies in developing countries cannot afford. In the author’s words:

    [C]onsider the lives of the 1.4 billion people in the world who have little to no access to electricity. As a result of ‘electricity deprivation’ or ‘energy poverty’, millions of women and children spend significant amounts of time searching for firewood, and then burning either it or charcoal indoors to heat their homes and prepare their meals, emitting localized pollution into the living space (Sovacool 2012: 54).

    Amid the search for more sustainable energy sources, there is the option of nuclear power.

    The discovery and use of this kind of system are more commonly linked to military studies referring to the peak of World War II (1939-1945), with the devastation of the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki by two nuclear bombs and its subsequent effects which lasted for decades after the end of the conflict. Those effects established some consensus on limiting the countries’ military actions, considering the great capability of the recently discovered material to cause damages that could not be fully managed. Thus, the very creation of the International Atomic Agency (1957) was one of the steps towards better controlling the use of radioactive substances.

    Nevertheless, although nuclear energy was employed to warlike ends, it was also used for civilian purposes: the growing industrialisation and the development of societies over the years deepened the need for energy production. In June 1954, the then Soviet Union inaugurated the first nuclear power plant to generate electricity through nuclear reactors, aiming to supply the city of Obninsk (Nóbrega 2005). Since this action, many other similar initiatives flourished all over the world, including in Brazil, which has the plants of Angra I, II, and III (still under construction) contributing along with other sources for the energy consumption in the country.

    According to IAEA, currently, nuclear power plants are responsible for generating almost 10% of the worlds’ electricity and can reach 20% in developed economies (IAEA n.d.). The use of nuclear energy was also encouraged by the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons which in its fourth article expresses the right of States to utilize it for peaceful purposes, as research, health applications, energy production and others (NPT 1968)1.

    Alwaeli and Mannheim (2022) observes that nuclear power is one of the possibilities for low-cost, clean, and sustainable energy that can compose a varied portfolio along with wind, solar and biofuels. Furthermore, the authors assert that the nuclear source could permanently replace fossil fuels, due to its low energy consumption, low cost of materials, high efficiency, and non-emission of polluting gases.

    In this perspective, Guitarrari, Couto and Fernandes (2024) point out that nuclear power was the world’s fourth major source of electricity in 2022 and its use varied from 10% to 20% throughout the planet. Regarding to the emission of polluting gases, the authors compare the power production from nuclear sources with others, as shown in Figure 1.

The authors also highlight that nuclear power plants can operate continuously, reaching almost 90% of their total capacity.

    However, one of the main challenges of dealing with this type of technology is managing the disposal of radioactive residues, a problem that will be discussed in the next section.

    Managing the disposal of radioactive waste from nuclear power plants

    Fossil fuels are the main source of emission of greenhouse gases, contributing to global warming. In this sense, the choice for nuclear power as a matrix of energy may be aligned with international efforts directed to environmental protection or linked to the search for other options due to possible limitations (territorial, financial, among others) on access to fossil fuels.

    Nuclear power plants emit polluting gases in derisory quantities when compared to other sources; nonetheless, the nuclear matrix produces dangerous and hazardous residues that may affect the environment in a much more complex way (Alwaeli and Mannheim 2022; Lana and Almeida 2016). Each State is responsible for developing policies and strategies on how to deal with the radioactive waste generated on the course of the State’s different activities, while agencies as the IAEA must provide support to ensure that this kind of energy is used and disposed of safely and efficiently (Ojovan and Steinmetz 2022).

    According to Corkhill and Hyatt (2018: viii), nuclear residues are ‘the radioactive by-product from nuclear power generation, nuclear weapons and medical isotope production’ and fall under the types of effluents most difficult to manage: due to its high levels of radioactivity, it must be safely isolated from the human life and from the environment until it is no longer harmful.

    For Rubbo and the Associação Brasileira de Engenharia Sanitária e Ambiental (1996: 4), ‘radioactive waste is the fission products resulting from the use of uranium or plutonium in nuclear reactors and nuclear fuel units that are irradiated when they are reused.’ The author states that those residues can be in liquid, solid or gaseous form; emit alpha, beta and gamma particles and have half-lives that can vary from minutes to billions of years. After losing the capacity of generating energy in an economically viable way, these materials must be discarded.

    Ojovan and Steinmetz (2022) assert that the disposal is the insertion of the toxic waste in some sort of facility, aiming to hinder and avoid any contact between the biosphere and the highly radioactive substances, especially natural water bodies. For the authors, this residues management:

    [C]omprises all administrative and operational activities involved in the handling, pre-treatment, treatment, conditioning, transport, storage, and disposal of radioactive waste. [...] Are directed to organising the disposal in such a way that even in the very far (remote) future the probability of any interference of radionuclides with the biosphere is practically absent or minimised to a negligible degree if potentially envisaged (Ojovan and Steinmetz 2022: 2).

    In 1970, the IAEA categorised the types of nuclear residues, establishing them as Low-level Waste (LLW), Intermediate-level Waste (ILW), and High-level Waste (HLW). These designations are used to indicate different levels of radiation from the substances, even so, they do not establish defined quantities of each one. In a practical manner, to categorise the elements, it is necessary to observe their half-life (IAEA 1970).

    The management of this waste can be done in two forms: storage or disposal. The first one can be done in wet or dry facilities: in the wet ones, the residues are stored in racks inserted in deep pools of water to reduce the heat exchange of the fuels and create a layer of protection from the radiation. In dry storage, the substances are loaded into stainless steel canisters that are inserted into hulls and transferred to vertical or horizontal storage modules (World Nuclear Association 2022; Eletrobras Eletronuclear n.d.). Both methods are indicated for dealing with highly radioactive elements.

    In relation to disposal, the World Nuclear Association (2024b) discusses some possibilities that may vary according to the level of radiation of the elements: among the most common there are the near-surface nuclear waste disposal—at ground level or in caves below ground level—and the deep geological disposal. In the first case, the residues are inserted in sort of vaults that are waterproofed and covered with topsoil; drainage and ventilation systems may be incorporated, as shown in Figure 2.

In the second case, the disposal occurs in dozens of metres from the surface, with the rejects’ repositories situated in even more deep caves that may be accessed through ‘shafts,’ as shown in Figure 3.

    Finland and Sweden are examples of countries that have adopted this model. In both cases, these facilities may be affected by climate change: the ones near the surface are prone to erosion and the deep cave ones, to glaciation. They are therefore only suitable for short-lived LLW and ILW, limited to 30 years.

    The deep geological disposal is recommended to long-lived ILW and HLW as it demonstrates better security due to its natural and engineered barriers, which creates layers of isolation for the disposed materials into stable geological formations. This is the most chosen method among countries and the waste vault is surrounded by elements such as clay, cement, or salt, creating another layer of protection. This approach is limited to underground repositories or coastal regions at depths ranging from 250 metres to 1000 metres. An example of this type of repository can be seen in Figure 4.

    As Lersow and Waggit (2020) state, to dispose of the waste pursuing this kind of strategy, it is necessary to follow various requirements and precautions such as proving the suitability of the chosen location and developing long-term safety procedures. For these authors, the main drawbacks of this disposal method are limitation of the ability to observe possible changes in the expected results, delay in perceiving the impact of geological movements that may occur and sites that prove to be unsuitable over time and cannot be reversed. Another factor highlighted is that the reversibility—the capacity of a future change in managing the waste—of HLW must be guaranteed for around 500 years, which is a challenge for the financial feasibility considering the limitations presented.

    For liquids, there are also other manners of dumping, such as deep well injection in layers of underground rocks, release as vapor, and liberation as hydrogen gas into the atmosphere. The first one demands that the layer is porous and permeable enough to accommodate the injection, in addition to having other layers of natural deposits above and below to act as barriers. This method may be used for any kind of nuclear waste that can be transformed into aqueous or sludgy solutions; Russia and the United States have implemented that kind of treatment (World Nuclear Association 2024b). However, recent research show that this method of waste disposal may cause earthquakes, as shown in Figure 5.

    In the two other waste disposal approaches, release as vapour and release as hydrogen gas into the atmosphere, the wastewater must be properly treated before being released. These methods are used mostly when the reject is Tritium, a hydrogen isotope that is very difficult to separate. If the disposal is not done properly, these approaches may cause damages as the entrance of the wastewater into the hydrological cycle, affecting human life and the natural environment. Besides, once the vapor is released, it is very challenging to control the level of radiation exposed in the atmosphere and its subsequent effects (Cheng 2023).

    Over the years other forms of dumping have been researched alongside efforts pursuing international regulation and standardisation to better control nuclear residues. This evolution will be addressed next, focusing on disposal at sea.

    International treaties and the disposal of nuclear waste at sea

    As presented by Dominique Calmet (1989), for centuries the sea was used as a means of disposing of many remains and effluents generated from human actions. The first disposal of nuclear waste at sea occurred in 1946 in the North Atlantic about 80 km off California’s coast. Until the year of the author’s research publication (1989), Calmet enhanced that LLW residues (strontium-90m, caesium-137, iron-55, cobalt-58, cobalt-60, iodine-125, carbon-14, and tritium) had been discharged in almost 50 locations in the northern Atlantic and Pacific oceans; yet, until that moment, no HLW had been proved to have been discharged at sea (Calmet 1989: 47).

    Nuclear residue disposal at sea followed the reasoning that the radioactivity would be diluted into the water and would have low concentration, therefore not affecting marine life. This perspective was challenged, and, during the decade of 1950, became one of the main themes debated along the United Nations Assemblies. In 1958, the first UN Conference on the Law of the Sea established four conventions and one protocol aiming to regulate the use of marine spaces and the exploitation of their resources, named the Convention on the Territorial Sea and the Contiguous Zone. One of the conventions dealt with the high seas and included the need that measures had to be adopted to prevent the pollution caused by the disposal of nuclear waste (United Nations 1958; Treves 2008).

    In 1972, the Convention on the Prevention of Marine Pollution by Dumping of Wastes and Other Matters (London Convention) established the individual and collective ‘control of all sources of pollution of the marine environment’ from ‘vessels, aircraft, platforms or other man-made structures at sea,’ aiming to prevent any kind of release of substances harmful to human and marine lives or that could interfere with the legitimate use of the sea.

    The document entered into force in 1975 and defined three categories: (1) material prohibited from being dumped in the ocean, (2) those which require a special permit to be dumped, and (3) other wastes and matter requiring only a general permit. The radioactive wastes and other radioactive matter were included in categories 1 and 2; the IAEA would also have the responsibility to determine which types would be prohibited and the possibility of issuing special permits for the materials not included into this category (Van As and Forster 1979: 25).

    Although the types of residues had been categorised in the 1970s, the IAEA did not intend to establish ‘national regulations’ but aimed to use these definitions to improve the communication among specialists who worked in the nuclear energy industry. For the agency, national regulation should be carried out by countries individually or collectively and it was not its responsibility to do so (IAEA 1970).

    In 1982, the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) was published and, among other aspects, standardised rights and obligations of States aiming to regulate the use of the sea, including issues of marine pollution and accountability of signatories (UNCLOS 1982). As enhanced by Kusuma and Putra (2024), UNCLOS made possible that countries affected by pollution or any kind of contamination caused by another could receive compensations from the responsible party, therefore fostering the commitment of nations to avoid disasters and safeguard the maritime environment.

    Despite the efforts to regulate State’s actions, as seen in the United Nations Responsibility of States for Internationally Wrongful Acts (2001) that attempts to establish legal consequences and reparations in case States causes problems to others, Black-Branch and Dieter (2016) highlight that the accountability system is not fully established and is pervaded with controversies. Regarding the radioactive waste, the authors point out that ‘This issue is far from being settled on a global scale. [...] There is a need for international cooperation to ensure compliance with such standards.’ (Black-Branch and Dieter 2016: 14). Even with the existence of international rules, some States may ignore them due to the lack of relevant accountability mechanisms, a situation that may cause diplomatic issues or jeopardise other countries, usually the developing ones.

    The Japanese case provokes challenging uncertainties, creating risks that could run out of control and significantly impact at local and global levels, in addition to restricting the access to nuclear energy by securitising the issue. This possibility will be discussed below.

    Energy securitisation?

    In the discussions on the concept of ‘security,’ Leboeuf and Broughton (2008) note that, for many decades, issues such as climate change and diseases (HIV, influenza, bird flu) were treated at national level, with specific approaches by the countries in a very technical and ‘secondary’ vision. Nevertheless, more recently, these subjects have taken over international news and forums. For the authors, this development brought the environment and health as part of security agendas and, according to their words:

    From the moment an issue is perceived to have potentially negative implications on “our” security – because it results in too many deaths, or inflicts too much damage, for instance, it takes on increased significance and importance; we will be more inclined to devote part of our limited resources to our own protection against this perceived threat (Leboeuf and Broughton 2008: 3).

    From the authors’ perspective, these issues have been ‘securitised.’ In other words, they have been treated by state actors, or actors with a broad capacity of influencing societies, as questions of great ‘risk’ or ‘threats’ (Buzan, Waever and Wilde 1998). ‘Securitisation’ is a theory developed by the Copenhagen School (1985) and presents that something not necessarily of military nature can be designed as a security issue and be treated as such.

    According to Buzan, Waever and Wilde (1998), the securitisation of some subjects happens through ‘speech acts’, in which key political actors begin to perceive as a threat something that was not previously seen as such. The audience believes that the issue must be dealt as a security matter and accepts that emergency measures should be taken even if they surpass rules that could not be ignored in other circumstances. The authors point out that ‘the task is not to assess some objective threat that “really” endanger some subject to be defended or secured; rather, it is to understand the processes of constructing a shared understanding of what is to be considered and collectively responded to as a threat’ (Buzan, Waever and Wilde 1998: 26). For Yim (2022: 2-3), ‘Safety is a basic human need’ and ‘because of the subjective value judgments involved, achieving safety or agreeing on what is safe becomes a very difficult task.’

    Regarding environmental issues, Peoples and Vaughan-Williams (2021) present that they can also be securitised. Citing Robert Kaplan’s ‘The Coming Anarchy’ (1994), they point out that the effects of climate change—water scarcity, pollution, deforestation, and rising sea levels — were already being seen in Global South countries as a national security problem. Therefore, according to the authors, Global North countries must treat the environment as a central matter for national security since those issues will severely affect them—such as food shortages and refugee influx.

    In relation to energy issues, although they are connected to the environment as their sources originate there, they are also connected to securitisation processes. An example is the invasion of Iraq by the United States (2003) under the guise of the ‘War on Terror’: many experts state that despite that, in discourse, the USA claimed to ‘release’ the country and put an end to ‘terrorism’, one of its main objectives was to control the oil fields of the Middle East and the allied petro-monarchies (Dagorn 2022: 73).

    Within this reasoning, other matters related to energy production and its sources could be securitised in a direct or indirect manner. As presented by Barnett (2001), the world is seen as an ecosystem and not only as isolated countries/individuals. This view may be used as justification to securitise the energy produced in nuclear power plants, especially when the site is in a country with a complicated background when it comes to managing chemical residues, as is the Japanese case.

    In the 1950s, the petrochemical company Chisso Corporation operated in Minamata and dumped around 27 tons of mercury in the city’s bay. The seafood had been contaminated and was later consumed by the people, causing many cases of intoxication. In 1956, the first case of poisoning by mercury was diagnosed, and almost 900 people died and more than 2 million were left with permanent symptoms (McCurry 2006). Still to this day, nearly 70 years after the first occurrence, there are legal actions underway seeking reparations for the victims and their families (Yamaguchi 2023).

    At nuclear power plants, research centres and radiopharmaceutical laboratories, the water is used as temporary or permanent storage of radioactive by-products, acting as a means of cooling materials or as a barrier to the radiation. Regarding the Fukushima power plant, Zhiyi (2022) notes that Japan was already preparing to release its wastewater since 2016, and many experts indicated different safe possibilities to do so, considering that some of the substances may pollute rivers and underground waters (Ma, Shen, Tong and Wang 2023).

    Tsui and Chi (2025) highlight the methods that had been indicated to Japan and its estimated costs: deep well injection (US$122m), release as vapor (US$238m), release as hydrogen gas into the atmosphere (US$680m), underground storage (US$1.65b) and release at sea (US$24m). The country chose the last one as it is the cheapest; in this kind of release, the residues are dumped in direct dispersion (after the element’s half-life or near to it) or in repositories that have protection against corrosion and that sink undamaged. Close to the half-life, the radionuclides may decay to the bottom of the sea, along with the sediments (World Nuclear Association 2024b).

    Carvalho (2012) argues that there is not a final decision regarding dumping of nuclear by-products, and, in the HLW case, continued exposure to radiation may endanger human health and the environment. Zhiyi (2022: 91) asserts that by opting to release nuclear wastewater into the oceans, Japan happens to be ‘transferring their own responsibility to all mankind, which is a very irresponsible behaviour.’

    Li and Wang (2023: 2) present that the many risk analyses conducted on this method of dumping indicate severe damages to marine life and local economy, which impacts not only the Fukushima region, but also other nations and populations in the immediate area. According to the authors, there will be ‘irreversible damage, multiple-faceted safety hazards, and worldwide consequences.’

    Victoria de Jesus (2021) studies this case as a violation to the UNCLOS (ratified by the Japanese government in 1996): as a signatory, Tokyo must adapt to the framework on marine and maritime activities that it has established. Otherwise, other States may carry out law enforcement through regulations or take ‘necessary measures’ for the implementation and observance of these rules. For the author, the Japanese decision on releasing nuclear wastewater into the ocean with Tritium residues violates UNCLOS’ article 1922, the general obligation to protect and preserve the marine environment, and article 2073, which institutes the compromise to protect it from possible land-based sources of pollution.

    While there is international disagreement over this matter, the IAEA explained that the action is consistent with established standards and has ‘negligible radiological impact to people and the environment’ (IAEA 2023a). Currently, there are almost 32 countries with nuclear plants for energy production: countries with the largest number of nuclear reactors are the United States (92), France (56) and China (54, and about to conclude its 55th reactor), while few Global South countries have nuclear power plants (IAEA 2023b).

    The aim of this section is to discuss if: in case more Global South countries choose to construct nuclear sites to produce energy, would they be supported by Global North countries, considering the challenges and limitations of nuclear waste management previously presented? Would these countries also be sovereign to choose for the same option of disposal and in a unilateral manner as done by Japan? If they were signatories of UNCLOS or other agreements, how would the law enforcement be carried out, in the event of non-compliance with the treaty?

    Peoples and Vaughan-Williams (2021) assert that to treat environmental issues within the optics of securitisation, it is necessary to understand which influences securitise the subject and what are the effects; what is the role of the term ‘security’ in environmental discourses; and whose interests would be served by the securitisation of this issue (Peoples and Vaughan-Williams 2021: 147).

    Kacper Szulecki (2020) studies the Polish energy sector case using this theoretical lens. The author points out that the access to nuclear power was treated as a security issue by the country itself aiming to control the theme and minimise the pressure from groups opposed to the construction of a nuclear power plant. As was the case domestically, the issue could have been extended to the international arena.

    Due to the nature and the always changing dynamics of the relations among States, if Japan were not a traditional United States’ ally, it is possible to consider that the international community reactions to the Japanese choice to release nuclear wastewater into the ocean would not be limited to the surrounding nations—as other types of transnational issues and crimes in Asia became global problems (Elliot 2007)—and, perhaps, the International Atomic Energy Agency itself would not approve the direct dispersion method.

    In 2024, Beijing and Tokyo established an agreement to monitor and control Fukushima’s power plant residual nuclear waters. China highlighted that it was an important step towards guaranteeing more transparency and reliability in the process (People’s Republic of China 2024). Recently, IAEA published a new report endorsing the security of the release that has been carried out by Japan, emphasising that the tritium levels are well below the established maximum (IAEA 2025).

    In this regard, in case countries are interested, issues such as the use of nuclear power may be securitised, having as a main argument the challenge of nuclear waste management. There are many possibilities for disposing of radioactive materials but some of them present notorious challenges, especially depending on the substances to be disposed of. Therefore, obstacles in waste management can be used to prevent new countries, especially those in the Global South, from developing their own nuclear power plants to generate electricity, remaining dependent on fossil fuels or energy imports generated from cleaner sources, usually imported from the Global North.

    Final remarks

    One of the most recent and significant events concerning nuclear power plants was the earthquake followed by tsunami that occurred in 2011 in Fukushima, Japan, which affected the city’s power plant. More than a decade after this natural incident, the Japanese government aims for the total decommissioning of this facility and, in order to do so, one of the first steps is the management of the nuclear waste stored there.

    After the International Atomic Energy Agency approval in 2023, Tokyo decided to manage the wastewater through the method of direct dispersion into the Pacific Ocean, to be completed in four stages throughout 30 years, assuming that the low concentration of radioactive substances would cause little or no impact. This decision caused some controversies: firstly, these waters contain Tritium, a hydrogen isotope generated during the nuclear energy production process, that cannot be filtered or removed as there is no technology for this yet; secondly, the Japanese government has not met with other Chiefs of State to discuss the decision. Another problem is the lack of transparency on how the process of releasing nuclear wastewater will be conducted. Finally, once the radioactive waters have been released into the open sea, there is no turning back, it is no longer possible to contain or filter them and therefore its effects cannot be controlled.

    Such questions inevitably evoke reflections on the level of damage to the environment, once Japanese history is already stained with problems caused by the improper disposal of chemical substances in water, as happened in the 1950s. Moreover, as they are in the open sea, the radioactive compounds may be taken to other parts of the world, crossing borders between countries, and becoming a global matter.

    In this regard, the perceptions on security are broadened encompassing new agendas such as the environment, which has been transformed into the term ‘environmental security’ and can be understood as ‘the state of protection of vital interests of the individual, society, and natural environment from threats resulting from anthropogenic and natural impacts on the environment’ (Asif 2021: 5).

    Furthermore, the perception of the need for countries to adapt and start to make choices aiming at sustainable development also includes energy production, given that fossil fuels are major polluters. Therefore, this subject has been turned into a security issue as well, and given its own term, ‘energy security’, that may be defined as ‘equitably providing available, affordable, reliable, efficient, environmentally benign, proactively governed, and socially acceptable energy services to end users’ (Sovacool 2012: 52).

    Nuclear energy may contribute to sustainability as it has low cost, low-polluting gases emission and uses high efficiency materials. Nevertheless, it presents the dilemma of how to properly manage the radioactive residues disposal whilst minimizing environmental problems as much as possible. Although there are many international standards, countries act aiming to fulfil or facilitate its own interests under the ‘sovereign decision’ argument.

    Following this rationale, these new subjects may be securitised, that is, treated as ‘threat’ issues that will justify more robust actions (Buzan, Weaver and Wilde 1998). Due to the risks posed by nuclear wastewater, the Japanese decision to release the substances directly into the Ocean may cause major problems that are not yet known nor controlled, and which may only be identified in the coming decades.

    The aim of this article was to offer some reflections on the Japanese decision. Amid the debates about environmental and energy securities, given that Japan is a traditional ally of the major Western powers and that it is in the Global North countries that the largest number of nuclear reactors can be found, if Global South countries intended to access this same possibility of energy production, would they have facilitated access—especially considering the management of radioactive disposal? If there is no such possibility or it is not accessible, which arguments could be used to justify this denial?

    As international relations are very dynamic, it is not possible to present a single and final answer. Szulecki (2020) states that due to issues related to energy transition, what is allowed and what is limited within this subject is increasingly being shaped by countries. The author argues that securitisation has a major role in the discussions of the ‘Critical Energy Security Studies.’ In his words, Szulecki highlights:

    Securitization is central to understanding the political (in)feasibility of necessary transition, and critical energy security studies have all the necessary tools to study this “production of non-change”, identifying where, why, and by whom change which is not only possible but needed – is stalled. (Szulecki 2020: 8)

    This article sought to contribute to the Critical Energy Security Studies, widening its approaches and themes, presenting as its core the challenges in managing nuclear waste generated from energy production plants and the mistrust over the possibility of more countries from the Global South gaining access to this matrix. In the latter case, the reason for securitising the issue could be the inefficiency of safe and less costly waste management measures.

    It can be concluded that the insufficiency or the underdevelopment of the disposal management mechanisms that are able to prevent risks could cause serious damage to the international community, as well as limiting other countries’ access to nuclear technology for generating electricity, jeopardising progress in the quest to balance environmental and energy security.
