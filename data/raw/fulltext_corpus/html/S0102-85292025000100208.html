RESEARCH ARTICLE • Contexto int. 47 (1) • Jan/Abr 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20254701e20230026 linkcopy

The Crises and Agony of International Development Cooperation: Consequences for Brazilian Foreign Policy

As crises e a agonia da cooperação internacional para o desenvolvimento: As consequências para a política externa brasileira

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    ﻿At the beginning of this century, International Development Cooperation (IDC) began to occupy a central position in Brazil’s international status. Political and social transformations created conditions for Brazil to begin acting as a relevant protagonist in international projects, especially in developing nations, through South-South Cooperation (SSC). However, a succession of crises after 2011 and the emergence of ideas opposed to cooperation during the Bolsonaro administration (2019-2022) altered the conditions that sustained IDC as part of Brazilian foreign policy. In this article, we will identify the political and economic conditions that ensured (1) the structuring and (2) the growth of Brazilian cooperation up until 2011, and we will argue that the loss of these conditions resulted in IDC’s (3) decline, leading to (4) its agony beginning in 2019. Finally, we will evaluate several consequences of the cooperation crisis based on an evaluation of Brazilian foreign policy in the global health agenda, and its commercial relationship with African nations.

    Keywords
    international cooperation; development; South-South cooperation; foreign policy; crises; Brazil

    Resumo

    No início deste século, a Cooperação Internacional para o Desenvolvimento (CID) passou a ocupar uma posição central no status internacional do Brasil. Transformações políticas e sociais criaram condições para que o Brasil começasse a atuar como ator relevante em projetos internacionais, especialmente em nações em desenvolvimento, por meio da Cooperação Sul-Sul (CSS). No entanto, uma sucessão de crises após 2011 e a emergência de ideias contrárias à cooperação durante o governo Bolsonaro (2019-2022) alteraram as condições que sustentavam a CID como parte da política externa brasileira. Neste artigo, identificaremos as condições políticas e econômicas que garantiram (1) a estruturação e (2) o crescimento da cooperação brasileira até 2011, e argumentaremos que a perda dessas condições resultou no (3) declínio da IDC, levando à (4) sua agonia a partir de 2019. Por fim, avaliaremos diversas consequências da crise da cooperação a partir de uma avaliação da política externa brasileira na agenda global de saúde e de sua relação comercial com nações africanas

    Palavras-chave
    Cooperação Internacional; Desenvolvimento; Cooperação Sul-Sul; Política Externa; Crises; Brasil

    Introduction

    During the first two decades of this century, Brazilian International Development Cooperation (IDC) was first stimulated and then institutionalized, playing a central role in Brazil’s relations with the world, before its decline culminated in the beginning of its agony in 2018. Ideological changes and a series of political and economic crises after 2010 weakened the conditions that had sustained the structuring and growth of cooperation since the 2000s, leading to its following decline and virtual disappearance. The consequences of this scenario affected not only the country’s international relations but also its ability to leverage economic and development opportunities.

    At the turn of 2000s Brazil witnessed a change in the paradigm regarding the role of cooperation that left a definitive mark on the way the country related with other nations (Souza 2012). Up through 2010, IDC figured as one of the pillars of Brazilian foreign policy. After being promoted as a notable political instrument during the administration of Fernando Henrique Cardoso (1995-2002), cooperation assumed a central role in Brazil’s foreign policy during the administrations of Lula da Silva (2003-2010). During these mandates, the form of cooperation changed, affecting how Brazilian foreign policy was designed, stimulating initiatives in greater numbers and intensity outside of the traditional North-South commercial cooperation axis (Ayllón 2007; Souza 2012; Saraiva 2013; Abdenur 2015; Besharati and Esteves 2015; Leite and Cavalcante 2016; Tomazini 2017; Schleicher and Barros-Platiau 2023; Trajber Waisbich 2023).

    Like other social public policies, IDC gained centrality in Brazilian foreign policy due to the presence of ideological-political conditions that sustained its growth and strengthened it. The successive political and economic crises that began in 2011 and deepened in 2014, however, made it difficult to maintain it. These crises were necessary but not sufficient to explain the near disappearance of Brazilian cooperation projects since then. The most relevant factor was the establishment of a set of conservative ideas within the state’s bureaucracy, which paved the way for the near disappearance of IDC among Brazilian international practices (Casarões and Farias 2021).

    The trajectory of the decline of Brazilian cooperation can be clearly observed based on the type of cooperation that is oriented towards other developing countries – the South-South Cooperation (SSC).1 In recent years, the literature has identified the reasons behind the emergence, maintenance, and changes in patterns of international cooperation. From a political point of view, SSC was linked to Brazil’s rising profile in multilateral negotiation forums (Ramanzini and Ribeiro 2013; Apolinário Júnior 2016; Bastos and Hiratuka 2020), its growing international status (Abdenur 2015; Trajber Waisbich 2023), and the internationalization of Brazilian governmental bodies, such as agencies and state-owned companies (Schleicher and Barros-Platiau 2023). From an economic point of view, SSC was linked to commercial and investment opportunities for Brazilian firms in developing countries, especially during Lula’s second administration (2006-2010), as well as Rousseff’s first administration (2011-2014), in which SSC became more explicitly commercial and less altruistic (Zanini 2017; Menezes and Fingermann 2020).

    This article organizes the main elements that explain the emergence and growth of SSC and describe how the political and economic crises after 2011, and especially after 2019, with the introduction of conservative ideological perspectives regarding foreign policy paved the way for the erosion of Brazilian SSC. The argument is that a combination of these elements undermined the pillars that sustained this public policy and this in turn brought with it undesirable consequences for the well-being of Brazilians themselves. To accomplish this, we have constructed a synthesis of Brazilian foreign policy from 2000 to 2020, highlighting the evolution of the role of IDC in these four phases, its relationship with these crises, and the main consequences of these dynamics for Brazilian society.

    This article is structured as follows: in addition to the introduction, we present a synthesis of Brazilian foreign policy beginning in 2000, highlighting the conditions which permitted the structuring and growth of Brazilian SSC and the role of cooperation in foreign policy over the past 20 years. This is followed by an exploration of arguments regarding the effects of the crises Brazil faced starting in 2011 on the conditions that sustained and maintained Brazilian SSC. The consequences of the decline and agony of cooperation are assessed through an analysis of Brazil’s loss of leadership in the global health agenda and the reduction in trade relations with African nations. The article concludes with our final considerations.

    International development cooperation in four phases

    The advent of IDC, and especially SSC, within Brazil’s international profile was an important pillar in the improvement of Brazil’s international status (Buarque 2020). During Lula da Silva’s government, the country intensified its establishment of bilateral agreements with countries in Latin America and Africa, without this representing an abandonment of the dialogue channels and partnerships that it had developed with developed countries up until that point (Rodrigues, Urdinez and Oliveira 2019).

    Figure 1 presents the official cooperation agreements drafted by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and signed by the federal government between 1998 and 2020. Even though the number of agreements is not an exclusive measure of Brazilian cooperation, it does make an interesting indicator that portrays at least four phases of IDC. These phases coincide with political cycles and domestic and foreign policy movements, and they are: structuring (1) and growth (2), which were the consequences of efforts to increase Brazilian international development cooperation engagement, and its decline (3) and agony (4) phases which marked a reversal of IDC’s role in foreign policy.

    Before exploring the reasons for this trajectory, we will outline the factors identified in the literature that explain the emergence and maintenance of IDC. The definition of a set of variables is based on the assumption that interactions between systemic and domestic dynamics shape foreign policy actions. In other words, our view is that while systemic conditions influence the material decisions of nations’ foreign policies, their effects are filtered, interpreted, and shaped by domestic political dynamics. Various approaches to foreign policy analysis are dedicated to the study of this type of interaction, clarifying how external incentives are processed through intervening domestic variables, such as the cognitive/ideological filter of incumbent politicians, the role of leadership, the existence of interest groups, the extractive capacity of the state, macroeconomic conditions, bargains among political bases, and bureaucratic dynamics, among other things (Rose, 1998; Foulon, 2015; Burges and Chagas Bastos, 2017; Götz, 2021; Schleicher and Barros-Platiau, 2023).3

    In Brazil, this type of approach requires an understanding of how changes in the international system over this 20-year period were interpreted by the various actors and institutions involved in the process of formulating and executing the country’s foreign relations. In addition to changes in the international scenario, changes in the profile of key actors and the transformations of the political, economic, and ideological conditions of the agents and power structures over two decades influenced Brazil’s international orientation. Given this perspective, we have opted to present a balance of the main interactions between systemic and domestic dynamics indicated by the specialized literature to explain Brazilian IDC between 2000 and 2020. After this balance, it will be possible to understand what explains the periods of its decline and agony. Our argument is the change from the growth phase (2) to the phases of decline (3) and agony (4) was driven, respectively, by the loss of the political and economic conditions necessary for its continuation and, starting in 2019, the internalization of a set of ideas within the nation’s bureaucracy which led to the ideological rejection of this form of international engagement.

    Why did it arise and how did it maintain itself? Conditions for the structuring and growth of IDC

    Brazilian international development cooperation oriented towards the nations of the Global South – SSC – traveled an uncertain path before establishing itself as a public policy (Abdenur 2015; Schleicher and Platiau 2017; Schleicher and Barros-Platiau 2023). In the first place, the emergence of cooperation sponsored by Global South countries such as Brazil, Turkey, India, China, and Venezuela followed a series of international systemic changes. Up until the mid-1990s, the multilateral development agenda, above all the one managed by specialized thematic bodies of the United Nations, would not have been capable of satisfactorily incorporating countries outside of the traditional global powers (Esteves and Assunção 2014; Abdenur 2015; Robledo 2015; Gosovic 2016). The launch of the United Nations Development Agenda in 1994 represented an expansion of this organization’s approach to various forms of international cooperation. An essential aspect of this change was the recognition of development in its various manifestations as a fundamental prerequisite for promoting peace and justice on a global scale. Based on this, the subject of development came to be understood as part of a collective effort by nations to deal with global inequalities (Milani 2012). The economic growth of countries like Brazil, China, and India after the turn of the century marked the ascension of a new group of countries which could contribute to the new agenda, and this represented the possibility of establishing alternative partnerships to the traditional channels for creating development projects.

    On the international level, the launch of the Millennium Objectives in 2000 – transformed into the Sustainable Development Objectives in 2015 – increased the attention paid by countries to different areas of international development. Recurring financial crises in the United States and the European Union, along with the strengthening of international coalitions of developing nations such as the India-Brazil-South Africa Forum (IBSA) and the intergovernmental organization that includes Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa (BRICS), consolidated the central role of SSC projects (Gosovic 2016; Schleicher and Platiau 2017). During this period, Brazilian foreign policy took advantage of and included these areas into its agenda, and most of the country’s international engagement dealt with these subjects.

    Also, the availability of free resources is essential to the creation and continuity of technical cooperation projects. To accomplish this, the existence of international private resources or loans from national development banks are frequently considered to be necessary conditions for the viability of international cooperation as a foreign policy (Bastos and Hiratuka 2020). Brazilian SSC benefited from the existence of economic conditions that were propitious for their structuring. The return of economic growth during the first decade of the century and its corresponding social transformations turned Brazil into a large political and social laboratory. The success of several policies implemented during this period became the object of cooperation projects, as well as the existence of economic resources for the establishment of international partnerships.

    The economic growth witnessed in this first decade sustained the profusion of strategies to fight poverty and inequality successfully implemented during this period, and this drew the attention of other governments around the world, especially those leading countries facing the same structural difficulties (Silva and Almeida Filho 2020a; Bastos and Hiratuka 2020; Menezes and Fingermann 2020). On the domestic front, between 2002 and 2015, the federal government’s social spending jumped from 59.9% (2002) to 67.3% of total spending in 2015 (Silva 2017).4 According to Osório (2015), the expansion of social spending had a significant effect on poverty and inequality: (1) extreme poverty went from 16.4% of the population in 1995 to 4.7% of the population in 2009; (2) during the same period the Gini Index fell from 0.599 to 0.539 and; (3) real GDP growth surpassed 7% in 2010, and averaged 4% between 2000 and 2010.

    The improvement in Brazil’s social well-being indicators elevated its potential contribution to international development projects. During Lula da Silva’s two terms (2003-2010), for example, various public policies that fought poverty and reduced inequality were established5 and soon recognized internationally (Cervo 2010; Berringer and Boito 2013; Visentini 2014). These experiences created opportunities for Brazilian foreign policy, given that the experiences of its domestic public policies enhanced the value and legitimacy of Brazil as a donor of technical expertise to other developing nations. This made it possible for Brazil to establish cooperation projects adapting formats and scope to recipient’s budgetary restrictions while providing expertise and legitimacy based on its local experiences.

    This is what the systematized data from the Institute of Applied Economics (IPEA) and the Brazilian Cooperation Agency (ABC) demonstrates. The results show that between 2005 and 2009, government spending on these types of projects was almost USD 1.43 b illion, divided into areas such as humanitarian aid, foreign student grants, technical, scientific, and technological cooperation, and contributions to international organizations, which ended up representing 56% of the cooperation spending during this period. The volume, thematic breadth, and growth of human resources mobilized demonstrated the central role that multilateral actions came to represent in the federal government (IPEA 2016).

    Other forms of cooperation also benefited from the improvement in economic conditions. Within the multilateral context, the increase in Brazilian financial contributions to initiatives developed in partnership with international organizations strengthened the government’s international ties and increased its social capital.6 These initiatives elevated the country’s profile through greater participation in the decisions of international organizations such as the UN General Assembly, the World Trade Organization, the International Monetary Fund, and the G20, among others (Vieira 2014; Apolinário Júnior 2016). This social capital also reverberated in the country’s economy with the expansion of international trade and investments flows (Souza 2012; Rodrigues and Gonçalves 2016).

    The maintenance of IDC as a public policy depends on the presence and continuity of political incentives. The political conditions include the diffusion of ideas that recognize the relevance and legitimacy of cooperation as a state policy, legislative support, the formation of a specialized technical corps, and the creation of specialized public agencies, among others. With the exception of a brief period during the administration of Collor de Mello (1990-1992), after the resumption of democracy (1988) Brazilian governments enjoyed positive political conditions for the expansion of cooperation projects. This was because there was an ideological-political continuity within the board of Brazil’s foreign policy – the President and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs – in favor of the benefits of Brazilian international participation oriented by an international development agenda (Saraiva 2007).

    In practice this meant the expansion of its relationships through active collaboration in cooperation projects and an acceleration in the process of making cooperation mechanisms with developing nations institutionalized. The strengthening of the Brazilian Cooperation Agency (ABC) between 2000 and 2010 allowed the professionalization of project management and the expansion of Brazilian partnerships with international cooperation agencies, multilateral development banks, civil society organizations, and developing nation governments (Ayllón 2007; Tomazini 2017). The novelty in the conducting of Brazilian foreign policy in the Lula administrations was emphasizing not only foreign trade, but also making the expansion of national development policies viable (Berringer and Boito 2013; Bastos and Hiratuka, 2020).

    In addition to the combination of the political and economic conditions that make it possible, international cooperation needs the prevalence of ideas which value international solidarity and the benefits of cooperation within public institutions. These ideas need to be institutionalized in some manner. In Brazil, this body of ideas achieved internal legitimacy during the second term of Fernando Henrique Cardoso (1999-2002) and the first term of Lula (2003-2006), to the extent that they advanced the professionalization of workers and bureaucrats dedicated to these functions (Abdenur 2015; Apolinário Júnior 2016; Leite and Cavalcante 2016; Schleicher and Platiau 2017; Menezes and Fingermann 2020).

    With the ascension of the Labor Party after the turn of the century, a set of ideas grounded in a discourse of international solidarity gained prominance within the government (Amorim 2010; Milani and Pinheiro 2013; Tomazini 2017). As a consequence, this vision’s incorporation into foreign policy actions increased the centrality of cooperation as a form of international engagement, resulting in the strengthening of the bureaucratic structure responsible for such initiatives. These efforts positioned Brazil as global power through its relationship with developing nations, particularly inAfrica and Latin America (Abdenur 2015; Schleicher and Platiau 2017). The proximity with these countries, for instance,r increased support for Brazil’s bid to become a permanent member of the United Nations Security Council, and reinforced the call for reforms in global governance institutions with limited representation of developing nations, such as the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank (Abdenur 2015; Apolinário Júnior 2016).

    During Lula’s presidency, Brazil expanded its diplomatic corps and reinforced the international divisions of agencies and ministries within the federal bureaucracy. The division of labor became a structural element in the expanding its capacity to manage international projects. In this framework, the Ministry of Foreign Affairs played the role of organizing demand, which was channeled and managed by the Brazilian Cooperation Agency. The ministry established memoranda and agreements, while the agency coordinated efforts to implement projects in partnership with other governmental bodies, civil society organizations, specialized consulting firms, and UN funds and agencies, such as the UNDP and UNESCO, among others (Abdenur 2015).

    In addition to the structure directly related to central administration, the federal government stimulated the contributions from state-owned companies, private foundations, and public interest organizations, such as Caixa Econômica Federal and Fiocruz (Oswaldo Cruz Foundation), among others. This expansion produced results in various locations, with demand quickly emerging. The National School of Public Administration, for example, developed internal skills to deal with international partners, particularly in providing technical support for the creation of analogous structures in African countries like Mozambique (Schleicher and Platiau 2017).

    The strengthening of the bureaucracy, the continuity of the economic scenario, and the country’s political stability became more difficult to sustain starting in the beginning of 2011, with the incoming administration of Dilma Rousseff. The new context marked the saturation of economic growth model driven by stimulus to the domestic market, the onset of rising inflation, and a significant political crisis that culminated in her impeachment in 2016. These developments led to notable changes in foreign policy and Brazilian IDC.

    The decline of official IDC: political crisis and fiscal adjustments

    For nearly two decades Brazil enjoyed conditions for the structuring and growth of IDC. The beginning of the Rousseff administration in 2011 marked the beginning of a change in this configuration, which became more evident after 2014 as the country’s political and economic crises intensified.

    At this time, the delayed effects of the global crisis of 2008 began to slow down GDP, which went from 7.5% in 2010 to 2.7% in 2011, 1.0% in 2012, and 2.3% in 2013.7 This slowdown was accompanied by rising inflation and persistently high government spending. Together, these dynamics contributed to an increased public debt relative to GDP, inaugurating a period of economic instability. This instability deepened further after the reelection of Rousseff in 2014 (Bastos and Hiratuka 2020).

    The international economic context also did not help. A rapid shift of global production toward China reduced Brazilian exports to the country in 2013, elevating the deficit in Brazil’s current account. Europe’s sluggish economic recovery was further destabilized by the American economic crisis of 2008/2009.Along with the return of protectionism to Latin American countries, this reduced Brazil’s trade options. In Latin America, domestic political challenges also affected Brazil’s relationship with countries such as Argentina and Venezuela, creating obstacles to its involvement in cooperation projects within the Union of South American Nations (Unasur) and Southern Common Market (Mercosur) itself (Bastos and Hiratuka 2020).

    The resources allocated for SSC were reduced during the Rousseff administration. However, her first term (2011-2014) sought to continue the initiatives started by Lula to expand the connection with international multilateral agencies, especially global ones. During this period, the amount spent on international organizations increased while the total destined to bilateral or trilateral cooperation projects gradually decreased (Baumann et al 2022a). After a decade of growth, total federal government spending on cooperation8 fell almost 40% between 2010 and 2011. This was followed by an increase of approximately 2% between 2011 and 2012, and a new decline of 14% between 2012 and 2013 (IPEA 2016). In the face of growing domestic tensions, the Rousseff government was focused on dealing with the economic and political crises that marked the beginning of her second term in 2015. International cooperation no longer occupied a central position in the government’s strategy, and the domestic scenario and the international context were unfavorable to the emergence of new IDC projects.

    The political crisis worsened the deterioration of the internal conditions for governability. In October 2015, MDB (Brazilian Democratic Movement), the party of then Vice President Michel Temer, along with the Ulysses Guimarães Foundation (linked to the party), launched the Bridge to the Future plan. This plan outlined a series of public policy recommendations aimed to stabilize and restart economic growth. The economic-political agenda presented in the document contained different objectives in comparison to the previous administrations, and was focused on restoring of economic growth through control of the public debt and austerity measures. The new plan also included a severe fiscal adjustment and proposals for labor and pension reforms (Silva and Almeida Filho 2020b).

    After the impeachment in 2016, the guidelines in the Bridge to the Future plan began to be implemented, affecting political spaces concerning the maintenance of IDC projects – such as social policies – especially due to the focus on fiscal adjustment. This reduced the capacity to finance domestic and international policies. An example was the approval of Constitutional Amendment 95 (EC/95) in December 2016, which limited the growth of public spending for the next 20 years – the so-called Public Spending Limits (Ulhôa and Almeida Filho 2018). This amendment stipulated that, for the next two decades (with a possible revision after 10 years), federal government spending could not exceed the previous year’s spending in real terms, adjusted for inflation. This meant that even with population growth or elevated unemployment rates – factors that put significant pressure on public services – public spending could not be adjusted to meet the growing demand.

    As a consequence of these fiscal containment measures, the initial effects of the spending limits were negative in the priority areas of social policy in Brazil. In health and education this represented a 3.1% reduction in spending between 2016 and 2017. In terms of social assistance, the main programs were maintained, but the projected readjustments were affected. The Bolsa Família, for example, increased its budget by 16.39% in 2017, but by just around 3% in 2018 (Silva and Almeida Filho 2020b). The growth of inflation and the increase in unemployment between 2018 and 2020 increased the wait lists for those hoping to be included in the program, and a possible increase in spending on this project was only projected for the 2021 budget.9

    Since social policy suffered under the new fiscal regime, the public programs and projects in the development area were reduced in profile compared to their heyday in the first decade of the 21st century. Thus, SSC related initiatives were negatively affected, because a significant number of them were related to the diffusion of best practices in social policy areas.10

    What leads to its end? IDC enters a phase of agony

    The election of Jair Bolsonaro in 2018 represented an important event in the descent of SSC, as it went from decline to its agony. The conditions were not positive during the Rousseff (2011-2016) and Temer (2016-2018) administrations, worsened substantially. From an economic point of view, Brazil maintained the same general conditions established after Rousseff’s impeachment. The indicators also remained the same, that is, low economic growth of around 1% per year,11 and the same rate of inflation despite the fiscal adjustments – roughly 4% – during the first two years of his administration. Beginning in 2020 with the arrival of the COVID-19 pandemic, the economic crisis worsened, and the government was forced to suspend the spending limit in 2020. Even so, the economic indicators worsened dramatically, with a roughly 4% decrease in GDP in 2020 and an inflation rate of over 10% in 2021.12

    The beginning of Jair Bolsonaro’s administration was marked by the new element of the rejection of IDC relevance. As the government’s plan was established, it became clear that there was a desire to contain public spending on social policies and implement a foreign policy that was ideologically averse to cooperation (Casarões and Farias 2021; Maringoni, Schutte and Berringer 2021). This ideological element marked the transition from the IDC’s decline to its agony phase.

    Within the foreign ministry, immediately after the election, the universalist principles that guided Brazilian foreign policy since the establishment of the New Republic in 1988 made way for a Western-oriented posture rooted in conservative values. This shift made Brazilian foreign policy less receptive to the political and cultural diversity of other developing nations. The government’s post-election rhetoric opposed globalism, cultural Marxism, and communism, while criticizing the discourse of international solidarity and multilateralism in favor of IDC (Casarões and Farias 2021).

    These worldviews undermined the ideological foundations that had been the remaining support for sustaining Brazilian cooperation. Bolsonaro’s government promoted a vision that rejected these principles by promoting one that assumed the acceptance of multilateralism meant the weakening of Western Christian societies in the face of political, economic, and ideological influence of non-Christian ones, particularly those of African and Asian nations (Araújo 2017).

Overall, it is possible to notice that the evolving conditions were not favorable to cooperation activities, particularly SSC. Despite a succession of economic and political crises, new SSC agreements were signed up until the middle of 2017, but were drastically reduced afterwards.

    The new SSC phase is a result of the aggravation of the conditions that have been deteriorating continuously since 2011. As we argue, the change in ideological perspective was the decisive element in the arrival of its agony after 2019. From the outset, the members of the Bolsonaro administration and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in particular did not hesitate to reject the predilection of previous governments for creating international initiatives in developing nations and having a relationship with countries such as Cuba, China, and Venezuela. This new ideological-political disposition significantly affected Brazilian foreign policy and generated uncertainties in terms of the continuity of cooperation projects in developing nations.

    The effect of Brazil’s international posture affected not only the level of discourse but also the bureaucratic structure which had sustained SSC in previous years. The result of these changes was the Brazilian Cooperation Agency’s complete loss of protagonism in conducting the Brazilian foreign policy agenda and the disarticulation of the institutional apparatus which made it possible to conduct projects in this area that counted on the participation of ministries, companies, and national and international agencies, such as IPEA, Embrapa (Brazilian Agriculture and Livestock Research Company) and various UN agencies. The removal of the foreign minister, Ernesto Araújo, and the change of government in the United States reduced this isolation, but they did not result in a great recovery of prestige.

    During the Bolsonaro administration, Brazilian international cooperation was mainly maintained domestically through the provision of public services like health and education to foreigners who reside in Brazil. Technical cooperation, meanwhile, was sustained by specialized agencies like Fiocruz, and domestic agencies like Fapesp (Research Foundation of the State of São Paulo) and the Butantan Institute (Baumann et al 2022b). The economic and ideological-political elements introduced by Bolsonaro reinforced the dismantlement of Brazilian IDC. While in previous years there was a quantitative decline, during the Bolsonaro administration there was a qualitative change in the perception of the relevance of IDC to Brazilian development. The intensification of the political and economic crises beginning in 2014, was followed by fiscal restrictions in 2016, and an ideological reorientation of Brazilian foreign policy in 2019, undermining the social, structural, and institutional conditions that sustained Brazilian SSC.

    The loss of protagonism during the agony phase of SSC

    The search for political and economic autonomy was the central piece in the motivations behind the country’s international relations during the entire 20th century (Spektor 2015). When Lula took power in 2003, foreign policy was given a key role in this search for autonomy, which came to be understood and valued as a prioritized public policy in the promotion of economic and social development (Hirst, Lima and Pinheiro 2010).

    The transformations the country experienced expanded the horizon of the objectives of autonomy and development and included the return of Brazil’s ambition to play a leading international role (Milani, Pinheiro and Lima 2017). As we have argued, up until 2014 SSC played a central role in this process (Abdenur 2015). The decline of SSC followed the various crises resulted in Brazil losing this leadership role in its foreign articulations. Beginning with this period, the country lost significant space in areas such as global health and trade in the African continent.

    The loss of protagonism in the global health agenda is an example that expresses this argument well. At the turn of the 21st century, health cooperation became one of the main themes of global governance, expanding further the space that the public health agenda occupied in Brazil’s foreign relations, and it even formed a foreign health policy (Almeida et al 2010; Esteves, Gomes and Fonseca 2016; Gómez and Perez 2016). From this point on, the participation of the country was based on the identification of this interest in playing an international role and having internal resources that included institutional expertise in health policy through organizations like Fiocruz. During the first decade of 2000s, international cooperation actions with important Brazilian participation were central to the control of epidemics, the sharing of technology – such as pharmaceuticals and vaccines – and having access to investments in health infrastructure (Almeida et al 2010; Buss and Tobar 2020; Ventura and Costa Bueno 2021).

    During the structuring and growth of IDC (2000-2010), Brazil positioned itself as a reference in the promotion of a developing nation agenda (Buss 2018). Despite fiscal contingencies which affected Brazilian public policy beginning in 2014 and the reduction in the number of SSC projects, Brazil kept international cooperation initiatives active in health products through the Ministry of Health’s Unified Health System and institutions such as Fiocruz (Buss and Tobar 2020; Baumann et al 2022b).

    The most significant impacts of the cooperation crisis on Brazilian participation in the health can be perceived more clearly beginning with the COVID-19 crisis. The humanitarian crisis intensified the tests for Brazilian foreign policy in a manner that was unprecedented in its recent history. In the face of this global tragedy, political leaders made efforts to alleviate the human, economic, political, and social effects of the pandemic.

    Because these circumstances, an international consortium named the Covax Facility was created in April 2020 to make access to vaccine distribution viable. Under the coordination of the World Health Organization (WHO) together with the Gavi Vaccine Alliance and CEPI (Coalition for Epidemic Preparedness Innovations), a series of agreements were signed between international health agents, vaccine and pharmaceutical manufacturers, scientists, the private sector, and civil society. These agreements sought to facilitate countries’ access to the international distribution of vaccines and medicine used to combat COVID-19, and they were instructed to give special access conditions and financing to the poorest countries.13

    Despite the international efforts to develop vaccines, Brazil chose not to join these international efforts and abstained from a larger role in international cooperation initiatives to deal with the various aspects of the pandemic. On a regional level, the unwillingness to maintain bilateral and regional cooperation mechanisms deteriorated the instruments that ensured the stability and coordination of regional actions in areas such as healthcare, border control, and epidemiological control - key components in managing health crises (Buss and Tobar 2020).

    Brazil’s participation in the Covax Facility consortium was officialized only in September 202014 – almost six months after its launch. The automatic alignment of the Bolsonaro government with Donald Trump’s isolationism resulted in Brazil’s initial unwillingness to participate in multilateral initiatives (Casarões and Magalhães 2021). During this period, Brazilian cooperation remained active only as a function of bodies relatively independent of the federal government’s decisions, such as Fiocruz, and subnational institutions linked to cities and municipalities, such as the Butantan Institute (the center for biological research linked to the São Paulo State Health Secretariat). The country had abdicated the leading role it had and had maintained through acting in international cooperation projects and in multilateral arenas of global health, such as PAHO (Pan American Health Organization) and WHO (Ventura and Costa Bueno 2021).

    The hesitation to participate in recent global initiatives in the global health area, the deterioration of regional cooperation mechanisms, and the loss of the country’s social capital resulted in the decrease of global relevance and greater difficulties in epidemiological control and access to essential resources in combating COVID-19, such as vaccines, respirators, Personal Protective Equipment (PPE) and medication (Ventura and Costa Bueno 2021).

    Another example from which we can observe the loss of Brazilian prestige is the country’s political and economic presence in the African continent. Ever since the turn of the century, the ties created by Brazilian cooperation helped state and private companies find new business in developing nations. African countries, especially those that speak Portuguese such as Mozambique and Angola, were some of the most regular recipients of Brazilian cooperation. Illustrating this was the extraordinary growth of national champions in industries such as construction (Pinheiro and Gaio 2014) and mining (Zanini 2017; Alencastro 2020) and bilateral programs for agricultural development such as ProSavana (Triangular Cooperation Program for the Agricultural Development of Mozambique’s Tropical Savannah) (Fingermann 2017). Despite this, from a political point of view, the predominant understanding was that the Brazilian government stimulated market opportunities without the intention to promote the exploitation of the natural resources of the recipient nations (Lechini 2012).

    Despite the solidarity intentions present in the discourse of Brazilian IDC, many studies point to a relationship between the increase of international cooperation – especially SSC – and the strengthening of economic ties between these countries, especially in terms of direct flows of foreign trade and investment (Souza 2012; Vieira 2014; Apolinário Júnior 2023). The logic behind this argument is that a greater proximity with these countries is capable of expanding channels of communication between public and private agents, facilitating the creation of business opportunities and increasing the degree of confidence that companies feel in relation to political uncertainties that can affect international economic transactions. In this sense, SSC made it possible for Brazil to establish closer relationships with countries potentially in favor of Brazilian wishes to change the architecture of international economic governance. This led to support for Brazil on various multilateral fronts such as existing coalitions within the World Trade Organization and the International Monetary Fund (Hirst, Lima and Pinheiro 2010). Cooperation also contributed to the expansion of alternatives in the international economic environment, such as the proximity of the Brazilian economy to value chains in which national companies are more competitive, such as the African regional market.

    In observing the behavior of trade flows between Brazil and this region, we have identified the rising growth of bilateral trade during periods of greater IDC intensity. During Lula’s first two administrations, trade flows (imports and exports) to African nations rose from a level of roughly USD 5 billion in 2003 to USD 20 billion in 2010 (ComexStat, 2023). Despite some annual variation, this growth trajectory was sustained until 2013. In 2020, the declining trajectory of trade flows between Brazil and Africa reached levels similar to 2005 of roughly USD 13 billion per year. The level of 2011 was only reestablished in 2022 due to the increase in Egyptian imports stimulated by economic growth and a tariff reduction between Mercosur and Egypt that was agreed upon in 2017.15

    Analyzing the scenario, the agony phase of Brazilian development cooperation did not permit taking advantage of access opportunities to strategic markets such as the African region. This argument became even more important with the beginning of the African Continent’s Free Trade Zone in 2021, which was oriented towards stimulating trade among the region’s countries.16 The reduction in cooperation projects and trade flows beginning in 2011 are important signs of the loss of Brazil’s political and economic relevance in Africa, and they signal a reorientation of objectives in terms of the country’s presence in the region that had been stimulated in previous years when SSC played a fundamental role.

    Final considerations

    International Development Cooperation was central to Brazilian foreign policy strategy for almost two decades. The paradigm of foreign policy was based on a multilateral attitude and a solidarity discourse as causes and policies oriented towards development generated the establishment of links and social capital between the Brazilian government and various countries and international organizations. Between the end of the 1990s and 2010, IDC went through structuring and growth phases, but it declined with the loss of the political and economic conditions that supported them starting in 2011, reaching its agony phase with the beginning of an administration ideologically opposed to this type of policy in 2019.

    A group of factors were responsible for the dismantling of IDC. First of all, the economic crisis which began in 2011 and worsened in 2014 resulted in an increase in demand for public services and assistance policies during a period of fiscal difficulties. During the Rousseff government, most cooperation spending went to already existing programs, and there were few incentives for creating new projects. During the administrations of Michel Temer and Jair Bolsonaro, fiscal tightening and reduced spending affected not only cooperation but social policies in general. While the expansion of public policies, especially social policies, had stimulated the establishment of cooperation projects with developing nations, spending limits drastically reduced this possibility. Another effect was the downsizing of the IDC budget, especially reductions in spending on the bureaucracy and participation in international organizations, and the discontinuation of cooperation projects.

    The economic and political crises which began in June 2013, continued through the impeachment of Dilma Rousseff in 2016 and the election of Jair Bolsonaro in 2018, and dragged on during the pandemic. These crises resulted in the dismantling of the institutional support that had maintained IDC as a relevant instrument in Brazilian foreign policy. However, it was the inclusion of an ideological component opposed to SSC that ended conditions to maintain it as a state policy. Internationally, this represented the government’s automatic alignment with Donald Trump’s administration, distancing it from developing nations and support for multilateral solutions.

    While SSC had been an important strategic instrument of Brazilian foreign policy, the repercussions of its agony had relevant implications for the possibilities of Brazilian social development. Brazilian foreign policy during the COVID-19 pandemic and an analysis of the country’s trade with Africa illustrate that the loss of IDC’s central role had consequences for the objectives of Brazilian protagonism internationally. Foremost, Brazil lost its leading role in global health and made little use of privileged access to vaccines and health equipment due to its deteriorating relationship with pioneering nations in their production (such as China), as well as its international status, which before had been sustained by its cooperative posture with international organizations and developing countries. In exchange, it also received little from traditional cooperation donors, and had difficulties in negotiating health supplies, equipment, and vaccines.

    A second example is that the country reduced its presence in Africa, and trade flows with African nations, which had been the highlight of Brazilian SSC for years, were also affected. At the turn of the century, the strengthening of Brazil’s relations with the region’s nations was accompanied by an increase in trade flows. Beginning in 2014, however, the decline in cooperation was accompanied by a reduction in the volume of trade, resulting in lost opportunities for business expansion. The ineffectiveness of Brazilian foreign policy in ameliorating the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic and the missed business opportunities with African nations demonstrate that the decline of cooperation mechanisms had a significant effect on Brazilian interests.

    IDC, whether received or provided, was an important element in strengthening Brazil’s ties with various countries in the world. The links formed in the past constituted social capital which amplified the country’s international status and provided legitimacy to Brazilian claims, facilitating the transit of negotiators in international negotiations. The resumption of the international cooperation depends on the reestablishment of domestic social well-being policies, and the resumption of a solidarity and multilateral orientation in Brazilian foreign policy – central elements to its rise and growth.

    Economic Fiscal Declining public debt;State spending’s expansion through social policies; Fiscal austerity;Spending limits rule in 2016;Reduction of investments in social policies.
    Macroeconomy Monetary stability;Average annual growth of about 4% per year;International crises (2008) with limited impact on the Brazilian economy;Expansion of domestic market. Economic contraction with rising inflation;Implementation of inflation control measures;Rise of public debt in 2020;Economic, political, and social instability in 2020 (COVID-19 pandemic).
    Political Bureaucracy Strengthening of the Brazilian Agency for Cooperation (ABC);Creation of cooperation offices in governmental agencies and state-owned companies;Alignment of state bureaucracy around IDC projects;Growth and encouragement of civil society initiatives supporting IDC. Significant reduction IDC’s budget;Coordination conflict and relevance decline of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MRE) and the ABC;Reduction in the number of agreements and activities related to cooperation;Reduction of IDC initiatives coordinated by the federal government;Disconnect of the civil society agenda from official IDC projects.
    Ideology Ideological orientation towards international solidarity rhetoric;Recognition IDC’s relevance in promoting Brazilian international leadership;Alignment between domestic (social) policies and the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs). Conservative and nationalist political-ideological orientation;Automatic alignment with the USA; Decline of multilateralism;Dismissal of tangible and intangible gains from establishing a relationship with developing countries.
