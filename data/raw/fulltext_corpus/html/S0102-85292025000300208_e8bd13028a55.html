Non-violent Civil Resistance and Human (Im)mobility: the Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC) in Defence of Ancestral Territory amid the Colombian Armed Conflict

Resistência Civil Não-violenta e (I)mobilidade Humana

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article aims to analyse the strategic actions of the Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC) in defence of ancestral territory in Colombia’s Pacific region, which has experienced an intensification of the armed conflict following the signing of the Peace Agreement between the Government and the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC-EP) in 2016. It is argued that the CRIC, established in the 1970s, acts to protect its communities from forced displacement, preventing their uprooting and thereby ensuring the pervivencia –both physical and cultural – of its peoples. To this end, the following theoretical and methodological steps are undertaken: first, a dialogue is established between the fields of forced migration and resistance studies, through the articulation of the analytical frameworks of crisis migration and non-violent civil resistance; next, primary data collected through a digital platform, alongside secondary data regarding CRIC’s actions in the department of Cauca, are analysed. The outcome is the development of the concept of ‘self-confinement’ as an act of non-violent civil resistance, collectively organised through the political agency of these subjects, as well as the creation of a short comic strip that seeks to articulate the argument above through a different language, in order to contribute to the democratisation of scientific knowledge.

    Key words
    Colombia; CRIC; forced migration; civil resistance; self-confinement; human (im)mobility

    Resumo

    O presente artigo tem como objetivo analisar a atuação estratégica do Conselho Regional Indígena do Cauca (CRIC) em defesa do território ancestral na região do Pacífico colombiano, a qual enfrenta um recrudescimento do conflito armado após a assinatura do Acordo de Paz entre o Governo e as Forças Armadas Revolucionárias da Colômbia (FARC-EP) em 2016. Argumenta-se que o CRIC, consolidado na década de 1970, atua protegendo suas comunidades do deslocamento forçado, evitando o seu desenraizamento e garantindo, assim, a pervivencia, física e cultural, de seus povos. Para isso, são realizados os seguintes passos teórico-metodológicos: primeiro, é promovido um diálogo entre os campos de estudos de migrações forçadas e de resistência por meio da articulação dos ferramentais analíticos de migrações de crise e de resistência civil não-violenta; em seguida, são levantados dados primários, coletados em plataforma digital, e secundários acerca da ação do CRIC no departamento Cauca. O resultado é o desenvolvimento do conceito de ‘autoconfinamento,’ como um ato de resistência civil não-violenta, coletivamente organizada desde o agenciamento político desses sujeitos, e a produção de uma mini história em quadrinhos (HQ), que visa explicitar o argumento supracitado em uma outra linguagem a fim de colaborar para a democratização do conhecimento científico

    Palavras-chave
    Conselho Regional Indígena do Cauca; Colômbia; Migrações Forçadas; Resistência civil; Autoconfinamento; (I)mobilidade humana

    Introduction

    According to Mary Kaldor (2012), contemporary armed conflicts are marked by the intersection of warfare, organised crime, and the massive violation of human rights. In such conflicts, there is both an erosion of the boundaries between the local and the global, and a focus of strategies and effects on the civilian population. Unsurprisingly, these dynamics have generated a significant number of refugees and internally displaced persons. Colombia, in this context, is home to one of the longest-running armed conflicts in the Western Hemisphere and, according to the final report of the Colombian Truth Commission (2022), the civilian population has been a strategic target of armed actors. With more than 9 million victims officially recognised and registered by the Colombian government agency Unidad para las Víctimas (UARIV), created under the framework of the Victims and Land Restitution Law (Law 1448/2011) (Colombia 2011), the country is facing one of the most severe humanitarian crises in Latin America (ICG 2003). Of this total, more than 8 million people have been internally displaced, forced to abandon their homes and livelihoods in search of protection. However, these individuals did not cross an international geographical border but remained within the confines of the national territory1.

    Even today, despite the Peace Agreement signed between the Colombian government and the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC-EP) in 2016, Colombia remains one of the countries with the highest number of internally displaced persons in the world (UNHCR 2024). This situation is due to the ongoing armed conflict in regions historically marginalised by the Colombian state, where there are intense territorial disputes over areas of significant geostrategic interest by remaining illegal armed groups. These include dissident FARC-EP factions that did not demobilise, the National Liberation Army (ELN), and the Gaitanist Self-Defence Forces of Colombia (AGC), also known as the Gulf Clan. Among these regions, the Colombian Pacific stands out, where there is a convergence – that is, an intersection – between ethno-territorial belonging, structural poverty, racism, natural resource wealth, a historically weak state presence, and armed violence (Arboleda Quiñonez 2008; Jesus 2023b). In this region, the armed conflict intensified during the government of Iván Duque (2018-2022) (Gutiérrez Sanín 2020; Aponte González and Larrat-Smith 2021), and was further exacerbated by the emergence of the Covid-19 pandemic in early 2020 (Jesus 2022).

    According to the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR 2025), more than 52 000 persons were internally displaced in Colombia in 2024 alone, with Nariño, Cauca and Chocó – all located in the pacific region – being the main departments responsible for the expulsion of populations from their life plans. Moreover, around 41% of these individuals are of African descent, and 21% are Indigenous, revealing the disproportionate impact of the armed conflict on ethnic peoples, which are minority social groups. According to the most recent census conducted by Colombia’s National Administrative Department of Statistics (DANE), Afro-descendant and Indigenous peoples are ethnic minorities in the country, representing approximately 6.8% and 4.3% of the national population, respectively2. Indeed, as stated by the Colombian Truth Commission (2022), the war in Colombia did not unfold on a blank slate, but rather in a society – and a nation-building project – whose dominant cultural model has operated through the political, social, and economic exclusion of the Other, of the difference, even to the point of its elimination. In a way which culture and violence have become mutually reinforcing over time, as highlighted in the report, the backdrop to the armed conflict in the country is the consolidation of a monocultural, racist, and patriarchal nation-state, inherited from the period of Spanish colonisation and, within the capitalist world-system, continuously serving the interests of transnational capital (Lozano Lerma 2016; Jesus 2023b). These are the structural elements and processes that shape this complex scenario of internationally recognised humanitarian crisis.

    Nonetheless, it is also in the Colombian Pacific region, specifically in the department of Cauca, that one of the country’s most emblematic experiences on non-violent civil resistance has emerged: the organisational process of the Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC). This is one of the most respected social organisations at both national and international levels, which since the 1970s has been advancing plural practices and strategies to defend the ancestral territory and cultural ways of life of the various ethnic groups it comprises, namely: Nasa, Guambiano, Totoroez, Polindara, Guanaco, Kokonuko, Kisgo, Yanacona, Inga and Eperara. Indeed, it is estimated that without the work of social ethno-territorial organisations such as CRIC, the number of internally displaced persons in the region would be even higher.

    That said, this article aims to analyse how CRIC strategically acts in defence of the ancestral territory in the department of Cauca. It is argued that its action seeks to prevent the forced displacement of its communities, thereby avoiding uprooting – that is, the rupture of the cognitive and symbolic bond of these peoples with their ancestral territory (Vergara-Figueroa 2018) – and thus guaranteeing their pervivencia (a term in Spanish used by the organisation). Pervivencia is a concept that has no direct translation, as it arises from the knowledge and experiences of the Indigenous communities of the region. Nevertheless, it is understood that, unlike the idea of survival, the term does not refer to the conservation of life in a material, physical, or biological and immediate sense, but rather to the guarantee of the cultural and identity continuity of these peoples across time and space – that is, through generations and within their ancestral territories.

    To this end, the present article brings into dialogue the fields of forced migration studies and resistance studies, and draws on both primary and secondary data on the subject. The primary data is based on the manual cataloguing of 699 videos produced by CRIC between 2018 and 2024 and shared on its official YouTube channel. These catalogues were compiled by the author using Google Forms and are stored in a secure folder, forming part of her postdoctoral research database. The secondary data consists of reports, academic articles, and books collected through archival research. This way, the article offers a critical reflection on CRIC’s collective action and on the importance of territory for Indigenous peoples, and the result is the development of the concept of ‘self-confinement’ as a non-violent civil resistance act, collectively organised by these subjects.

    With the aim of contributing to the democratisation and recognition of scientific knowledge production, this article also presents a short comic strip entitled ‘An Act of Resistance,’ which seeks to convey the aforementioned argument through a different language, being thus a product of science communication, designed to reach a broader, non-specialist audience. The comic was developed with the technical support of illustrator and comic artist Joanna Maciel, who created the illustrations based on a previously established script, ensuring the inclusion of all textual and non-textual elements communicated by the author, who holds the copyright to the image. Furthermore, it is worth noting that the comic was submitted as the final project of the course on science communication and public engagement with science, held in the first semester of 2024 by Federal University of ABC (UFABC) in partnership with the Federal University of São Carlos (UFSCar).

    In light of the above, the article is structured into two main sections, in addition to the introduction and final considerations. The first section brings into dialogue the subfields of ‘crises migration’ and ‘non-violent civil resistance’ in order to problematise core dichotomies within the field of migration studies, namely: compulsion and voluntariness, agency and structure, and mobility and immobility. The second section then explores how this theoretical framework helps to understand CRIC’s actions in the context of armed conflict, through the development of the concept of self-confinement, which aims to problematise human immobility from the perspective of the political agency of the subjects, thus the decision to remain in their territory as an act of resistance, even in the midst of armed conflict and humanitarian crisis. This provides the article with an innovative contribution to the field of migration and (im)mobility studies, as well as to the broader understanding of social practices of these actors.

    Crisis-Induced Migration and Non-violent Civil Resistance: a necessary dialogue

    The topic of migration, and human (im)mobility, has gained significant prominence in recent decades, whether in academic, political, or media debates. In particular, from the 1980s onwards, and especially during the 1990s, academia has witnessed an expansion of the fields of forced migration and refugee studies (Chimni 2008), which are characterised by an analytical focus on the compulsory, or non-voluntary, aspect of human mobility. Although refugee studies may be situated within the broader field of forced migration – which encompasses, for example, the issue of internally displaced persons – it is evident that the former has been prioritised in both academic literature and policy-making, to the detriment of this and other migratory categories (Jesus 2023b). This tendency within academia can be partly explained by the fact that the research agenda has been shaped by the concerns of the West, which, from the post-Cold War period onwards, began to witness an increase in the number of refugees and asylum seekers from the Global South arriving in Northern countries (Chatty and Marfleet 2013). Although such movements today occur predominantly along South–South axes (UNHCR 2024), this tendency within the field of study remains.

    In a modern international system organised around nation-states, where each person is understood to belong to a specific place and to be subject to a particular authority (Haddad 2008), the annual increase in the number of refugees (UNHCR 2024) – those fleeing armed conflicts and/or large-scale human rights violations in search of protection and the safeguarding of their rights in another country – has generated considerable interest in the public arena. This is either because such movements are perceived as a potential threat to the modern international order (Haddad 2008), or due to the fear and tension they may provoke within host societies. Such sentiment is frequently encapsulated in the term ‘crisis.’ As a result, migration is framed as a ‘problem’ to be solved, giving rise to migration policies that are often restrictive, securitised, and aimed at containing the perceived excess, which, at their most extreme, culminate in the criminalisation of migrants and refugees (Moulin 2019), those who dared to escape the misery to which they would otherwise have been consigned (Souza 2016). In this sense, by invoking the discourse of a ‘migration crisis, the issue of migration in the contemporary world ceases to concern how to ensure and uphold individuals’ rights, and becomes instead a problem to be governed and controlled (Hyndman 2000; Agier 2006).

    Corroborating this perspective, Heather Johnson (2014) argues that, with changes in the patterns of migratory movements – prompted by decolonisation processes in Africa and Asia, combined with the emergence of armed conflicts in Latin America, and later, following the end of the Cold War – a different international politics of human mobility was delineated. This new framework is grounded in a logic of prevention and containment of migratory flows, giving rise to what has been termed the ‘(im)mobility turn’ in migration studies. This shift stems from the growing number of studies concerned with examining the nexus between human mobility and immobility from the perspective of restrictive national policies and other social constraints that hinder individuals from migrating. In other words, from the standpoint of involuntary, or forced, immobility at both international and local scales (Bélanger and Silvey 2020). Yet few studies engage with immobility through the lens of human agency, as in the specialised literature, mobility alone is typically framed as a form of emancipation (Adey 2006).

    According to Julia Moreira and Janine Borba (2021), the discourse of migration crisis is embedded in a rationality that frames migratory movements as sources of problems. As a result, there is a process of blaming migrants and refugees, who are received as threats to the well-being of society and to national identity. Furthermore, this discourse tends to neglect the reasons why people migrate. In response to this narrative, the authors highlight that a subfield of study has emerged in Western Europe and the United States – referred to as ‘crisis migration’ – with key contributions by: Olivier Clochard (2007); Susan Martin, Sanjula Weerasinghe and Abbie Taylor (2013); Jane McAdam (2014); and Richard Black and Michael Collyer (2014). These authors problematise not only with the notion of migration crisis, but also with key concepts underpinning the contemporary migration debate, whether referring to forced or voluntary movements. Accordingly, for the purpose of this article, two core aspects of this theoretical framework are highlighted: the inversion of the crisis focus, and the problematisation of the binaries ‘compulsoriness vs. voluntariness,’ ‘agency vs. structure,’ and ‘mobility vs. immobility,’ which are constitutive of the field of migration studies.

    Accordingly, the subfield of crisis migration draws attention to the fact that migratory movements, rather than being the cause of crises, are in fact the result of humanitarian crises (Moreira and Borba 2021). Humanitarian crises are defined as ‘situations in which there is a widespread threat to life, physical safety, health, or basic subsistence that is beyond the coping capacity of individuals and the communities in which they reside – often have significant implications for migration’ (Martin, Weerasinghe and Taylor 2013: 123). As previously noted, such crises are linked to structural elements and processes that blur the boundaries between the local and the global. In this regard, McAdam (2014) argues that a combination of social, political, economic, and environmental factors give rise to crisis migration, therefore specific events or processes – such as the Colombian armed conflict – are intrinsically connected to systemic inequalities that render certain groups more vulnerable to forced displacement.

    In effect, armed conflicts in the Global South in general, and in Colombia in particular, are rooted in colonial, neoliberal, and patriarchal dynamics (Lozano Lerma 2016; Tickner 2020; Jesus 2023b). As a result, they disproportionately affect women, Indigenous peoples, and Afro-descendants, who constitute the social groups most vulnerable to forced displacement in Colombia. Recognising this condition of vulnerability, the Colombian Constitutional Court issued, in 2008, Legal Order 092 on the protection of women (Colombia 2008), and in 2009, Legal Order 004 on the protection of Indigenous communities (Colombia 2009a) and Order 005 on the protection of Afro-descendant communities (Colombia 2009b), all of which are situated within the framework of Law 387/1997 (Colombia 1997). In line with the international document Guiding Principles Internal Displacement (OCHA 2004), which identifies States as bearing the primary responsibility for preventing forced displacement and protecting displaced persons within their national territories, it is worth noting that Law 387/1997 sets out the responsibility of the Colombian State to prevent, protect, and provide assistance to internally displaced persons generated by the armed conflict. This law has been internationally recognised as one of the most sophisticated legal frameworks currently in place on the subject (Ferris 2011), although practical limitations and protection gaps persist, particularly with regard to ethnic communities (Jesus 2023b).

    Moreover, Moreira and Borba (2021) highlight that the analytical tools of crisis migrations is also important for understanding immobility or non-movement. That is, how people remain in a territory despite adversity. In this sense, it becomes apparent that it occurs either because individuals are trapped or confined and, therefore, lack the minimum conditions to migrate (Black and Collyer 2014), or because they make the conscious, informed, and yet extra-rational (emotional, cultural, and identity-based) decision to remain in resistance (Jesus 2022, 2023a; Jesus and Moreira 2023). In this regard, it is argued that, in the latter scenario, immobility – or ‘self-confinement’ – emerges not as an external imposition by armed groups or by the surrounding conditions, but rather as an act of resistance which, therefore, arises from the political agency of these subjects. This, in turn, challenges the constitutive binaries of the field of migration, as previously discussed, by questioning the Manichean manner in which such categories are traditionally mobilised in political discourse and in academia. Indeed, according to Martin, Weerasinghe and Taylor (2013: 05-06),

    [C]ategorising movements related to humanitarian crises presents many dilemmas for scholars and policymakers alike. It is increasingly recognised that few migrants are wholly voluntary or wholly forced; almost all migration involves a degree of compulsion, just as almost all migration involves choices. […] those who are compelled to move when confronted with acute violence, conflict or disaster make choices, albeit within a limited range of possibilities, particularly as to where they will move. Any secondary movement, including the choice of destination, may also be shaped by considerations concerning livelihood, betterment or other life-chances. Even in the direst humanitarian crises, for many there is still an element of choice, since some may choose to stay, risking their lives rather than leave their homes.

    In this manner, as all migratory movement involves a certain a certain degree of agency, however limited (Mainwaring 2016), the choice to migrate forms part of the subject’s ‘right to resist’ – a right that encompasses not only the pursuit of survival but also the pursuit of ‘more life,’ as argued by Fabricio de Souza (2014). In a similar vein, it is inferred that the decision to remain also constitutes part of the subject’s right to resist, insofar as they understand that the only way to live – with dignity and a sense of wholeness – is by remaining in resistance, that is, by self-confining. Even though this decision may lead to the worsening of the social vulnerability of those who choose to stay, it is still part of their right to resist. Therefore, it is understood that what distinguishes one situation from the other is the perspective on life in relation to the possibilities and limitations imposed by the surrounding environment (Jesus 2023b; Jesus and Moreira 2023). This understanding is essential for comprehending why Indigenous peoples of the Colombian Pacific make the collective and organised decision to remain in resistance, defending both life and ancestral territory. Life and territory, in fact, are the very foundation of the collective action undertaken by CRIC in the department of Cauca, as will be discussed in the following section. Thus, it becomes evident that it is necessary to problematise the nexus between mobility and immobility, moving beyond traditional academic interpretations of this relationship, and fostering a deeper dialogue between the fields of migration and resistance studies.

    According to Mikael Baaz et al. (2016), the field of resistance studies is both expanding and fragmented, with different authors offering distinct conceptions of what constitutes resistance. Even so, two characteristics are commonly agreed upon: resistance is an act, and it is always positioned in opposition to some form of power. Furthermore, the authors note that, for a long time, the concept of resistance was associated with antisocial and reactionary behaviours, revolutionary ideologies, and violent acts. Despite this, they highlight that ‘resistance could also be productive, plural and fluid, and integrated into everyday social life. Resistance holds the potential to constructively transform societies and change history’ (Baaz et al. 2016: 138). This conception of resistance is aligned with the thinking of Michel Foucault (2019), who understands power and resistance as co-constitutive elements exercised throughout society in networked forms. Moreover, Baaz et al. (2016) emphasise that, within the field of resistance studies, other concepts have emerged, such as ‘everyday resistance,’ whose principal exponent is James C. Scott (1987, 2002), also credited with developing the notion of ‘micro-resistance,’ as well as the concept of ‘civil resistance.’

    Drawing on peasant resistance experiences in Malaysia, Scott (1987, 2002) argues that resistance is an act carried out by an individual or group of individuals aimed at mitigating or challenging impositions and/or advancing claims within contexts of oppression and social inequality. The author further contends that such acts – better understood as forms of micro-resistance – do not require a high degree of social organisation, insofar as they do not seek to challenge the established power structure. The primary objective, therefore, is to ensure the survival of these individuals within the system, rather than to transform it. In this regard, collective forms of resistance that deliberately aim to subvert the status quo, promoting transformations in power relations and potentially structural changes, are excluded from the author’s conception. Even so, it becomes clear that this concept is insufficient to comprehend the actions of the CRIC in Colombia, insofar as these are collectively organised and politically grounded and directed. Accordingly, it is considered that the concept of ‘civil resistance,’ or ‘non-violent civil resistance,’ as a subfield within resistance studies, is the most appropriate for addressing the issue under analysis.

    Thus, according to Cécile Mouly and Esperanza Hernández Delgado (2019), civil resistance and non-violent resistance are interchangeable terms that refer to the use of various non-violent and non-conventional means by ordinary people in opposition to a power that possesses greater material capacities. Gene Sharp (2020) defines non-violent action as situations in which people collectively resist by refusing to cooperate with the oppressive figure persisting in disobeying and challenging it. Moreover, he emphasises that, as a technique, non-violent action is not passive, as it constitutes action. Therefore, it is not an attempt to avoid or ignore conflict, but rather a way of engaging actively in politics, which may manifest as protest to express dissent or to change public opinion; non-cooperation, social, political, and economic; or intervention, such as obstruction, occupation, or the establishment of a parallel government. According to the author, if people engage in such actions in sufficient numbers and over a long enough period of time, the action will produce effects and, potentially, the oppressive figure will be defeated. Even so, Cassy Dorff (2019) observes that the fields of peace and armed conflict studies tend to marginalise the role played by civilians, thereby disregarding the significance of non-violent civil resistance, including in post-conflict contexts. This is because such experiences ‘[...] can also leave enduring legacies. […] better equip[ping] communities to actively contribute to peacebuilding […]’ (Hallward, Masullo and Mouly 2017: 5). Civilians are understood as ‘[....] all persons who live in a given local territory, do not participate in hostilities, and are not full-time members of any state or non-state armed organization’ (Arjona 2017: 758).

    In this regard, Oliver Kaplan (2017) explains that, in general, within the context of armed conflict, civilians are perceived either as defenceless victims, and thus lacking agency, or as collaborators of armed groups. Similarly, as previously highlighted, research on forced migration tends to overlook the political agency of such individuals. Accordingly, Kaplan (2017) emphasises that this dichotomous and limited perspective constitutes an obstacle to understanding how individuals embedded in contexts of violence develop diverse strategies of protection in order to preserve their autonomy and self-government. The author argues that, in such scenarios, community cooperation and social organisation render resistance possible. In this sense, as noted by Krzysztof Krakowski (2017), depending on the degree of social cohesion within a given community, as well as the extent to which its members engage in decision-making processes, these individuals may become more or less likely to be displaced when threatened by armed actors. In other words, the greater the social cohesion of a given community, the more resources it will possess to remain in its territory in resistance. Therefore, in contexts of war, collectivity and solidarity are fundamental factors for resistance, and this is crucial to understanding the CRIC’s decision to self-confine – that is, to remain immobile – as part of its community protection strategy, given the cultural and ancestral ties these peoples maintain with their homeland or ancestral territory.

    The Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC) in defence of life and territory

    The Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC) is a social organisation established in the early 1970s in the department of Cauca, located in Colombia’s Pacific region. It played an active role in the drafting of the 1991 Political Constitution, which finally recognised Colombia as a pluriethnic and multicultural nation-state (Colombia 1991). Its organisational history is deeply influenced by Manuel Quintín Lame, an indigenous leader of Nasa ethnicity, and is linked to the process of land claiming by the various Indigenous peoples that comprise the organisation. Born in 1883, Quintín Lame was a self-taught intellectual who, throughout the twentieth century, fought tirelessly for improved living conditions and for the recognition of the cultural and territorial rights of Indigenous peoples in Colombia. According to Yolanda Ramos Ruiz (2018), Quintín Lame was one of the key figures behind the ‘Indigenous awakening’ in the country, both through the Mingas – spaces for gathering, debate, and the claiming of rights promoted by Indigenous communities in Cauca in response to armed conflict – and through the Quintinadas, their armed expression, which came to an end during the last century. Furthermore, Mónica Espinosa Arango (2003) underscores that his Indigenous liberation thought was also a decolonising one, inasmuch as it succeeded in

    [...] To articulate, within an ‘Indigenous’ discourse, one that is temporally far-reaching and politically consequential, the experiences, memories, and concerns shared by those who have been classified as ‘Indians’ since the conquest and expelled from their territories or controlled within them, deprived of their autonomy, and subjected to relations of subjugation and racial discrimination (Espinosa Arango 2003: 144, my translation).

    During its early years, the CRIC was associated with the peasant movement, forging an alliance which, according to the organisation, was instrumental in reaffirming the deep-rooted connection of these peoples’ to Madre Tierra (a Spanish term used to refer to nature and territory), even though they understand their identity to extend beyond their condition as peasants (CRIC 2021). According to Myriam Galeano Lozano (2006), over time, the differences between Indigenous and peasant struggles became increasingly apparent, ultimately leading to a formal separation between the two groups. In this regard,

    [W]hereas for the peasants the struggle ended once the land had been recovered, for the Indigenous peoples this was only the beginning of a long journey to be recognised as peoples with territorial and cultural autonomy, as their ancestors had been before the arrival of the Spanish (Galeano Lozano 2006: 58, my translation).

    Furthermore, the author explains that, during the 1970s and 1980s, the method used to recover lands usurped during the colonial period involved the night-time occupation of such lands by Indigenous communities and their subsequent preparation for cultivation, in the face of violence perpetrated by public security forces – particularly the Army – who were sent in to forcibly remove them. In addition, Indigenous peoples negotiated with landowners and with the State, through the Colombian Institute for Agrarian Reform (INCORA), which purchased these lands and legalised them in the name of the Indigenous and/or peasant groups engaged in the struggle for their rights.

    Indeed, the issue of land – or more precisely, of territory understood as a space for life – is essential to Indigenous peoples, and within the CRIC’s platform of struggle, the first and arguably most important point is the recovery and healing and spiritual restoration of lands, followed by the expansion of Indigenous reserves (CRIC 2021). At present, CRIC’s political platform comprises ten objectives, all of which are guided by the principles of unity, land, and culture. Beyond those already mentioned, the platform also includes: the strengthening of the Cabildos (representative authorities with jurisdictional autonomy) (Galeano Lozano 2006); the refusal to pay terraje (a servile and feudal arrangement under which Indigenous people were required to provide unpaid labour in exchange for the right to inhabit and use a small plot of land that had originally belonged to them prior to colonisation) (Ramos Ruiz 2018); the knowledge and enforcement of Indigenous law; the defence of Indigenous history, language, and customs; the training of Indigenous teachers for a bilingual education rooted in their own knowledge systems; the strengthening of community-based economic enterprises; the recovery, defence, and protection of life-sustaining spaces in harmony and balance with Madre Tierra; and the protection of the family, understood as the primary nucleus of social life3. Furthermore, it is important to highlight that, for the CRIC (2021b), unity in diversity is at the heart of all things. According to the organisation, the principle of unity is fundamental to the defence of life and the collective interests of its constituent communities. This unity, in turn, is woven from the familial sphere, in accordance with the law of origin, ancestral rights, and the spiritual guidance of the elders.

    Thus, it is evident that, through the principles of unity and land, the CRIC considers the cultural and identity strengthening of its communities to be possible. In this regard, unity, land, and culture become mutually reinforcing elements that ensure the autonomy and pervivencia of these peoples across time and space. The problem, as previously highlighted, is that the intensification of the armed conflict in the Pacific region – particularly in the department of Cauca – has been one of the main obstacles faced by the organisation in exercising its rights. The CRIC (2021c) attributes the worsening of the socio-economic vulnerability in Cauca and the escalation of the armed conflict in Indigenous territories in recent years to the government of Iván Duque (2018-2022), denouncing it for failing to fully implement the Peace Agreement with the FARC-EP. According to the organisation, the presence of armed groups, illicit crop cultivation, the militarisation of its territories, and the assassinations of social leaders, among other factors, have severely affected Indigenous peoples, with no clear proposal from the national government to address this structural issue. The CRIC asserts that there was a lack of political will on the part of Duque’s administration to engage in dialogue and to find solutions to the problem. In light of this reality, Aida Quilcué – a member of the organisation and elected senator in 2022 – states that this constitutes a situation of accelerated genocide of Indigenous peoples, with the risk of not only physical but also cultural extermination, and she issues an appeal to the international community and civil society to support them in defence of life and human rights (CRIC 2020b).

    Accordingly, the forced displacements resulting from the armed conflict constitute a scenario of humanitarian crisis and ‘ecogenoetnocide,’ faced not only by Indigenous peoples but also by Afro-descendants in the Colombian Pacific region. That is, what Santiago Arboleda Quiñonez (2019) identifies as the continuation of colonial and racist violence, responsible for the expulsion of these peoples from their ancestral territories and for their physical and cultural extermination – with the direct, negligent, and/or complicit participation of the Colombian state. A crime that threatens the physical, cultural, and ecological survival of ethnic peoples as collective subjects in a relationship of harmony and reciprocity with nature. And, in doing so, a crime against diversity and plurality as essential conditions of humanity. In this regard, for the author, to discuss forced displacement in Colombia without considering its historical and sociocultural structure is to reduce the complexity of this issue and to conceal its colonial and racist logic. Thus, it becomes evident that it is essential to take into account the structural elements and processes that shape this scenario of humanitarian crisis, as indicated by the subfield of crisis migration studies. Indeed, when the CRIC addresses the issue of armed conflict and violence in Indigenous territories – understood by its members as territorial disharmonies – the organisation links this problem to the country’s colonial history and to the capitalist interests of the state and multinational corporations in rural areas (CRIC 2020a).

    Although the CRIC (2020a) declares itself a non-participant actor in the armed conflict – neither collaborating nor cooperating with armed groups – the organisation and the communities that comprise it are not passive victims of violence, as analysed by Kaplan (2017). This is because its authorities see themselves as responsible for liberating Madre Tierra from the armed actors, undertaking an exercise of care, control, and territorial governance. This process is supported by the Indigenous Guard, who, bearing their command batons and without resorting to weapons, defend life and the ancestral territory (CRIC 2022). Indeed, there are multiple experiences of civil resistance in Colombia. The so-called ‘peace communities’ established in Samaniego, in the department of Nariño (Belén Garrido and Mouly 2018), and in San José de Apartadó – formed by internally displaced persons in the Urubá region, in the department of Antioquia (Mahecha 2018) – are among the most emblematic examples, which join numerous initiatives of memory, symbolic reparation, healing – both spiritual and emotional – , and right claims, which are mobilised by different social actors. What they all share is the pursuit of reconstructing the community’s social fabric by restoring social relations and bonds of trust and solidarity ruptured by the armed conflict (Jesus 2023b). Nevertheless, some losses are irreparable, such as the loss of the cognitive and ancestral bond with the territory, which constitutes the very source of subjectivation (Jesus 2025). In this context, self-confinement emerges as a condition of possibility for a dignified life.

    That said, it is evident that, since its inception, the CRIC has advanced plural forms of non-violent civil resistance, among which the collective and organised decision to remain within the territory stands out (Jesus 2022, 2023b; Jesus and Moreira 2023). In other words, through self-confinement, the CRIC understands that, as collective subjects, fleeing or being displaced may lead to cultural and identity uprooting, including for future generations, given the vital bond they hold with the land and, thus, the significance of their ancestral territory (Jesus 2022, 2023b; Jesus and Moreira 2023). In this regard, although the decision to remain in resistance may further aggravate the vulnerability of those who choose to stay, it is noteworthy that, due to cultural factors and the perspectives they hold on life, self-confinement as an act of resistance ‘[...] will never be a celebration of sacrifice or death, but rather the highest affirmation of desire and life’ (Souza 2014: 112, my translation). Life here is understood in a broader sense, not merely material or biological, but also cultural and identity-based (Jesus and Moreira 2023).

    However, it is important to highlight that, as a consequence of their exercise of civil resistance in defence of ancestral territory and life, in recent years many Indigenous social leaders have been assassinated by armed groups in the department of Cauca. These killings constitute a strategy to dismantle the resistance of self-confined communities – that is, those that have made the collective and organised decision to remain and defend their territory – so that armed groups may advance their political, economic, and military interests in lands of high value to transnational capital. Indeed, according to data from the Colombian think tank Instituto de Estudios para el Desarrollo y la Paz (Indepaz), since the signing of the Peace Agreement with the FARC-EP, in 2016, 1789 social leaders have been assassinated in Colombia, the majority of them being men, including 358 in the department of Cauca alone. Among these, 140 were indigenous, 83 were peasants, and 32 were Afro-descendants4.

    The comic strip ‘An Act of Resistance’ (Figure ), produced with the technical support of illustrator and comic artist Joanna Maciel, aims to make explicit the argument developed throughout this article regarding self-confinement. In Figure 1, a number of key elements stand out that are essential to understanding the decision to remain in resistance as a strategy of community protection promoted by CRIC amid the context of armed conflict and humanitarian crisis. Moreover, the colours and symbols used in the illustration seek to reference the organisation, thereby affirming the cultural identity and diversity of the peoples represented therein. The illustration also constitutes a form of academic dissemination.

    Figure 1
    An Act of Resistance

    The aim of the first panel is to highlight the role of the Indigenous radio station as a tool of self-managed communication, intended to inform and alert CRIC-affiliated communities to potential threats. The second panel emphasises the importance of autonomous education as a means of reinforcing the organisation’s guiding principles – unity, land, culture, and autonomy – as previously outlined. The third and fourth panel draw attention to how the deliberative process among authorities and other community leaders unfolds in response to the arrival of armed conflict in their territories. These panels demonstrate that the decision to remain in resistance is taken in Assembly and is, therefore, collectively organised. They also highlight gender and generational diversity, as well as the presence of the Indigenous Guard, recognisable by their ceremonial staffs of authority. In order to support their decision to remain in defence of life and territory, these communities mobilise international organisations and traditional media outlets to exert pressure on the national government, with the aim of securing protection for their lives and ensuring the safeguarding of their rights. Finally, the fifth panel seeks to underscore that these peoples, in harmony with nature – with Madre Tierra – declare their non-participation in the armed conflict and demand that their rights be respected. To this end, they call for a stable and lasting peace.

    Final Considerations

    The main objective of this article was to analyse the role of the Regional Indigenous Council of Cauca (CRIC), in the department of Cauca, in response to the scenarios of armed conflict and humanitarian crisis in the Colombian Pacific region, which have worsened even further following the signing of the Peace Agreement between the national Government and the Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia (FARC-EP) in 2016 (Gutiérrez Sanín 2020; Aponte González and Larrat-Smith 2021). Indeed, according to UNHCR (2025), three of the five departments with the highest number of internally displaced persons in 2024 are located in the Colombian Pacific. Furthermore, the Agency points out that approximately 41% of these persons are of African descent and 21% are Indigenous, highlighting the disproportionate impact of violence on ethnic peoples. Nevertheless, it is estimated that, without the strategies of resistance and community protection adopted by these communities, these figures would be even higher.

    In this regard, it is argued that the collectively organised action of the CRIC has operated as a means to prevent the forced displacement of the Indigenous communities that comprise the organisation, thereby avoiding their uprooting – that is, the rupture of the cognitive and symbolic bond these peoples maintain with their ancestral territory. For the CRIC, the preservation of this bond is essential to ensure their pervivencia, both physical and cultural, across time and space. Nonetheless, data indicates a growing number of assassinated social leaders in the region, which highlights a possible link between self-confinement – as an act of resistance in defence of life – and the perpetration of such killings by armed groups. This potential connection requires further investigation in future research.

    To this end, a theoretical framework drawn from the fields of forced migration studies and resistance studies was mobilised, bringing them into dialogue. More specifically, the articulation between the subfields of crisis-induced migration and non-violent civil resistance was addressed as a productive approach to understanding the issue of self-confinement, insofar as both offer reflections on the political agency of subjects amidst scenarios of war and humanitarian emergency. These reflections are crucial for problematising key binaries that structure the field of migration studies, namely: voluntariness vs. compulsion, agency vs. structure, and mobility vs. immobility. In this way, this research advances both the theoretical interconnections between the literatures on migration and resistance – which still require greater engagement from an academic standpoint – and the understanding of the practices of social groups situated in contexts of adversity and violence.

    Thus, through primary data collected from the official YouTube channel of the CRIC, as well as secondary data, light is shed on how the organisation, grounded in the principles of unity, land, culture, and autonomy, has advanced plural forms of non-violent civil resistance. Among these, the collective, organised, and informed decision to remain in the territory – self-confining – as a strategy of community protection in defence of life and ancestral land is particularly highlighted. In this regard, it is argued that, although remaining in place may exacerbate the vulnerability of these communities (Moreira and Borba 2021), such an act is never a ‘celebration of sacrifice or death,’ but rather a pursuit of ‘more life,’ as argued by Souza (2014: 112) in his analysis of the ‘right to resist’ of the migrant subject. It is, therefore, a life that takes on a broader meaning, beyond the material or biological, and is especially cultural and identity-based, which can only be fully lived with dignity and completeness when one remains in the territory, even if in resistance.

    Considering the above, and with the aim of making explicit the argument developed throughout the text, a short comic strip entitled ‘An Act of Resistance’ was employed, produced with the technical support of the illustrator and comic artist Joanna Maciel. It is also important to highlight that this comic strip is a product of the public engagement course on science communication and public understanding of science, offered by the Federal University of ABC (UFABC) in partnership with the Federal University of São Carlos (UFSCar) during the first semester of 2024. The objective is that, through an alternative form of language, this research may also transcend the walls of academia, reaching not only our peers but also a broader, non-specialist audience. In doing so, it is hoped that the work may contribute to the democratisation and valuing of scientific knowledge production.

    Finally, it is important to highlight that, despite the recent shift in studies on im(mobility), migration as a last resort and immobility as a result of individuals’ decision-making processes, rather than solely as a product of social structures of domination and oppression – that is, immobility as agency and emancipation – remain underexplored topics and represent an emerging field of study. Consequently, this area offers fertile ground for further research and investigation. Furthermore, this is an issue that demands careful analysis in order for normative instruments and public policies aimed at these populations to be properly developed.
