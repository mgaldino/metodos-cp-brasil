RESEARCH ARTICLE • Contexto int. 47 (1) • Jan/Abr 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20254701e20230017 linkcopy

Paradiplomacy, Its Actors and Trends: State of the Art and Additional Contributions

Paradiplomacia, seus atores e tendências: estado da arte e contribuições adicionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The current pandemic scenario has brought relevance to the paradiplomatic activities carried out by subnational entities, insofar as it has challenged the traditional models of International Relations, pushing decentralized actors to seek solutions to their problems through external agreements. Despite its importance, scientific production on the subject of paradiplomacy is scattered. In this sense, we sought to analyse the state of the art of current scientific production on paradiplomacy, as well as whether this production is characterized by a stop-and-go trend, as seen in the empirical logic of the international activities of sub-national entities. To this end, bibliometric analysis and a systematic literature review were carried out based on 75 scientific papers available on the Web of Science (WOS), Scopus and the Scientific Electronic Library (Scielo). The main results, therefore, pointed to a stop-and-go trend in scientific production on paradiplomacy, which in turn was driven by the situation imposed by the COVID-19 pandemic (2021), and the rise of studies on paradiplomatic activities carried out by Chinese (2021 and 2020), Canadian (2019) and, above all, Latin American (2018) subnational entities. Finally, as a contribution to the literature on the subject, the use of a little-used methodology to examine the role of subnational entities in International Relations is verified, which therefore provides an alternative way to interpret the results and present new insights on the subject.

    Keywords
    COVID-19; subnational diplomacy; China; Canada; Latin America; paradiplomacy

    Resumo

    O cenário pandêmico atual trouxe relevância às atividades paradiplomáticas exercidas pelos entes subnacionais, na medida em que desafiou os modelos tradicionais das Relações Internacionais, impulsionando os atores descentralizados a buscarem soluções para os seus problemas por meio de acordos externos. Apesar da sua importância, a produção cientifica sobre o tema encontra-se dispersa. Neste sentido, buscou-se analisar o estado da arte da atual produção científica sobre paradiplomacia, bem como se tal produção se caracteriza por uma tendência de stop and go, tal como se verifica na lógica empírica das atividades internacionais dos entes subnacionais. Para tanto, realizou-se análises bibliométricas e revisão sistemática de literatura com base em 75 trabalhos científicos dispostos na Web of Science (WOS), Scopus e Scientific Electronic Library (Scielo). Os principais resultados, portanto, apontaram para a tendência de stop and go da produção científica sobre a paradiplomacia, que por sua vez, fora impulsionada pela conjuntura imposta pela pandemia de COVID-19 (2021), pela ascensão dos estudos advindos de atividades paradiplomáticas realizadas pelos entes subnacionais chineses (2021 e 2020), canadenses (2019) e, sobretudo, latino-americanos (2018). Por fim, como contribuição à literatura sobre o tema, verifica-se a utilização de uma metodologia pouco utilizada para examinar a atuação dos entes subnacionais nas Relações Internacionais, o que confere, portanto, uma maneira alternativa para se interpretar os resultados e apresentar novos insights sobre o tema.

    Palavras-chave
    COVID-19; diplomacia subnacional; China; Canadá; América Latina; paradiplomacia

    Introduction

    From a historical perspective, the study of International Relations has traditionally focused on analysing the dynamics of nation-states, their interaction within the International System and the political realm. However, in recent decades, the discipline has shifted towards understanding the changing landscape by examining the emergence of a global society. This new paradigm transcends state interests and encompasses a wider range of actors within the International System, including intergovernmental organizations, transnational corporations, non-governmental organizations (NGOs), and various types of networks (Barnett and Sikkink 2008: 1).

    Within this context and drawing from the existing literature, the diplomacy of subnational governments, commonly referred to as paradiplomacy, can be seen as a phenomenon associated with global society and the process of globalization (Clemente 2018: 320). Although the conceptual understanding of paradiplomacy remains a significant challenge for the field, Ribeiro (2009: 33) identifies that the neologism ‘paradiplomacy’ first appeared in the scientific literature in the 1980s, describing the international activities of Canadian and American federated states within the context of globalization. Furthermore, the author expands on Zabala’s concept (2000) by noting that the prefix ‘para’ would indicate, besides something parallel, something associated with an accessory or subsidiary capacity, referring to the ‘diplomatic’ actions of subnational governments (Ribeiro 2009: 33).

    Despite the ongoing need for further definition and discussion on its scope, paradiplomatic activity has been linked to the engagement of subnational governments in International Relations. This involvement encompasses a range of formal and informal international actions undertaken by these governments, aiming to promote socioeconomic, political, or other external outcomes within their constitutional competence (Cornago Pietro 2004: 251). According to Tavares (2014: 12), the performance of actors within this context encompasses a series of international actions carried out by non-central governments. This new dimension of International Relations is a natural result of two driving forces: the rapid process of globalization and the trend towards decentralization of public power.

    In this context, the pandemic brought about by the novel coronavirus highlights the profound impact of subnational actors’ international engagement on the sociopolitical fabric at local and national levels. For instance, the actions taken by the state of São Paulo in relation to vaccine production and procurement through an agreement with the Chinese biopharmaceutical company Sinovac serve as an illustrative example. Additionally, the state of Maranhão’s procurement of respiratory equipment through an alternative and international route can also be cited.

    Furthermore, Keating (1998) characterizes the international activities of subnational entities, emphasizing their non-ideological origins and lack of well-defined criteria. Unlike nation-states, which typically establish and plan their own foreign policies, subnational actors engage in the international arena for pragmatic reasons, with limited consistency.

    Similarly, Vigevani (2005: 128) identifies the prevalence of stop-and-go strategies within this context. According to the author, the international engagement of subnational entities exhibits this characteristic, contingent upon the level of entrepreneurialism displayed by local leaders who, driven by practical considerations, eventually involve themselves in international affairs. Thus, the activism of these bureaucrats becomes a gauge for paradiplomatic activity, fluctuating between periods of increased involvement (go) and and decreased engagement (stop) based on their actions and directives.

    From another perspective, the academic community has demonstrated keen interest in the phenomenon of paradiplomacy. Despite being overlooked by major theoretical currents in the fields of International Relations and Political Science, recent scholarly works and empirical events unquestionably validate paradiplomacy as a relevant subject for analysis within these disciplines.

    Therefore, on one hand, there is a lack of consensus regarding a precise and universally applicable definition of paradiplomacy, and the significance of the topic is questioned by prominent existing theoretical currents. On the other hand, the importance of paradiplomatic activities is evident, as exemplified by the ongoing pandemic, wherein such activities have contributed to addressing issues that primarily impact regional and municipal scales within federal entities.

    In light of the aforementioned considerations, this study aims to address two overarching questions: First, what is the current state of the art of scientific production pertaining to the theme of paradiplomacy? Second, to what extent does this production reflect a stop-and-go pattern, contingent upon empirical evidence related to the international activities of subnational entities? Consequently, this article endeavours to analyse the contemporary landscape of scientific production on paradiplomacy, while exploring the presence of a stop-and-go trend evident in the empirical dynamics of subnational entities’ international activities.

    The rationale behind this study stems from the absence of existing works dedicated to a similar analysis within the realm of Systematic Literature Reviews and bibliometric analyses, particularly in the past five years (2017 to 2021). Consequently, this article seeks to contribute to the existing knowledge gap by examining the current state of the art in paradiplomacy. By doing so, it aims to enhance our understanding of the potential of paradiplomatic actions and serve as a foundation for future research in this field.

    Lastly, it is worth highlighting that paradiplomatic potential, as exemplified in the context of the new coronavirus pandemic, may present novel models for addressing local-level problem-solving and urban planning. The efficacy of top-down, one-size-fits-all policies has been called into question, necessitating the relevance of paradiplomacy and subnational autonomy in addressing their own challenges amidst the evident absence of the National State. This is especially crucial given the increasing socio-environmental vulnerabilities arising from this situation.

    Methodology

    The present research is characterized as an exploratory and qualitative study. Accordingly, a bibliometric analysis focused on specialized literature about paradiplomacy from the past five years (2017 to September 2021) was conducted. Bibliometrics, as defined by Spinak (1996: 35), examines the organization of scientific and technological sectors based on bibliographic sources and patents to identify actors, their contributions, relationships, and trends. Moreover, Donthu et al. (2021: 285) assert that bibliometric analysis is a popular and rigorous method for analysing and exploring large volumes of scientific data, unveiling evolutionary nuances within a specific field while highlighting emerging areas connected to the same area of observation (Donthu et al. 2021: 286).

    The documentary corpus used for the bibliometric analyses was derived from a Systematic Literature Review (SLR), which served as the foundation for selecting the studies to be included in the overall analysis and for explaining the results of the bibliometric analyses. A systematic literature review is characterized by following specific protocols and aims to comprehend and provide logical consistency to a substantial document corpus (Galvão and Ricarte 2020: 58). This method seeks to gather all empirical evidence that meets pre-specified eligibility criteria in order to address a specific research question (Higgins et al. 2019). Consequently, explicit and systematic methods are chosen to minimize biases, yielding more reliable findings from which conclusions can be drawn and decisions made (Higgins et al. 2019).

    To conduct the SLR, three bibliographic databases were utilized to collect information: Web of Science (WOS), Scopus, and Scientific Electronic Library (Scielo). The search criteria predominantly encompassed the broadest search field, typically labelled as ‘all fields’ In terms of the search strings, it was observed that the terms ‘paradiplomacia,’ ‘paradiplomacía,’ and ‘paradiplomacy’ significantly stood out compared to other terms used to refer to the international activities of subnational entities. Hence, the decision was made to exclusively employ these three terms.

    By adopting the last five years (2017 to September 2021) as the temporal analysis period, a preliminary selection of 120 works related to the central object of analysis was made. Subsequently, through reviewing the titles and abstracts of these materials and applying eligibility and inclusion criteria, as outlined in Table 1, a total of 75 scientific productions were selected. These selected works were read in their entirety to fulfil the objectives set forth in this study.

    Results and discussions

    Based on the analysis of the 75 selected scientific papers, as described in the methodology section, it is possible to observe a trend of continuity (go) and discontinuity (stop) in the works on paradiplomacy throughout the years under consideration, as depicted in Figure 1.

    For instance, in 2017, a total of 17 papers were published, and there was a slight decrease in research related to the field between 2018 and 2019. In 2020, there was a stagnation of academic activity compared to the previous year. However, in 2021, there was a significant increase, with a total of 18 productions until September. It is worth noting that a more comprehensive analysis covering the entire year might reveal even more productions.

    Furthermore, as previously mentioned, the objective of this study is to explore whether this stop-and-go trend in scientific production on paradiplomacy is correlated with political empiricism or specific conjunctures within each period. Thus, in addition to examining the quantity of works, this study also aims to investigate the thematic correlation between case studies and the years in which they were published, as well as the empirical events related to subnational diplomacy during those periods. By doing so, we hope to provide valuable insights for future research and contribute to a more comprehensive understanding of the topic.

    Regarding the year 2021, it is noteworthy to highlight its significance in the study of the internationalization of subnational entities, considering the substantial amount of work produced during this period. One hypothesis for the increased scientific production in 2021 is the impact of the new coronavirus pandemic.

    In fact, upon reviewing the literature, it is evident that approximately one-third of the works within the selected timeframe make considerations regarding the COVID-19 pandemic and its potential impacts on paradiplomacy. For example, the work of Yu et al. (2021) emphasizes the global health challenges brought forth by the pandemic, which have compelled city diplomacy to prioritize international cooperation in sharing best urban health practices and facilitating the donation of medical supplies among sister cities.

    Another example can be found in Guimarães and Silva (2021), who conducted an analysis of the involvement of the Brazilian Federal Government and its subnational entities in adopting policies recommended by the international community to address the COVID-19 pandemic. The article aimed to understand the paradiplomatic actions in this situation and their potential consequences for the Brazilian Federal System. The authors concluded that the participation of subnational entities was significant and important, especially considering the challenges faced by the federal government in establishing a national plan for combating the pandemic and vaccine distribution.

    However, it is important to acknowledge that reservations can be made regarding these measures when considering popular approval and disapproval. Although the paradiplomatic guidelines adopted by local governments were approved by many citizens, they also faced considerable opposition from society, despite being aligned with international norms for pandemic control. As the authors mentioned, one contributing factor for this objection may be the difficulties faced by federative entities in developing robust strategies to overcome the health crisis. This difficulty could be attributed to the lack of action plans and guidance from the federal government to support the public policies implemented by subnational entities in this field (Guimarães and Silva 2021).

    The work of Bhöm (2021) is also noteworthy, as it aimed to demonstrate the escalation of unilateralism brought about by the COVID-19 pandemic. The author highlighted the restrictions on cross-border flows and cooperation arrangements within the European Union, which were proposed by federal governments of member countries as measures to control people’s movement, a characteristic referred to as ‘covidfencing.’ The new coronavirus health crisis reintroduced borders within regional integration, creating complexity for individuals and paradiplomatic activities in border regions. The author discussed the relevance of cross-border cooperation and outlined five main roles derived from this practice, illustrating the influence of the pandemic outbreak on each of them.

    Furthermore, the strategic competition of influence in the Indo-Pacific region between the United States and China extends beyond the prerogatives of the national state. Subnational entities have played a central role in the development of actions within the geopolitical chessboard of the region. Tidwell (2021) conducted a study focusing precisely on this context, observing Beijing’s role in the Chinese Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) and the involvement of US federated entities such as Nevada, Wisconsin, and Washington in promoting and supporting the national defence of Pacific Island countries, including Tonga, Fiji, and Papua New Guinea.

    Therefore, Tidwell (2021) lightly explores the potential impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on future scenarios envisioned for paradiplomacy and the strategic relationships in the Indo-Pacific region. The author highlights four central factors that will influence the future context in the region, one of which is the economic consequences imposed by the pandemic. Tidwell argues that this economic concern will lead to a redirection in the search for Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) and new markets. Referring to the economic outlook presented by the World Bank, the author emphasizes the lasting scars that the coronavirus pandemic is likely to leave, including reduced investments, erosion of unemployed human capital, and a contraction in global trade and supply linkages. These predictions underscore the need to explore new forms of external investment and export markets, where paradiplomacy can play a vital role in achieving these objectives.

    However, the analysis of the correlation between variables inherent to the foreign policy of the United States and China, specifically regarding the international activities of subnational entities and the potential impacts of the pandemic in this context, was not exclusively conducted by Tidwell. Tubilewicz and Omond (2021) focused on illustrating the international engagement of subnational units in relation to the United States and its relations with China and Taiwan from 1949 to 2020. The authors argue that paradiplomacy should be seen not only as a low-profile policy of economic and cultural cooperation but also as high politics with due diplomatic recognition.

    The coronavirus pandemic has introduced new dynamics to the relationships between the countries involved. Tubilewicz and Omond (2021) found that paradiplomatic relations were initially developed through assistance from the governments of the Republic of China and Taiwan, mainly in the form of financial and surgical mask donations to central and subnational governments of the United States. On the other hand, some subnational entities, such as Florida and Missouri, took legal action against the Chinese government, accusing them of negligent and misleading behaviour during the early stages of the pandemic.

    Considering the discussions presented in this section, it is evident that paradiplomacy and the current global pandemic situation are indeed interconnected. By excluding the studies that examined the correlation between the coronavirus and the international engagement of subnational entities, the year 2021 would regress to the same level as 2020 in terms of the number of works within the analysed period. This reaffirms the trend of continuity and discontinuity in paradiplomacy studies as previously highlighted and suggests that real-world cases may have influenced this trend.

    The objective of this observation is to understand whether, during the year when the COVID-19 pandemic was consolidated (2020), research papers already addressed the health crisis as an influential factor in paradiplomatic affairs. This investigation aims to determine whether the stability in the number of research papers during this period, compared to the previous year (2019) which already showed a decline, was due to events related to the pandemic, potentially leading to an increase in research output, or whether there were other underlying reasons for this stabilization. This clarification would provide the final piece of information necessary to assess whether the new coronavirus indeed had a positive impact on the scientific production within the field of paradiplomacy.

    In this context, Alvarenga et al. (2020) exclusively focused on observing paradiplomatic relations in light of the COVID-19 pandemic. Their analysis centred on the international actions of the state of Maranhão, specifically the signing of internal and external agreements for the procurement of respiratory equipment without the consent of the federal government. This occurred during a period of intense competition for medical supplies and equipment. From this elucidation, it can be concluded that the international engagement of the Maranhão state government was driven by several factors: 1) the inefficiency and absence of assistance from the central government in combating the pandemic; 2) the exceptional circumstances created by the health crisis caused by the new coronavirus, which prompted subnational actors to seek alternative solutions to address their respective problems; and 3) Maranhão’s preexisting involvement in paradiplomatic affairs, which allowed for the affirmation of its commitment to Global Health Diplomacy.

    In a more concise study, Badillo Sarmiento, Galeano David and Rodríguez Molano (2020) analysed the paradiplomatic relations between Barranquilla (Colombia) and Nanjing (China) in the context of COVID-19. The mayor of Barranquilla, Jaime Pumarejo Heins, held a meeting with the Chinese ambassador to Colombia, Lan Hu, with the aim of establishing sister city ties between the two municipalities. As a result, Nanjing announced the donation of approximately 30 000 protective masks for health care professionals in Barranquilla. Additionally, the Colombian mayor expressed a commitment to strengthening the relations between the cities not only during times of crisis but also in the post-pandemic recovery phase.

    In summary, the two aforementioned studies encompass the entirety of scientific works that have examined the correlation between paradiplomacy and the health crisis caused by the new coronavirus, and were published in 2020. Therefore, the impact of COVID-19 on the growth of academic production related to paradiplomacy in recent years can be understood through the following points:

    The reality that the international system has been going through, in addition to the consequent efforts arising from subnational entities to deal with their own problems arising from the health crisis, could have acted as a beacon for academia to turn its attention to paradiplomatic activities. This reality, in a way, would also be able to demonstrate the relevance of paradiplomacy in issues involving their respective local problems. This is because, as observed throughout this section, almost 30% of the works published in this period mentioned COVID-19 as an influential factor on the international activity of subnational entities in such a context.

    A fundamental aspect of academic work is to provide fertile ground for ideas and future research to advance in the field. In this regard, the analyses conducted in this study suggest an upward trend in scientific research on paradiplomacy, likely driven by the pandemic. However, the confirmation of this hypothesis can only be solidified in the coming years, as we are still in the early stages of assimilating the implications of this scenario. Therefore, future publications will provide a more accurate assessment of whether the new coronavirus had a substantial impact on the academic sphere concerning paradiplomacy in 2021.

    Another important consideration relates to the precise timing of the elaboration of the works that were theoretically analysed as belonging to the year 2021. Although the publication date of the articles is the main criterion considered in this investigation, one should not underestimate the context in which the research was conducted. Therefore, some of these publications may have been in progress or under review in 2020. As a result, there is a retroactivity variable inherent in this analysis, implying that the idea proposed in point two regarding the future assessment of academic production on paradiplomacy and COVID-19 may indicate a surge in such works towards the end of 2021, consolidating further in 2022. This is the hypothesis put forward by this study.

    Returning to the initial point, where the aim is to understand whether scientific production on paradiplomacy, similar to praxis, can be characterized as stop-and-go, other aspects remain relevant and deserve special attention. Firstly, this research will further analyse the works that do not correlate the coronavirus and paradiplomacy in 2021 to explore from another perspective the reasons behind the observed exponential increase in that year.

    To examine the participation of countries in scientific productions related to the theme of paradiplomacy, Figure 2 was developed based on the spatial distribution of the 75 studies selected for this study.

    Figure 2 shows the geographic distribution of scientific productions on paradiplomacy analysed through Scopus, Web of Science, and Scielo (2017-2021). Notably, China stands out among the countries that have presented studies on paradiplomacy during the considered period. The international activities of Chinese provinces have played a significant role in scientific production on paradiplomacy. Just as approximately one-third of the analysed works in 2021 addressed the relationship between subnational diplomacy and COVID-19, an equal number of studies focused on China and its subnational entities, accounting for six case studies out of 18 works related to that year.

    In addition to the works already analysed on Chinese sub-state diplomacy in this literature review, other notable studies include those by Kossa, Lomaeva and Saunavaara (2021), Liu and Song (2021) and Stella Juste (2021). Figure 3 represents the thematic distribution of scientific productions throughout the year 2021, providing further insight into the discussion.

    However, it is worth noting that in the previous year (2020), four more productions focused on case studies examining the international activities of Chinese provinces, including works by Cheung (2020), Liu and Song (2020), Oviedo (2020), and Ginanjar et al. (2020). Therefore, the period between 2020 and 2021 accounted for a total of 10 scientific research studies on this subject, compared to the remaining 13 productions over the five-year period analysed. The remaining three works are distributed among other years, with one publication in 2019 (Chan 2019), another in 2018 (Neves 2018), and one more in 2017 (Tubilewics 2017). Thus, based on this bibliometric analysis, it can be concluded that studies on subnational entities’ paradiplomacy in China, along with the pandemic scenario imposed by COVID-19, have had a substantial impact on academic productions in 2020 and 2021.

    Moreover, it is also worth noting that in these last two observed years, other countries have also made significant contributions. For instance, the United States of America accounted for three productions in 2021, as mentioned by Tidwell (2021) and Tubilewicz and Omond (2021), who analysed paradiplomacy in the context of China-USA relations, and Jaursch (2021) who discussed the participation of subnational US entities in transatlantic trade negotiations.

    Brazil, on the other hand, had three other productions in 2020. These include: 1) Teixeira and Cichovski (2020) depicting the issue of federalism in the management of the Amazon Rainforest; 2) the previously mentioned production by Alvarenga et al. (2020) on the state of Maranhão’s response to the COVID-19 pandemic; and 3) Froio and Medeiros (2020) who examined paradiplomacy between Brazil and Europe.

    Furthermore, the remaining research did not exhibit significant continuity for an analysis within the scope of the years 2021 and 2020. The case studies addressed various specific themes without an established connection. In fact, this has been a recurring trend over the observed years: the prominence of a certain subject in a given period of time followed by random or isolated case studies. The year 2019, for example, exemplifies this characteristic well, with Canada accounting for five productions out of the 13 works related to the year, followed by Argentina (three) and a diverse range of random thematic cases.

    Having presented the geographical distribution of scientific production on the subject study, particularly highlighting the weight of Chinese paradiplomacy productions in 2020 and 2021, the works that focused on paradiplomacy from the perspective of 2019 will now be discussed, particularly emphasizing the prominence of Canadian provinces in this context.

    Kharel, Romsdahl and Kirilenko (2019) examined the idiosyncrasies related to flood management in the Devils Lake area, situated on the border between Canada and the United States. The research highlights that the persistent problem in the region is unlikely to be solved through formal actions. The authors conclude that current mitigation plans and watershed management strategies for Devils Lake are based on historical understandings of socio-economic dynamics related to climate and are guided by traditional tools that have proven inadequate in addressing these issues over time. Furthermore, these policies have been found to be inflexible in dealing with issues that require emergency and immediate actions. Therefore, the authors propose a framework known as ‘green paradiplomacy’ and argue that ‘we believe that green paradiplomacy has the potential for long-term management of transboundary environmental problems through collaboration and negotiation between local stakeholders’ (Kharel, Romsdahl and Kirilenko 2020: 16).

    Sarson (2019), on the other hand, brings attention to the limited recognition given to paradiplomacy carried out by indigenous subnational governments. The work demonstrates the impact of these actors in negotiating new relationships internally and externally, as well as their role in forming economic cooperation, engaging private companies to raise capital, financing economic missions, and establishing offices in strategic locations to enhance investment opportunities in resource extraction projects. The research focuses on paradiplomacy of indigenous governments from the perspective of Canada and the United States, specifically within the context of the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA).

    On the other hand, Stren and Friendly (2017) conduct a comparative examination of the international activities of the municipalities of São Paulo and Toronto. They investigate how mayors have adapted to new challenges and opportunities brought about by urbanization and economic development, and how paradiplomacy in this context can contribute to changing the understanding of local policies.

    The authors highlight some inconsistent paths taken by the municipalities throughout their history of paradiplomacy. Toronto’s trajectory is similar to what was addressed at the beginning of the article, where paradiplomacy is directly linked to the entrepreneurial approach of government leaders who sometimes prioritize international activities according to their directives (go) or give them little importance (stop). However, according to Stren and Friendly (2017), since 1990, Toronto has experienced fluctuations in its paradiplomatic activities, and the current mayor, John Tory, has undertaken various external initiatives and signed international agreements since taking office in 2014.

    Regarding the second research question about whether scientific productions on paradiplomacy follow a stop-and-go pattern in practice or autonomously, the text suggests that in 2019, academic studies on the topic heavily focused on Canada and its subnational entities. Interestingly, four years after John Tory’s election as mayor, there was a boom in academic activity examining the paradiplomacy of Canadian cities during the period under review.

    Therefore, it is inevitable to question this situation: is it possible to infer, then, that the entrepreneurial approach imposed by the Mayor of Toronto helped boost studies on the diplomacy of subnational entities in the country? In this context, it is plausible to believe that the realities presented during the government of John Tory may have shed light on issues related to paradiplomacy, which consequently became the focus of academic inquiry.

    Furthermore, works related to Canada and the significance of its subnational entities in the global arena can be found in the works of Ackrén (2019), who elucidates paradiplomatic relations in the North Atlantic and Artic region, using the Faroe Islands (a dependent territory of Denmark), Greenland (an autonomous territory belonging to Denmark), Nunavut (Canada) and Svalbard (Norway). Cantir (2019), on the other hand, provides a theoretical analysis of literature related to sub-state diplomacy, drawing insights derived from ethnic conflicts and nationalist studies. The author examines Quebec, South Tyrol, and the Åland Islands (Finland) as case studies and their roles in exacerbating or reducing conflicts between these subnational actors and their respective kin states (France-Canada, Austria-Italy, and Sweden-Finland, respectively).

    From a new perspective, while the analysed works from 2019 to 2021 presented emphasis on specific countries or situations – such as China, Canada, and COVID-19 – the year 2018 did not exhibit such characteristics. However, what could be observed is that, for that year, scientific productions focused on the continental context. Thus, Latin America, for example, was represented in seven case studies on the diplomacy of subnational units in the region, with works distributed across countries such as Chile, Brazil, Bolivia, Peru, Paraguay, and Argentina. In general, it can be observed that the continent accounted for exactly half of the academic research for the year. Consequently, as previously mentioned, there was a diverse range of random works that encompassed regions such as Poland, the Czech Republic, the United Arab Emirates, the United States, Mexico, China, and Indonesia.

    In addition to the prominent presence of Latin America in studies on paradiplomacy during this period, there is also a trend towards works that sought to analyse the diplomacy of subnational entities with a focus on cross-border issues. Oddone, Rodríguez Vázquez and Quiroga Barrera Oro (2018) paid particular attention to these paradiplomatic experiences that encompassed Latin America, considering the Thematic Unit of Environment and Sustainable Development of the Mercocities Network, as well as the European Union through the Pacto del Alcalde en Cambio Climático.

    On the other hand, Ovando-Santana and Sergio González-Miranda (2018) analysed the role played by paradiplomacy carried out by actors from the societies of Tacna (Peru) and Arica (Chile) with the aim of achieving social peace that would benefit them. In this scenario, border agencies and their respective consulates had significant participation, often going against the guidelines imposed by the foreign policy of their countries.

    However, the authors did not limit themselves to the aforementioned case study. In ‘La dimensión identitaria de las expresiones paradiplomáticas entre Bolivia y Chile: una lectura desde dos otredades,’ the objective of the study was to understand the identity dimension of local groups and the paradiplomatic expressions between northern Chile and the central-west region of Bolivia, primarily in regions such as Oruro and Tarapacá. Feitosa do Nascimento and Machado de Oliveira (2018), on the other hand, viewed paradiplomacy as a tool for cross-border cooperation to solve local problems, as well as an object of opportunity prediction that could be shared among actors in border regions, both in relation to Brazil and Paraguay and Bolivia. The study, therefore, focused on the cities of Ponta Porã, Pedro Juan Caballero, and Corumbá/Ladário – Puerto Quijarro/Puerto Suárez.

    Finally, Nava and Córdova (2018), through a descriptive study that addressed cross-border paradiplomacy from the perspective of the Reynosa (Mexico) and McAllen (United States) regions, shed light on issues related to the economic development of these cities in the context of the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA). The study observed interactions between the governments of Reynosa and Tamaulipas and the McAllen Economic Development Corporation (MEDC), which facilitated employment and investment in the region, generating economic development in this border area.

    Therefore, the year 2018 demonstrated the contribution that studies on paradiplomacy in Latin America, particularly those involving cross-border cooperation, during that period. Unlike the years dominated by scientific productions on Chinese and Canadian subnational entities and the COVID-19 pandemic, where there were empirical reasons for the increase in academic works on these topics, the explanation for the prevalence of cases involving Latin American subnational actors in 2018 cannot be inferred from the included works. Thus, it is suggested that further studies analyse the reasons behind this trend, delving deeper into the literature review specifically for Latin American subnational entities, to understand the factors that led to the high number of scientific productions on the topic not only in 2018 but also in 2017.

    On the other hand, contrary to the patterns observed in other years, the year 2017 did not demonstrate any thematic linearity. However, it is worth noting the significant number of works on paradiplomacy in 2017, making it the second-highest year in terms of scientific productions within the presented timeframe.

    As seen in previous years, while there was a specific theme that stood out among various random cases, the year 2017 showed the opposite trend, with a relatively higher number of cases without thematic coherence compared to previous years. Therefore, the hypothesis proposed by this study suggests that in the years prior to 2017, some significant events may have influenced the surge in studies on paradiplomacy in specific contexts, particularly in the American and European continents, as depicted in Figure 4.

    According to the illustration, continents such as America and Europe experienced a peak in publications during the investigated period. To comprehend the reasons behind this prominence, it is necessary to analyse the years preceding 2017 in order to identify nuances, conjunctures, and events that could explain both the increase in random cases in the American and European continents and the deviation from the observed trend in the subsequent years (2018, 2019, 2020, and 2021). However, this exercise is beyond the scope of this work, which encourages further research to delve into and unravel the problem elucidated here.

    Final considerations

    The present study aimed to assess the state of the art of recent scientific production (in the last five years) on the topic of paradiplomacy. Additionally, it sought to determine whether such production followed the stop-and-go trend, as pointed out by Vigevani (2006), and to observe if this trend was driven by paradiplomatic empiricism or by a specific conjuncture. Therefore, the analysis of the periods and the quantity of studies existing in these time frames allows us to conclude that, in general, scientific production on paradiplomacy follows a stop-and-go trend. This characteristic is represented by the prominence of specific cases over the years, as observed in 2021, 2020, 2019, and 2018, moments in which works on subnational diplomacy focused on certain topics that stood out during the bibliometric analysis, such as the rise of productions related to COVID-19 (2021), China (2021 and 2020), Canada (2019), and Latin America (2018).

    However, the reason for the decline in these productions between 2018 and 2020, despite the prominence of works related to specific themes in those years, remains uncertain. One hypothesis raised here concerns random cases, as in the years with the highest number of scientific productions (such as 2017 and 2021), there were considerable works analysing isolated cases. Perhaps, just as in political reality, these random cases could be stimulated by the entrepreneurship of the bureaucrats responsible for the international actions of subnational entities and, therefore, could influence the academic field that deals with the topic. This may explain what was observed in 2017. However, it is important to highlight the importance of further studies that can expand the analyses carried out in this work.

    From another perspective, the year 2021 brought to light the coexistence of two main issues: the international activities of Chinese provinces and the COVID-19 pandemic. As observed during the analyses related to this period, random cases could be influenced not only by the entrepreneurship of bureaucrats responsible for paradiplomacy but also by specific conjunctures that highlight the importance of paradiplomatic movements on certain occasions. This could explain the abundance of random cases in 2021. Thus, the paradiplomatic performance of subnational entities in the fight against the COVID-19 pandemic may have influenced academia to observe real cases about paradiplomacy and its importance. Therefore, the pandemic, in addition to increasing the number of studies on paradiplomacy within its scope, may have boosted paradiplomatic studies as a whole.

    Regarding the increase in studies on paradiplomacy related to Chinese subnational entities in 2021 and 2020, the hypotheses that could demonstrate the relevance of the subject during these periods are somewhat obscure. According to studies conducted on the region, studies on paradiplomacy in China did not include the international activities carried out by subnational entities in the country for a long time, given that China is a non-democratic and authoritarian country. This culminated in difficulties in understanding this scenario over the years. However, the current observed change in this spectrum, with an increase in cases related to Chinese paradiplomacy, along with some factors that could have influenced the increase in scientific production on the subject, suggests that the creation of China’s Belt & Road Initiative (BRI) may have played a role. This external policy proposed by Xi Jiping has brought prominence to the theme, as indicated by all the academic research observed in 2021.

    In 2019, the relevance of Toronto for issues related to municipal paradiplomacy in Canada was evident in this research. The city’s international performance may have redirected attention to non-central Canadian paradiplomatic actors.

    Finally, when this work aimed to analyse the prominence of paradiplomatic activity in Latin America in 2018, as well as the preponderance of the American continent on this issue, the difficulty of correlating the variables was already understood. Although cases inherent to paradiplomacy in Latin America are often accompanied by studies that dealt with border issues, it cannot be accurately affirmed that this theme was driven by some empirical case, as paradiplomacy and porosity between borders have been objects of debate since the emergence of the theoretical field. Likewise, there are still no systematic analyses to understand why the American continent is a central pillar in the scientific production on paradiplomacy.

    Therefore, in order to answer the first guiding question of this study, which aimed to assess the state of the art of current scientific production on paradiplomacy, the following can be concluded: the state of the art on contemporary paradiplomacy, from a bibliometric perspective, is characterized by the he stop-and-go trend regarding scientific production in the field, along with specific themes that have emerged throughout each period, accompanied by random cases that may or may not be influenced by bureaucratic entrepreneurship or specific circumstances. Furthermore, there is a predominance of productions related to China, the COVID-19 pandemic, Canada, and the American continent.

    In conclusion, this study emphasizes the importance of the proposed research for a comprehensive understanding of paradiplomacy and its nuances, particularly in a period when the subject is being closely observed. The denial of science, reinforced by the dissemination of fake news, may threaten the relevance of paradiplomacy’s potential for problem-solving at the local level.

    Therefore, this research, by venturing into a relatively unexplored space within the specialized literature on paradiplomacy, sheds light on some questions that emerged from the bibliometric analysis. In this regard, it offers new insights to the theoretical field, which can be further explored by future studies. This may be the main contribution of this work to the academic sphere that encompasses the subject, as it proposes a methodological model that has received little attention in examining the performance of subnational entities in international relations and the corresponding outcomes that arise from this context.

    Furthermore, considering the significance of other cases and studies related to the subject matter, which could not be included in this article due to its temporal scope and selection of databases, we suggest that future research delve deeper into these contexts. It is recommended to expand both the number of bibliographic databases utilized and the period considered in order to enhance the analysis and contributions made in this article.

    Finally, in line with Ribeiro’s observations (2009), this work reaffirms the importance of paradiplomacy in problem-solving at the local level. Throughout the discussion of the results, it was evident that paradiplomatic actions play a role in addressing issues in cities, provinces, and other federated entities. These actions encompass cross-border cooperation, municipal twinning, joint strategies in addressing socio-environmental problems, technical and economic cooperation, and other forms of activities in this context. Thus, this clarification helps demonstrate the intrinsic relevance and effectiveness of paradiplomatic policies. It underscores their significance not only in the academic dimension, where these attributes are often overlooked, but also as a guide for decision-makers at various levels.
