The Central American Northern Triangle States’ (NTS) relationship with Israel and Palestine: Understanding small states’ foreign policy decision-making

As relações dos Estados do Triângulo do Norte com Israel e Palestina: entendendo as mudanças na política externa de pequenos Estados para além da dominação

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    In recent years, El Salvador, Honduras and Guatemala - which make up the so-called Northern Triangle States (NTS) - have maintained changing relations with Israel and Palestine. Alternating between rapprochement and distancing, it is intriguing that such small states intervene in diplomatic issues that are geographically far from them and costly in diplomatic terms. If NTSs have historically been under the influence of Washington, we take some distance from mainstream conceptions of alignment and hegemonic domination, by exploring the various determinants of three Central American states’ foreign policy changes. We argue that the NTS’ foreign policies toward Israel and Palestine is a means to speak to Washington and a diplomatic relationship that is highly reactive to democratic regime changes, presidential preferences and diasporic influences. Building upon Hermann’s conceptualization of foreign policy change, we examine empirically the variations of the NTSs’ foreign policy toward Israel and Palestine between 2009 and 2023. This article makes a major contribution to the comparative analysis of Latin American foreign policy and addresses a wider IR audience by demonstrating that despite the strong structural characteristic of smallness, domestic and leadership factors should not be underestimated when it comes to understanding changes in small states’ foreign policies.

    Key words
    foreign policy changes; small states; Israel; Palestine; Northern Triangle States

    Resumo

    Nos últimos anos, El Salvador, Honduras e Guatemala - que compõem os chamados Estados do Triângulo do Norte (ETN) - têm mantido relações mutáveis com Israel e Palestina. Alternando entre aproximação e distanciamento, intriga o fato de que tais pequenos Estados intervenham em questões diplomáticas geograficamente distantes e custosas em termos diplomáticos. Se historicamente os ETNs estiveram sob a influência de Washington, tomamos alguma distância das concepções predominantes de alinhamento e dominação hegemônica, ao explorar os diversos determinantes das mudanças na política externa de três Estados centro-americanos. Argumentamos que as políticas externas dos ETNs em relação a Israel e Palestina constituem um meio de se dirigir a Washington e uma relação diplomática altamente reativa a mudanças de regime democrático, preferências presidenciais e influências diaspóricas. Com base na conceitualização de mudança de política externa de Hermann, examinamos empiricamente as variações da política externa dos ETNs em relação a Israel e Palestina entre 2009 e 2023. Este artigo oferece uma contribuição relevante para a análise comparada da política externa latino-americana e dialoga com um público mais amplo de Relações Internacionais ao demonstrar que, apesar da forte característica estrutural da pequenez, fatores domésticos e de liderança não devem ser subestimados quando se trata de compreender mudanças na política externa de pequenos Estados.

    Palavras-chave
    mudanças na política externa; pequenos Estados; Israel; Palestina; Estados do Triângulo do Norte

    Introduction

    Since the 1970s, the positions of Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador towards the Israeli-Palestinian question have been varied, echoing a long and heterogeneous path of diplomatic relationships of these so-called Northern Triangle States (NTS) with Israel and Palestine. Throughout the period of civil war and of right-wing dictatorships (1970s – 1980s), the three countries maintained strong relations with Israel, largely based on military support for right-wing dictatorships. In the 1970s, Honduras and El Salvador (along with Costa Rica) even moved their embassies from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem. Later, during the democratic transitions of the 1990s, bilateral investment treaties with Israel were signed by El Salvador and Guatemala (Özşahin and Tekin 2020). Nonetheless, in November 2011, the NTS voted in favour of the resolution to include Palestine as an observer state to the United Nations Organization (UN). One month earlier, in October 2011, they had supported the entry of Palestine to the United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization (UNESCO).

    Then, into the second decade of the 21st century, choices towards Israel seemingly became more frequent. In December 2017, Guatemalan President Jimmy Morales decided to move its Embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem (December 2017), followed, two years later, by the Honduran President Juan Orlando Hernandez. In contrast, in El Salvador, President Sánchez-Cerén opened a Palestinian Embassy in 2018. In 2023, after the Hamas’s terrorist attacks in Israel on 7 October, Guatemala and Honduras were quick to condemn the assault and to reiterate their strong support for Israel, while El Salvador’s president described Hamas’ members as ‘savage beasts’.

    In face of those diplomatic vagaries, it remains intriguing that such small states act in a highly global strategic and divisive political issue in ways which make them vulnerable to important material costs coming from, for instance, shifts in US economic policy or international stigmatization (Adler-Nissen 2014). The smallness of Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador is both structural and relational (Long 2022) and has major implications for their foreign policy (Cooper and Shaw 2009; Panke 2013; Braveboy-Wagner 2016). Historically, a prevailing logic of alignment and limited autonomy have resulted from Washington’s influence. Indeed, the mainstream realist conception explains that these Central American states have bandwagoned systematically with the United States (Waltz 1979; Sanchez 2003).

    We propose to take some distance from mainstream conceptions of alignment and hegemonic domination, by asking the following questions: How to account for variations either in orientation or intensity in the NTSs’ relationship with Israel/Palestine and, more importantly, what do they say about their foreign policy decision-making?

    Our argument is twofold. We argue that foreign policy making in our cases is best deciphered by looking at the synergy between the domestic context – particularly, the evolution of institutional-political arrangements – and the necessity to talk to Washington. In domestic politics, Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador have been going through an aggravation of authoritarianism by a democratic breakdown (Schedler 2010) in the case of Honduras 2009, and a slow and incremental democratic regression, in the cases of Guatemala and El Salvador. Democratic regression is understood as ‘incremental strangulation of democracy by elected executives who gradually eviscerate institutional checks, political opposition, independent media, and other forces of scrutiny and resistance in civil society’ (Diamond 2021). This evolution has been marked normally accompanied by public insecurity, economic difficulties and rising societal (opposition) demands, as well as authoritarianism. It has also steered a deterioration of the relationship with the USA (Parthenay 2020). In face of this kind of scenario, key decision-makers in the three countries perceive a window of opportunity to engage Israel and/or Palestine and act accordingly.

    This research will contribute mainly to the examination of patterns of change and continuity in Latin American foreign policies (Gardini and Lambert 2011), the discussion of the regime type-foreign policy link, the analytical perspective that calls to overcome the dichotomy between International Relations’ focus on structures and Foreign Policy Analysis’ emphasis on the domestic and individual factors of state action (Kaarbo 2015).

    The article is structured as follows. In the first section, we critically review the state of the art on the foreign policies of Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador among the Latin American foreign policy literature and the international relations perspective on small states. In the second part we present the key components of our analytical framework, oriented towards both the foreign policy choices and the decision-making process. Part three traces empirically the actions taken by NTS with Israel and Palestine from 2009 to 2024. In part four, we evaluate the foreign policy decisions in light of the executives’ role in the interplay between the domestic and the international pressures. Finally, we offer some concluding remarks and identify paths for future research.

    Small states’ Latin American foreign policies: where stand the Central American Northern Triangle States (NTS)?

    The foreign policies of NTS are caught in a double-blind spot: the first is in the field of Latin American foreign policy studies, in a comparative politics dimension, and the second is in the field of foreign policy studies of small states in a more international relations dimension.

    NTS and Latin American foreign policies

    Among Latin American foreign policy studies, the scarce analysis of the three NTS’s foreign policies can be partly due to the excessive focus on the logic of alignment (and rare misalignments) with Washington, determined either by economic and security considerations and, more recently, by ideological ones – with the arrival of leftist governments (Portillo Vaquerano 2010; Solis and Matul 2004; González Davison 2023). In this regard, the NTSs’ external policies have been examined mainly through the impact of neighbouring states’ actions (Canada, USA and Mexico), and on the variations of those neighbours’ policies towards the NTS (Nuñez and Davila 2022; Díaz 2018). The only exception to this traditional pattern of external dominance would be the Contadora Peace initiative in the end of the 1980s (Rouquié 1994; Ojeda 2007), that saw a peace proposal emerge from and for the region. Since the 1990s, several works have examined the consequences of the Central American democratic transition on foreign policy —mostly focusing on regional cooperation (Sanahuja and Sotillo 1998). Few other studies focus on the links between civil conflict and foreign relations (Schulz 1994); border conflictivity or legal disputes have also captured academic attention, particularly those between Honduras and El Salvador, or Guatemala and Belize (Kauil Poot 2015). Still, there has been relatively little work on their external behaviour. Even in the books by Gardini and Lambert (2011), and Mora and Hey (2004), the foreign policies of these states are viewed solely through the prism of their relations with the United States. Most have highlighted structural determinants: poverty on the one hand –as a vector of dependence on the outside world–, and political instability and insecurity (or criminality) on the other –as justifications for external assistance or intervention, mainly by the USA. The US intervention in Guatemala by the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) to overthrow the Arbenz regime (1954), justified by the fight against the spread of communism, is one of the most emblematic episodes in this regard. Later, Rosenberg and Solis pointed out that the central dynamics in Central America in the post-Cold War context was the neoliberal agenda (Rosenberg and Solís 2007: 51), particularly in the frame of the negotiation of the Central American Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA) signed in 2004 and effective in 2006. Certainly, the US security agenda in the region along with economic cooperation projects (Central American Free Trade Agreement) monopolized the attention and imposed a specific reading of the region’s foreign relations, exclusively felling under US dominance. At the end of the 2000s, a new divide emerged, particularly about cooperation with the so-called Bolivarian initiatives promoted by Hugo Chavez’s Venezuela. Some states started to develop closer relationship with Venezuela but following the same asymmetrical dimension, that is say economic dependency (Petrocaribei) and political regional hegemony in the Bolivarian regional organization (ALBA). The academic discussion so far has not undertaken systematic explanations either of the diversification of their international relations or their foreign policy processes.

    NTS and small states foreign policies

    The characteristics of NTS states raise questions about the very notion of state. Both Honduras, El Salvador and Guatemala navigate among multiple conceptions: from a flexible definition of ‘small states; (Long 2022), to more radical definitions of ‘quasi-states’ (Jackson 1991; Alden and Aran 2017), ‘captured’ or even ‘failed states’ (Gomez 2020). Smallness is a structural feature; it is part and parcel of their identity and contributes to shape their international status. Deemed as small states in IR, they share specific features that impact their foreign policies decision-making.

    First, there are elements relating to the economy (Gross National Product, GNP), the size of the country, or its demography (Panke 2013). These elements converge to limit the states’ capabilities to regulate at the domestic level and project their interests at the international level. Through the period under study (2009-2024), the main indicators that make it possible to objectify the NTS smallness do not change. Second, small states are confronted with an asymmetrical relationship with great powers. In our case, the US presence has historically been significant. Indeed, the hegemonic status of the USA in the hemisphere has been a strong conditioning factor of NTS decisions in a range of foreign policy issue-areas. Today, extra regional presence also includes China, and to a lesser extent Russia (at least until the outbreak of the Ukrainian war, in February 2022). Third, small states are also in situations of vulnerability to exogenous factors (natural disasters, climate change, economic shocks) (Cooper and Shaw 2009), and are closely linked to international institutions for providing goods. It is particularly true for NTS that are annually affected by natural disasters (hurricane, floods, droughts) (Parthenay 2021). Fourth, small states have low projection capabilities outside their immediate (regional) environment (Panke 2013). This explains why so few studies deal with NTSs’ foreign policies beyond the US influence, although it has been stated that ‘even the smallest states need not necessarily today limit themselves to subregional influence but can have some outreach well beyond that level’ (Braveboy-Wagner 2016: 8). This potential can be deployed under certain conditions and leadership (Braveboy Wagner 2010) and is normally reflected in the choice of specific foreign policy instruments. For instance, small states tend to invest in multilateral strategies to benefit from leverage effects (Panke 2013) and/or guarantee their security; they might also resort to more pragmatic strategies including diversification, cooperation, alliances, and coalition-building. As an empirical and analytical blind spot, this perspective must be explored for NTS.

    Another feature of small states is the structural predominance of political leaders, mostly executives, in the foreign policy decision-making (Hey 2003). In that regard, the question of the regime-type is crucial, as well as its nexus with the foreign policy. Regarding this regime type foreign policy nexus, increasing attention has been paid to authoritarian practices, understood as ‘patterns of action that sabotage accountability to people over whom a political actor exerts control, or their representatives, by means of secrecy, disinformation and disabling voice’ (Glasius 2018: 517). Arguing that authoritarian executive powers affect foreign policy (Kaarbo and Lantis 2024), some have demonstrated that in political systems where autocratic practices have the upper hand, foreign policy serves to normalize the process whereby oppositional power blocks are excluded, marginalized, and silenced (Weeks and Crunkilton 2017), and/or where domestic struggles for power and identity take place (Yavuz 2022). Recent research on Latin America has focused on the effect of authoritarianism and populism on the external behaviour of Latin American countries (Brun, Rodríguez and Rico 2022). They demonstrate that one feature of the authoritarian turn is the centralization of foreign policy decision-making around the leader. Throughout the past decade, the political regimes of Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador have shown autocratic excesses and hyper-presidentialisation. Such evolution has been tainted with the exacerbation of personalisation, as well as by an increasing militarisation of politics, seen as an effective means to control the population and silence the opposition. Personalisation correlates with ‘presidential preferences’ rather than (political) ideology, since the latter has a lower imprint on political competition and foreign policy programs in Central America (Dabène 2023). While it is asserted that foreign policy of regimes whose democracies are backsliding often fall through the emergence of ‘strong men’ or autocratic leaders, we posit that variations may exist within regime types and affect differently the foreign policy decision-making. If we agree on the importance of leaders, further research and explanation of the factors leading to the decision-making of these same leaders is needed. In Latin America, this is particularly relevant not least because Latin American countries’ constitutions give prerogative to heads of states in foreign policy (Tawil 2016; Tawil and Brun 2022; Baeza 2012). In Latin America, the presidents are the intervening agents between the political system and the international system, in the sense that they are the one who defines which foreign policy instruments are prioritised, and what type of change is needed. Merke, Reynoso and Schenoni (2020) find, for instance, that presidential systems in Latin America that exhibit higher levels of concentration of power in the head of state are inherently more prone to policy changes. In the NTS, the positioning towards the Palestine-Israel question is not driven by the diplomatic bureaucracy, but by Presidents and research must be conducted to understand how the positions change. In that regard, we explore the constraints on presidents and authoritarian leaders primarily stemming from within the system of government, and from how power is shared between the executive and other governmental institutions. We also contend that the power of the executive may depend on the support, cooperation, or subjugation of other key political actors (Kaarbo and Lantis 2024: 328-329). Within states, foreign policy decision-makers depend on the support of certain constituencies as well as of the political elite. Concerning the latter, NTS face stable and persistent oligarchical social models inherited from colonial times (Torres Rivas 2013; Casaús Arzú 2010). With regards to constituencies, even though the institutional characteristics of presidential power are relatively constant, presidential autonomy from Palestinian and pro-Israeli groups (which support alternative foreign policy courses) in the NTS can vary over time and under different domestic political arrangements, leading to different degrees of commitment towards them. They must be accounted for.

    Data and method

    In this article, we assess foreign policy decision-making in light of four indicators. First of all, the number of visits and meetings between official authorities (with both Israel and Palestine). Official visits at the highest level, or the absence thereof, embody personal commitment or disengagement in a bilateral relationship (Brun 2018) and can reflect radical shifts in a country’s traditional positions. Second, the transfers (or will to) of Embassies from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem, as it is a highly symbolic diplomatic gesture in favour of Israel. This highly symbolic move is a useful tool to assess change in foreign policy. Third, the cooperation in the military and economic fields (policy cooperation). Not only does the development of cooperation signal the strength of a country’s commitment to another, but military cooperation reflects a special bond in that it is part of a framework linked to national security and closely related to sovereignty. Economic cooperation, meanwhile, can be indicative of a desire to diversify relations. Four, the official recognition of Palestine as a state is a divisive diplomatic commitment that allows for better positioning of the foreign policy pursued by a presidential administration and the interests associated with it. All these indicators will help determine variations in the foreign policies of NTS states on the Israel/Palestine issue, which will then make it possible to explain these changes through an exploration of decision-making.

    To proceed to the empirical examination, we explore the period 2009-2024. Our study begins in 2009, considering the Honduran coup d’état as a turning point in the region, both in terms of domestic politics and relations with the USA. It ends in 2024, a few months after the Hamas attacks that led to more global diplomatic changes. We use a qualitative methodology based on data collected from official documents by Ministries of Foreign Relations of the three states, in particular the Annual Reports (Informe de Labores or Memorias Institucionales). Those documents give information on activities developed at the bilateral level between countries, for instance on the number of official visits (who, when, what purpose), the agreements (MoUs, treaties, conventions, etc.) adopted between national authorities and bilateral cooperation. We also draw upon media coverage and secondary sources from academic studies. The adoption of such a triangulation strategy increases the credibility of findings.

    NTS’s foreign policies toward Israel and Palestine: an empirical analysis.

    From 2009 to 2024, NTSs’ stances reflect a changing and diverse combination of domestic and external variables whose influence must be weighed in line with each topic or period analysed. The hegemonic status of the USA in the hemisphere undoubtedly has beard heavily on their behaviour, as it has for other countries of the continent. But recognizing the importance of this structural variable is not the same as saying that it dictates meaning to governments. As will be shown below, the key decision-makers (see Annex 1) in our NTS manoeuvre international actors and forces by engaging in specific actions in the diplomatic field towards Israel and Palestine, specifically when relations are weakly institutionalised, as it is the case for the links between Latin America and the Middle East (Tawil and Brun 2022).

    Honduras

    The coup which overthrew President Manuel Zelaya in June 2009 and put Porfirio Lobo in his place (after Roberto Micheletti’s interim) was received by the US administration with a congratulation to Lobo ‘for restoring democratic and constitutional order”. One of the first actions towards the Middle East taken by Porfirio Lobo’s presidency was the recognition of Palestine as a ‘free, sovereign and independent State’ in 2011. In May 2013, Palestinian Foreign Minister Riyad Al-Maliki visited Honduras, and agreements were signed aiming at establishing permanent embassies. Israel reacted to Honduras’s recognition of the Palestine state with surprise and indignation. The Deputy Director General for Latin America, Dorit Shavit, reminded the Honduran Ambassador that ‘Israel had been with Honduras two years ago, when it went through an institutional crisis that led to widespread condemnation throughout the world’, a reference to the 2009’s coup (Keinon 2011).

    Porfirio Lobo was succeeded by President Juan Orlando Hernández in 2014. Hernández, who had been accused of having links with drug trafficking, declared victory in a highly contested presidential election, sparking massive street protests and helping fuel outward migration. Under his presidency, relations with Israel took hold while democratic regression aggravated. Authoritarian practices through constitutional circumvention, intensification of the control and repression of opposition of all kinds (political or emanating from civil society) were denounced by the US administration. Washington’s reaction was one of disapproval. Just two months before Hernández first visit to Israel, 21 members of Congress asked US secretary of state John Kerry to reconsider whether military aid to Honduras was permissible under US law, in light of the Honduran security forces’ involvement in suppressing internal dissent (Mac 2022). In late October 2015, Hernández visited Israel together with the Honduran Defence minister and army chief of staff. The interest was mutual. Israeli Prime Minister B. Netanyahu sought to leverage the ascendance of right-wing governments in Latin America, paying several visits to the region; it was his fourth since 2007. Honduras turned to Israel. In December 2016, Honduras signed with Israel an agreement which included $200 million in defence contracts and entailed the refurbishment of the Honduran Air Force’s entire helicopter fleet, as well as many of its fixed-wing aircraft (Mac 2022).

    In return for Hernández’s support in international forums, the Netanyahu government not only armed the President’s security forces, but seemingly aided Hernández’s re-election in November 2017. Indeed, a month before the Honduran election, in October 2017, the Israeli Ambassador in Honduras, Matanya Cohen, met with the Honduran Defence minister (Mac 2022). The later issued an official statement that was meant to serve Hernández’s election campaign, quoting Ambassador Cohen about a plan to strengthen the Honduran navy, air force and ground forces. While details of the security agreement were classified in Israel (Staff 2022), it is known that part of this agreement included equipment that could also be used for the suppression of domestic opponents. The following decision came therefore as no surprise: in December 2017, Honduras was one of the small number of countries that joined Israel in voting against the UN resolution that opposed the USA moving its embassy to Jerusalem (under Trump’s administration). It would later turn out that he had been promised by a ‘senior official’ in Netanyahu’s government that in exchange for moving his embassy to Jerusalem, Israel would help him deal with the USA (Staff 2022).

    In 2017, Hernández was elected for a second term (2018-2022), at a moment when the White House under Donald Trump presidency (2017-2021) like cutting off aid, criminalising immigration and disengaging from development programs. So, when President Trump threatened Hernández, saying he would cut defence aid to Tegucigalpa due to the large number of Honduran refugees arriving at the southern border of the USA, Israeli prime minister Netanyahu offered its mediation in a meeting with Hernandez and Secretary of State Mike Pompeo, held in Brasilia. Meanwhile, Israeli arms sales to Central America soared as Israel took advantage of Trump’s presidency to deepen its ties to countries like Honduras and Guatemala. In March 2018, Honduras purchased $200m worth of Israeli drones (Mac 2022), and in December of that year negotiations to move the Embassy to Jerusalem began –in September 2019, Honduras opened a trade office in Jerusalem as a ‘first step’. Tegucigalpa took another step to gain Washington’s favour, when it officially declared the Lebanese political group and militia Hezbollah a terrorist organization (the year before, Argentina and Paraguay had blacklisted Hezbollah). Finally, Honduras’s Embassy in Jerusalem was inaugurated in June 2021, earning President Hernández the qualification of a ‘true friend of Israel’ (Mac 2022). The Hernández government also alleged economic and social benefits resulting from closer relations with Israel. During the state visit to Israel of 2019, he inaugurated a trade mission in Jerusalem on September 1– and at the same time, his Foreign Minister and the government’s website stated that 5 000 job opportunities would be created for Hondurans in Israel (Goichman 2019). In the end, this did not impede the Honduran president’s extradition to the USA in 2022, on gun and drug trafficking charges.

    The victory of Xiomara Castro of the leftist LIBRE Party in January 2022 ended twelve years of right-wing rule in Honduras. On 7 October, Tegucigalpa issued a statement condemning ‘the attacks by Hamas […] from Gaza against Israel.’ (SwissInfo 2023) Almost a month later, on 3 November 2023, Honduras recalled its ambassador in Israel for consultations, expressing its concern for the deaths of Palestinian civilians. On 19 March 2024, as Pro Tempore of Comunidad de Estados Latinoamericanos y Caribeños (CELAC), Honduras signed an urgent call to address the situation in Gaza. The request included the recognition, without reservations, of the existence of the Palestinian State.

    However, the scope of these stances in favour of Palestine was limited, considering Tegucigalpa’s continuing deals with the Israelis. For instance, after recalling its ambassador to Israel, the Honduran Foreign Minister, Eduardo Enrique Reina, was careful to clarify that bilateral relations with Israel persisted and that officials representing Honduras remained in the embassy. Second, Tegucigalpa did not take a formal position in relation to the lawsuit filed by South Africa against Israel at the International Court of Justice. In addition, in 2023 and 2024 the Ministry of Security purchased a fleet of vehicles, motorcycles and Black Mamba Sandcats from Israel for the ‘fight against crime’ (Silva 2024). And, although members of the Palestinian community in Honduras asked the president Xiomara Castro to move the embassy back to Tel Aviv, Foreign Minister Reina only mentioned that the decision was still being discussed (Euronews 2022).

    Guatemala

    Guatemala had pursued a military and economic diplomacy with Israel (Wallace 2017) since the government headed by Alvaro Colom (2008-2012); negotiations for a trade agreement even go back to December 2011. But bilateral relations never really took off until Otto Pérez Molina’s government (2012-2015). Although Guatemala recognized the Palestinian State in April 2013, relations with Israel were kept intact, and they progressively took hold in parallel of a rapid democratic regression in Guatemala. In 2013, President Pérez travelled to Israel seeking to acquire planes and weapons for its police and army. On a second official visit in August 2014, a new purchase of Israeli weapons was announced (Guardian 2013; La Nación 2014). In this context, it is worth noting the increasing pressure from President Obama’s administration, in particular his Special Envoy for Central America, vice-president Joe Biden, against Pérez Molina’s corrupt administration.

    Under Jimmy Morales’s presidency (2016-2020), the corruption perception index reached the highest levels in history, the weakening of Judicial and Legislative branches continued and the military, linked to the bloodiest events of the 1980s, regained power. Against these domestic developments, considerable efforts were made to curry favour with the USA through rapprochement with Israel. So, on 28 November 2016, after Donald J. Trump’s election as 45th US President, Morales made an official visit to Israel seeking investments and in December 2017, he declared the decision to start the transfer of the Embassy of Guatemala from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem (right after Trump’s decision to do so). Guatemala became one of the few countries to recognize Jerusalem as Israel’s capital (May 2018), and the second country to transfer its embassy there. A week later, it was revealed that Israeli businessmen would invest US$2b in the Guatemala-Israel Fund for Investment and Development in agriculture, medicine, and education projects. Meanwhile, the democratic regression went on. In August 2018, Morales expelled the United Nations Commission against Impunity, a body that had fought against corruption and had prosecuted more than 600 businessmen, soldiers, politicians, and a quarter of the deputies for corruption. As a way out of potential threats emanating from Washington, in July 2019 Guatemala signed an agreement with Washington whereby the country became a safe third country, forcing it to receive thousands of refugees, mainly Hondurans and Salvadorans, while they manage their status in the USA. By 2019, Guatemala had emerged as a prominent trade partner for Israel in Central America (Cumes 2018). Israeli prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu’s visited in 2019, and in September 2022, a bilateral trade agreement was signed. Simultaneously, cooperation in several fields have been constant since 2011. Scholarships offered to Guatemalans to attend agricultural training programs in Israel stand out; they continued under President Alejandro Giammattei’s administration (2020-2024). The problem of unemployment had been on the table as well. In September 2020, it was reported that Guatemala was looking for new and greater international temporary job opportunities who wished to go to Israel.

    President Alejandro Giammattei, of right-wing Vamos Party, took office in January 2020. The new President’s program focused on economic reactivation and security. Throughout 2020, Giammattei’s administration pushed for more trade with Israel and cooperation in the agricultural sector (Ministerio de Agricultura Ganadería y Alimentación Guatemala 2021). In political issues, Giammattei, who had visited Israel in 2019 (that is, before becoming president), had promised to put Hezbollah on the list of ‘terrorist organizations’ and announced his decision to maintain the Guatemalan Embassy in Jerusalem. With Israeli President Rivlin, he had declared: ‘The friends of Israel are our friends; the enemies of Israel are our enemies’ (The Times of Israel 2019). Soon after the Hama’s terrorist attacks in Israel on 7 October 2023, Guatemala expressed its condolences and support to Israel for the ‘unjustified’ attack. In the first days of January 2024, Guatemala regretted the South African complaint against Israel before the International Court of Justice.

    In mid-January 2024, Bernardo Arévalo, of the centre-left party Semilla, won the presidential elections, bringing new hopes of a better government. On 10 May, Guatemala voted in favour of Palestine becoming a full State in the United Nations (UN). This move might have been unexpected, since Bernardo Arévalo has a close personal relationship with the State of Israel: the government headed by, Juan José Arévalo, Bernardo’s father and former president of Guatemala (1945-1951), voted in favour of the UN resolution establishing the state of Israel in 1947.

    El Salvador

    The relationship between El Salvador and Israel has a long history. El Salvador was one of the first states to recognise the State of Israel. Although we explore the period 2009-2024, it is important in the Salvadoran case to recall that in the early 2000s, conservative President Antonio Saca, of Palestinian origin and very close to the country’s Palestinian community, decided to move the Salvadoran Embassy from Jerusalem to Tel Aviv in 2006, as a signal of diplomatic support sent to the Palestinian cause. At that time, El Salvador and Costa Rica were the last two countries to maintain their embassies in Jerusalem. The Minister of Foreign Relations, Francisco Lainez, said that the move was to comply with the 1984 United Nations Security Council resolution (condemning the annexation of East Jerusalem as contrary to international law). In 2009, President Saca visited the city of Bethlehem in Palestine, where he declared that ‘Palestine must exist, but Israel must also have secure borders, and this is the position that the country will continue to maintain with regard to the Palestinian people and the Jewish people’.

    It is in that context that President Mauricio Funes came into power (2009-2014). In October 2011, Palestinian President Mahmoud Abbas made an official visit to El Salvador and met President Funes. During their meeting, President Funes stated: ‘we want to strengthen our relations with Palestine... and contribute to the restoration of talks between Palestine and Israel’. In 2012, El Salvador supported the incorporation of Palestine as a non-member observer state of the United Nations. Taking as a pretext the will to work for peace, El Salvador then found itself in a sequence where the relationship with the USA was disintegrating, the Obama administration having other priorities. The high point came when President Funes officially recognised Palestine as an independent country and the two countries established diplomatic relations in May 2013, with the visit of Palestinian Foreign Minister Riyad al-Maliki to El Salvador. Faced with such a rapprochement, Israel took the decision in 2016 to close its embassy in El Salvador although El Salvador maintained its embassy in Israel. Under Salvador Sanchez Céren (2014-2019) of the leftist party Frente Farabundo Martí para la Liberación Nacional (FMLN) it was decided to maintain the Embassy in Tel Aviv in the spirit of ‘respect for the peace process’. Two years later, the relationship with Palestine intensified with the opening in October 2018 of a Palestinian Embassy in San Salvador and the appointment of an ambassador to the country.

    After two FMLN administrations, the election of President Nayib Bukele (with a third way movement) was a political detonator in the country. Bukele initially tried to bring El Salvador closer to Washington during the last years of Trump’s term. Before his election, he had travelled to Israel in 2018, at the invitation of the International Conference of Mayors. During Bukele’s first presidency (as he was re-elected in 2024), relations with Israel improved considerably and intensified, while Bukele’s authoritarian extravagances earned him increasing criticism from Joe Biden’s administration. Diplomatic relations increased with Israel, with the nomination of an Ambassador to El Salvador (Mattanya Cohen in January 2022). A Memorandum of Understanding was signed between Israel and El Salvador on innovation issues, and honorary Salvadorian consuls were appointed to Israel. The economic ties are currently promoting start-ups and the development of crypto money. The Salvadoran Minister of Economy thus participated in the ‘Israel Crypto Conference 2022’, a major event for the sector, and a strategic policy for El Salvador, which has made Bitcoin the second official currency (Martínez 2022). In contrast, the contacts with Palestinian authorities drastically curbed down. Only one high-level meeting (between the Palestinian Foreign Relations Ministers and the Salvadoran Vice-President) took place in 2023, on the bangs of the transfer of power in Colombia and the inauguration of Gustavo Petro, outside Salvadorian territory. Between 2022 and 2023, the ‘Rondas de Consultas Bilaterales entre El Salvador e Israel’ (Bilateral consultations between El Salvador and Israel) were reactivated as a basis for promoting new cooperation agendas in trade, investment, tourism and agriculture. After the terrorist attacks in Israel on 7 October 2023, President Nayib Bukele immediately and firmly condemned Hamas, comparing this movement to the ‘rapist and murderous gangs’ of El Salvador, adding that best thing for the Palestinian people would be for Hamas to ‘completely disappear.’

    From the previous review it follows that there were periods when relations with Israel intensified (Honduras under Juan Orlando Hernández, El Salvador under Nayib Bukele, Guatemala under Jimmy Morales). Conversely, there were also moments of strong rapprochement with Palestine (Honduras under Porfirio Lobo; El Salvador under Antonio Saca and then Mauricio Funes; Guatemala, on the other hand, had no such period). Another finding of the empirical evidence is that positions towards Israel and/or Palestine are not determined by political parties. While intuition would suggest that right-wing and conservative political forces are more favourable to Israel, the cases of Lobo in Honduras and Saca in El Salvador are counterexamples. Similarly, Bukele’s pro-Israeli policy in El Salvador seems at first sight surprising, as he has Palestinian origins. Therefore, these relationships are governed by a more complex tangle of factors that explain foreign policy-making and choices.

    Our analytical framework helps elucidate the dynamics between domestic factors and external actors, and the weight of international developments on policy-making (Keukeleire and Schunz 2015). Their interplay affects executive powers, and the later, in turn, use their powers vis-à-vis global pressures, inducing a variation in diplomatic stands regarding the Palestinian question (either in terms of orientation and goals, or of degrees of engagement). Furthermore, as Kaarbo and Lantis put it: ‘executive powers provide context in which decisions are made, and decisions are expressions of executive powers’ (Kaarbo and Lantis 2024: 331).

    It is worth mentioning at this point that we leave most of the psychology of decision-making, although we note some connections. For instance, the personal preferences of leaders can affect how they manage societal and external pressures. Juan Orlando Hernández’s two mandates (2014-2021) were tainted with electoral frauds, repressive policies against the opposition and the surge of massive corruption and drug-trafficking-related criminality, which could all be traced right back to the state apparatus. Against this background, together with US threats, Hernández visited in Israel. Bilateral and security and military cooperation between Tegucigalpa and Tel Aviv ensued, including arms dealings, the transfer of the Embassy to Jerusalem and reciprocal presidential visits. A similar logic appeared under Donald Trump’s first term and Hernandez’ second term as Presidents. While Trump threatened Tegucigalpa (under Hernández’ second term) to cut off aids, criminalise immigration and disengage from development programs, Honduras joined Israel in voting against the UN resolution that opposed the USA moving its embassy to Jerusalem.

    Honduran President Hernández had the conviction, the power, and the energy to force his government to elevate the levels of exchange with Israel, especially in the military and cooperation areas. ‘As long as I am president, Honduras will support Israel’ (World Jewish Congress, 29 October 2015), he said in a speech during a visit to Israel in October 2015. The fact that Hernández studied in Israel in 1992 benefiting from an international development program developed by Israel’s Agency for International Development Cooperation (MASHAV), must be considered too. A lawyer with wide political experience, Hernández imposed his own vision of the basic reorientation needed on foreign policy to serve domestic political needs.

    The victory of centre-left Xiomara Castro disrupted the two-party system that had persisted for more than 100 years in Honduras. Although she embodied the hope of pursuing structural reform, there has been a sequence of corruption scandals, a diplomatic switch from Taiwan to China, an aligning with Cuba, Venezuela and Nicaragua, and a nepotistic governance style. These trends have soured bilateral ties with the USA (Berg 2024). In addition, the style and preferences of Xiomara Castro, seem a combination of heterodoxy and pragmatism (BBC 2021), traits which may have led her, for instance, to establish social alliances with groups that could not be directly linked to the progressive sectors, also to abstain from inviting the presidents of Venezuela, Nicaragua, and Cuba to her inauguration. In this line can be interpreted Castro’s administration decision to make only symbolic adjustments in favour of Palestinians (one of them recalling Honduras’ ambassador to Israel), moves which barely conceal the interest of avoiding straining relations with Tel Aviv and Washington.

    In Guatemala, politician and military officer Otto Pérez Molina (2011-2015) was the first president to make an official visit to Israel. Even though his government recognized the Palestinian state, Foreign Minister Fernando Carrera made clear that the measure was made in line with the Latin American bloc, which mostly recognized Palestine. Bilateral investments and a bilateral trade agreement with Israel and, in 2017, the transfer of the Embassy to Jerusalem, all took place in the framework of the democratic breakdown and increasing corruption with Pérez Molina’s presidency (2011-2015). They continued with Jimmy Morales’ administration (2015-2019). Morales extended his country’s cooperation with Israel in several fields and announced the transfer of the Embassy from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem, at a time of threats by US president Donald Trump. This active alignment strategy in Guatemala’s policy towards Israel was in fact a reflection of domestic strategic interests (to secure a relationship with Trump and a channel of communication via Benjamin Netanyahu) and individual preferences. Besides being a television comedian, comic actor, and producer, also an accountant and professor of evangelical theology with a master’s degree in defence studies, Jimmy Morales is a practising evangelical who identified the Evangelical sector (around 45% of Guatemala’s population) as his main base of electoral support. The Evangelical Alliance of Guatemala immediately expressed its ‘pleasure and joy’ at the decision of its government to transfer the Guatemalan embassy to Jerusalem, saying it reflected ‘the feelings of the majority of the Guatemalan people’ and was ‘similar to the Bible’ (Wallace 2017).

    In El Salvador, two main political parties, Alianza Republicana Nacionalista (ARENA) and FMLN, had governed without interruption for more than two decades, until Nayib Bukele was elected. Right after his election as president in 2019, Bukele initially invested on its relationship with Israel. The election of Joe Biden, however, in 2020, slowed temporarily the momentum, as Biden frequently denounced Bukele’s authoritarian excesses. Indeed, since March 2022. The Legislative Assembly, at Bukele’s request, declared a state of emergency, suspending constitutional rights and relaxing rules for arrests. Since then, a ruthless anti-gang campaign has seen security forces arresting several thousands of people. The state of emergency continued, despite widespread allegations by human rights organisations and media outlets about arbitrary detentions based on little or no evidence, as well as a lack of due process, including the inability to access a defence. Not invited to the Summit of Democracies promoted by Biden in 2021, El Salvador and Honduras have also seen the Treasury Department impose economic sanctions against certain personalities because of corruption cases.

    In Bukele’s case, it is particularly interesting to observe the coincidence between the re-intensification of relations with Israel and the resumption of dialogue with the Trumpian fringe of the US Republican. For Bukele, this strategy, despite US criticism of its authoritarian turn in power, allowed the country to remain in the bosom of US interests, while awaiting the end of Biden’s Democratic administration and betting on a Republican return to power with Donald Trump. This loyalty-seeking strategy serves the political purpose of obtaining international legitimacy for his controversial policies against gangs in El Salvador and the authoritarian turn of his regime. So, at first glance, Bukele’s case may be the only anomaly, given his Palestinian origins. However, the singular trajectory of a ‘millennial’ more influenced by American culture than by his Palestinian origins should be highlighted. Additionally, he shows narcissist traits, associated to an extremely pragmatic, voluble and opportunist behaviour. These traits translate too in the way Bukele instrumentalized his political family, the FMLN, which he abandoned once Bukele’s authority started to grow (Blitzer 2022). Since then, he has relied mostly on his new party, Nuevas Ideas, and on family members and old friends to consolidate his power. On the other hand, the major political figures linked to this diaspora in the country, notably Schafik Handal, belong for the most part to the left, to the FMLN.

    Conversely, in the absence of US pressure, a more visible or compelling rapprochement vis-à-vis the Palestinian state unfolds in our three cases. Choices in the direction of the Palestinian state have taken place in periods when Washington ignored the Central American region, as happened during US president Barack Obama’s first term in office. So, Antonio Saca in El Salvador decided to move the Embassy from Jerusalem to Tel Aviv (2006) to show support to the Palestinian cause. His personal origins largely explain the reasons for this rapprochement with Palestine and the cooling of relations with Israel, without, however, risking the relationship with the USA. At that time, El Salvador was led by a conservative and liberal leader of the party ARENA, Antonio (‘Tony’) Saca, traditionally very close to Washington. Saca’s statement of diplomatic compromise with Palestine satisfied the personal ties of the President without offending Washington. In 2018, a Palestinian Embassy was established in San Salvador. The continuous estrangement of the USA led Saca’s successors, Mauricio Funes to recognise Palestine as a state (2013) and Sánchez Cerén to open an Embassy of Palestine in San Salvador (2018). Also, under both presidents, Funes and Sánchez Cerén, two official visits by the Palestinian Authority’s Minister of Foreign Affairs were organized (August 2011 and February 2016, respectively).

    In Honduras, against a backdrop of an increasing benign neglect from the USA, with President Obama divesting himself from this area for fear of overreach (White House 2015), and faced with the failure and subsequent abandonment of the Central American security strategyii (promoted by the USA), President Lobo embraced the Palestinian cause, recognising the Palestinian State as early as 2011, by opening a Palestinian Embassy in Honduras and receiving the Palestinian Authority’s Minister of Foreign Affairs. Lobo relied on the support of the Honduran Palestinian community –which is the second largest in Latin America after Chile, and part and parcel of the Honduran economic and political elite. For instance, Miguel Facussé, one of the country’s wealthiest and most influential entrepreneurs of Palestinian origin, strongly supported the coup against Manuel Zelaya (Baeza 2014).

    Likewise, Guatemala under President Bernardo Arévalo (Jimmy Morales’ successor since January 2024) has adopted a policy of equidistance since coming to power, while his country entered in a collaborative relationship the USA of Joe Biden (Grammer 2024). Bernardo Arévalo. He is a 64-year-old sociologist who ran for the centre-left Semilla (Seed) party, whose victory marked a departure from a political scene dominated by right-leaning figures aligned with the economic elite. Bernardo Arévalo lived in Israel for a total of ten years, where he completed his university studies and served as a diplomat for his country. He graduated with a degree in Sociology from the Hebrew University of Jerusalem, where he conducted academic research. In the 1980s, he worked as First Secretary and Consul at the Guatemalan Embassy in Israel. In spite of this closeness to Israel, he was an outspoken critic of Guatemala’s decision to move its embassy in Israel from Tel Aviv to Jerusalem in 2018, qualifying the move as ‘thoughtless, irresponsible, and misguided.’ His progressive views may explain why Guatemala voted in favour of the resolution which recognized Palestine as a full member of the United Nations Organization (10 May 2024).

    Our assessment is a reminder that executives face constraints from interest and pressure groups as well as external actors in the international system. It also shows that, although these actors may influence what the NTSs’ presidents do in foreign policy, the potential constraints of domestic and external actors in the international system are far from deterministic. We think that it works both ways, the two reasons or motivations are not exclusive, but convergent with each other.

    In addition, the three countries elicit interrogation about the transnational strategies leaders use to address regime security and legitimacy concerns. In this regard, it is interesting to note that among the domestic factors with the potential of affecting those concerns, the economic crisis does not seem to stand as the main driver of NTS decision to cooperate with Tel Aviv Undoubtedly, regional and global sources of socioeconomic crisis (like the 2008-2009 financial crisis or the economic contraction after the COVID pandemic) aggravated poverty and unemployment rates in all three countries. However, the most consistent cooperation with Israel by the NTS governments has been associated more with defence and intelligence diplomacy, with the preoccupation of dealing with the sharp increase in homicide rates registered in the past 15 years.

    Concluding remarks

    This article has highlighted the role of executive power in the choices made by Guatemala, Honduras and El Salvador towards Israel and Palestine from 2009 to 2024. The interplay between a changing political landscape (characterised by process for more than a decade of increasing militarisation and/or hyper-personalization of power), coupled with the policies of the United States, lead to specific actions with regards to both Israel and Palestine.

    The policies traced from 2009 to 2024 are heterogeneous and not simultaneous, proof of the absence of a common diplomatic position by the NTS on the issue. This contradicts the still dominant idea that US hegemony somehow uniformizes the small Northern Triangle States’ external behaviour. An examination of relations with Israel and Palestine shows that, despite this dual structural characteristic (smallness and asymmetrical relationship with the United States), NTS states are nevertheless able to develop specific and autonomous foreign policies (Long 2020; Nexon 2010). In this respect, the factors traditionally considered as limiting the autonomy of the foreign policy of small states –the complex geopolitical environment and/or the limited diplomatic-bureaucratic capacities– must not be overestimated.

    Leaders’ responses to domestic and international pressures are conditioned by several factors, including their own beliefs, perceptions and preferences. Bernardo Arévalo in Guatemala shows, for instance, that leaders sometimes ignore or manipulate domestic constraints and are not automatically determined by them. The same holds for international constraints. The nuance resulting from taking executives –whether they act as individuals or as part of small groups– seriously is not tantamount to neglecting the specificities of state-building processes in the Global South nor to denying the significant influence of international factors on their calculations. Instead, it is a call to recognise the combination of political context and external pressures on policy judgments.

    Finally, the article illustrates empirically how a leader’s preferences and beliefs control the decision-making process in the Israeli-Palestinian issue, according to the opportunities and costs they calculate. As demonstrated here, leaders not only act in response to structural changes, but they can also exploit such changes in order to attend to rising domestic crises, even to realise very personal aims. This fact points to the consequences that the excessive centralisation of authority (personalisation —as opposed to personalist regimes as such) together with institutional weakness or deinstitutionalisation, can have for foreign policy.

    Also, our three cases are a reminder of the importance of developing comparative analyses of cases which replicate patterns of entanglement between domestic factors and external powers, and of the role of executives as a necessary intervening variable. This opens some paths for future research. One is to extend this study to the other states of the Central American sub-region (Costa Rica, Nicaragua, Panama); these countries share several characteristics but have their own unique features. This article adds to the literature on Latin American foreign policy by shedding light on these small Central American states, but further research is needed to remedy a longstanding oversight. In addition, our cases show that further academic work should engage in comparative analyses of how other authoritarian leaders’ frame (as part of their informal powers), formulate and implement policies choices vis-à-vis Palestine and/or similar highly strategic and controversial global issues (i.e. Taiwan), and by asking the extent to which authoritarian frames constrain what the authoritarian leaders can do.
