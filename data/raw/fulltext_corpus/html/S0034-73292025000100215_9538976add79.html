Measuring Brazilian Foreign Policy Engagement: composition and validation of the Foreign Policy Index

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article presents the foreign policy index, a quantitative empirical instrument whose objective is to measure Brazilian foreign policy engagement in a time series with 191 countries and 26 years. Through a factor analysis of 6 key variables for Brazil’s international insertion, we constructed the foreign policy index based on three central dimensions: commercial, diplomatic and regional. In order to assess the validity of the index, we resorted to a systematic analysis of the most cited articles in the specialized literature on Brazilian foreign policy, analyzing the convergence between the arguments mobilized in the texts and the index scores for the sample presented. The results indicate good convergence between both, consolidating the perception of the validity and usefulness of the index as an empirical instrument to measure Brazilian foreign policy engagement.

    Keywords
    Foreign Policy Index; Brazil; Quantitative Methods; Validation

    Introduction

    Scholars analyzing Brazilian Foreign Policy (BFP) examine the country’s international behavior through distinct conceptual frameworks. Several studies adopt frameworks centered on the notion of autonomy (Vigevani and Cepaluni 2007a; Vigevani et al. 2003). Others emphasize international insertion strategies, such as universalization and deepening South-South relations (Amorim 2010; Burges 2013; Lima and Hirst 2006). There are also research dedicated to exploring the State’s function as a driver of development (Cervo 2010; Saraiva 2007) while further work investigates the degree of openness and pluralism among actors in the domestic decision-making process (Faria 2008; 2012; Milani and Pinheiro 2013).

    Seeking to contribute to validating these analyses, this article presents the Foreign Policy Index (FPI), a quantitative approach to systematizing and evaluate Brazil’s international engagement over the defined temporal scope. This instrument enables researchers to deepen the empirical foundation supporting arguments in the existing literature. As the field of foreign policy analysis in Brazil has developed significantly in recent decades (Salomón and Pinheiro 2013), developing tools that facilitate the evaluation, year-to-year comparison and simplification of numerous relevant indicators of Brazil’s international insertion could broaden the scope of foreign policy analyses.

    The FPI was developed by combining portion of Rodrigues et al. (2019) framework with aggregated variables covering trade, support in international and regional institutions, presidential travel, and bilateral agreements. The dataset encompasses Brazil’s relations with 191 countries worldwide from 1998 to 2024, integrating distinct dimensions of Brazilian international actions. Researchers widely use the variables that comprise the index, as we demonstrate in a dedicated section of this article. Beyond methodological alignment in variable selection, we also provide a validity analysis of the index against key theoretical arguments in the BFP literature, linking these propositions to the empirical patterns revealed by the FPI.

    We argue that there is a high degree of convergence between the literature’s propositions and the FPI, thereby corroborating the instrument’s validity for analyzing Brazilian international engagement. The FPI will add empirical robustness to the most influential interpretations of BFP, advancing quantitative approaches in this research agenda. We also emphasize the index’s potential for teaching Brazilian Foreign Policy and Foreign Policy Analysis in International Relations courses. Its graphical illustrations summarize numerous events into synthetic visualizations, simplify historical overview, and transform it into a useful classroom tool for interpretation.

    This article proceeds as follows. First, we provide a detailed exposition of the Foreign Policy Index (FPI), detailing its component variables, methodological approach (notably the use of exploratory factor analysis), and a critical appraisal of its strengths and limitations. Subsequently, it is furnished the findings of a bibliometric analysis, bridging the qualitative scholarly discourse with the empirical regularities identified by the FPI to validate our measurement strategy. The paper concludes with a summary of key findings and their implications.

    The Foreign Policy Index (FPI)

    We establish the validity of measuring a phenomenon when the measurement values substantively capture the ideas within the corresponding concept (Adcock and Collier 2001). Therefore, we must first define the foreign policy concept employed to construct the index. Consolidating a clear foreign policy concept represents an important attribute in developing widely accepted premises about the phenomenon (Hermann 1983). Although the literature exhibits considerable conceptual polysemy, scholars can identify a consensual element across different foreign policy definitions: its orientation towards the external environment of the nation-state (Gibson 1944; Modelski 1963; Frankel 1968; Wallace 1974; Padelford and Lincoln 1977; White 1989; Hill 2003; Hudson 2005; Salomón and Pinheiro 2013). We add to this consensus the delimitation that authorized agents of a sovereign state bear responsibility for foreign policy (Milner and Tingley 2016; Tayfur 1994). Accordingly, the FPI captures the actions for which the Brazilian Executive Branch is responsible in conducting foreign policy, excluding the activities of other actors. With these two elements, the decisional institutional locus and the sphere of policy action, we consolidate a basic premise for the empirical operationalization of foreign policy and its validation.

    We advance the index construction by selecting variables capable of measuring Brazilian foreign policy. We draw on specialized literature to select relevant variables. Our primary reference is Rodrigues et al. (2019), whose work pioneered the proposal for a foreign policy index. So, initially, this study adopts their stipulated variables: imports/exports, bilateral agreements, international cooperation projects1, presidential travels, diplomatic presence, participation in regional blocs, and support in international organizations (Rodrigues et al. 2019, 378). We structured these variables following the authors’ framework and updated them to 2024, since that Rodrigues et al.’s index covered 1998–2014. We organized the data in a panel format with country-year as the unit of analysis, covering 191 countries over 26 years (1998–2024), for a total of 5,157 observations.

    We calculate imports and exports as the annual sum in US dollars of Brazil’s bilateral trade volume with each sample country and apply the same annual country-sum approach to bilateral agreements, explicitly excluding multilateral and trilateral accords. We measure presidential travel frequency as the yearly count of Brazilian head-of-state visits to each nation and code diplomatic presence dichotomously (1/0) based on Brazil’s embassy status per country annually. For regional and inter-regional bloc participation, we include cooperation groups (regional: PROSUL, UNASUR, ORCA, ALADI, CELAC, MERCOSUR; inter-regional: BRICS, IBSA, ASA, ASPA, CPLP), assigning dichotomous membership code for each bloc in which Brazil participates. Congruence is then calculated as the mean value across blocs, with BRICS and CPLP weighted twice in inter-regional congruence and MERCOSUR weighted twice in regional congruence, following Rodrigues et al. (2019).

    MERCOSUR is framed as the foundational instrument for Brazil’s regional leadership and a platform for its competitive global insertion. The bloc was considered a “top priority” and the “original cell” of the broader South American integration project (Amorim, 2010). Although Brazilian elites have historically wrestled with a tension between a commitment to regional integration and the broader traditions of universalism and autonomy, MERCOSUR was conceived as a project that could amplify, rather than limit, Brazil’s international capacity (Vigevani et al., 2008). It has been a constant feature of Brazilian diplomacy, from being a centerpiece of the “autonomy through integration” strategy during the Cardoso administration (Vigevani et al., 2003) to being the political cornerstone for building a politically stable and united South America under President Lula (Saraiva, 2010; Vigevani and Cepaluni, 2007). It represents the primary and most institutionalized axis of Brazil’s regional policy.

    The BRICS grouping represents the culmination of a broader strategic shift in Brazilian diplomacy toward the formation of South-South coalitions to reform global governance. Unlike other inter-regional forums, BRICS was established with the explicit political aim of coordinating diplomatic action among major emerging countries (Cervo; Lessa, 2014). This approach is a core component of the “autonomy through diversification” strategy, which seeks to forge alliances with non-traditional partners to reduce asymmetries in relations with powerful countries (Vigevani and Cepaluni, 2007). The CPLP has been instrumental in deepening Brazil’s relations with African nations, particularly the Portuguese-speaking countries (Amorim, 2010). Unlike the strategic and economic focus of BRICS, the CPLP operates primarily on a cultural and diplomatic level; while its direct economic and strategic returns are considered modest, it focuses on areas such as cultural diplomacy, technical cooperation, and political coordination on issues of common interest, serving as a pillar of soft power for Brazil (Cervo, 2010). Through such coalitions, Brazil positions itself as a bridge between the North and South, aggregating and channeling the interests of developing nations to increase its own leverage in global negotiations (Burges, 2013). Therefore, they constitute the most relevant inter-regional forum for deepening South-South relations, a central agenda of Brazilian foreign policy.

    Regarding international organization alignment, we use two variables: financial congruence and United National General Assembly (UNGA) affinity. For financial congruence, we assess alignment with the IMF, World Bank (WB), and WTO. For the IMF, we compute the annual mean of two dichotomous indicators—support for Brazil’s Executive Board leadership (weight = 2) and Brazil’s participation in the G20 Council. For the World Bank, we record dichotomous annual support for Brazilian Executive Director candidates across IBRD, MIGA, IDA, and IFC boards and compute the annual mean. For the WTO, we apply this same methodology to CAIRNS, MERCOSUR, G20, NAMA11, FAN, and W52 groups, deriving the final financial congruence measure as the mean across IMF, WB, and WTO scores. For UNGA affinity, we measure the annual ideal point distance between each country and Brazil using their voting records.

    Having described the variables employed, we note their established use in the specialized literature, which validates the relevance of our data selection for index construction. Prior studies actively utilize UNGA voting behavior (Amorim Neto 2011; Mourón and Urdinez 2014), presidential travel patterns (Cason and Power 2009; Milani et al. 2015), trade flows (Burges 2008; Saraiva 2007, 2010), bilateral agreements (Amorim 2010; Cervo 2010; Cervo and Lessa 2014), and regional/inter-regional bloc congruence (Lima and Hirst 2006; Burges 2008; Amorim 2010)—demonstrating scholarly consensus on these indicators.

    We apply exploratory factor analysis as our core statistical method to reduce multiple variables into a single index, thereby synthesizing Brazil’s international engagement dimensions. This multivariate technique enjoys widespread application in social sciences (Brown 2015; Fabrigar and Wegener 2012) precisely because it distills numerous indicators into fewer components. We selected exploratory factor analysis due to the unknown underlying structure of our observed variables. After extracting initial factors, we implemented varimax rotation to enhance interpretability, maintain factor orthogonality, and simplify factor loadings.

    In our initial estimate, we included all the variables described above, keeping the same variables used by Rodrigues et al. (2019) and updating them to 2024. However, exploratory factor analysis requires a variable selection criterion for the final model: communalities, defined as the proportion of a variable’s variance explained by extracted factors. Low communality (below 0.5) indicates that a variable lacks strong association with any factor (or dimension) and requires exclusion (Rogers 2022). Table 1 below displays the scores extracted from the factorial model with all initial variables. Following the criteria described, we have dropped diplomatic presence, inter-regional congruence, and UNGA affinity from the final index for failing to meet this criterion. It is very possible that the sample increase of data modified the original estimates made by Rodrigues et al. (2019), returning a leaner composition in this updated version of the index.

    Analysis of the validity of the Foreign Policy Index in relation to specialized literature

    The literature of BFP has grown significantly in the twenty-first century. To analyze this corpus and demonstrate the FPI’s contribution, we selected the most cited works from Web of Science (WoS) and SciELO. Although non-exhaustive, this sample is representative given the prominence and their databases and the breadth of their journal coverage (Almeida and Gracio 2019). We searched “Brazilian Foreign Policy” from 1945-2023 in WoS (yielding 227 articles) and 2002-2023 in SciELO (yielding 136 articles). WoS results showed 1,814 total citations (mean=7.99, h-index=20), while SciELO 433 citations (mean=3.18, h-index=9).

    We selected the top 10 most-cited works from each repository for detailed analysis. We conducted a qualitative textual analysis of these works to identify how the index variables were mobilized in the authors’ central arguments. Appendix 1 illustrates variable employment with further examples from the selected literature. The sample’s representativeness is reinforced by its inclusion of the field’s leading journals. In these 20 seminal BFP studies, we interpret arguments about Brazil’s international behavior and map them to our factor analysis dimensions (Table 3): trade, diplomatic, and regional/international organizations. In explaining Brazil’s foreign affairs, the literature addresses core themes like trade diversification, presidential leadership, regional integration, and international institutional reform, all of which converge with the FPI dimensions. These arguments are in some cases combined across articles, addressing aspects like decision-making processes or foreign policy strategies. In Table 6, gray shading indicates argument presence and dimension correspondence, enabling visual validation of FPI measurement against Adcock and Collier’s (2001) criterion: numerical index values must align with conceptual systematization. Our central validation test examines convergence between index behavior and specialized literature arguments.

    Diplomatic Dimension

    Scholars consistently highlight presidents’ growing influence over Brazilian foreign policy—both in formulation and execution. Cason and Power (2009) and Danese (2017, 67) conceptualize this as presidentialization or presidential diplomacy, with the second defining it as “the personal conduct of foreign policy matters by the president, beyond routine or ex officio duties.” Analyses frequently use presidential administrations as analytical units, whether for case studies or cross-government comparisons (Amorim 2010; Casarões and Farias 2022; Cervo and Lessa 2014; Saraiva 2010; Vigevani and Cepaluni 2007b). These studies also identify variations in presidential leadership styles (Burin and Feliu 2022), the significance of presidential ideology (Rodrigues et al. 2019; Saraiva 2010), and differing levels of presidential interest in foreign affairs (Burges and Chagas Bastos 2017).

    Our index captures this in its diplomatic dimension by incorporating both presidential visits and bilateral agreements. Empirically, presidential travel frequency serves as a primary indicator of presidentialization. Cason and Power (2009, p. 122), for instance, notes that Cardoso “left Brazil 92 times in his eight years in office” and that “Lula da Silva easily surpassed Cardoso, surrendering the presidency more than 60 times in only four years”. Amorim (2010, p. 233) also highlighted the relevance of Africa for Lula’s foreign policy by writing that he visited the continent 12 times. Presidential visits frequently coincide with signing negotiated agreements, where the Brazilian head of state’s presence signals the importance of these accords. Complementarily, agreements are usually evaluated as a direct consequence of this engagement. Saraiva (2007, p. 13) highlights that during a visit to Brazil, President Zemin signed a cooperation protocol, while Cervo (2010, p. 24) illustrates the proximity between Bush and Lula with the “special partnership under the technological cooperation agreement signed in Camp David in March 2007”. Thus, the diplomatic dimension of the FPI effectively quantifies presidential activism abroad.

    Scholars consistently identify presidential activism during Fernando Henrique Cardoso’s second term and especially Lula’s first two administrations (Amorim Neto and Malamud 2019; Cason and Power 2009; Danese 2017). Foreign policy’s prioritization in early Workers’ Party governments boosted Brazil’s international engagement and global projection. The FPI enables us to empirically track these diplomatic engagement fluctuations across presidential terms, objectively measuring expansion, retraction, or stability in foreign policy outcomes. It tracks strategic partnership variations (Cervo and Lessa 2014) and engagement shifts with previously peripheral nations (Casarões and Farias 2022).

    The FPI simplifies interpretation through its scalable metric, enables cross-presidential comparability, and quantifies the magnitude of change. Figure 2 box plots of diplomatic dimension scores per presidential term confirm Brazil’s peak international activism during FHC’s second term and Lula’s first two terms, followed by decline starting with Rousseff’s first administration.

    Figure 2
    – Means of the Diplomatic Dimension of the Foreign Policy Index by presidential term with 95% confidence interval

    Figure 2 plots the diplomatic dimension scores from our Foreign Policy Index across successive presidential administrations. The results provide quantitative validation for established scholarly assessments. The mean scores for the FHC II, Lula I, and Lula II administrations are significantly higher than those of their peers, objectively confirming the period of heightened diplomatic activism described in the literature. Conversely, the Bolsonaro administration registers the lowest score, indicating a pronounced retreat in diplomatic engagement. The remaining administrations demonstrate statistically comparable levels of activity. This precise quantification of foreign policy priorities not only captures the core substantive arguments regarding presidential leadership but also demonstrates the index’s utility in translating qualitative observations into measurable, comparable data.

    Trade Dimension

    Brazil’s trade insertion is a cornerstone of its international agenda. Across successive republican periods, foreign policy has consistently pursued economic development goals, functioning as a pivotal instrument for industrialization and national capital expansion. Economic considerations consistently rank highly in Brazil’s decision-making hierarchy, representing a core issue across diverse domestic arena and actors. Consequently, trade strategy fundamentally underpins Brasília’s international insertion approach.

    Accordingly, scholars have used trade data as empirical evidence for foreign policy salience and strategy evaluation. To demonstrate relevance, Saraiva (2019, p. 7) notes that “since 2009, China became Brazil’s main trade partner.” Conversely, trade data has also been used to show irrelevance or engagement inefficiency: “Africa’s share of total Brazilian trade hardly moved between 2003 and 2011, going from 5.1 per cent to 5.7 per cent” (Burges, 2013, p. 8). Shifting trade patterns were cited as evidence of the political strategy’s consequences. As Vigevani and Cepaluni (2007, p. 14) note, “we see that imports from the traditional markets (USA, EU, Japan, Canada, Mexico and Mercosur) decreased from 79.3% to 67.2% of the total between 2002 and 2005”.

    Since World War II, Brazil has oscillated between U.S.-alignment strategies and universalist approaches seeking alternative partnerships, particularly South-South alliances (Merke et al. 2020). U.S. alignment entails incorporating Washington’s interests into Brazil’s agenda and prioritizing them to secure material benefits such as market access, technical cooperation, and investments. Conversely, the universalization strategy reflects deliberate diplomatic efforts (Lafer 2001), typically embodying pragmatic partner selection over Americanist ideology (Lima 1994; Pinheiro 2000).

    During FHC’s second term (our index’s starting point), Vigevani, Oliveira and Cintra (2003) demonstrated that Brazil consolidated its commercial interests through diversified market-seeking agendas. Under “autonomy through integration,” Brazil sought external commercial insertion without exclusive alignments, frequently strengthening positions by opposing Washington’s agenda. Subsequent Lula administrations maintained this posture while deepening South-South cooperation—a strategy Vigevani and Cepaluni (2007a) termed “autonomy through diversification.” This universalization strategy aligns with autonomy concepts: multiplying partnerships enhances Brazil’s bargaining power and reduces economic dependence on any single nation. Consequently, the trade dimension of partnership diversification remains critical for evaluating strategy’s effectiveness, constituting a core element of Brazil’s international engagement.

    The FPI enables direct visualization and measurement of partnership universalization strategy outcomes (Figure 1). Given its country-disaggregated structure and comprehensive coverage of 191 nations, researchers can precisely track bilateral relationship intensity over time, making the index an optimal tool for observing the partnership universalization process. As previously argued, this strategy dominated foreign policy agendas from late FHC through the Lula and Dilma administrations, as clearly evidenced by intensified coloration across several global regions in Figure 1.

    Scholars identify the Global South as Brazil’s highest-potential partnership growth area, noting a possible ceiling effect with Northern nations (Lima 2005). Increased Southern engagement never reduced Northern ties (Amorim 2010; Vigevani and Cepaluni 2007a, 2007b), with Brazil potentially functioning as mediator between these blocs (Amorim 2010; Burges 2008, 2013). To visualize Brazilian engagement over time with respect to the North-South diplomatic divide, Figure 3 plots sample years on the horizontal axis and FPI scores on the vertical axis. The gray line represents the average scores for Global South countries, whereas the bold line indicates the averages for Global North countries.

    Figure 3
    – Foreign Policy Index by Global South and Global North

    Figure 3 demonstrates the growing relative importance of the Global South, particularly during Lula’s first two administrations, aligning with scholarly observations. From 2004 onward, both trendlines converge, indicating equivalent levels of Brazil’s international engagement with both country groups until 2008. After 2010 (Lula’s second term final year), both lines declined until 2014 and then stabilized through the end of the series. Notably, the rise in Southern engagement occurs without any apparent detriment to relations with Northern countries, further corroborating established interpretations in the literature.

    An interesting aspect is the perception in the literature of a retraction in foreign policy from Dilma Rousseff’s first term onwards (Cervo and Lessa 2014; Doctor 2017). What the index in figure 3 shows is a significant expansion of foreign policy from 2003 to 2010, followed by a small decline and subsequent stability of Brazil’s level of engagement. After the super-expansion of foreign policy during Lula’s first two terms (Schenoni et al. 2022), the deceleration observed in figure 3 and documented in the literature can be considered expected. The magnitude of the decline, better expressed in the form of the year-by-year index and with a quantitative measurement, could mitigate interpretations centered on Dilma’s presidential leadership, or its absence, as one of the explanatory factors for the retraction of Brazilian foreign policy. The historical series shows that Lula’s two terms stand out as exceptions in terms of heightened engagement. Furthermore, the trajectory of the index’s average scores over the 15 years after 2010 indicate that this reflects a cessation of growth, given the maintenance of a higher level in subsequent years of the index when compared to 2003. Thus, the index not only allows us to track the evolution of foreign relations at a country level but also to analyze broader patterns, such as North/South or South/South, alongside other categorizations available in the database.

    Regional/IO Dimension

    Scholarship extensively explores Brazil’s regional integration efforts and leadership aspirations as central themes in its foreign policy agenda. The prevailing debate examines the tensions in exercising regional leadership vis-à-vis neighboring states and other South American contenders (Malamud 2011), as well as Brazil’s instrumentalization of its regional position to pursue global prominence within international organizations (Burges 2008, 2013; Lima and Hirst 2006; Saraiva 2010). Regional leadership ambitions serve as platforms for enhanced global influence. Although leadership remains a subjective role contingent to others’ acceptance, the FPI quantifies Brazil’s material investment in the region and gauges partner countries’ support for its positions and coalitions within international organization.

    Our regional/IO dimension, aggregating regional congruence and international financial institution alignment, captures this critical arena of Brazilian international engagement. The focus on regional integration is consistently highlighted, with analysts pointing to the “relative importance granted by Brazilian diplomacy to recent cooperation and integration efforts, more specifically the Union of South American Nations (Unasur) and Mercosur” (Saraiva, 2010, p. 152). This regional emphasis is reinforced as a pillar of South-South cooperation, given that for Brazilian diplomacy, “Mercosur and South American cooperation in more general terms, which, within the scope of the country’s external actions, is the type of South-South cooperation identified as a priority” (Saraiva, 2007, p. 43, authors translation). Complementarily, this same logic extends to diplomatic coordination at the global sphere, including in international financial institutions, where Brazilian diplomacy sought the “reinforcement of the southern coalitions aimed at reforming the international financial system, the IMF, and the World Bank” (Cervo, 2010, p. 8).

    Researchers can compare South America’s relative importance against other global regions and track annual evolution of Brazil’s regional engagement. As Figure 1 illustrates, South America has consistently been prioritized in Brazil’s foreign policy, demonstrated by intense coloration from 1998 to 2024. Regional integration, particularly MECOSUR, has constituted a key Brazilian trade strategy since the 1990s, advancing both intra-bloc trade liberalization within the post-Cold War multilateral framework and strengthening the bloc’s extra-regional bargaining power. The index confirms the region’s persistent relevance across presidencies, evidenced by sustained high coloration during 2019-2022 despite Bolsonaro’s criticism of MECOSUR’s negotiating mandate. These findings corroborate analyses of Bolsonaro’s failed attempt to change BFP (Guimarães and Silva 2021; Belém Lopes et al. 2022).

    Our analysis reveals substantive congruence between the FPI’s three dimensions—trade, diplomatic, regional/IO—and literature arguments. The outcomes of foreign policy can be summarized by the degree of universalization of partnerships in both trade and diplomatic spheres, as well as the increased or sustained intensity of relations with the countries of the region. Overall, we observe strong convergence between the index’s scores and scholarly interpretations of Brazil’s international behavior, confirming our indicator selection’s consistency with foreign policy objectives.

    We note that only Faria (2008; 2012), Salomón and Pinheiro (2013) and Milani and Pinheiro’s (2013) texts lack convergence with the FPI in Table 6. This divergence stems from their specific focus on decision-making processes, which prioritizes domestic actors over Brazil’s international behavior. These works examine the potential decline of Itamaraty’s bureaucratic insulation and increased influence from other state actors and non-state actors on Brazilian Foreign Policy (Faria 2008; 2012). They attribute these shifts to post-Cold War transformations and Brazil’s post-democratization politics. This debate has gained significant space for those who characterize foreign policy as one of the public policies (Milani and Pinheiro 2013).

    Although arguments in these texts resist direct correlation with the FPI’s three dimensions, they intersect with the index’s utility for Brazilian foreign policy analysis. Salomón and Pinheiro (2013) highlight methodological challenges in establishing objectives and identifying results due to foreign policy implementation occurring beyond national borders, a difficulty that scholars widely acknowledge. The FPI directly addresses this gap by offering annual measurements of Brazil’s engagement with every global partner. It facilitates the identification of foreign policy outcomes and advances the research agenda on evaluating foreign policy as a public policy.

    We conclude this section by summarizing key findings from our bibliographic analysis of BFP literature and its alignment with the FPI. Our analysis clearly establishes high convergence between scholarly descriptions of Brazil’s international behavior and the FPI’s numerical expression, confirming the index’s conceptual validity. This convergence stems directly from matching variables employed in both the literature and our index. The index makes a significant contribution by systematizing empirical elements that previously appeared diffusely across existing scholarship, overcoming the field’s lack of consensual measurement tools through a unified intensity scale of Brazil’s international engagement. By consolidating the concept of foreign policy and operationalizing it via the FPI, this framework underpins theoretical development and enhances the generalization of findings in the field (Hermann 1983, 3). Furthermore, the index broadens the range of potential research designs in BFP studies, particularly by facilitating quantitative approaches, by providing a suitable dependent variable for statistical modeling. It also enables direct historical comparison using standardized metrics, advancing comparative research methodologies.

    Conclusion

    Brazilian Foreign Policy literature has grown significantly since the twenty-first century. Using bibliometric analysis of Web of Science and SciELO databases, we identify the three dimensions of our Foreign Policy Index, commercial, diplomatic, and regional/Ios, reflecting core arguments across scholarly works. This identification enables empirical validation of the index through convergence between literature descriptions of Brazil’s international behavior and the index’s numerical variation across time and nations. For example, scholarly accounts of heightened international activism during Lula’s first two administrations clearly appear in Figure 1’s index visualization. The index also provides precise measurement for evaluating partnership universalization strategy, revealing expanded Global South engagement without compromising Global North relations, fully aligning with literature analyses. Furthermore, the index captures Brazil’s capacity to deepen regional ties, quantifying South America’s persistent importance in Brazil’s agenda and Brasília’s consistent regional leadership commitment.

    Beyond its academic contribution, the Foreign Policy Index can significantly advance the foreign policy evaluation agenda. By providing a clear metric of the Brazilian Foreign Policy Engagement, the index enables the setting of tangible, measurable objectives for international initiatives, an essential requirement for assessing governmental strategies and expenditure efficiency abroad. This capability directly addresses demands from both the Legislative branch and Executive oversight bodies. The Senate Foreign Relations Committee highlighted this need through its 2016 Report No. 4, which mandated assessment of diplomatic policy effectiveness, particularly evaluating embassy establishment impacts. Similarly, the Federal Court of Accounts (TCU) instructed the Foreign Ministry’s General Secretariat (SG/MRE) via Ruling 391/2018 to submit detailed strategic planning documentation, including responsible parties, timelines, deliverables, and metrics, specifically requiring development of monitoring and evaluation tools for foreign policy outcomes. Moreover, the Foreign Policy Index’s graphical visualization capabilities offer significant pedagogical value for teaching Brazilian foreign policy, functioning as a multifunctional teaching tool in academic settings.

    The methodological potential of the index is also far from exhausted. We envision its application in several largely used quantitative frameworks in the international relations field. Panel Data Regression is one of them. Employing the FPI as a dependent variable in a panel data model would allow researchers to isolate the specific effects of economic determinants, domestic variables, and international shocks on foreign policy behavior. Moving on to causal methods for observational data, the index is well-suited for methods like Difference-in-Differences (DiD) or Synthetic Control Methods to rigorously assess the causal impact of specific policy shifts or political events.

    Beyond its several quantitative applications, the index also serves as a tool for selecting cases for qualitative analysis. Scholars interested in identifying processes of foreign policy change, for example, might focus on observations with extreme values. Conversely, a researcher could select a deviant case to generate new hypotheses. In each instance, the index provides a transparent justification for case selection, setting the stage for theory-testing process tracing.

    While the FPI provides a robust foundation, we acknowledge some limitations that could catalysts for future research. The current variables used to compose the foreign policy index, while comprehensive, could be expanded to include other critical aspects of foreign policy, such as data on technical cooperation projects, cultural diplomacy, or defense agreements, to create an even more granular picture. Another possibility of improvement is to apply this methodological framework to other countries. Constructing comparable FPIs for regional peers or strategic partners would enable systematic cross-national comparisons, moving beyond the Brazilian case to build and test broader theories of foreign policy. In short, the FPI offers a relevant contribution to the development of the academic agenda on Brazilian Foreign Policy Engagement, to the teaching of Brazilian foreign policy and, finally, to the evaluation of the performance of foreign policy as a public policy.
