Foreign Policy and Identity: A Contribution from a Bourdieusian-Inspired Framework of Analysis

Política externa e identidade: uma contribuição de uma estrutura de análise de inspiração bourdieusiana

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    While the knowledge that identities matter is reasonably consolidated in International Relations, the evaluation of how identities matter and how they work in setting the boundaries of political actions is still a work in progress. The present article connects this debate with International Political Sociology (IPS) by bringing Bourdieu’s concepts of habitus, field, and doxa to the analysis of identity and foreign policy, which can be used as heuristic devices to address identity without essentializing it. Hence, this article makes a twofold contribution. First, it translates Bourdieu’s concepts of habitus, field, and doxa to Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) and shows how they help to accommodate the binary logic of agent/structure around which the debate on foreign policy and identity is structured. Second, it provides an analytical framework through which one can operationalize Bourdieu’s concepts and identify how identity is mobilized through discourses not only because it is a part of a nation’s idea of itself but also because they improve the chances of a decision-maker to guarantee approval for its foreign policy actions.

    Keywords
    Foreign Policy Analysis; identity; International Political Sociology; Bourdieu

    Resumo

    Embora o conhecimento de que as identidades importam esteja razoavelmente consolidado nas Relações Internacionais, a avaliação de como as identidades importam e como elas funcionam na definição dos limites das ações políticas ainda é um trabalho em andamento. O presente artigo conecta esse debate com a Sociologia Política Internacional (SPI) ao trazer os conceitos de Bourdieu de habitus, campo e doxa para a análise de identidade e política externa, que podem ser usados como dispositivos heurísticos para abordar a identidade sem essencializá-la. Portanto, este artigo faz uma dupla contribuição. Primeiro, ele traduz os conceitos de Bourdieu de habitus, campo e doxa para a Análise de Política Externa (APF) e mostra como eles ajudam a acomodar a lógica binária de agente/estrutura em torno da qual o debate sobre política externa e identidade está estruturado. Em segundo lugar, fornece uma estrutura analítica por meio da qual é possível operacionalizar os conceitos de Bourdieu e identificar como a identidade é mobilizada por meio de discursos, não apenas porque faz parte da ideia que uma nação tem de si mesma, mas também porque aumenta as chances de um tomador de decisão garantir a aprovação de suas ações de política externa

    Palavras-chave
    Análise de Política Externa; Identidade; Sociologia Política Internacional

    Introduction

    Identity, at first sight, is permeated by a series of contradictions. It is stable yet plastic; an outcome of the national socio-historical process and the cause of a national sense of belonging; it comes from social agents but at the same time has a degree of independence that affects collectivities; it is a form of structure, but one that is not immutable; it has a relationship with the material world but the narration of this same material world is what gives its meaning, through processes of simplifications, resignification, and interpretation. Identity has entered the common jargon of International Relations (IR) but some (Onuf 2003; Berenskoetter 2017) have pointed out that these supposed contradictions and fluid nature make the concept vague and difficult to use. Vagueness, however, is not a solid reason to disengage from the debate. Although there is a reasonably consolidated knowledge that identities matter, addressing how they matter and in what way they work to set the boundaries of political actions is still a work in progress.

    Works on identity and foreign policy can be organized into many clusters. A set of works concentrated on the relationship between identity and culture (Katzenstein 1996; Lapid and Kratochwil 1996; Barnett 1999; Weldes et al. 1999) and the debate on identity and ideas (Checkel 1993; Keohane and Goldstein 1993; Ruggie 1997; Foley 2007). They paved the way for identity-based research, regardless of explicitly using the concept or not. Others used identity as a variable to understand foreign policy (Campbell 1992; Hansen 2006; Lebow 2008); the study of conflict (Ringmar 1996; Weldes 1999) and the construction of peace relations (Adler and Barnett 1998); anarchy transformation (Wendt 1992) or the construction of an international hierarchy (Davis 2018); the states’ search for status and recognition (Carvalho and Neumann 2014); and the notion of identity as the very thing that needs to be secured (Mitzen 2006; Kinnvall and Mitzen 2020).

    On epistemological grounds, there is no possible true or false theory on identity and its influence over political practices. However, one main difficulty in this large debate is the agent-structure nexus. This paper will engage in this debate through a bridge-building effort between it and International Political Sociology (IPS). It does so by bringing Bourdieu’s conceptualizations of habitus, field, and doxa to the analysis of identity formation and how it informs foreign policy. This endeavour is grounded on the understanding that Bourdieu’s approach can address this dichotomy, although not solve it. For instance, because Bourdieu’s concepts can be regarded as multi-scalar, which travel through time, they give us a sense of how the social environment becomes largely stable without losing the possibility of change and creativity. Therefore, they can be used as heuristic devices to understand identity without essentializing it.

    This article presents a twofold contribution. First, it translates Bourdieu’s concepts of habitus, field, and doxa to Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) and shows how they help to accommodate the binary logic of agent/structure around which the debate on foreign policy and identity is structured. Second, it provides an analytical framework, called the identity-in-play model, through which one can operationalize Bourdieu’s concepts and pinpoint how certain ideas that are constitutive of one’s identity are mobilized through discourses not only because they are part of a nation’s idea of itself but also because they improve the chances of a decision-maker to guarantee approval for its foreign policy actions.

    The paper is structured as follows. The first section discusses the literature on identity and foreign policy, focusing on the agent/structure divide. The second section presents Bourdieu’s theory and how the notions of habitus, field, capital, and doxa help to accommodate this dichotomy. The third section further develops a framework for analysis and the fourth section presents some empirical illustrations of the US foreign policy after the Cold War, showing how it can be used for further investigations addressing the identity-foreign policy nexus.

    Identity and the agent/structure nexus

    The agent/structure binarism resonates with the foundational debates in IR (Wendt 1987; Dessler 1989; Carlsnaes 1992; Friedman and Starr 1997; Suganami 1999; Wight 1999, 2006; Nishimura 2011). It is attached to a broader philosophical question about the extent to which the social world is an expression of one’s free will or is determined by some kind of material or ideational structure, responsible for constraining human agency. Hudson (2005) put this debate somehow at ease, affirming that foreign policy analysis is not only an actor-specific but also an agent-oriented theory. In trying to establish FPA’s singularity in relation to the general IR theoretical debate, Hudson chooses to privilege agent-driven analysis, steering away from the state as a useful abstraction and focusing on human beings as the only true agents (Hudson 2005:3).

    In FPA, approaches that resorted to ideas and identity reinforced the debate on agency and structure. On the agent side, concepts like ‘the image’ (Boulding 1956), ‘belief systems’ (Holsti 1962), or ‘operational code’ (George 1969) emphasized how individuals read exterior information and derive their behaviour. Even though perceptions are taken into consideration, many of these approaches (e.g. Holsti 1962) do not question how those images and, therefore, belief systems are constructed. They are usually presented as a pre-given stable feature, through which information is filtered, evaluated, and a decision is achieved. Although putting the decision-maker in the forefront, some cognitive approaches dismiss social components intrinsic to identity formation. They end up reinforcing this dichotomy as they leave little room for agents’ cognitive change. Although ‘individual agents might be preserved […] agency is sacrificed’ (Jackson 2006: 6).

    Researchers resorting to group-think dynamics (Janis 1972; ’t Hart, Stern and Sundelius 1997; Kowert 2002; Garrison 2003; Badie 2010), ideas (Keohane and Goldstein 1993), and in the constructivist tradition – either in its identity and norms connection (Checkel 1993; Katzenstein 1996) or in explaining foreign policy through a relational external other (Weldes et al. 1999) – built interesting avenues to balance agency and structure. For instance, in her empirical analysis, Weldes deals with the social structure as a reservoir that, through articulation and interpellation, individuals tap into to produce intersubjective meaning and a specific identity and interests. In this sense, her work considers the meaning systems of a social structure even though to some extent prioritizing the agent.

    On the side of structure, another ‘traditional’ FPA framework, role-theory (Kaarbo 2003; Breuning 2011; Nabers 2011) has also contributed to future works on identity and the agent-structure debate. Works on national role conceptions (NRCs) (Holsti 1970; Thies and Breuning 2012) established the linkage between social structures and calculating agents thus helping research questions aimed at understanding states’ interactions and how their decision-makers bargained over their respective roles. Although explaining foreign policy choices through social-cognitive components, role-theory usually treats NRCs as independent variables (Cantir and Kaarbo 2012), assuming a somehow – even if only methodologically – precedence of structure over agency.

    In an attempt to balance these two poles, both constructivist works that explained foreign policy through practices of identification within the state (Hopf 2002) and poststructuralism (Campbell 1992; Doty 1993; Zehfuss 2001) helped to expand the debate. For instance, in his work on Russian foreign policy, Hopf (2002) emphasizes the social cognitive structure as he intends to understand how many discursive formations and identities form a national identity. Following this objective, agency is subsumed over structure and considered on the habitual and everyday forms of action. With a similar move, Sjöstedt (2007) consciously tilts the scale to structure over agency as her objective is to understand how freedom and creativity are constrained by dominant discourses that enable specific policy actions.

    Another avenue that came close to giving equal weight between agency and structure in FPA is the ontological security theory (OST) (Mitzen 2006; Rumelili 2015; Subotić 2015; Ejdus 2018; Vieira 2018; Kinnvall and Mitzen 2020). Nevertheless, as many OST works follow the logic of (re)establishing an identity equilibrium (Wendt 1999), one might question whether the space of agency is reduced to fixing the ideational structure back in place and delivering a ‘homeostatic direction’ (Wendt 1999).

    Regarding poststructuralism, depending on how one engages with identity, the agent/structure dichotomy is more or less balanced (Campbell 1992; Doty 1993; Hansen and Waever 2002; Hansen 2006; Neumann 1996, 1999). In his work, instead of using identity as the starting point of his analysis, Campbell (1992) understands that identity and how it is, to some extent, secured and established is the very element that needs to be explained. This different point of entry combined with how the self/other relations are articulated produces other consequences for the agent/structure debate. As posed by Hansen (2006), because in Campbell identities are constructed through the juxtaposition of a radically distinct and threatening other, agency has limited ways in which to construct this relational identity and transcend conflict in IR. Her work, on the other hand, opens more space for agency when considering multiple degrees of otherness.

    As I will further develop in the third section, the poststructuralist tradition has provided many insights to the identity-in-play model, especially its theorization of language to explain foreign policy. Concepts like discourse (Campbell 1992), nodal points (Diez 2001; Laclau and Mouffe 2001), and master-signifier (Mouffe and Laclau 2001) are sources of inspiration as to how the proposed model incorporates language as practice. However, the way discourse is treated impacts the dynamics intersection between agent and structure.

    In Doty’s (1993: 302) work, to develop the Discursive Practices Approach and steer clear of the cognitive FPA tradition the author emphasizes the linguistic structure and the autonomy of language over ‘individual or collective subjects as the loci of meaning.’ Here, one could argue as long as language is inextricably connected to agents, Doty’s approach does not completely exclude agency and, hence, presents a sort of combination of both. However, this form of balance puts more weight on (language) structure.

    Another explored avenue was speech-act theory. Resorting mainly to Austin’s and Searle’s work, many authors (Buzan, Waever and Wilde. 1998; Balzacq 2010; Sjöstedt, Kovacs and Themnér 2019) have emphasized that language has an ‘illocutionary force’ beyond the locutionary act (Austin 1962). In other words, language has a capacity to do things instead of merely describing reality. It is by its performative power that language is understood as an instrument for (political) actions. Within the FPA tradition, speech-act was mobilized to explain how key political figures can inaugurate an interpretation of reality and legitimize a specific action. One could easily see how speech-act theory would help understand the link between national identity and foreign policy. By mobilizing a view of the nation’s identity to justify a foreign action, political actors (re)inscribe identity’s central tenets to existent context and perform it through language. In its original take, speech-act theory follows a similar path to Doty’s work. The explanatory weight that is given to language combined with fewer theorizations on, for example, the securitizing actor and the audience privileges a linguistic structure over agency.

    Some have tried to supplement speech-act theory by incorporating context (Balzacq 2005; Ciutã 2009), the politics of discourse (Diez 1999), and the relation to macro-structures (Buzan and Waever 2009). Depending on how one treats language, the agent/structure divide comes closest to a balance as, for instance, when Balzacq (2005) – among others – not only considers the perlocutionary force of language but also the speaker’s strategies and the audience’s experience.

    On the other hand, Political Discourse Theory (PDT) by Laclau (2000; 2014) and Mouffe (2001) has the benefit of not perceiving the discourse as fixed. Applied in FPA (Nabers 2009; Nabers 2015; Erdogan 2017), some of its central theoretical tenets might be of help to scholars interested in understanding foreign policy through identity. Through PDT, identity can be understood as a representation that articulates both ‘logics of difference’ and ‘logics of equivalence’ and establishes a hegemonic relation. The process of (identity) hegemonization constructs a ‘contaminated universality: (1) it lives in this unresolvable tension between universality and particularity; (2) its function of hegemonic universality is not acquired for good but is, on the contrary, always reversible’ (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: xiii). As already mentioned, contrasting PDT with the proposed translation of Bourdieu into FPA, both approaches do not treat discourse as fixed. Identity is always rearticulated by means of words and change is always on the horizon. However, both theorizations also understand that change usually occurs within certain parameters. For Laclau and Mouffe, the hegemonization ‘of a task or an ensemble of political forces belongs to the terrain of historical contingency’ and ‘does not, therefore, involve a simple conjunctural or momentary agreement; it has to build a structurally new relation’ (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: xiii).

    But Laclau and Mouffe understand discourse ‘as material in a post-Gramscian sense’ (Stengel and Nabers 2019: 254) because it is ‘embodied in institutions and apparatuses’ that ‘welds together a historical bloc’ (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: 67). In this sense, one could understand that PDT’s approach ties the analysis not to a linguistic structure, but to a material one. And, although it is possible to point out that in Bourdieu’s work discourses are also embodied, its sociology segments this ‘embodiment’ in two realms. The social – having discourse as one of its forms of representation – is deposited in both individuals (in the form of habitus) and things (in circumscribed fields), as will be developed in the next section. This subtle difference affects how one epistemologically situates language in the agent and structure divide and methodologically conducts research.

    First, discourses are important but still part of a set of other social practices. Second, and this critique can be addressed either to PDT or to speech-act theory, how one treats discourse might exclude multiple ways in which language can be considered. Third, following PDT’s notion of discourse embodied in ‘institutions and apparatuses,’ one might use these same institutions and apparatuses as entry points for the analysis. Step one would be identifying them and then proceed with discourse analysis. Instead, with Bourdieu, the field is constructed and identified through retracing the practices that inaugurated a (in our case, foreign policy) course of action.

    As I discuss in the next section, Bourdieu’s concepts and the inspiration his theory provided to the identity-in-play framework continue the effort of poststructuralism research on identity and foreign policy in its attempt to ease the possibility of reifying either agency or structure. Even though not resolving this debate, Bourdieu’s theory helps develop a conceptual framework that does not leave many of the analytical choices to the empirics. By pre-ordaining some elements of identity analysis, time and history play a bigger part than it does, for example, in Hansen’s (2006) work.

    From structuration theory to Bourdieu’s contribution to FPA

    Giddens (1984) coined the denomination ‘theory of structuration.’ However, theories placing the same weight on agency and structure were developed by several other intellectuals. Theorists like Philip Abrams, Roy Bhaskar, Pierre Bourdieu, and Derek Layde are also part of this effort (Thrift 1983), although Bourdieu and Giddens are the ones more often considered in IR. The choice of approaching identity through Bourdieu’s works and not Giddens’ was due to two main reasons. First, Giddens establishes four main levels through which empirical research should be conducted – the hermeneutic investigations of frames of meaning, the investigations of practical consciousness, the identification of bounds of agent’s knowledgeability and the identification of the principal institutional components of a social system – and affirms that all should be considered for an analytical equilibrium but, depending on the researcher’s intentions, one level could be given more emphasis than another (Giddens 1984; Thrift 1985: 615). The main concern is that, because of Giddens’ division, one could inadvertently fall into a trap of reifying either agent or structure. Plus, through his methodological bracketing, he does not ‘think it’s conceivable to suppose that all forms of social analysis are able to produce a wholly rounded account which would connect agency and structure in a systematic and satisfactory fashion’ (Giddens 1984: 127). In Bourdieu’s theory, the heuristic key habitus/field seems to propose a solution to it without imposing methodological limitations on the mutually constituted theoretical stance over the agent-structure relationship.

    Second, and most importantly, Giddens’ dissociation of practical consciousness from discursive consciousness might bring more problems than solutions to an identity-guided foreign policy investigation. Practical consciousness, in Giddens’ work, is the day-to-day processes of routinization through which agents build their stocks of ‘mutual knowledge,’ ‘incorporated in encounters [that are] not directly accessible to the consciousness of actors’1 (Giddens 1984: 4), while discursive consciousness ‘means being able to put things into words’ (Giddens 1984: 45). This stratification model that separates practical from discursive consciousness puts discursive consciousness as potentially non-routinized and not constitutive of knowledge in agents’ mode of socialization. Even if practical consciousness is ‘what actors know (believe) about social conditions […] but cannot express discursively’ (Giddens 1984: 375), what agents know is undoubtedly informed and constructed by routinizations of language, regardless of their capacity to consciously explain it with words. In this sense, an evaluation of identity using Bourdieu’s concepts of habitus, field, and doxa puts language in an important role, so the ontological presupposition of mutually dependent agent-structure relation is not detached from its methodological standpoint.

    Bourdieu’s theory relies mostly on the concept of habitus, a ‘heuristic device’ that ‘refers to a person’s taken-for-granted, unreflected – hence largely habitual – way of thinking and acting’ (Leander 2009: 3). Because of its habitual component, the habitus is the notion responsible for the mediation between the agent and the social structure. In synthesis, for Bourdieu, the habitus is a set of dispositions formed by conative2, cognitive, and emotive components acquired and ‘carried out collectively in practice through mimesis and osmosis’ (Wacquant 2014: 126). In other words, every component in the forging of habitus is developed through a process of collective interaction. The habitus is then the agglomerate of dispositions organized in a system of durable and transposable structured structures (by effects of past social environments) and structuring structures (regarding present emotions, actions, and perceptions), guiding practices in a systematic character through the ‘unchosen principle of all choices’ (Wacquant 2016: 67). Choices then ‘can be objectively adapted to their outcomes without presupposing a conscious aiming at ends or an express mastery of the operations necessary in order to attain them’ (Bourdieu 1992: 53). In summary, the habitus informs practices that are not per se the result of strategic intention but are ‘objectively “regulated” and “regular” without being in any way the product of obedience to rules.’ Practices can be ‘collectively orchestrated without being the product of the organizing action of a conductor’ (Bourdieu 1992: 53). With the conceptualization of habitus, Bourdieu deals with the question of how the social environment is largely stable, despite its variations, addressing the agent-structure dichotomization and the division between modes of conscious and unconscious practices.

    Even though Bourdieu stresses the habitus’ tendency to become stable ‘with its operant milieu, and relatively resistant to conscious manipulation’ (Wacquant 2014: 128), this should not be mistaken by a quest for invariance, especially because to advocate for the impossibility of habitus to revise itself is to remove the component of creativity from the relations between agents and the social world. Its stabilization results, then, from ‘powerful mechanisms of selection, on the side of the agent as well as the side of the social world, that work to ensure the minimal coherence, congruence, and persistence of dispositions’ (Wacquant 2014: 128). Habitus’ assimilative and yet adaptative characteristic offers explanatory gain to understand the mediation of identity and foreign policy practices.

    Moreover, the relation among space, temporality, and history in the notion of habitus is what permits advocating for stability and change in the social world without reducing them to idiosyncrasies. The ‘malleability of habitus [is] due to its “permanent revision” in practice’ (Wacquant 2016: 68). History, therefore, is not accounted as a mere move towards contextualization, but as something transformed into body and nature. As the habitus ‘carr[ies] over the whole sedimented (and ongoing) social history, individual and collective, of the agent’ it enables one to understand ‘the body as an “ongoing practical achievement”’ (Wacquant 2014: 121). So, history is activated and made active by the encounter between past ingrained dispositions and present experiences, emotions, and thoughts, and empirically identified in practices. After all, habitus is the immediate expression of passive and prior dispositions made dormant structures in social actors (Fröhlich and Rehbein 2014). This sedimentation identified in the notion of habitus is what gives practices their relative autonomy regarding the present and the past, the latter ‘enacted and acting, which, functioning as accumulated capital, produces history on the basis of history and so ensures permanence within change that makes the individual agent a world within the world’ (Bourdieu 1992: 56). The notion of habitus in its conceptualization of history does not only introduce temporality, as it begs for the location of practices in social time, but also spatiality as it stresses the necessity to consider the social space, or ‘the series of nested microcosms pertinent to manufacturing the practice studied’ (Wacquant 2014: 131).

    Although Bourdieu does not expressly mention the existence of a national habitus, he nevertheless points to a state-level field of action. Fields, in Bourdieu’s conceptualization, are demarcated spheres of social action that entail a specific habitus, that is, specific rules or modes of behaviour that inform how actors establish their interpersonal relations as they seek power, influence, and/or status. Fields encompass a ‘set of objective power relations imposed on all those who enter the field, relations which are not reducible to the intentions of individual agents or even to direct interactions between agents’ (Bourdieu 1991: 230). The accumulation of field-relevant capital is what determines one’s position within the field since capital is the ‘accumulated labour (in its materialized form or its “incorporated” embodied form) which, when appropriated on a private, i.e., exclusive, basis by agents or groups of agents, enables them to appropriate social energy in the form of reified or living labour’ (Bourdieu 1986: 241). Forms of capital are as many as the possibility of fields delimitations and the power relations within a field are settled through the agent’s ability to render a form of capital relevant or irrelevant.

    Habitus and field are hence multi-scalar concepts that one can use at different levels of social activity (either from individual to the civilizational) and across degrees and types of aggregation (settings, institutions, and collectives) (Wacquant 2014: 120). When theorizing about the exchange rates among fields, Bourdieu stresses that the capacity to structure fields and set the rate of exchange within and across them is also a form of capital in itself. This so-called form of ‘meta-capital’ is associated by the author to the figure of the state. In his words, the state has ‘the ability to impose in a universal fashion, on the scale of a certain territorial foundation, principles of vision and division, symbolic forms, principles of classification’ (Bourdieu 2014: 166) and because ‘the different forms of accumulation of military, economic and symbolic capital are interdependent and form a whole,’ ‘it is this totalization that makes for the specificity of the state’ (Bourdieu 2014: 197).

    Following this conceptualization, if one can scale up the scope of field3 to the state level, it is also possible to think of a national habitus4, a set of individually shared ways of thinking and acting, that encompasses and influences, in a greater or lesser extent, specific intra-national fields and their respective doxic dispositions. As Wacquant (2014: 120) interestingly points out, the habitus is a form of ‘container’ of social action within which each collectivity ‘tends to produce joint ways of thinking, feeling, and acting’ as well as ‘common sets of expectations.’ In this sense, one could think of containers inside containers, going ‘up’ to the international level and ‘down’ to smaller social collectivities (local, regional, ethnic, religion-, linguistic-, and class-based, among others).

    One can still ask how to empirically work with the concepts of habitus, field, and doxa when researching identity. First, if the conceptualization of habitus comports the delimitation of a national habitus, one can define identity as an individual but nationally oriented form of habitus. Just like the notion of habitus, identity is also formed by conative, cognitive, and emotive components established in collective interaction, ingrained in social time and social space, that point towards stability without losing the possibility of change and creativity. And, just like the habitus, identity is suspended between consciousness and unconsciousness and guides practices without strategic planning of intentions or merely by default. The ingrained dispositions of a state’s identity, acquired throughout a lifetime of national socialization, are the embodiment of a national habitus. Still using the metaphor of habitus as a container, this form of habitus is a national social container that embraces shared ideas, modes of thinking, expressing and presenting oneself to others, and that informs, as they are carried in-body, each and every individual’s doxic experience towards its own country.

    The national habitus and its dispositions travel through time and, despite their re-representations and resignifications, still ‘glue’ people of different social strata, races, religions, and party affiliations together just by matters of national membership. They are ‘united in a relationship of homology, that is, of diversity within homogeneity’ in which ‘each individual system of dispositions is a structural variant of the others, expressing the singularity of its position within the class [or the group] and its trajectory’ (Bourdieu 1992: 60). Through this approach one can theorize identity addressing the structure/agency linkage. Instead of making a theoretical choice to depart from substances and then derive the social relations among them; or, in other words, instead of departing from individuals and their agency component to understanding the construction of national identity or from the identity structure to capture individual behaviours – a point of departure that if not denotes an ontological choice, at least presents a methodological one – the habitus/field device allows the researcher to move from a substantialism approach to a relationalist one (Emirbayer 1997; Jackson and Nexon 1999). Moreover, as language is usually a means of expressing one’s habitus, while at the same time feeding and taping into a field’s structure of meaning, one way to identify the nexus between habitus, field, and doxa is through discursive practices.

    The national habitus and field work in tandem and the national field instructs the other national subfields, which may vary indefinitely as may vary the different spheres of social life. The relationship of ‘immediate adherence that is established in practice between habitus and the field to which it is attuned’ (Bourdieu 1992: 68) is the definition of doxa. In other words, the doxic experience of the world flows from the perceived common sense, established in the ‘undisputed, pre-reflexive, naive, native compliance with the fundamental presuppositions of the field’ (Bourdieu 1992: 68). To understand the common sense created by identity’s dispositions and how they affect foreign policy decisions, the next section proposes an analytical framework, which I called the ‘identity-in-play’ model.

    Identity in play model: a framework for analysis

    The identity-in-play model brings insights from the poststructuralist literature but presents Bourdieu’s concepts and theory as a better avenue to understand social relations in its agent/structure conundrum and hence the connection between identity and foreign policy. The model proposed here is composed of three levels that orient the investigation on identity and foreign policy. Each level represents a field with its specific logic, although these fields overlap, interpenetrate, and mutually determine each other (Pouliot and Mérand 2013). The first level refers to the national field, while the second to the bureaucratic (sub)field, and the third to the (sub)foreign policy field. One should note that although both the bureaucratic (sub)field and the foreign policy (sub)field constitute a field on their own, with their own logic, habitus, and doxa, here we also propose that they are subsumed by the national field and influenced by its ‘meta-capital.’ To conduct a research that needs to delve into the different ontologies of habitus, field, and the practices therein a mixed-method might be of value. At each level, a set of methods to grasp dispositions, positions, and practices will be presented. Some methods were not used in previous work (Motta 2018) but are nonetheless suggested for further studies.

    In the first level, the model focuses on the most general and abstract meaning reservoirs within a collective’s identity – either being a state’s identity or not. Understanding foreign policy in the most traditional sense, as a state’s set of practices towards the international system, those general points would be national ones. Here, direct access to practices is complicated as virtually any nationally circumscribed practice would be methodologically valid. The research then proceeds with a set of abstract identity components called here ‘anchor points’ as proxies to grasp practices in the national habitus-field relationship. The sense behind the nomenclature is the notion that when deployed they have the capacity to anchor or to ground identity and discursively sustain it, reinforcing, evolving, emerging, or fixing it when it is breaking down. Using Bourdieu’s social theory, the first level intends to present a general map of systematic dispositions that find their concrete expressions in an individual’s habitus.

    Dialoguing with PDT, instead of ‘anchor points,’ the concept of ‘master signifier’ could be employed. By definition, a master-signifier is a word or an idea that assumes ‘a “universal” structuring function’ (Laclau and Mouffe 2001: xi); in other words, it ‘empties itself from multiple particular meanings and becomes a “blank canvas”’ upon which context-dependent meanings can be constructed (Stengel and Nabers 2019: 258). As I shall illustrate in the empirical remarks section, the anchor points of democracy, liberty/freedom, equality, and justice, used as proxies to the habitus-field relation in translating identity into foreign policy, might be viewed as recurrent master-signifiers mobilized by Americans from daily practices to international interventions. The option of not using this concept is not due to its definition, but mainly because of two elements: the necessity to steer away from PDT so as to not bring a theorization that in my view has less capacity to address agent/structure debate than Bourdieu and a sense of imagery attached to the term ‘anchor points.’

    The anchor points can be chosen through both deductive and inductive logic. Deductively, using a nation’s history as a background, one can select the most important and recurrent ideas. Throughout the empirical research, and hence inductively, the researcher might evaluate whether his/her selection was consistent. This first step is important in two senses. First, it allows the researcher to start the analysis with a component that has an equal interface with both agent and structure. Second, one can start by something that is not an entity, but rather a disposition that establishes a relation among entities. Once the selection is finalized, the anchor points can be used to analyse how agents deployed them to legitimize foreign policy decisions.

    Going back to the habitus-field key, the analysis follows a historical account of the anchor points to uncover their range in meanings within and for the national field. This process presents them embedded in their historical compositions, or at least in some of the most relevant historical moments through which they were largely socialized, hence contributing to setting their range of meaning. Here, one can mobilize multiple sources such as literary and academic works, festivities, myths, visual images, religious and/or cultural traditions, iconic public speeches, memorials, and museums and their exhibitions, thus casting a wider net and gathering multiple examples. From the multi-method perspective adopted in this model, the historical mode of analysis is the one emphasized at this level. Different national subfields will present a country’s identity anchor points in their own specific way. In general, however, identity’s dispositions are, to a greater or lesser extent, always present in national subfields. Obviously, any selection is not exhaustive. Others might find it compelling to emphasize different anchor points.

    In the empirical research, although the national field has the meta-capital to exert its influence within and across other fields, including the practical sense of the foreign policy (sub)field, the anchor points suffer a process of contingent specification when they are deployed in a specific subfield. In the bureaucratic and foreign policy (sub)fields, the anchor points are usually narrowed down by a specific party and/or government to present its current view of the needs and interests to be addressed.

    As displayed in Figure 1, within the national habitus-field relationship, identity’s anchor points assume the widest possible range of meanings encompassed in a nation’s mentalité. As they establish the vocabulary (by means of spoken language or not) around which the practical sense of the national field is sustained, at this level, the selected anchor points present the most abstract and general spectrum of identity. As they cut through other (sub)fields, they assume contingent narrower ranges of meaning and are deployed differently depending on which form of capital is more relevant to a specific (sub)field. At the national level, the anchor points are enacted by modes that go from ordinary daily practices to national parties, hymns, holidays, monuments, school literature classics, and many other forms.

    Following the model, to interpret how the anchor points are deployed in the foreign policy subfield, I propose two stages of specification. In the intermediary level, the anchor points are converted into commonplaces – terminology borrowed from Jackson (2006a) – that is, through a process of delimitation each administration – or other agents with enough power within the bureaucratic (sub)field (Bourdieu, Wacquant and Farage 1994) – set the boundaries within which they understand the usage in meaning of national anchor points. In the level of commonplaces, the bureaucratic (sub)field narrows down the anchor points to present their idea of US challenges at that government’s moment and what are seen as the most preeminent threats and interests.

    At this and the next level, the research reconstructed the dispositional and positional logics in each field (Pouliot 2012), having the anchor points as a general guide to retrace the confluence of dispositions and positions into practices. Analysing the bureaucratic subfield, the dispositional logic might be reconstructed through interviews, discourse and text analysis, and a ‘relational biography’ (Dezalay and Garth 2002) to ‘locate, in the social space, the position from which’ key political figures ‘express their views’ (Bourdieu 1999: 625). As access to actors such as presidents, secretaries of state, and defence, among others at the top of a country’s administration is often restricted, the researcher can follow the two last methods.

    As employed in previous work (Motta 2018), discourse and content analysis can be applied as an indirect way to access practices. Instead of encompassing a wide range of sources, as carried out in the first level, this second level focuses on key documents, speeches, and memos, e.g. the National Security Strategy and the State of the Union, when analysing the USA. On a note, considering that national identity is an all-encompassing element that permeates every sphere of public debate and decision-making, instead of following the common path of discourse analysis, gradually increasing the scope of discourses (Hansen 2006) – from official to general ones – here we followed the inverse procedure. However, this does not prevent the narrower range of discourses from folding back and help with the previous step in its conceptualization of the anchor points.

    Although presented here in a linear fashion for the purpose of didactics, the process of narrowing down the anchor points meanings to a winning version of them (despite the actual foreign policy decision towards a situation) is not a linear one. Many political figures might not seize their position of authority to establish a contingent common sense around identity’s anchor points. When they do not set the debate and transport the anchor points to their commonplaces version, (i) it renders the operationalization process more difficult, since the spectrum of the possible range of meanings is still largely wide, and (ii) it extends the space for divergent contestant propositions as to how to operationalize the anchor points (Motta 2018). In general, both processes of specification and operationalization might be developed by any agent that orbits the bureaucratic and foreign policy (sub)field, e.g. the media, Congress, other members of the administration, and the president. However, the president’s authority places her/him in a position of prevalence in both processes, mainly because one’s ‘capacity to speak and act in the name of the state plays a causally relevant role in the success or failure to [a] legitimation attempt’ (Jackson 2006a: 31).

    The third and last stage is the operationalization process, done within the foreign policy (sub)field. In this stage, agents within this subfield, usually in crucial occasions, as in moments of decisions to go to war or to intervene abroad, deploy the previously narrowed anchor points in commonplace formats to justify their positions. As many agents involved in the bureaucratic (sub)field were also situated in the foreign policy one and because a ‘relational biography’ can be carried out in the second and third stages, to retrace dispositions at this level the research can complement the discourse analysis conducting interviews. The analysis of official documents justifying a foreign policy decision plus the interviews with available practitioners help establish a ‘participant observation’ (Pouliot 2012). Besides reconstructing the practitioners’ point of view, the interviews are key to ‘locate, in the social space, the position from which interviewees express their views’ (Pouliot 2012: 52) and treat them as ‘performances, or practices, meaning that they are meant to do something in and on the world’ (Pouliot 2012: 49).

    To reconstruct the positional logic within the foreign policy field, the discourses were separated in two different representations of the foreign policy decision, one defending and the other disapproving of it. The objective is to construct a topographical image with two depictions of how identity was played out. Retracing how different discourses were constructed is important to present the struggles to impose a specific vision of things. Also, the interviews and discourse analysis are crucial to identify the relationship among individuals and map out the intersubjective rules of the game and the distribution of resources at that point in time when the decision was made. The diachronic analysis performed at the second level combined with the synchronic one carried out at the third level brings history into both position and dispositional logics.

    Empirical remarks

    In a previous work (Motta 2018) the identity-in-play model was applied to four US interventions after the Cold War. Regarding the first level, the anchor points were selected deductively and inductively. In a deductive fashion, and using US liberal history as a background, I singled out some of the most important ideas in the long durée of US ideational composition, as, for instance, the anchor points ‘equality,’ ‘liberty/freedom,’ ‘individualism,’ ‘justice’ and ‘democracy.’ The research used multiple sources such as national literature hallmarks, American-famous political speeches (e.g. The Gettysburg Address), formative political documents and works (e.g. The Federalist Papers), academic works on US political and economic liberalism, and cultural traditions, among others. During each empirical case, and hence inductively, this selection was further developed as depending on the case some anchor points played a major role than others.

    As mentioned above, since access to key political figures is usually limited, the research adopted both discourse and text analysis, and a ‘relational biography.’ For each case, I selected a set of crucial political documents of each administration responsible for legitimating one of the selected interventions analysed. The central characteristic of these documents was the administration’s intention to establish its major goals, either foreign or domestic, thus presenting a frame of reference based on a contingent interpretation of the US needs and interests. Sources such as National Security Strategies, State of Union, among other documents and speeches were analysed to understand how national anchor points were narrowed down into commonplaces by the bureaucratic (sub)field.

    As presented in previous research (Motta 2018), the translation of the anchor point of ‘democracy’ into a contingent commonplace performed by the Bill Clinton and George W. Bush administrations is an interesting example. Among the large spectrum of possible meanings for ‘democracy,’ whereas Clinton seemed to deploy democracy more in its due process meaning, Bush usually emphasized its transcendental and higher moral value meaning. In the bureaucratic (sub)field, this process of crafting a contingent general meaning helps set the limits in which the foreign policy (sub)field will operationalize identity’s anchor points, thus restricting their possible meaning usages.

    The ‘relational biography’ helped question the personal, political, and career trajectories of the ones involved in constructing an administration’s understanding of the US place in the world, the state of the union, and the general objectives and goals. In this sense, this method was important to bring habitus to the fore and map out diachronically agents’ ‘embodied inclinations acquired through exposure and experience in various positions and games.’ (Pouliot 2012: 48) History is not treated as mere context, but as past ingrained dispositions brought to present positions and circumstances. This method was applied to both the bureaucratic and foreign policy (sub)fields, either to key figures that were open to an interview or not.

    To illustrate this point, it is interesting to bring some components of Donald Rumsfeld’s trajectory, as he was directly involved in two of the four interventions analysed: Afghanistan, in 2001, and Iraq, in 2003. Besides holding the position of Secretary of Defence twice, under presidents Ford and George W. Bush, Rumsfeld attended an Ivy League University, Princeton, graduating with a degree in political science, served in the Navy, and as a US Congressman from Illinois. Understanding that he was socialized in major American traditional institutions shed light not only on what he brings in terms of expertise but also on how previous social environments play a role in his developed habitus and forms of capital accumulated. Besides, retracing his connections and career trajectory is important to examine the divisions, disputes, and convergences that characterized the bureaucratic and foreign policy (sub)fields during the George W. Bush administration and the two aforementioned interventions. For instance, his connections with Dick Cheney, vice-president during the George W. Bush administration, that dated back at least to the 1970s, when Cheney was recruited to substitute Rumsfeld as Ford’s chief of staff, and his affiliation with the Project for the New American Century, a neoconservative think tank, in its view that the USA had to change its foreign policy towards Iraq.

    The third level can be illustrated by the case of the US intervention in Afghanistan. Here, besides still applying the relational biography and discourse analysis, interviews with available practitioners were also conducted. After 9/11, the discourses in foreign policy (sub)field (administration and Congress) from September 11th to October 5th shaped two discourses: the war discourse and the criminal discourse. Comparing the two, while the anchor points of democracy and liberty/freedom follow a similar connotation, the anchor points of equality and justice are shaped in a different meaning, offering clear differences in policy recommendations.

    Along with the question ‘who did this to the USA?,’ members of the foreign policy (sub)field had yet to explain the ‘why’ question. In both discourses, America ‘was targeted for attack because [it is] the brightest beacon for freedom and opportunity in the world’ (Bush 2001). Terrorists hated the USA for its ‘democratically elected government’ and not Americans ‘as individuals’ but rather ‘our civilization’ (Cox 2001), and their ‘inten[t is] to weaken the very core of our national identity’ (Napolitano 2001).

    Throughout the development of these discourses, the ‘why’ question needed to be complemented by the object definition or, in other words, the ‘what was attacked?’ question. Both discourses presented the object of the attacks as something bigger than the USA, like freedom and democracy themselves. The victims of the 9/11 attacks might have been the innocent lives in the World Trade Center, but what was attacked were, in fact, the abstract concepts of freedom, democracy, and the American ‘way of life,’ because terrorists ‘believe they can destroy our values and our freedom’ (Dunn 2001) as the ‘attacks were directed to the very idea of America itself’ (Lowey 2001).

    In the war discourse, since terrorism is presented as an idea that must be combated in the hearts and minds, there is nothing capable of better fighting an idea than another idea, in this case, the American identity’s anchor points. More than acting as an example, the USA needed to remove the so-called terrorist idea and spread democracy, liberty/freedom, and equality to other countries ‘in the service of civilization in this world; and that understanding runs deep in America. It, too, is who we are’ (Armey 2001). Since terrorists do not accept these values there is no place for them in the kind of international society the USA tries to order. By extension, the military solution is reaffirmed as the best course of action. This ‘simple language’ and the ‘political impact’ it caused was emphasized by one government member who served on the National Commission on Terrorist Attacks Upon the United States interviewed5 for this research.     One can see how US anchor points are shaped in its attempt to ‘talk identity’ within the foreign policy (sub)field. For instance, when Congressman Rehberg (2001) states that ‘America must not treat yesterday’s tragedy as a criminal case, or simply seek justice […] through subpoenas and courtrooms’ one can infer two possible meanings: first, justice (in general or as a legal case) is not enough, or, second, that justice is equated with revenge (or at least with the feeling that terrorists and countries that harbour them should pay accordingly to the USA suffering). Using Congressman Breaux’s (2001) words, terrorists ‘are not entitled to a trial by jury of their peers, and they are not entitled to the services of a free attorney;’ they ‘will be judged’ in the ‘battlefield and not a courtroom.’ Here, democracy assumes not merely the meaning of a form of governmental organization, but it is deployed in its widest ‘ideological’ connotation. Like liberty/freedom, democracy is a civilizational idea bigger than the USA. An idea that not only informs internal institutional arrangements but organizes internationally shared understandings and ways of behaviour.

    The other competing discourse in the foreign policy (sub)field was the criminal one, which presented different interpretations for these same pillars. The evaluation that terrorism is an idea is not dismissed, even though it focuses on the individuals who perpetrated the attacks. Plus, there is no such thing as enforcing an idea to combat another. Here, the criminal discourse takes the exemplarist version of the US foreign policy grand narrative and advocates for a course of action that defeats terrorism by example. Otherwise, the USA would end up behaving just like the terrorists. Congressman Gregg (2001) words are illustrative when he affirms the USA ‘must be careful to use the rule of law so that we do not abandon what has made us great in order to confront this type of evil.’ The best way to uphold the same values that were attacked is, hence, to reinforce them at home. The criminal narrative shapes the attacks as a crime against humanity and, in this sense, to the rhetorical question ‘what does a democracy do to punish criminals?’ the logical answer was ‘we put them on trial. If found guilty, we imprison them’ (Kucinich 2001).

    In this operationalization of the anchor point of justice, its connotation diverges from the one proposed in the war discourse. Justice is for everyone, is one basic standard that defines humanity. Regardless of the attack’s gravity, all humans are equal towards the law and although the attacks are characterized as acts against humanity, terrorism needs to be fought ‘within traditional constitutional boundaries’ (Jackson 2001). As justice is one of Western civilization and American bedrock, the US cannot act otherwise.

    By the topographical image above, it is possible to see that the connotation given to democracy, though also assumed in its widest ideological sense, diverges from the one proposed in the war discourse. First, it reverses the use of this anchor point to question US/Western civilization about what democracies should do. In other words, the ones that share not only a form of governmental organization, but Western values need to deal with terrorism through democratic policies, easing the ‘ocean of despair’ and ‘the waves of pain and injustice’ from which terrorism can flourish and ‘replac[ing] the despair and hopelessness of the terrorist’s supporters with a policy that brings dignity, respect, and justice’ (Jackson 2001).

    Final remarks

    The objective of this paper was to make a friendly intervention in the debate on identity and foreign policy through a bridge-building effort between FPA and IPS. Using Bourdieu’s theory and the concepts of habitus, field, and doxa the article presented a framework that helps engage in further empirical research on identity and foreign policy.

    Here, a Bourdieusian-inspired approach can direct the agent/structure dichotomy in the level of epistemology and ontology. The ‘intermediary-level epistemology’ proposed by this theory informs that the ontological choice should not be one that negates or falls outside the co-determinant, mutually dependent agent-structure level of analysis. Therefore, the identity-in-play model is built around an ontology that proposes an in-between locus of the agent-structure relations, that is, as the empirical illustrations and the framework show, in the discursive practices that establish relations among entities. As the model proposes, especially in its second and third levels, the discursive practices are one way of expressing one’s habitus, while at the same time feeding and taping into a field’s structure of meaning. In other words, the discursive practices are one way of (re)constructing the symbolic capital around which the habitus-field relation exists.

    As the empirical illustrations show, the discursive foreign policy practices were chosen because ‘practices are by nature repeated and patterned’ and therefore ‘one may heuristically abstract them away from context in the form of various social mechanisms’ (Pouliot 2015: 240). In general, as language is part of the social world and one can construct things with it, the anchor points can be abstracted away as a form of social mechanism that, through the process of negotiations and contingent (re)definition of their meanings, generates the necessary condition for actions to take place. Since language can produce effects, its efficacy rests on the meanings that are bound up in them throughout history, time, and space.
