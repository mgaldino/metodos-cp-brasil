Carl Schmitt on the Move: Spatial Politics and the (political) Sacrifice of the Sea

Carl Schmitt em Movimento: Política Espacial e o Sacrifício (político) do Mar

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article critically examines Carl Schmitt’s spatial thought, focusing on his land–sea dichotomy and the marginalisation of maritime spaces in his political framework. While Schmitt’s tripartite model of appropriation (<italic>Nehmen</italic>), division (<italic>Teilen</italic>), and exploitation (<italic>Weiden</italic>) privileges land as the locus of sovereignty, it fails to acknowledge the constitutive role of the sea—particularly the Atlantic Ocean—in shaping the global spatial order. Drawing on Lauren Benton’s concept of fluid sovereignty, the article challenges Schmitt’s rigid territorial logic and highlights how his marginal treatment of the sea undermines the coherence of his global vision. It further argues that Schmitt’s privileging of land reflects a Eurocentric bias, incompatible with the realities of maritime empires, oceanic trade, and interconnected sovereignties. This intervention adds a new dimension to Schmitt scholarship by foregrounding the strategic and conceptual significance of the land–sea relationship.

    Key words
    Carl Schmitt; spatial politics; fluid sovereignty; sea; critical geography

    Resumo

    Este artigo examina criticamente o pensamento espacial de Carl Schmitt, com foco em sua dicotomia terra–mar e na marginalização dos espaços marítimos em seu arcabouço político. Enquanto o modelo tripartite de Schmitt de apropriação (<italic>Nehmen</italic>), divisão (<italic>Teilen</italic>) e exploração (<italic>Weiden</italic>) privilegia a terra como o lócus da soberania, ele falha em reconhecer o papel constitutivo do mar — particularmente do Oceano Atlântico — na formação da ordem espacial global. Com base no conceito de soberania fluida de Lauren Benton, o artigo desafia a lógica territorial rígida de Schmitt e destaca como seu tratamento marginal do mar compromete a coerência de sua visão global. Argumenta-se ainda que a ênfase de Schmitt na terra reflete um viés eurocêntrico, incompatível com as realidades dos impérios marítimos, do comércio oceânico e das soberanias interconectadas. Esta intervenção acrescenta uma nova dimensão à bibliografia schmittiana ao trazer à tona a importância estratégica e conceitual da relação terra–mar.

    Palavras-chave
    Carl Schmitt; política espacial; soberania fluida; mar; geografia crítica

    Introduction

    The extensive theoretical literature on Carl Schmitt has traditionally focused on his decisionist argument — defining who holds sovereignty — along with his distinction between friend-foe to situate politics, as Claudio Minca and Nick Vaughan-Williams noted (Minca and Willians 2012: 757). Nevertheless, as these scholars observe, the 2003 English translation of The Nomos of the Earth marked the beginning of significant attention to Schmitt’s theorisation of space and the spatial dimensions of the political (Minca and Willians 2012: 757). This shift prompted a deeper engagement with Schmitt’s spatialised thinking, particularly regarding borders and the demarcation of sovereign lines. Despite this engagement, the maritime dimension remained largely overlooked in these analyses. Perhaps the work of Joshua Derman, which deals specifically with Schmitt’s text Land and Sea, is the most in-depth research on the subject. Some others follow but do not pay due attention to the problem of maritime space in Schmitt. This paper addresses this gap by contributing to the ongoing exploration of Schmittian spatialised political thought.

    Schmitt’s question of maritime space arises from his conceptual framework, which closely ties the notion of political community to the creation of sovereign lines that are visually discernible, demarcated, and capable of being mapped. As rightly noted by Minca and Willians, Schmitt argues that the border, both as a symbolic and physical ‘line in the sand’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 35–36) is essential for defining the spaces of the political and enabling the formation of a political community capable of engaging in war and acting ‘politically’. This border underpins the imaginative political geographies of enmity central to his theory of the political. For Schmitt, actual borders must be visible, clear, concrete, and spatially defined qualities he highly values. Additionally, borders result from the spatial appropriation of land, which he arrogates as a foundational ontological act constituting any political community (Minca and Willians 2012).

    Minca and Williams critically engage with Carl Schmitt’s work and the work of other scholars, such as David Armitage, Joshua Derman, and Wolfgang Palaver, in this passage. They emphasise Schmitt’s revival of the concept of Nomos as a central pillar to rigidly and narrowly underpin his theoretical framework regarding order and the spatial locus of politics. These critics argue that Schmitt’s reliance on Nomos serves as a foundational element to justify his vision of political order. However, they question the limitations and inflexibility of this approach in addressing broader geopolitical and theoretical complexities. Schmitt emphasises the significance of land appropriations as foundational acts of concrete orders (Palaver 1996: 108). This argument overlooks the importance of the relationship between land and sea, a central element in sustaining Schmitt’s conception of the Nomos.

    Land represents the domain of order, property, decision-making, and sovereignty. It is the realm of the state, politics, and ‘limited’ war, where the enemy is clearly defined, and norms constrain violence. In contrast, the sea embodies a space of freedom, commerce, mobility, and the absence of centralised authority. It is the realm of adventure, piracy, and ‘total’ war, where the distinction between friend and foe becomes fluid, and violence knows no bounds.

    In Schmitt’s thought, the sea is pivotal as the antithesis of the terrestrial order. In Schmitt’s framework, the sea is deemed inapproachable and cannot be appropriated, thus distinguishing it from the land, where history, according to Schmitt, is constituted through territorial appropriation. By negating this aspect and treating the sea as an empty, history-less space, one inadvertently reinforces an irreversible element within Schmitt’s political-theoretical structure. Within Schmitt’s dichotomous framework, the exclusion of the sea—or the denial of its constitutive role in political modernity—is fundamental to asserting land as the primary locus of politics. In this context, Schmitt privileges the spatialisation of politics within terrestrial domains, relegating maritime space to a secondary and marginal position. The revival of the concept of Nomos necessitates the exclusion or marginalisation of the sea, as the maritime domain is characterised by the absence of conditions required to establish acts of concrete order.

    Considering this, this article’s contribution lies in its effort to examine the significance of the sea in Carl Schmitt’s political thought. It sheds light on the schematic fragility of his spatial political thinking in trying to sacrifice the constitutive role of the sea for modern politics. This analysis aims to enlighten the reader about Schmitt’s spatial thinking and critically assess his land-centred political framework, which prioritises terrestrial domains over maritime spaces. This perspective disregards the practices associated with the sea that have also played a crucial role in shaping the secular state and the global spatial order.

    I have organised this paper as follows: first, I will engage with Schmittian’s thoughts, elaborated in The Nomos of the Earth in the International Law of Jus Publicum Europaeum, favouring land over the sea, without forgetting that in one of his previous works, Land and Sea, the sea assumed immense importance given the hostility between Germany and the United Kingdom when he was writing the text, the interwar period. I will critically engage with his argument regarding the division of political space, which is predominantly associated with land rather than the sea. In doing so, I will explore the revival of the concept of Nomos as a theoretical foundation to justify the prioritisation of land over the maritime domain. This discussion will examine how Nomos reinforces the idea that land, as a bounded and ordered space, is central to establishing political and legal structures, in contrast to the sea, which is often portrayed as a space of fluidity and absence of concrete order. Through this analysis, I aim to interrogate the implications of this terrestrial bias in shaping our understanding of political spatiality.

    Second, I will deal with the concept of Rayas. This crucial element serves Schmitt in raising the first questions about the political division of space in early modernity. A division that surpassed the topographic limits as a possibility for organising political life. At this point, it is essential to mention that an important step in this section is articulating Schmitt’s ‘practises of conceptualisation’ about the sea1. The objective is to map how the sea is situated in the relationship between land in Schmitt’s spatial political thought, both in the text Land and Sea and The Nomos of the Earth in the International Law of Jus Publicum, but strictly in the second, given the chronology of the publications.

    Following this debate, I turn to another category of maritime boundaries: amity lines. This discussion explores the intersection between the concept of Rayas and amity lines, focusing on how the ‘confrontation’ between these demarcations redefined the role of the sea and how the sea contributed to both the creation and collision of these lines. Schmitt’s interpretation of Rayas and amity lines has faced significant criticism, and this section examines one such critique, which challenges the centrality of demarcation lines in understanding the spatial and political dynamics of the colonial era. Specifically, it argues that Schmitt underestimates the sea’s role in shaping the complexity of religious relations and the power dynamics among European nations, thereby offering a more nuanced perspective on the interplay between maritime space and colonial politics. As Anna More (2014) and Lauren Benton (2014) advocate, Schmitt’s theory is criticised for its static conception of space, which is focused on dry land and overlooks the role of maritime routes and transatlantic trade.

    Finally, I identify a highly intricate dynamic in Schmitt’s argument in The Nomos of the Earth: the political sacrifice of the sea in favour of solid land, followed by its political resurrection, only to be sacrificed once again. The Schmittian project I highlight continually reinforces the sea’s political sacrifice to uphold the mainland as the exclusive space suitable for politics. This effort reflects a persistent attempt to confine sovereign practice within defined boundaries by identifying the spaces where it can be exercised. However, the sea cannot truly be sacrificed. It must instead be recognised as the ‘non-space’ of politics in Schmitt’s terms—a concept that contrasts with the perspectives of scholars like More and Benton, who argue that the sea is neither a void of politics nor history (as will be discussed). Without this conceptualisation of the sea, Schmitt loses the framework that allows him, through a process of alterity, to distinguish between spaces unsuitable for the sovereign state and those suitable for sovereign practice. It is through this dichotomy that Schmitt’s global spatial order is constituted.

    Nomos: Interpreting Carl Schmitt’s spatial political thought

    In this section, I will examine Schmitt’s spatial political thought in greater depth, focusing on his conception of order and its initial implications for the political-spatial relationship between land and sea. I will explore the notion of spatial division, which Schmitt associates primarily with land rather than sea. Additionally, I will discuss the revival of the term Nomos, his attempt to establish an origin that justifies dominion over a specific space, and the necessity of defining geographical boundaries to enable concrete measurement, thereby creating a framework in which authority becomes self-contained. Finally, I will analyse Land and Sea and The Nomos of the Earth, considering their distinct objectives while articulating interpretations of the underlying archetype that privileges land over sea.

    Firstly, it is necessary to highlight the difference between dominion and sovereignty, as although they may seem synonymous, they have distinct political conceptions in Carl Schmitt, as pointed out by Andreas Kalyvas. The distinction between sovereignty and dominion is crucial in Schmitt’s political theory, as it clarifies two key concepts that have profound implications for understanding the spatial and legal order. As Schmitt views it, sovereignty refers to the authority to make decisions in moments of crisis, particularly concerning the decision to declare war or establish peace. This is closely linked to the notion of the exception, where the sovereign can act beyond the established legal framework, as highlighted in Schmitt’s theory of the state of exception. In contrast, dominion refers to control over space and territory, where power is exercised through territorial claims and material control, often through force or colonisation (Kalyvas 2018).

    As Andreas Kalyvas notes, sovereignty is about the ability to establish the rules of the game, while dominion concerns the spatial conditions that make those rules enforceable. This distinction is particularly significant in international relations, where states often dominate territories. Still, the question of sovereignty arises when states face decisions about the legitimacy of such dominion or the need to act beyond the legal order. Clarifying this difference enables a more nuanced understanding of state power, particularly in the context of imperialism, territorial disputes, and the relationship between law and force (Kalyvas 2018).

    In Land and Sea, Schmitt sought to analyse the fundamental changes in the world and their causes, contrasting the ‘Age of Navigation’ and the period between the world wars, as Giorgi Tavadze points out (Tavadze 2013: 45). In Schmitt’s political-spatial framework, world transformations are linked to spatial revolutions, articulating changes in the international order. Continuing his argument on the relationship between space and order, Schmitt, in The Nomos of the Earth, ‘returns’ to Greece and recovers the concept of Nomos to make sense of the idea of the original apportion of the soil (land), which guarantees the immediate form in which the political and social order of a people becomes spatially visible (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 68). He attempts to escape the legal-positivist tangle from the 19th century, as he states (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 49).

    Following the process of land appropriation, a critical phase of spatial demarcation emerges, characterised by the partitioning of grazing territories under the ancient Greek concept of Nomos. This aspect is of central importance in Schmitt’s framework, as visualisation introduces a tangible, measurable quality to delineating spatial boundaries, clearly defining each grazing area’s start and endpoints. In this context, Nomos is elucidated as a conceptual wall that establishes the spatial parameters of grazing zones and serves the purpose of locating sacred sites2. In this context, Schmitt’s theological politics is fully realised. A deliberate restriction is placed on potential further land appropriation through the sacralisation of specific sites. This is underscored by Schmitt’s careful identification of areas where such land acquisitions might occur.

    Essentially, the conceptualisation of initial land appropriation serves as a deterrent against any attempts to seize land within the boundaries of Europe. In this way, Schmitt assumes that any land grab on the other side of the Atlantic3 is permissible and even necessary. By removing the concept of land appropriation from the European context due to its prior occurrence, division, and use for grazing purposes, Schmitt advocates for establishing a terrestrial spatial order. This represents a declaration that wars of annihilation, particularly those aimed at land appropriation, are prohibited within the European domain.

    Schmitt asserts that new Nomos can emerge in response to historical events in the ‘New World’, but it always stems from a new spatial division. Nomos is the foundational event that divides terrestrial space, occurring in early modernity, which Schmitt sees as clearly defined. This tripartite logic—sovereignty arising from the first appropriation, division, and utilisation of land (Nehmen/Teilen/Weiden)—provides the framework for identifying borders that define territorial limits and, consequently, the space of politics. This process of land appropriation in early modernity serves as the foundation for the international law of Jus Publicum Europaeum, as highlighted by Lauren Benton’s A Search for Sovereignty. Law and Geography in European Empires, 1400–1900 states (2007).

    Notwithstanding, Schmitt fails to demonstrate how the first of the three processes of tripartite logic, land-appropriation, occurs. Schmitt cannot explain how the first act is legally organised, leaving no gaps to think that the act of violence is the act that enables land appropriation and builds political authority. To a large extent, as Benno Teschke (Teschke 2011b) states, ‘the ‘concrete’ is mostly factual’ (Teschke 2011b: 214). The antecedent act of appropriation of the land has no sociological derivation; it refers, uniquely and exclusively, to an almost divine act of transcendental affirmation that cannot be denied. Schmitt thus silences history itself and sociology; Schmitt’s land-appropriation is disjointed from any dynamics of the social field (Teschke 2011b: 214).

    Colonial history underpins these claims. For Schmitt, colonial history is a series of spatially determined foundational events where order and location intersect. I follow Kalyvas in this debate, who even adds an important colonial critique: ‘The very existence of the Jus Publicum Europaeum depended upon a free colonial soil outside Europe as it did on the sea’ (Kalyvas 2018: 3) ‘The colony is the basic spatial fact of hitherto existing European international law’, its ‘foundation’ and ‘basis’ (Schmitt 1945: translation by Timothy Numan 2011b).

    The international law of Jus Publicum Europaeum warrants the exploitation of non-European soil and justifies warfare in these yet-to-be-territorialised spaces. As such, the founding of a colony is viewed as the initial act of land appropriation. Fillipo Ruschi (2008) in Space, Law and Power in Carl Schmitt remind us that this does not alter the Nomos, as these spaces are not bound by the international law of Jus Publicum Europaeum; rather, they form a fundamental part of it, existing on the other side of the Atlantic (Ruschi 2008).

    Thus, spatial division is the means of ordering, as Minca and Rowan suggest, since managing conflicts is the aim of order, as both also suggest. The spatial division manages conflicts and organises any social activity, not only the experience of the conflict. The spatial division informs the legitimacy of the narrative that can be organised from the boundaries not only of political topography but also of the limits of the local authority. That’s where politics dwells. That imagination was reinforced in Schmitt’s texts year after year, and this founding trait of the order promoted by spatial politics became more evident. In sum, ‘the spatialisation of the political was explicitly theorised as a primaeval act of order founding’ (Minca and Rowan 2015: 274).

    As Schmitt notes, peace and law are founded upon spatial enclosures, ‘[t]he enclosing ring, the fence formed by men, the men-ring, is an original form of the cultic, legal and political living together’ (Schmitt, 2003: [1950]). For instance, Schmitt first introduced the concept of Nomos in a lecture on 3 October, 1933, as Palaver (1996) reminds us, concluding by stating that Hitler’s will represent the Nomos of the German people (Palaver 1996: 106). In doing so, Schmitt suggests that decisionist and institutional elements can coexist. Nomos encompasses decisions and orders, with Schmitt seemingly attempting to resolve the tension between these concepts (Palaver 1996: 106).

    In his spatial ordering scheme, it is essential to note that only the firm land is the site of the location ordering event that underlies the international law of Jus Publicum Europaeum and the global space order. Therefore, he refers ‘to the relation between space and nomos by emphasising the historical and geopolitical difference between land and sea’ (Palaver 1996: 108). For Schmitt, the first nomos of the earth ‘was based on a particular relation between the spatial order of firm land and the spatial order of the free sea, and for 400 years it supported a Eurocentric international law: the jus publicum Europaeum’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 49).

    In conclusion, Schmitt’s spatial-political thought centres on the division between land and sea as a foundational element of political order. By conceptualising Nomos as the event that demarcates terrestrial space, Schmitt positions land as the primary site of sovereignty and concrete order. However, this emphasis on land leads to a significant oversight: the sea’s role in shaping global order, particularly within colonial contexts. Schmitt’s framework fails to account for maritime spaces that were exploited through colonial practices, such as privateering and the transatlantic slave trade, which played a crucial role in the development of global political and legal structures. This omission creates tension in his theory, leaving the maritime dimension of political order inadequately addressed. Ultimately, Schmitt’s spatial division between land and sea proves not only theoretically problematic but also historically inaccurate, as it disregards the interconnectedness and interdependence of these domains in forming a global order.

    Rayas, the first sovereign line attempt

    Carl Schmitt opens the second part of The Nomos of The Earth by telling us that a ‘hitherto unimaginable problem’ ([1950] 2003: 86) had just arisen: ‘the spatial ordering of the earth in terms of international law’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 87), and that this problem was the result of the awareness of the totality of planet Earth and its shape: a globe. Circumnavigation and the awareness of what was contained on this globe presented the concreteness of the planet to Europeans during the 15th and 16th centuries. An opportunity opened to put into gear the modern international law that lasted until the 20th century. From this opportunity, at first, derives the struggle to seize land and sea; lines will be drawn to divide the globe and will function, each in its own specific way, as weapons for these struggles. According to Schmitt, the lines represent efforts to establish the boundaries of a space that encompasses the entire globe, particularly the ‘New World’. It is worth emphasising that he argues these lines are not merely geographical separations but also carry underlying assumptions about the representation of political space4.

    This context, Schmitt suggests, presents the ‘New World’ as a free space for European occupation and expansion and not as an enemy like Islam was in the Middle Ages, for example (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 90). This free space became the object of an intra-European struggle, and it was from this equation that a new spatial order emerged (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 88). New divisions of the Earth were underway, and a new spatial order, which was becoming a global one, was being crafted. To guide the distribution and division of these new free spaces, a new way of thinking about space was created: global lines. These were drawn up on the cartographic maps that developed as Europeans became aware of the planet’s space and the treaties forged during conflicts between European kingdoms and states.

    In 1493 The papal bulls, Aeterni Raegis and Inter Caetera, were released (Policante 2015), which aimed to justify this universal mission of Christendom, inaugurating a globally contoured order that would ease tensions between the largest Catholic kingdoms: that of Portugal and Spain. Rome would divide the world by an imaginary line that would cut any space, regardless of the topography, whether sea or land. Already on 4 May, 1494, in Pope Alexander VI’s edict Inter Caetera Divinae, the first line was drawn dividing the ‘New World’ between Portugal and Spain through a meridian located 100 leagues5 west of the Cape Verde archipelago; what was west of the meridian would be Spanish, and what was east Portuguese.

    In the wake of this papal decree, almost one month later, the Christian Catholic Kingdoms of Portugal and Spain, situated on the edge of the Atlantic Ocean, established another world division in the Treaty of Tordesillas. A line called Rayas, drawn from pole to pole, established the division of the whole world. One hundred leagues west and south of the Azores, all the firm land found would be destined for Spain, and, with that, the control of the maritime space would also be of Spain; on the east side, the unknown space (land or sea) would belong to Portugal.

    The Rayas came into force by stipulating ‘the boundary between their respective missionary zones’ (Policante 2015: 31). Filippo Ruschi (2008) points out that the papal decree was of unique importance since it was the closest legal order to the universal, the most hierarchical order possible, the divine order. It was an act of dividing and distributing this new global space in religious terms, as Odysseos and Petito (2009) point out. People of other faiths were the targets of this spatial missionary zone. In the end, the Rayas ‘aimed at the internal division of the new lands between two land-appropriating Christian princes within the spatial order of the Respublica Christiana and guaranteed by the common authority of the pope as head of the Roman Catholic Church’ (Odysseos and Petito 2009: 309). The missionary condition of the Papal mandate allows the occupation of non-Christian spaces (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 86). This assumes, for Schmitt, anchored in his reading of Francisco de Vitória, the founding condition of spatial order, the appropriation of land, even for religious reasons. The founding act of the spatial order, the first land appropriation, was organised.

    Still, without distinguishing between ‘taking’ land and ‘taking’ sea, these lines (Rayas) not only inaugurated the movement of Catholic Christian kingdoms to conquer new non-European spaces but also inaugurated a new debate on how to deal with the High Seas in political terms, as well as (beginning) to promote a new global spatial order according to the International Law of the Jus Publicum Europaeum. Moreover, Schmitt argues that the ‘New World’ created the conditions necessary for the displacement of total wars. The location of the total war over land disputes was being established away from Europe. This statement receives assertive criticism underlying a certain fanciful story, as Beatrice Heuser states: ‘he constructed a fantastic benign past in which European law produced a balanced system of sovereign States with the unrestrained right to go to war… supposedly had the aim of limiting and containing war, not of abolishing it; Schmitt claimed that the net effect of this limited aim was to limit the wars themselves’ (Heuser 2022: 42). This perspective critiques the modern international legal framework, highlighting Schmitt’s belief in the inherent tension between sovereignty and conflict regulation.

    As mentioned earlier, the concept of Rayas does not address the distinction between land and sea in terms of appropriation. It, therefore, serves ‘only’ to divide the space of the missionary and ‘civilising’ mandate between Portugal and Spain. A mandate that will require the appropriation of land to be carried out. According to Schmitt’s political cosmology, the indistinction between the appropriation of land and sea is problematic, given the impossibility of topography to divide the sea, since maritime topography does not allow the border to be visualised. What is at stake, for Schmitt, is not the how and the why but what effects are produced by this indistinction.

    For Schmitt, the Rayas serve as the first step towards the constitution of the International Law of the Jus Publicum Europaeum, towards the beginning of a new spatial understanding that will organise a new order. Still, the Rayas do not organise the concreteness that only the first appropriation, apportionment, and exploitation - tripartite logic - makes possible. Only this logic would articulate the contours for the clear identification of the borders that will define the territorial limits and, therefore, localise the place where politics takes place. At sea, in Schmitt, this identification is impossible.

    Schmitt’s account of the emergence of modernity situates its origins in the ‘age of great navigations’, shaped by a new spatial awareness defined by the limits of the Atlantic Ocean, the ‘discovery’ of lands beyond it, and the recognition of the Earth as a globe. According to Schmitt, this transformation required a reconfiguration of spatial order, distinct from the medieval framework, which had been structured around Islam as the defining adversary and Rome and Jerusalem as the central points of the world (Ruschi 2008).

    In this context, the Rayas, for Schmitt, become the first ‘performative’ element of spatial thinking. It allows spatial meaning to the world that has just been ‘discovered’ and begins to organise the taking of the lands presented by this ‘new world’. By claiming this act as the founder of a new concept of space, Schmitt removes the experiences, practices and dynamics that develop on the high seas from the set of elements that will bring about European expansion. As Derman criticises: ‘The order of solid land is based on the fact that it is divided into state territories; the high seas, on the contrary, are free, i.e. free of the state and subjected to no territorial state sovereignty’ (Derman 2011: 188).

    As Anna More explains, the Raya is a fundamental component of the Nomos of the Earth, representing the myth of land distribution as the foundational act of political community formation (More 2014: 3). However, Schmitt overlooks that the Raya, as the initial mechanism for organising land appropriation, is not an isolated construct but part of a broader Atlantic system that emerged alongside it and subsequent territorial demarcations. The Raya alone did not structure appropriation, division, and exploitation; instead, it set a series of interconnected processes that shaped the spatial and political order in motion.

    Another essential point, in opposition to Schmitt’s argument, is that the elaboration of imaginary lines manifested in the papal donation of 1493 and reinforced in the Treaty of Tordesillas of 1494 somehow inaugurated the role of imaginary maps - representations - in understanding the global spatial order (Teschke 2011). In this context, imaginary lines in abstract spaces, such as Rayas, differ from the category of ‘geographical cartography.’ In The Nomos of the Earth, Schmitt overlooks that representations of events in the New World, whether on land or at sea, influence how cartographies are constructed. As Schmitt himself notes, these cartographies both shape and are shaped by a new spatial conception of the world. Experiences, rather than topography or geometry, became the primary basis for mapping the maritime and terrestrial spaces to the west of Europe. This mapping mode generated definitions of distribution and order, creating a sense of domination and control over these newly encountered spaces.

    Schmitt’s analysis of the spatial ordering of the world, particularly through the division of the ‘New World’ by the papal bulls and the Treaty of Tordesillas, highlights the foundational role of legal demarcations in shaping international order. However, his framework prioritises land appropriation as the central act of spatial organisation while marginalising the complexities of maritime space. Focusing on the Raya as the key performative element of spatial thought, Schmitt neglects the interdependence between terrestrial conquest and marine expansion, failing to recognise how control over the seas was essential to maintaining European dominance. The blurring of boundaries between land and sea in the early modern international order was theoretical and practical, as maritime routes, trade networks, and naval power were critical to the viability of territorial claims.

    Furthermore, Schmitt’s perspective overlooks the role of maritime practices in shaping the conditions of European imperial expansion. While the Raya imposed a spatial order on land, the fluidity of the seas facilitated mobility, commerce, and conflicts that were instrumental in defining the emerging global system. Ultimately, although Schmitt’s analysis sheds light on the juridical logic of European expansion, his narrative remains incomplete without fully incorporating the dialectical relationship between land and sea as co-constitutive forces in forming the modern global order.

    Amity lines in motion

    According to Schmitt, the amity lines are based on entirely different assumptions about the European seizure of land and sea in the New World (Schmitt [1950] 2003). The constitution of the line is derived from the religious wars between Catholic and Protestant maritime powers in Europe. It is important to note, at this moment, that Schmitt makes it very clear that European ‘land powers’ do not participate in the constitution of this new line and that this new line would open the way for English privateers to plunder freely (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 92). Furthermore, the line freed Catholic France to join forces with ‘dangerous heretics’, savage pirates, freebooters and buccaneers against the Catholic king of Spain (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 93). Schmitt seeks to organise space to facilitate a war of annihilation while preventing its manifestation as violence on the eastern side of the line. The amity line established the boundaries of ‘European public law’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 93), delineating where its norms and principles applied and where they did not. This conceptual division aimed to contain conflict within a structured framework, ensuring that the violence of annihilation remained confined to spaces outside the jurisdiction of the European legal order.

    The history of these lines began in 1559 when the Treaty of Cateau Cambrésis was signed between Spain and France. It’s important to note that neither the Portuguese nor the English were party to this treaty, which didn’t stop the English from benefiting. The Treaty was intended to stabilise European borders on the continent (a consequence of the struggle between France, Spain, and the Holy Roman Empire for control of what I now call Italy) along a rigid sovereign spatial line. The French-Spain ‘amity lines’ were based on entirely different premises, incorporating an agonal character, as described by Odysseos and Petito (2009: 309). Such lines had a distinct character from the Rayas; subsequently, apart from the amity lines, the Jus Publicum Europaeum over the sea would have no effect (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 93-4). There were ‘no legal limits to war, only the law of the strongest was applied’. West of the first meridian, ‘violence committed by one party to the other should not be considered a violation of the treaties’6.

    Schmitt asserts that a new line of amity was formally established in 1634 when Cardinal Richelieu, on behalf of the French King, prohibited the pillaging of Portuguese and Spanish ships north of the Tropic of Cancer (Schmitt [1950] 2003). Vertical and horizontal lines were drawn in various treaties, yet their construction remained ambiguous, especially at sea, where defining precise boundaries was challenging. Schmitt’s theory emphasises these geometrical divisions within fixed geographical limits, reflecting Cartesian thought. However, his focus on stationary boundaries overlooks the spatial dynamics of sea routes, trade networks, and vessel movements. The critique centres on the argument that movement, rather than static spatial thinking, offers a more revealing lens for understanding spatial order, thereby challenging Schmitt’s emphasis on immobility and fixed structures.

    The establishment of amity lines marked a shift in spatial consciousness and the concept of space, as Schmitt highlights in The Nomos of the Earth ([1950] 2003). Odysseos and Petito elaborate on this transformation, noting that these lines distinguished two distinct ‘open’ or ‘free’ spaces: ‘On the one hand, the land mass of the ‘New World’, which was not recognised as belonging to the native populations, and on the other, the newly mapped and navigable seas’ (Odysseos and Petito 2009: 309-10). Within these open spaces, European powers exercised violence to appropriate Indigenous lands and engaged in open warfare against one another. As Schmitt observes, ‘[e]verything that occurred ‘beyond the line’ remained outside the legal, moral, and political values recognised on this side of the line’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 94). This framework facilitated expansionist endeavours, particularly by England and Holland, two dominant Protestant maritime powers that strongly opposed the Rayas, further redefining global spatial order.

    For Schmitt, the Rayas represented the contest for land appropriation in the New World under a common authority. At the same time, the amity lines reflected the religious conflicts between Catholics and Protestants, driven by the struggle for territorial control. However, this perspective tends to overlook or downplay major wars within Europe, both before and after the Treaty of Cateau-Cambrésis, that exhibited annihilatory characteristics and were motivated not only by religious divisions but also by political and territorial disputes.

    Notable examples, such as the Peasants’ War (1524–1525), the Eighty Years’ War (1568–1648), and the Thirty Years’ War (1618–1648), demonstrate the persistence of devastating conflicts in Europe. These wars, driven by religious and political motives, reshaped the continent’s political landscape. Schmitt’s claim that amity lines created a definitive spatial division under the Jus Publicum Europaeum, suspending wars of annihilation, is difficult to substantiate. He argues these lines established clear territorial divisions and a shared legal framework, implying extreme conflicts were ended. However, historical evidence shows that wars of annihilation continued, contradicting Schmitt’s assertion. The ongoing violence undermines his view that amity lines immediately ceased such struggles within Europe.

    Neither the Rayas nor the amity lines were peacefully established or universally accepted by all European parties. Their consolidation resulted from prolonged struggles, and for a considerable period, the two lines coexisted. As previously mentioned, the backdrop to this transformation was the conflict between Protestants and Catholics, which gradually eased the tensions surrounding European territorial sovereignty. Key events, such as the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, the Treaty of Utrecht in 1713, and the Congress of Vienna in 1815, played pivotal roles in mitigating these conflicts and shaping the evolving political order in Europe7.

    Schmitt argues that the Protestant divisions influenced the geographical orientation of European powers, with the Calvinists, predominantly English, venturing into the sea, while Germans, adhering to Lutheran Protestantism, favoured land. He suggests that this was one of the factors that led Germany to remain a land power. The Dutch, following a combination of Lutheran, Anabaptist, and predominantly Calvinist traditions along the coast, present a more complex case. According to Schmitt, this helps explain why Germany remained land-based while the Dutch and English turned to maritime exploration. Therefore, the attachment to land or sea was shaped by the specific form of Protestantism practised. France, staunchly Catholic, remained a land power, resisting the Huguenots8 until 1572, and only later began engaging more actively in maritime ventures (Palaver 1996: 115).

    On the other side of this religious dispute were Portugal and Spain, Catholic Kingdoms, embarking upon maritime expeditions not with the primary intent of maritime dominion but rather to reach and conquer the lands beyond the sea, which would differ from the English. For Schmitt, as Palaver states, despite the conquest of America, both Portugal and Spain remained land powers and did not become maritime powers (Palaver 1996: 116). Contrary to Schmitt’s assertion, Palaver demonstrates that, in the case of Portugal and Spain, the sea was not merely a passage to new lands but a complex network of trade routes that facilitated their expansion. Schmitt’s argument, which simplifies the conquest of the Americas as just a link between the Old and New Worlds, overlooks the Atlantic Ocean’s pivotal role in establishing a new spatial order. Rather than serving as a mere conduit, the sea was integral to shaping the geopolitical dynamics and the global networks of trade, colonisation, and power. This perspective highlights the Atlantic’s central role in creating a new world order beyond the simplistic view of land conquest.

    The Atlantic, with its archipelagos—Madeiras, Azores, and the Canary Islands—served as vital control and logistical support nodes. These islands were strategically placed to facilitate maritime trade routes, including those for enslaved black people, gold, and silver (Palaver 1996). The sea, therefore, was not just a space for movement but a medium through which Spain and Portugal asserted dominance, shaping global commerce and political power. Failing to recognise the Atlantic Ocean’s role in the ‘first land appropriation’ limits our understanding of the spatial revolution central to Schmitt’s theory.

    Portugal and Spain, in their pursuit of territorial expansion in the ‘New World,’ extended their influence over the sea—not to assert outright maritime dominance but as a necessary condition for securing vast territories across the Atlantic. On the Protestant side, English and Dutch vessels, operating under letters of marque, introduced privateers into this dynamic. Here, it is crucial to distinguish between dominion in the seas versus dominion over them. While the Rayas previously articulated control over the seas, the amity lines enacted control within them.

    As Policante observes, this struggle was not merely territorial but involved ‘a struggle over the very foundation of the Res Publica Christiana.’ (Policante 2015: 39). The expansion beyond the Rayas was about land appropriation and competing religious missions—whether Catholic or Protestant—aimed at ‘civilising’ the Indigenous populations. Schmitt, however, does not fully engage with how this civilisational dispute influenced the Rayas and amity lines, shaping the maritime order. Thus, colonising the ‘New World’ extended beyond Schmitt’s tripartite land appropriation, division, and exploitation logic. While he sees spatial consciousness as central to any spatial revolution, he grants little significance to the religious contest over the Res Publica Christiana despite its profound impact on shaping order and transforming spatial understanding.

    During this period, a specific difference between terrestrial and maritime spaces began to be articulated; according to Schmitt, these spaces were distinguished in political-legal terms precisely to establish the premises for appropriating the new firm land. At first, such spatial political imagination assumed by Schmitt seems to offer a logical explanation to make sense of his tripartite logic. Nevertheless, the conditions of possibility for appropriating the lands of the ‘New World’ materialise as the maritime routes between Europe, Africa and the Americas are structured in the Atlantic Ocean (Benton 2007; More 2014).

    Routes that are articulated, as previously mentioned, to circulate, initially, gold and silver, and in a second moment, to supply black slave labour for the continued extraction of metals and raw materials, in addition to the production of food, such as sugar, corn and coffee. It is the circulation of enslaved Black people, raw materials and plantation production that subsidises the appropriation of solid land in the ‘new’ world. In this sense, the constitution of these maritime routes cannot, in any way, be interpreted simply as conduits between two continents, as Schmitt does, but instead as a sovereign and spatially fluid practice, as Lauren Benton and Anna More articulate.

    The concept of amity lines contributes to the reconfiguration of spatiality, establishing two distinct forms of open space. On one hand, free land was available for appropriation; on the other, the free sea, encompassing the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific Oceans, were designated as open zones for English, Dutch, and later French expansion. In both domains, violence operated beyond the constraints of the Jus Publicum Europaeum. Consequently, the sea was perceived as an unregulated and untamed expanse devoid of historical and political significance. However, contrary to Schmitt’s perspective, the absence of public law does not render the sea an empty or ahistorical space. Rather, the maritime realm is a fundamental historical and political arena shaped by and shaping terrestrial events. Land and sea spatialities function mutually reinforcing, contributing to the broader spatial order.

    As previously discussed, the sea is not a passive expanse but an active geopolitical space, both influencing and being influenced by terrestrial events. The dynamic interplay between land and sea reinforces the broader spatial order, challenging perspectives that marginalise maritime dynamics. Furthermore, the claim that no overarching authority, such as the Catholic Pope, directed wars against non-Christian peoples to impose moral, legal, or political constraints across these spatial divisions is overly simplistic. It overlooks the transformations already unfolding in the Atlantic shortly after 1492. Failing to recognise the significance of the Wars of Annihilation in 16th - and 17th century Europe — conflicts that reflected distinct spatial configurations and modes of warfare —constitutes a critical gap in understanding early modern spatial order. Similarly, neglecting the network of organised trade routes, particularly in the Atlantic, which enabled the conquest of the ‘New World’, reflects a narrow view of the sea’s historical and political importance.

    In conclusion, the transformation of spatial order in early modernity cannot be fully grasped without recognising the central role of the sea and the importance of the amity lines. The Atlantic was not merely a conduit between continents but a dynamic and politically charged space integral to European expansion and the structuring of global trade networks. Schmitt’s focus on territorial appropriation and land-based power neglects the critical role of maritime routes in shaping the modern spatial order. Transatlantic trade, the movement of goods and people—especially the trafficking of enslaved individuals—and strategic control over the seas were pivotal in consolidating European dominance.

    Moreover, due to the absence of a European public law framework, Schmitt’s portrayal of the sea as an ‘empty’ space overlooks its historical and political significance. The amity lines redefined spatial consciousness, establishing new power divisions that extended beyond territorial conquest. These lines were not neutral boundaries but marked zones where European powers could pursue violent expansion free from the constraints of the Jus Publicum Europaeum. The sea was not a passive medium but an active force shaping geopolitical structures, both influenced by and influencing terrestrial events.

    A broader analysis of the amity lines and the religious conflicts that shaped them reveals that Schmitt’s tripartite logic of land appropriation, division, and exploitation is inadequate to explain the complexity of early modern spatial transformations. Recognising the sea as a fundamental component of global order—rather than an ahistorical void—offers a more comprehensive understanding of the spatial revolution that redefined early modernity and reshaped global power dynamics.

    The Sacrifice of the Sea and the Fluid Sovereignty

    ‘Man is a terrestrial, a groundling’ (Schmitt [1942/1954] 1997: 1) that is the sentence that opens Land and Sea. This sentence holds a lot of meaning in Schmitt’s political thought. He informs the reader, from the start, that land is a priority over the sea, that individuals are imperatives of the land, inherent to terrestrial space, and live in a space that they can pasture and cultivate, the land space. Individuals cannot be dispossessed of land, and it is essential to clarify that anything outside this framework of belonging lies beyond the bounds of normality for Schmitt. This section explores the enduring significance of this assertion, which originates in Land and Sea and resonates throughout The Nomos of the Earth. Within this context, I highlight an inconsistency in Schmitt’s framework, which prioritises terrestrial domains over maritime spaces. I support this critique by analysing the expanded conceptualisation of sovereignty, demonstrating how it challenges Schmitt’s terrestrial bias and underscores the need for a more balanced understanding of spatial order.

    Schmitt’s narrative lacks precision and is marked by significant distortions, as Joshua Derman (2011: 188), among others, has pointed out. Derman (2011: 188) argues that ‘the stability of interstate relations was not eroded by the opposition of land and sea but rather sustained by them.’ Schmitt’s intellectual perspective was heavily influenced by his immersion in German politics and the struggle for hegemony between England and Germany, particularly in Europe. This focus led him to overlook more nuanced political thought and instead portray the English as the antagonists of history. Schmitt conceptualises the sea as a space of plunder for imperial ambitions, framing it as an extension of empire—specifically, the British Empire, which he depicts as a malevolent force requiring containment. This external dimension reflects Schmitt’s tendency to simplify complex geopolitical dynamics, reducing them to a binary opposition between land and sea.

    Starting on this premise, I will explore key questions regarding Schmitt’s political thought on the sea: his conceptualisation of it as a non-place devoid of history, sacrificed to reinforce his political-spatial logic. Simultaneously, I will further develop the notion of fluid sovereignty, as articulated by scholars such as Anna More (2014) and Lauren Benton (2007), to challenge the political and historical marginalisation of the sea in Schmitt’s framework and to emphasise its crucial role in shaping modernity. The central argument is that sovereignty can be understood through the movement of sovereign authorities within fluid spaces like the Atlantic Ocean. Examples include privateer vessels targeting specific political communities or private ships supporting trade routes that facilitated colonisation. In such fluid spaces, sovereignty becomes fluid, adapting to maritime space’s dynamic and interconnected nature. This perspective destabilises Schmitt’s rigid terrestrial focus and underscores the sea’s significance in articulating modern political and spatial order.

    In A Search for Sovereignty: Law and Geography in European Empires, 1400–1900, Lauren Benton (2007) develops the concept of fluid sovereignty to describe how sovereign power was not uniformly exercised across vast territories but rather manifested in fragmented and contested ways, particularly in colonial and maritime spaces. She argues that European empires did not impose sovereignty as a fixed territorial authority but adapted it to local conditions, engaging in negotiations with indigenous populations, traders, and rival powers. At sea, sovereignty was not anchored in absolute territorial control. Still, it flowed through networks, such as selective legal enforcement, intermittent military presence, and strategic control over ports and trade routes. This concept challenges the conventional view of sovereignty as an absolute and indivisible attribute of the state, instead presenting imperial rule as operating within legally ambiguous spaces where multiple normative orders coexisted. Fluid sovereignty thus highlights a dynamic model in which power was exercised intermittently and negotiated across imperial peripheries, shaping colonial governance’s legal and political landscapes.

    Lauren Benton (2007) advances this discussion by examining the conflicts between vessels seeking to obstruct their competitors’ movement in the New World colonisation and the circulation of capital across the ocean. She argues that authority on the high seas was inherently fluid, materialising through the presence of ships flying the flags of European states, which operated without fixed spatial limits to restrict movement. These vessels, endowed with authority by their patron states, embodied a form of mobile sovereignty. The only means of impeding their passage was a confrontation with another ship, which resulted in a clash between two sovereign authorities—an event that further underscores the fluid nature of sovereignty.

    In sum, sovereignty, articulated by Benton (2007), is not territorially fixed but continuously shifting, flowing across maritime spaces. While it is omnipresent in a broad sense, it becomes specifically localised to individual vessels at moments when they exercise authority. This insight is crucial, highlighting the profound ambivalence in the relationship between space and sovereignty, challenging conventional notions of authority as rigidly tied to territorial domains.

    Expanding the critique, Anna More, in From Lines to Networks: Carl Schmitt’s Nomos and the Early Atlantic System, critically examines Carl Schmitt’s conception of sovereignty, particularly concerning European expansion in Africa and the transatlantic enslaved black people trade. More contends that contrary to Schmitt’s rigid distinction between state and non-state spaces, European expansion was characterised by sub-state practises and complex power networks transcending clear geographical boundaries (More 2014). She highlights that Africa, far from being a region devoid of sovereignty before European arrival, was a space of continuous conflict and negotiation, governed by sub-state entities such as trading corporations and religious orders, most notably the Jesuits. These actors operated through networks connecting disparate regions, challenging the notion of a homogeneous and territorially fixed state sovereignty (More 2014).

    More argues that Africa’s incorporation into the European global economic system did not occur through the straightforward extension of European sovereignty over unoccupied territories, as Schmitt suggests in the case of the Americas (More 2014). Instead, it unfolded through the continuous and indefinite expansion of market forces, in which multiple normative orders coexisted and interacted. This perspective reveals a form of fluid sovereignty, manifesting locally through specific entities such as flagged vessels or commercial enclaves, yet resisting rigid spatial divisions and traditional conceptions of political authority. Thus, More contributes to a more nuanced and interconnected understanding of the relationship between space and sovereignty during the era of Atlantic expansion.

    Both Anna More and Lauren Benton contend that conceptualising sovereignty through the spatial dimension of the maritime domain requires a rethinking of traditional understandings, which rigid and fixed elements have historically defined. Their analysis proposes a broader theoretical framework in which sovereignty at sea is understood as a dynamic and fluid phenomenon—what might be termed ‘sovereignty in motion.’ Schmitt would firmly reject this interpretation, as it fundamentally challenges the political-spatial reconfiguration central to his vision of order, which he sought to anchor predominantly in terrestrial space. This divergence highlights the tension between static, land-based conceptions of sovereignty and the more adaptable, maritime-oriented perspectives advanced by More and Benton.

    More and Benton’s arguments prompt a critical reassessment of the historical narrative in The Nomos of the Earth in the International Law of the Jus Publicum Europaeum. They reframe Schmitt’s claims regarding the constitution of the global spatial order through his tripartite logic. Their perspective underscores the inherent limitations of Schmitt’s historiographical approach, highlighting the challenges of relying on his work as a historical account rather than a theoretical intervention shaped by his political commitments (Heuser 2022).

    The period between the ‘discoveries’ of the New World and the circumnavigation, which happened from the 15th century to the 17th century, means, for Schmitt, ‘the first complete spatial revolution on a planetary scale’ (Schmitt [1942/ 1954] 1997: 33), once a ‘planetary consciousness’ (Schmitt [1950] 2003: 86), as Minca and Rowan claim. This observation is essential because it contributes to revealing the weight that Schmitt adds to the concept of space. Planetary consciousness produces changes of revolutions and is only possible from circumnavigation and the limits articulated by the Rayas.

    For Schmitt, the first spatial revolution occurred precisely because Europeans had come to recognise the geographical limits of the planet. This newfound awareness rendered space finite on land and at sea, leading to two key developments in his thought. First, it redefined the conceptualisation of space and its representations. Second, it reshaped how individuals understood themselves concerning a now-limited world. For Schmitt, ‘the quantitative enlargement of the geographic skyline’ (Schmitt [1942/1954] 1997: 29)—and the realisation of its boundaries—became a foundational element in structuring the global spatial order, specifically the European-led order. Thus, in Schmitt’s framework, the reorganisation of global order was made possible only through a fundamental shift in spatial consciousness.

    Within Schmitt’s framework, the terrestrial realm is presented as the appropriate domain for organised human activities rooted in appropriation, division, and exploitation. These processes are viable only on land, where constraints—particularly those restricting movement—can be effectively imposed. In contrast, the sea is characterised by its inherent resistance to division, enclosure, or allocation. In the dichotomy between sea and land, the sea is depicted as a void lacking historical sedimentation, as Minca observes. Politically and historically, the sea, as a constitutive element of modern politics, is subjected to a process of sacrifice, enabling the dominance of terrestrial history and preserving the state-centric world order. Nevertheless, the sea remains a constant presence at the periphery of land, as a perpetual reminder that the established order is precarious and subject to potential disruption at any moment.

    Schmitt argues that the sea serves as a vital medium for politically defining terrestrial space through a logic of differentiation. This process relies on the continuous political sacrifice of the sea, which reinforces land as the primary domain of sovereignty and political agency. Within this binary framework, the sea remains indispensable, as its opposition to land legitimises territorial authority. In Schmitt’s spatial theory, the political boundary between land and sea is not arbitrary but emerges from their inherent geophysical differences. This demarcation is crucial for structuring claims to sovereignty within the global spatial order. Consequently, in Schmitt’s conceptualisation, the sea functions as a material conduit and a symbolic marker of political distinction.

    The political sacrifice of the sea reinforces the tripartite logic that legitimises both sovereignty and the sovereign state. At the same time, within the spatial framework of international law, the reclamation of the sea becomes essential in defining its relationship to land, ultimately shaping the spatial order of the Jus Publicum Europaeum. Thus, Schmitt’s conceptual framework relies on a dual process: the symbolic sacrifice of the sea as a foundational element of political modernity and its subsequent reclamation. These acts are essential to the differentiation that supports his geopolitical vision, reinforcing the dichotomy between land and sea as central to global spatial and political order organisation.

    Although the sea plays a crucial role, its significance lies in its sacrificial positioning in favour of the land. Political theology once again asserts itself, with sacrifice being exercised in the name of a greater order—the terrestrial spatial and legal framework structured by Schmitt’s tripartite logic of sovereignty. The dichotomy between land and sea, Germany and England, Behemoth and Leviathan, and elephant and whale demands a specific type of sacrifice: a political sacrifice of the sea. This act becomes fundamental to Schmitt’s epistemology, reinforcing the primacy of terrestrial sovereignty.

    Schmitt’s argument gains traction when he asserts that land ownership arises from the ‘speed’ of the appropriation process. Unlike the sea, where appropriation is immediate and unprepared, land appropriation is gradual and non-violent, relying on sustained, incremental use rather than immediate exploitation, such as harvesting fruit from a tree. This deliberate process contrasts with the direct act of fishing, which lacks the preparatory effort seen on land. Fishing does not constitute an act of transforming space or working within it in the sense of cultivation, shepherding, or preparing space to yield productive outcomes. Unlike these activities, which involve shaping and developing land to generate tangible results, fishing operates within a different spatial logic, which does not entail the same spatial modification or cultivation processes.

    For Schmitt, fishing is a booty act (Steinberg 2001: 269), unlike when a field is sown (Steinberg 2001: 269). This distinction raises a significant issue: the impossibility of controlling activities in a space with geo-elementary specificities typical of fluidity. In the absence of boundaries and authority, and with no constraints on free will at sea, the central issue lies in the potential for absolute freedom, where nothing prevents the ‘plundering’ of resources. A completely free space, ungoverned and without order, poses a significant challenge. This is why Schmitt finds the sea so problematic. Given his rigid conceptualisation of sovereignty, physically and theoretically, the sea resists any attempt at control or taming.

    The political sacrifice of the sea arises from its central role in Schmitt’s paradigm. To preserve the integrity of his political framework, Schmitt necessitates the conceptual elimination of the sea, as its indeterminacy threatens his rigid sovereignty structure. In both practical and metaphorical terms, the sea resists domestication, defying any attempt to fit it within a fixed sovereign framework. Faced with the challenge of subjecting the sea to such control, Schmitt’s only solution is its exclusion from the political realm. Thus, he characterises the sea as a space without historical or political significance, marked only by its waves and devoid of substantive content. This exclusion exemplifies the deliberate political and historical sacrifice of the sea in Schmitt’s theory.

    Benton’s concept of fluid sovereignty challenges the fixed boundaries of sovereignty established in the early modern period. While traditional territorial demarcations have emphasised static sovereign practises, the dynamic movements within the maritime domain also play a crucial role. Rather than rigid, sovereignty is fluid and continually reshaped through adaptation, encoding, and decoding processes. This fluidity highlights a key aspect of modernity, revealing the ongoing and often violent consequences of sovereignty’s redefinition. The Atlantic Ocean exemplifies this fluidity, offering a new perspective on the practice and conceptualisation of sovereignty as a political construct. Benton’s framework questions Schmitt’s spatial archetype, which relies on the political and historical sacrifice of the sea to privilege land.

    Schmitt’s attempt to marginalise the sea in favour of rigid, land-centred sovereignty ultimately fails. The sea’s inherent fluidity resists fixed spatial and legal structures, exposing Schmitt’s spatial politics’ limitations. His framework depends on a strict land-sea dichotomy, yet this opposition underscores the impossibility of fully excluding the maritime domain from political considerations. Despite Schmitt’s efforts, the sea remains a vital contestation, movement, and power space.

    Benton’s articulation of fluid sovereignty directly challenges Schmitt’s rigid spatial order by demonstrating that sovereignty operates through dynamic and adaptive processes rather than static territorial boundaries. Far from being a historical void, the Atlantic emerges as a critical arena where sovereignty is exercised through power, commerce, and conflict networks. By emphasising the interplay between land and sea, Benton destabilises Schmitt’s logic of sacrifice, revealing how maritime spaces remain central to articulating modern political authority. Ultimately, the sea is not sacrificed—it persists, actively shaping and being shaped by sovereignty in motion.

    Coda

    This article contributes to the ongoing critical engagement with Schmitt’s spatial theory, focusing specifically on a hitherto under-examined dimension: the role of the sea. Schmitt’s conception of Nomos is limited by a dichotomous logic that links geography and politics. In his formulation, Nomos can only be conceived in concrete territories, where land can be appropriated, demarcated, and organised within the boundaries of sovereignty. Thus, unlike land, the sea is conceptualised as an abstract space, an unappropriable domain lacking fixed borders. This binary opposition enables Schmitt to construct a political theory in which sovereignty is confined to defined, static territorial spaces.

    However, this analysis posits that sovereignty is a far more dynamic concept than Schmitt suggests, particularly when we analyse the role of the sea in shaping modern politics. Far from merely a conduit between sovereign and non-sovereign spaces, the Atlantic Ocean actively contributed to organising the global order. The sea connected territories and structured political, commercial, and colonial relations, central to forming modernity. Lauren Benton’s ‘fluid sovereignty’ concept directly challenges Schmitt’s spatial political thought by illustrating how sovereignty was historically exercised beyond rigid territorial demarcations. Instead of being a space of chaos, the sea functioned as an arena where overlapping claims of authority and jurisdiction coexisted.

    Schmitt’s attempt to eliminate the land/sea dichotomy from the political imagination of the twentieth century is rooted in his concern with the ‘spatial revolution’ that was dissolving these boundaries. However, his analysis overlooks the fundamental role of the sea in the dynamics of colonisation, the commodification of enslaved bodies, and the very development of the modern political order. Sovereign practises were not confined to the terrestrial domain. Still, they were also exercised along transatlantic routes, in acts of plunder, and in the interactions between political entities navigating the ocean.
