Special Issue - Redefining Alliances: The impact of China´s Rise on Latin American International Relations • Rev. Bras. Polít. Int. 68 (2) • Nov 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/0034-7329202500223 linkcopy

Defining the Belt and Road: How Ideas and Interests Shaped Argentina’s Approach to the Initiative

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    How do ideas and identities affect how countries evaluate joining China’s Belt and Road Initiative? This article employs process tracing to analyze Argentina’s negotiations prior to its accession in February 2022. Argentina is a crucial case to explain because both the Macri and Fernández governments faced similar material benefits of infrastructure finance. However, the Macri government defined the BRI as a form of alignment with China, while the Fernández government interpreted it as South-South cooperation and autonomy from Western-led financial institutions. These findings show that developing countries evaluate both the material and symbolic costs and benefits of the BRI.

    Keywords
    Belt and Road Initiative; Development Finance; Argentina; China

    Introduction

    At the China-Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC) forum in Santiago, Chile in January 2018, China officially invited countries from the region to join the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) (“Declaración de Santiago II Reunión Ministerial Del Foro CELAC-China.” 2018). This formal invitation followed Panama’s signature of a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) joining the BRI the previous year, soon after it established diplomatic relations with China. Panama has since become the first in the region to withdraw from the BRI, in February 2025 (Valle Guerrero 2025). As of September 2025, 22 Latin American and Caribbean (LAC) countries are members (Nedopil 2025). Nineteen of these countries had joined by the end of 2019. Argentina, which had received nearly $6 billion in loans from China for the types of projects that the BRI intended to support (Ray and Myers 2023), did not join until February 2022.

    What explains countries’ decisions to join the BRI or refrain from doing so? Why did Argentina wait four years from China’s invitation to join? Why did it do so in that particular moment? This article traces the process of Argentina’s negotiations with China prior to the signature of its BRI MOU in February 2022. Drawing on elite interviews and policy documents, it shows that Argentina’s political leadership evaluated both material and symbolic costs and benefits of the initiative. For the government of Mauricio Macri (2015-2019), the symbolic costs of “aligning with China” were too high, and Argentina successfully extracted the material benefits of additional project financing without joining the BRI. When Alberto Fernández (2019-2023) took office, the symbolism of the BRI as a “Global South initiative” transformed into a benefit rather than a cost, and political leadership calculated that joining would also come with more project finance.

    Although both the Macri and Fernández governments faced similar material realities, the ideas they held about China and the BRI shaped their responses to the initiative. Materially, they evaluated whether the BRI would contribute new and different financing for key infrastructure projects, building on the series of loans already signed with China. Symbolically, they considered how joining the BRI would affect Argentina’s positioning within an increasingly tense geopolitical landscape. Both governments were negotiating with the International Monetary Fund (IMF) at the same time they were considering membership in the BRI, which added another layer of material and ideational factors to consider. Previous research has shown that the lack of clarity on what ‘the BRI’ means has allowed a diverse set of Chinese actors to shape it (Ye 2020). This article demonstrates that this characteristic has also enabled member countries to define what the BRI symbolizes for them and their international relations. Varying interpretations of the BRI thus contribute to countries’ decisions whether or not to join the initiative.

    Since its arrival in the region, the BRI has not drastically changed China-LAC relations, with the forms of trade, investment, and finance relations remaining largely stable before and after countries signed MOUs (Jenkins 2022; Serrano et al. 2020). The BRI has not necessarily smoothed out the complex political processes and risks facing major infrastructure projects in Latin America (Leiva 2021), and it has not significantly impacted public opinion towards China in the region either (Morante and Wu 2023). Serrano et al. (2020) argue that the initiative has primarily changed the discourse surrounding China-LAC relations, rather than their substance. This article shows that precisely because the discursive element has been so central, the symbolic costs and benefits of joining have taken on outsized importance in addition to – and in some cases beyond – the potential material benefits of doing so. Argentina’s internal and international negotiations about the BRI illustrate this phenomenon.

    To explain how ideas and identities affect countries’ decisions to join the BRI, this article employs a process tracing methodology. Process tracing defines a sequence of steps in a causal process, testing whether and how a given variable affects the outcome of interest (Bennett and Checkel 2015). It is especially useful for identifying the moment at which a change occurs in a key variable and assessing whether it leads to a change in the outcome. The single case study of Argentina is useful because it is possible to exploit over-time variation in the variables of interest: material and symbolic costs and benefits. While the former remained static, the latter changed, providing an ideal test of whether and how a shift in symbolic interpretations of the BRI changed the country’s decision to join.

    The data are drawn from a combination of elite interviews and policy documents. Using a non-random, purposive sampling strategy (Lynch 2013), interviewees were selected based on their proximity and participation in key decisions or negotiations related to BRI accession. They were identified through a combination of public records and snowball sampling. In total, 31 interviews were conducted between May and July 2024, with an overall response rate of 62%. To supplement interview data, where available, policy documents such as meeting minutes, cooperation agreements, and press releases were consulted. As recommended by Bleich and Pekkanen (2013), Appendix A provides a detailed description of the interview methodology and a list of all interviews conducted.1

    The rest of this article proceeds as follows. Section 2 provides a brief overview of Argentina-China relations in the 21st century and the factors influencing Argentina’s foreign policies towards China. Section 3 discusses the Macri government’s approach to the BRI, including the state visit to China during the 2017 Belt and Road Forum and the subsequent decision to not join the initiative before his term ended. Section 4 follows the decision of the Fernández government to join the BRI and the negotiation process leading to the MOU signed in February 2022. The final section concludes.

    Argentina-China Relations in the 21st Century

    The dynamics of China-Argentina relations are heavily shaped by the contradictory preferences of powerful domestic actors: the agrifood exporters seeking integration with China, and the industrial sector seeking to exclude China. Through the commodity boom of the 2000s and 2010s, the agrifood sector generally came to see China as the “great consumer” of their exports, especially soy products (ARG_2, ARG_6). By 2019, China was the destination for over 70% of Argentina’s beef exports and over 80% of soy exports (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2019), raising some concerns about dependency (ARG_11). Given that the BRI promises to facilitate trade between China and member countries, Argentina’s commodity exporters stand to benefit from membership. The BRI’s emphasis on infrastructure will also benefit these groups because improved transportation to Argentina’s ports enables exports to China and other markets.

    At the opposite end of the spectrum, the rise of China has implied a competitive threat for Argentina’s domestic industry. One interviewee described China as the worst country in the world for Argentina’s industrial productivity (ARG_4), while another described it as a haunting specter (ARG_5). Since 2003, China has been the top target of Argentina’s anti-dumping investigations (Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022). Argentina’s industrial sector thus stands to lose if the BRI increases trade with China. Furthermore, the BRI’s goal to support Chinese companies’ activities abroad also implies a competitive threat for Argentine industry in terms of sub-contracts on infrastructure projects. For example, during the Macri government, an Argentine steel company, Techint, and the Unión Industrial Argentina (UIA) successfully torpedoed a government contract between the province of Córdoba and Chinese companies for a pipeline because the steel would have been imported from China (Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022).

    BRI membership not only implies material costs and benefits, but also symbolic costs and benefits, and political actors in Argentina are directly affected by these less tangible elements as well. Argentina’s foreign policy has historically tended to oscillate between two poles: one emphasizing alignment with the US and free trade, and another committing to autonomy from the US and a state-led development model (Russell and Tokatlian 2013; Treacy 2021; Rubiolo and Telias 2023). In the 21st century, China has emerged as an alternative source of development finance from the US and as a successful model of state-led economic development (ARG_8, ARG_31; Mazzina and González Cambel 2018). Leftist governments in the region have taken advantage of these dynamics to both legitimize and fund their own state-led development projects (Russell and Tokatlian 2013; Treacy 2021; Rubiolo and Telias 2023). Therefore, a closer relationship with China, including through mechanisms such as the BRI, benefits political actors favoring an autonomist-statist foreign policy by demonstrating international support. On the other hand, for more pro-US and pro-neoliberalism political actors, joining the BRI would impose a heavy symbolic cost because it would signify abandoning their international political and economic convictions.

    The governments of Néstor Kirchner and Cristina Fernández de Kirchner from 2003-2015 marked the beginning of a new phase of Argentina-China relations, coinciding with China’s rise on the global stage, its massively expanded demand for primary commodities, and the fallout from the 2008 global financial crisis. Argentina and China established a Strategic Partnership in 2004, which they upgraded to a Comprehensive Strategic Partnership in 2014 (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2014). The Fernández de Kirchner government pursued a “counter-hegemonic” (ARG_6) international position and “neglected” (ARG_24) relations with the West (Mazzina and González Cambel 2018; Monestier 2019). This approach was closely intertwined with the challenges Argentina faced in renegotiating its debt with US-based private creditors and the opportunities China presented as an “alternative” to finance from US-backed institutions such as the World Bank and IMF (ARG_8, ARG_31).

    Alongside the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership, Argentina and China established the Strategic Dialogue for Economic Cooperation and Coordination (DECCE), which has become the primary institutional mechanism for the relationship and which played a key role in the BRI negotiations (“Argentina y China dieron inicio al mecanismo de diálogo estratégico para la cooperación.” 2014). Since its establishment, the DECCE has met five times. These meetings are typically led by the foreign minister, with participation from other officials in the Ministry of Foreign Affairs, International Trade and Culture (MRECIC), the Argentine embassy in China, and other ministries, such as economy, finance, transportation, and production. The detailed agendas and attendee lists are not usually public, but those available highlight infrastructure projects, macroeconomic conditions, and the investment environment (“Argentina y China dieron inicio al mecanismo de diálogo estratégico para la cooperación.” 2014; República Argentina 2017). A former official who participated in these meetings explained that this is where decisions are made about which infrastructure projects will receive priority consideration (ARG_15).

    During the Fernández de Kirchner government from 2008-2015, Argentina and China signed eight loans for a total of nearly $5.3 billion (Ray and Myers 2023). These included loans for flagship projects such as the Belgrano Cargas and San Martín railway rehabilitations and two hydroelectric facilities in Santa Cruz, both of which were part of the government’s commitment to state-led development efforts. The Joint Action Plan for the 2014-2018 period was established within the DECCE framework, but also covered political, social, and security areas, such as education exchange, counternarcotics cooperation, nuclear energy, outer space research, fishing, and environmental sustainability (República de Argentina, and People’s Republic of China 2014). This was the context of Argentina-China relations facing the Macri government when it came into office in December 2015: a close bilateral relationship covering a broad scope of issue areas, a series of large loans for major infrastructure projects, and several institutional mechanisms for negotiation.

    Macri and the BRI: A Polite Observer

    China’s invitation for LAC countries to join the BRI arrived during the government of Mauricio Macri, a center-right president who sought to establish a more conservative economic model by lowering export taxes, removing price and currency controls, and ending many subsidies (Monestier 2019). His government looked favorably upon globalization and more neoliberal policies, which inclined them towards the US and Western Europe over China and Russia (ARG_7; Busso and Barreto 2020; Treacy 2021). President Macri’s foreign policy platform aimed to “re-balance” Argentina’s international relations and deprioritize China (“Anexo I: plataforma electoral Cambiemos.” 2015; Mazzina and González Cambel 2018). This goal to “cool down” relations with China (ARG_8) led the government to renegotiate terms on projects such as the Santa Cruz hydroelectric facilities and the space observation station in Neuquén (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2016).

    Despite this, the Macri government did not “break with China” (ARG_7, ARG_8), largely due to the central role that agrifood exporters played in the political coalition. Expanding Argentina’s exports to China and attracting investment in sectors such as infrastructure, agrobusiness, energy, and finance were stated goals of the Macri government (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2018b). However, this government did view China with more suspicion than their predecessors, and their initial efforts to curtail relations were met with China’s decision to halt imports of Argentine soybean oil in 2016. The agrifood sector responded forcefully, lobbying the Macri government to repair relations and regain market access (Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022). Foreign Minister Jorge Faurie met with sector associations and major conglomerates to discuss their export strategies, market access, and limitations they faced in certain countries (Ministerio Del Interior, 2025). In August 2017, the Ministry of Agroindustry coordinated a visit to China for several grain and oilseed companies to negotiate with Chinese importers (Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022). In October 2018, after several rounds of negotiations, Argentina persuaded China to reopen its market to soybean oil imports (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2018a). In May of the same year, China allowed imports of Argentine beef products after 15 years of negotiations (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2018d). Pressure from this major political actor pushed the Macri government towards a more moderate stance on China in order to secure export revenues (Treacy 2021; Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022).

    In its campaign platform, the Macri government stated its intention to produce an “infrastructure shock” to Argentina’s economy, which aimed to spur growth by lowering logistical costs and promoting development throughout all the provinces (“Anexo I: plataforma electoral Cambiemos.” 2015; Mazzina and González Cambel 2018). The platform specifically referred to ports, water transport, railways, roads, energy generation, and electricity distribution. President Macri’s subsequent Plan Belgrano promised $16 billion in infrastructure-related investment in Argentina’s ten Northern provinces, many of which also export soy and other commodities to China (“¿En qué consiste el ‘Plan Belgrano’ de Mauricio Macri para desarrollar el norte del país?” 2015). Given that it had already provided financing for multiple transportation and energy projects in Argentina, China was a logical funding source to cover some of this gap, and its new global flagship program, the BRI, specifically aimed to do that. However, at each possible juncture, the Macri government chose not to join the BRI, instead achieving its goals through other mechanisms of cooperation.

    Argentina’s first brush with the BRI came during President Macri’s state visit to China in May 2017, where he attended the Belt and Road Forum for International Cooperation. The Joint Communique of the Leaders Roundtable following the summit noted that the BRI was “open to other regions such as Africa and South America” and outlined several priorities for infrastructure construction, including “roads, railways, ports, maritime and inland water transport, aviation, energy pipelines, electricity, fiber optic including trans-oceanic cable, telecommunications and information and communication technology” (“Joint Communique” 2017). President Macri’s own speech at the forum praised the initiative’s commitment to building infrastructure in developing countries and likened it to his domestic Plan Belgrano (“El Presidente expuso en el foro ‘una franja y una ruta para la cooperación internacional’.” 2017). The press leading up to the visit speculated about which projects would be discussed and how much financing Argentina would be able to secure (López 2017; Dapelo 2017). Interestingly, the BRI forum was more of a footnote in the debate about what would happen with the two potential nuclear plants, the delayed hydroelectric facilities in Santa Cruz, and the San Martín railway project.

    Rather than join the BRI, Argentina negotiated a project pipeline through the DECCE meeting in April 2017, only a month prior to Macri’s China trip. At that meeting, the two countries agreed on a list of 16 priority projects, including five railway rehabilitation projects, four hydropower stations, a solar plant, the two nuclear power plants, and the Santa Cruz dams (República Argentina 2017). China had repeatedly pressured the Macri government to move forward with the Santa Cruz dams and nuclear power plant after it paused them for further review upon taking office. The meeting minutes reported that China “strongly required” Argentina to meet a specific timeline for environmental review of the dams (República Argentina 2017). The Five-Year Integrated Plan for Infrastructure Cooperation 2017-2021 (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2017) agreed upon at that meeting contained much more detailed project information than the typical MOU to join the BRI, and the creation of this document in the context of planning President Macri’s China trip indicated that Argentina could still secure projects without being a BRI member. There was thus not a clear material benefit to joining the BRI itself, since China was already planning to support at least 16 projects.

    National leaders are known to reward subnational geographies who are politically aligned with them by directing foreign investment and development finance to projects in those regions (Tang 2021; Giraudy et al. 2024). Figure 1 illustrates the geographical distribution of the projects listed in the 2017 DECCE pipeline and the provincial-level results for the 2015 presidential elections. Solid black provinces are those that Macri won and that received projects, while provinces in solid gray received projects but did not vote for Macri. Stripes indicate a province that Macri lost and that did not receive a project. Appendix B provides the project data used to construct this figure.2 While all provinces that Macri won in 2015 received projects, several provinces that he lost received projects as well. The map also shows that of the ten Northern provinces identified in his Plan Belgrano, eight received projects, indicating that Chinese finance did support the government’s major infrastructure development agenda. This geographical analysis reiterates the conclusion that at the time, the BRI may not have promised sufficient benefits in terms of project financing above what Argentina had already been able to achieve via the DECCE.

    The prospect of Argentina joining the BRI was raised again during Foreign Minister Wang Yi’s visit to Argentina in May 2018 for the ministerial-level meeting of the G20. China highlighted the complementarity between the BRI’s goals and the Macri government’s own plans for infrastructure development (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2018b). Over the course of 2018, thirteen LAC countries joined the BRI, including Chile, led at the time by the center-right government of Sebastián Piñera (Nedopil 2025). Argentina began to face direct pressure from China to join the initiative, but the Macri government continued to refuse (ARG_5). The press release following President Xi’s state visit to Argentina alongside the G20 leaders’ meeting in November 2018 reflects China’s efforts at convincing Argentina; it framed the BRI as a natural extension of the Comprehensive Strategic Partnership (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2018c). China also provided material benefits to Argentina around the summit, including donations of security equipment and the reopening of its market for Argentine beef exports (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2018). The following year, Foreign Minister Faurie attended the second BRI forum in Beijing, where he repeated the language used in the joint statement following President Xi’s visit: that the “strategic partnership is extendable” to the BRI (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2019). However, Argentina chose not to actually extend the relationship by joining.

    Given the Macri government’s continued pursuit of financing from China, its participation in BRI-related events, and the growing number of LAC member countries, Argentina’s refusal to join is puzzling. The material impacts of membership would likely be positive, even if not substantial. For example, Foreign Minister Faurie highlighted the BRI’s potential to facilitate Argentina’s economic growth via trade and better connectivity between the producing regions and ports (“Entrevista Con Jorge Faurie, Canciller de Argentina.” 2019). Joining the BRI may also have appeased a China concerned about Argentina’s commitment to completing its flagship hydroelectric, railway, and nuclear energy projects. China’s decision to halt soybean oil imports was a clear signal early on in the Macri government of the costs that China could impose if Argentina’s political swings threatened its interests. A more institutionalized framework such as the BRI could have provided some insurance against these shifts.

    The symbolic nature that the Macri government ascribed to the BRI explains Argentina’s refusal to join the BRI, despite the potential material benefits and almost non-existent material costs of doing so. Political leaders primarily conceptualized it as a “geopolitical” initiative (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2019). Right-leaning newspapers associated with the government were more likely to frame the initiative as a threat than left-leaning media closer to the opposition, especially before Argentina formally joined (Vila-Seoane 2023). Even the BRI’s economic qualities took on more suspect characteristics. Officials in the Macri government explained that when it came to China, economic affairs increasingly took on security dimensions, raising questions about whether investments from China would negatively impact national security (ARG_5, ARG_29). As US-China competition escalated over the course of the Macri government, some Argentine officials received light warnings from the US against joining the BRI, but stated that they did not experience the type of pressure later governments did (ARG_5). Around the G20 leaders’ summit in November 2018, some speculated that Argentina’s continued refusal to join the BRI resulted partially from President Macri’s desire to avoid a direct confrontation with Presidents Trump and Xi both physically located in Buenos Aires (Niebieskikwiat 2018; Serrano et al. 2020). However, US pressure was unnecessary to provoke Argentina’s reluctance to join.

    The idea of Argentina joining the BRI acquired an association with ‘aligning’ with China among political leadership (ARG_5), many of whom saw themselves as ‘Western’ (ARG_12). This interpretation was extremely problematic for a government that had taken office intending to re-establish its credibility with the US and Europe and limit deep political integration with China (“Anexo I: plataforma electoral Cambiemos.” 2015). The Macri government had formulated its foreign policy around the concept of “intelligent insertion,” which meant economic opening and market diversification (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2016). They aimed to rebuild relationships with Argentina’s “traditional partners” in the US and Europe (ARG_12). Joining what political leaders perceived as China’s primarily geopolitical initiative was understood as a type of ideological alignment antithetical to these goals. This self-perception of ‘Western-ness’ was especially characteristic of the private sector economic elites that Macri appointed to his cabinet and other political positions (Escudé 2015; Treacy 2021). An interviewee explained that the rural agrifood sector has an underlying current of anti-China sentiments (ARG_2). Rather than any material concern, this sentiment stems from this self-identification as ‘Western’ (Escudé 2015). This identity was tied in with their ideological preferences for Western modes of political economic organization, chiefly globalization and neoliberalism (Treacy 2021). These ideas and identity competed with their material interests vis-à-vis China, resulting in a preference against joining a more overtly political initiative led by a country seen as challenging the West. As a result, the Macri government refrained from formally joining the BRI, preferring to secure the material benefits of project finance through joint action plans and the DECCE meetings.

    The Fernández Government: Argentina Joins the BRI

    Argentina finally joined the BRI in February 2022 during President Alberto Fernández’s state visit to China. The MOU itself was a fairly short document, committing the countries to cooperation on development policies, infrastructure connectivity, trade and investment, financial integration, people-to-people diplomacy, and government procurement (Ministerio de relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2022b). The headlines instead focused on the nearly $24 billion in financing announced alongside the MOU (Ministerio de Relaciones Exteriores, Comercio Internacional y Culto 2022a). Of this, $14 billion had already been agreed as part of the list of projects negotiated for the fifth DECCE meeting, and Argentina would present proposals for the rest of the financing. As these developments indicate, the material benefits of the BRI still centered around project finance. However, the Fernández government ascribed a different symbolic meaning to the initiative, seeing it as an example of South-South cooperation and means for Argentina to pursue its development goals independent of conditionalities imposed by Western finance. It was this change in the symbolic benefits, despite relatively static material benefits, that led the Fernández government to join the BRI where the Macri government had refrained.

    The negotiations for Argentina to join the BRI took place during a complex period of Argentina-China relations. The COVID-19 pandemic began only a few months into the Fernández government, which necessarily shifted his government’s foreign policy goals towards pandemic assistance and recovery. Argentina received $5.62 million in pandemic-related donations from China, equivalent to $0.12 per capita (Telias and Urdinez 2022). At the same time, Argentina was facing debt repayment issues, and the IMF attempted to leverage its position to force Argentina to limit its relations with China in certain areas, especially 5G (ARG_2). In July 2023, Argentina activated $1.7 billion of its currency swap with China as a bridge loan to repay IMF debts, and repaid China the following month with a disbursement from its IMF Extended Fund Facility (Arnold 2023; Internantional Monetary Fund 2023). Despite its financial troubles, the Argentina-China relationship was productive during the Fernández government. The Permanent Bilateral Commission met virtually in 2020, the Comixta trade meeting took place in 2022, and the DECCE met officially in 2022 (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2020; 2022). President Fernández visited China twice, once in 2022 when Argentina joined the BRI, and once in 2023 where he attended the BRI forum.

    The Fernández government began negotiating to join the BRI in 2020, shortly after taking office (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2020). Like their predecessors, political leaders saw project financing as the chief material benefit of the initiative. President Fernández’s first ambassador to China, Luis María Kreckler, announced in November 2020 that Argentina planned to join the BRI “very soon” (Zárate 2020). He referred to the opportunities for infrastructure financing and construction in transportation, energy, and telecommunications as areas where Argentina needs support. Two government actors managed these negotiations: the Secretariat of Strategic Affairs (SAE), an office within the Casa Rosada, and the Embassy in Beijing. Interviewees explained that the SAE functioned as a mini-Ministry of Foreign Affairs handling high-level international economic, political, and security policy (ARG_15, ARG_24, ARG_32). The “whole foreign policy agenda passed through” the SAE, and this office led negotiations on the BRI (ARG_32). These comments are consistent with research showing that in Argentina, a “parallel bureaucracy” of political appointees manages most public policy decisions (Spiller and Tommasi 2008; Sanz Cerbino et al. 2022).

    The embassy in Beijing was strongly in favor of Argentina joining the BRI, but other areas of the government were more skeptical. The SAE itself, despite its leadership role, sought to negotiate more slowly and ensure that joining would bring tangible benefits (ARG_32). They organized an inter-ministerial roundtable in 2020 to design a roadmap of public-private investment between China and Argentina (“Memoria Detallada Del Estado de La Nación.” 2020). The Ministry of the Economy, led by Martín Guzmán, lacked clarity on what material benefits membership could bring Argentina and believed the potential costs were high (ARG_1). On the other hand, the Ministry of Defense, led by Jorge Taiana, was eager for Argentina to join (ARG_15). The Ministry of Production was another proponent of joining. The Minister of Foreign Affairs, Felipe Solá, was largely absent from the BRI negotiations (Dinatale 2021), although his successor Santiago Cafiero took a more active role. These lines were drawn along a larger political cleavage within the Fernández government’s coalition: a center-left group led by the president, aiming for a more “balanced” relationship with China, and a leftist group led by the vice president, seeking closer relations with China and distance from the West (Busso 2022).

    Like the Macri government before it, the material priority for the Fernández government was project finance. Over the course of 2021, though the DECCE, Argentina and China negotiated over a list of 15-20 priority projects to receive financing, which included many of the projects previously outlined in the 2017 plan (“El canciller Cafiero habló de las obras con China en el CARI.” 2021). According to one official, the Argentine side wanted to add as many projects as possible to the list (ARG_15). The same official explained that China’s top priorities were the nuclear power plant, which had been stalled for nearly ten years, and the Santa Cruz dams, which were again paralyzed in 2020. China had already pressed Argentina on the timeline for the dams at the 2017 DECCE meeting (República Argentina 2017), and the constant delays frustrated Chinese officials and companies (ARG_15). Throughout these negotiations, China had requested that Argentina refrain from publishing a specific dollar amount, in keeping with the norm of confidentiality in previous DECCE meetings. However, an estimate of $30 billion leaked to the press (Dinatale 2021), irritating China when there were already frictions over whether to prioritize new or existing projects (ARG_15). Argentina ultimately submitted 16 proposals for BRI projects in addition to the ten DECCE projects already negotiated (“Las inversiones que vienen.” 2023). The DECCE projects included those on both governments’ lists, as well as the Gas Pipeline, Cierre Energético Norte, and the Buenos Aires City (CABA) Electricity Transmission Line; all others listed in 2022 were BRI proposals.

    As Table 1 shows, there was substantial continuity in the project list between its 2017 and 2022 iterations. Several specific projects were carried over, and nearly all the new projects were in transportation, energy, and water management, sectors which had dominated the Macri government’s list as well. The debate over whether to join the BRI took place within the longer-term negotiations over which projects China would fund in Argentina and how Argentina would manage the construction of Chinese-funded projects. The Fernández government viewed the BRI as an opportunity to build on pre-existing collaboration with China for projects in key infrastructure sectors. President Fernández’s second ambassador to China, Sabino Vaca Narvaja, highlighted the bilateral cooperation plan signed alongside the MOU as a unique achievement among BRI member countries, indicating that membership itself is a mechanism for achieving the underlying goal of project finance (Fontevecchia 2023).

    Figure 2 shows the geographical distribution of the DECCE and BRI projects from 2022 alongside the provincial-level results for the presidential election in 2019. Like Table 1, it shows continuity in the locations of the projects between the two governments. There were only four provinces listed with projects in 2017 that did not appear in the 2022 lists: Mendoza, Misiones, Neuquén, and Santiago del Estero. Fernández won three of those provinces, indicating it is unlikely he was punishing the political opposition. The three provinces that gained projects – Catamarca, Chubut, and Formosa – voted for Fernández. Of the eleven provinces listed for BRI projects specifically, Fernández won eight and lost three in the 2019 presidential election. Thirteen provinces were marked for DECCE projects, largely overlapping with the list for BRI projects; Fernández won eight of them. This finding suggests that BRI projects were slightly more likely to be located in provinces that the current government had won than the DECCE projects were. The BRI may therefore have provided some material benefit for political leadership to distribute projects to political allies, but four provinces that Fernández lost still received projects. As in 2017, the projects were distributed across provinces that the current government had both won and lost; this was true for both BRI and DECCE projects. This further supports the conclusion that the change in Argentina’s membership status was due to the change in symbolic benefits rather than material ones.

    The Fernández government defined the BRI very differently than their predecessors. They saw the BRI as a form of South-South cooperation, an alternative to the hegemony of Western countries in international financial institutions. In an emerging multipolar international system, China was a key player, and the BRI was its flagship global program (ARG_12, ARG_16, ARG_17; “Frente de Todos plataforma electoral.” 2019). Ambassador Vaca Narvaja referred to the BRI as a “multilateral forum, but accessed bilaterally” (Centro Venezolano de Estudios sobre China 2021). From this perspective, the BRI was not a unilateral geopolitical agenda, but an open invitation for countries to join a multilateral initiative focusing on development. Political leaders saw a “Eurocentric world” in decline (Fontevecchia 2023), and they did not share the sense of ‘Western-ness’ as some officials in the Macri government. Instead, they saw Argentina as a ‘peripheral’ country, a developing economy, and member of the Global South (ARG_17, ARG_27, ARG_32). The BRI and its emphasis on infrastructure as a means to development offered Argentina membership in a group that validated not only its views on domestic development but also its commitment to elevating Global South countries as international actors.

    For the Fernández government, the BRI was a symbol of autonomy, rather than alignment. The reference was the US and the IMF, which political leaders saw as the primary threats to Argentina’s foreign policy independence (“Frente de Todos plataforma electoral.” 2019). The Kirchnerist wing of the governing coalition was highly critical of the IMF, as they had been during her two terms as President, further underscoring the urgency of locating alternative sources of credit (Mazzina and González Cambel 2018; Busso 2022). The notion of autonomy was especially salient given the pressure Argentina and other Latin American countries faced from the US to curtail their engagement with China. One official explained that the US expressed a “lack of joy” over Argentina’s potential BRI membership (ARG_20). This is consistent with US officials’ public statements criticizing the BRI as a subversive tool China could use to coerce Latin American countries (U.S. Southern Command 2021; 2022). Privately, according to an official present at the time, the IMF attempted to use debt relief as a bargaining chip to secure a commitment from the Fernández government to exclude Huawei from Argentina’s 5G networks (ARG_2). These comments further underscore the conclusion that US pressure was neither necessary nor sufficient for the Macri government to eschew BRI membership.

    However, not all areas of the government saw the IMF and China as rivals (ARG_14). Instead, in the Ministry of Economy, the currency swap with China served as proof to the IMF that Argentina could access external financing and that it held some foreign reserves (ARG_13, ARG_14). In the weeks prior to President Fernández’s trip to China, Argentina and the IMF reached a baseline understanding of the main terms for a program, and the IMF Executive Board approved a $44 billion program in March 2022 (IMF 2022). The program documents discussed key fiscal and monetary policies that Argentina would need to implement in order to remain in good standing, including reducing energy subsidies (Internantional Monetary Fund 2022). On the other hand, Chinese loans do not come with policy conditionalities requiring macroeconomic reforms in return for funds (Kaplan 2021). Thus, the contrast between the IMF’s and China’s approach to financing, even if they were not seen as rivalrous options, was particularly salient.

    The Fernández government confronted a BRI largely the same as that which had faced the Macri government but decided that it was to Argentina’s advantage to formally join the initiative, rather than simply attend related events and comment favorably on its goals. The key difference lies in the interpretation of what the BRI means. For the Macri government, it meant alignment with China, an idea that was unacceptable for a government trying to rebuild its relations with the West. For the Fernández government, it symbolized autonomy from the long history of US and IMF interventionism in Argentina.

    Conclusions

    This article has traced the process of Argentina’s delayed incorporation to the BRI in 2022, nearly four years after most other LAC countries. It demonstrates that shifting interpretations of what the BRI means to potential member countries helps explain countries’ decisions to either join or refrain from doing so. Both the Macri and Fernández governments sought financing for a long list of infrastructure projects. In fact, despite the vast differences in political ideology and foreign policy goals between them, over 50% of the projects were on both governments’ lists. This indicates that the material component of the BRI was relatively stable over time. In contrast, the two governments defined the BRI very differently, leading the Macri government to avoid joining and pushing the Fernández government towards membership. For the former, the BRI was a symbol of ‘alignment’ with China. For the latter, it was a symbol of ‘autonomy’ from the US and IMF, and a symbol of South-South solidarity.

    Much like the ambiguity in the original BRI proposals allowed Chinese actors to shape the initiative downstream, the contested discourse surrounding the BRI enables potential member countries to define it for themselves. Those interpretations subsequently shape their evaluations about the costs and benefits of membership. The vague nature of the BRI and the lack of concrete material impacts that the initiative has had on China-LAC relations allow potential member countries to ascribe their own meanings to the initiative. By leaving it open ended, actors within China and member countries shape it according to their own ideas and interests.
