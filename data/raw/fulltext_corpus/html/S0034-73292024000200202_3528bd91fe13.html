Universal Periodic Review: contemporaneity, originality and research agendas

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The Universal Periodic Review (UPR) is the most recent addition to the United Nations’ mechanisms to monitor human rights worldwide. Its creation in 2007 symbolized a more cooperative and less asymmetrical alternative for the treatment of human rights than more traditional mechanisms. The body of literature on UPR usually offers two attributes to it: contemporaneity and originality. While both generically denote the emergence of a “new” element in the international system; individually, each one makes up a different idea. This article proposes a precise and analytically dense elaboration on these attributes by examining (i) the political-normative paradigm at its creation, as well as (ii) the UPR’s originality factors compared to other traditional human rights mechanisms. This specific exam helps resolve possible analytical-conceptual mismatches between the UPR and the more traditional human rights mechanisms. It also allows for the identification of gaps and potentials to expand the academic knowledge and the professional performance in the UPR’s practical field.

    International Institutions; International Organizations; Human Rights; Universal Periodic Review; United Nations Human Rights Council

    Introduction

    The Universal Periodic Review (UPR) is an international mechanism for the regular monitoring of the human rights situation in all 193 UN member states. Unlike the traditional human rights mechanisms created in the Post-Second World War, the establishment of the UPR in 2007 was not originally aimed at filling a specific gap in the international system. The universal human rights system already had the capacity to deal with a myriad of human rights issues, whether from the general human rights procedures established in the Post-Second World War, or through mechanisms derived from the International Conventions on Human Rights, characteristic of the Cold War and Post-Cold War periods.

    In this context, the creation of the UPR can be understood as a symbol of the search for a more cooperative treatment of human rights, as it established a peer-reviewing system – instead of human rights experts review as in other traditional mechanisms – and submitted all UN member states to a general review of the human rights situation on their territory following a pre-determined and cyclical agenda. The mechanisms stemmed from a particular glimpse of its time: the thesis of the necessary modernization of the international human rights system raised by several states, international bureaucrats, independent experts and non-governmental organizations (NGOs) at the UN during the first decade of the 21st century. Among other measures, such “modernization” developed through the introduction of the UPR as an alternative dynamic for monitoring the human rights situation around the globe.

    In operation since 2008, the UPR has already concluded three monitoring cycles and generated an impressive volume of primary data from meetings, reports and decisions documented by the Human Rights Council (Council). The fourth cycle, which began in 2022 with the Review of Brazil and other eleven countries, renewed the opportunity for discussion around the UPR, especially within a country that has traditionally occupied a prominent position regarding the establishment of a more cooperative and universal approach to human rights.

    In reaction to the recently created UPR, scholars in the fields of International Relations, International Law and Political Science started to generate an accumulation of knowledge on the subject, whose findings have given important contributions to the understanding of an increasingly disputed international mechanism. The result is a whole new body of literature dedicated to analyzing the UPR through different focal points, such as the politics behind suggesting and accepting recommendations (Terman and Voeten 2018; Burger et al. 2021) or case studies that allow deeper comprehension of the impacts of the mechanisms on the ground, under the perspective of states (Chow 2017; Smith 2013) or civil society actors (Baird 2015; Gomez and Ramcharan 2018; McGaughey 2017).

    However, the assumptions underlying part of the understandings on the UPR reflect biases of knowledge arising from the political-normative environment prevailing in the Post-Second World War. The disregard of the UPR’s particularities can produce analytical-contextual mismatches, which inadvertently generate hasty conclusions about the legitimation of actors and key dynamics in the Review, as well as its impact and effectiveness.

    Therefore, since the assumptions and characteristics imprinted in the UPR differ significantly from traditional human rights mechanisms, it is crucial to establish critical points of differentiation between the UPR and other international mechanisms. Because of its multidisciplinary characteristic, IR studies allow for dialogue between literatures from different fields of social and political science and seems to offer an interesting space to foster reflections upon the UPR as a research object. In this article, we define these critical aspects based on the UPR’s attributes of contemporaneity and originality. While evaluating whether and to what extent the existing literature considers these attributes as analytical factors to the study of the UPR, we develop a comprehensive examination of the main research agendas on the subject. From this systematization of the UPR’s literature, we identified gaps and potential to expand the academic body of knowledge, as well as to improve professional performance in the practical field.

    This article consists of three sections. In the first, we address the UPR’s contemporaneity attribute and the context of its creation. Then, we elaborate on the originality attribute, through which we identify its unique characteristics and mobilize comparative parameters with other international human rights mechanisms. In the third section, we offer an original organization of main research agendas on the UPR, including the perspective of different academic fields such as International Relations, International Law and Political Science, examining the presence and analytical weight given to the attributes of contemporaneity and originality. In the conclusion, we point out ways to explore gaps and potential in the most promising research and practical agendas on the UPR.

    Contemporaneity: a new international human rights mechanism in a diverse political-normative paradigm

    The construction of the international human rights apparatus dates back to the end of the Second World War. At the time, the international community defined the legitimate interest in the reconstruction of human rights as a paradigm and ethical reference to guide the contemporary international order (Garbin 2021). The first international human rights norms and institutions were formally implemented through processes of legalization and institutionalization, as seen by the emergence of several international organizations (Cançado Trindade 2010). At the UN, for example, the United Nations Charter enshrined the human rights language (1945), the Commission on Human Rights assumed the bureaucratic body for the promotion and protection of human rights (1946) and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR) enlisted protected rights (1948). All these movements aligned with the idea of building an architecture for the protection of human rights that would work from “outside” the states, that is, from the international level (Lindgren Alves 2015).

    The human rights system at the United Nations – later identified as the universal human rights system – centered for 60 years on the work of the Commission on Human Rights (Commission). Despite the importance of the inaugural and structuring work undertaken by the Commission, such as the drafting of the UDHR and the International Covenants on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights and on Civil and Political Rights, as well as the adoption of the Special Procedures with ad hoc mandates for investigating violations within states, the body began to accumulate criticism around the 2000s.

    Critics from states, international bureaucrats and civil society organizations (CSOs) progressively led to the discrediting of the body. Disapproval ranged from a general inefficiency in the Commission’s work – low number of annual sessions, inability to act in the face of emergency situations, and deficiencies in the operational structure – to accusations of partiality and selectivity (Cox 2010; Schrijver 2007). Another factor generating setbacks in the international community was the Commission’s membership – which included countries that systematically violated human rights and used their member status to shield themselves from criticism.

    Taken together, all sorts of criticisms spurred movements towards the reformation of the Commission, a rare moment of convergence in international politics, at least with regard to three points: the Commission failed and needed to be replaced; a new body, with new rules, should be created; and the UN’s institutional human rights machinery needed to be strengthened (Alston 2006). The process of international negotiations to replace the Commission happened amid the reformist impulse led by then-UN Secretary-General Kofi Annan and involved proposals from states from different regional and political positions, recommendations from the Secretary-General and suggestions from the High-Level Panel, composed of independent experts (Castro and Ramanzini 2020).

    The ambivalent process of replacing the Commission for the Human Rights Council (Council) on March 15, 2006 (Resolution 60/251 of the General Assembly), marked a crucial point in the protection of human rights and represented a source of both changes and continuity in international human rights policy. Among the aspects of continuity, the routines and procedures established by the Commission stand out, such as the Special Procedures. As for the novelties, there are (i) the new bureaucracy – reduction in membership and geographical distribution of seats, increase in the number of regular sessions, the possibility of convening special meetings, change of status to a subsidiary body of the UNGA, and (ii) the adoption of the UPR, a symbol of the search for a more cooperative and less asymmetrical alternative for the treatment of human rights (United Nations 2005). The expectation was that the establishment of the UPR would reduce the influence of more powerful states and the defense of private interests.

    As history shows, the UPR did not result from international efforts aimed at filling significant gaps in the structuring of the international apparatus for the protection of human rights. The universal human rights system already had the capacity to deal with the most diverse human rights issues, whether from the general human rights procedures (Special Procedures) established in the Post-Second World War, or through mechanisms derived from the International Conventions on Human Rights, characteristic of the Cold War and Post-Cold War periods.

    Alternatively, the UPR emerged from a particular glimpse of its time. In the political environment of the 2000’s the international community witnessed the outbreak of the indiscriminate “war on terror” after the terrorist attacks of September 11 in the United States. The following invasions of Afghanistan (2001) and Iraq (2003) resulted in documented practices of torture and summary executions, in an evident disregard for human rights (Sikkink and Schmidt 2019), and represented several setbacks in multilateral and ethical structures.

    Beginning in 2003, the UN’s system itself was going through a reform process to better prepare the organization for the challenges typical from the turn of the millennium, such as terrorism, increased civil and ethnic conflicts and overlapping economic, social and environmental crises. In this sense, the conception of the UPR intersects with the proposal to modernize the international human rights system, providing an alternative to monitor human rights in an integrated way with security and development objectives (United Nations 2004).

    Finally, the normative environment on which the UPR was inserted differs significantly from the one that shaped the international protection of human rights in the Post-Second World War. Formally, until 1950, the normative paradigm of consent prevailed in the international system, according to which the will of states constituted the basis of international legal obligations. Within this paradigm, only states were legitimated to participate in international normative construction, as well as in the dynamics around the new-born international institutions. Gradually, with the development of international jurisprudence, the paradigm of consent started to be confronted by the paradigm of international consensus, according to which the conscience of the international community would become the basis of international legal obligations. Within this paradigm, subjects other than the State (such as international organizations, groups and individuals) would find themselves legitimated to participate in the normative construction and to take part in the many international dynamics (Cançado Trindade 2010).

    The UPR, therefore, is a human rights mechanism adopted in a diverse international environment, where competing normative paradigms coexist, but the paradigm of consent seems consolidated among traditional international actors. The paradigm of consensus, on the other hand, is rapidly developing, although still not as persuasive among international organizations and practitioners. Given the historical circumstances, the attribute of UPR contemporaneity shows that this mechanisms’ examination entails attention to the assumptions of knowledge brought from previous constructions in the international human rights realm. In any case, the underlying assumptions on UPR guide research and practice agendas on the subject, with potential impact for further analysis, as we will evaluate in more depth in section 3. Before that, we move on to the formulation of the attribute of the UPR’s originality, whose characteristics allow us to define the UPR as a mechanism that is not only new, but rather unique in the international human rights apparatus.

    Originality: a new international human rights mechanism with unique characteristics

    The UPR is usually presented as an unprecedented international mechanism in the international protection of human rights, insofar as it has introduced unique international practices for monitoring human rights. The peer-review model involves all 193 UN member states. Every five years, each State under Review (SuR) undergoes a hearing and receives recommendations for actions to improve its human rights performance. In Table 1, we systematized the functioning of the UPR based on the main documents and information produced throughout a cycle, according to each stage foreseen in this international mechanism.

    Table 1
    Universal Periodic Review Cycle: documents and main information

    The UPR cycle starts with document preparation about a year before the review of the Working Group. For each SuR, three preliminary reports on the human rights situation are prepared: the National Report, prepared by the State, and two other summary reports prepared by the Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights (OHCHR) with contributions from civil society, National Human Rights Institutions (NHRIs) and regional organizations, and from other UN human rights mechanisms, such as Special Procedures or Conventional Bodies. To prepare the National Report, which can have twice the length of the other two, the OHCHR recommends states to conduct broad national consultations and update the international community on the implementation of recommendations from previous cycles.

    Since the second UPR cycle (2012-2017), the mechanism counts with a new institutionality, the Pre-Sessions. These meetings organized by UPR-Info, an international NGO dedicated to organizing information and promoting the UPR, provide opportunities for stakeholders to present their views on the SuR and advocate for recommendations from states. The review is conducted about a month later by a Working Group, formed by members of the Council and interested states, in a three and a half hour meeting in Geneva. Each year the UPR reviews around 42 countries, divided into three sessions. During the review, also known as Interactive Dialogue or sabbatina, the SuR presents its National Report, answers previous questions submitted in advance, and receives recommendations from their peers, which may include UN observer members as well (A/HRC/RES/5/1). The troika, a group of three states that act as rapporteurs during the sabbatina, prepares the Working Group Report, listing the recommendations and/or conclusions made by the participating delegations (A/HRC/PRST/8/1).

    The SuR has up to three or four months until the next Human Rights Council session to position itself on the recommendations received – accepting or taking notes, justifying its answer in the last case, and expressing voluntary commitments. During the plenary session, other states, Council members or Observers, as well as other interested parties such as NGOs with consultative status and NHRIs are also given the opportunity to express their views on the report prior to its adoption. The comments made at that session integrate the final report of the Council session (A/HRC/RES/5/1). Both the Working Group Report and the Addendum containing the SuR’s responses to recommendations are adopted by the Council under item 6 of the agenda – dedicated to the UPR. Until the next review, states, stakeholders and UN mechanisms are encouraged to submit, on a voluntary basis, Mid-Term Reports informing the Council on the implementation of recommendations (A/HRC/RES/16/21).

    As can be seen from the above systematization, the UPR introduces a complex international mechanism, which establishes unique practices. It is now time to specify critical points of differentiation between the UPR and other international mechanisms.

    The UPR’s Originality Factors

    We determined five comparative parameters to highlight critical points of difference between the UPR and traditional human rights mechanisms, as organized in Table 2.

    Thumbnail
    Table 2
    Comparative Parameters between the Traditional International Human Rights Mechanism and the UPR

    Coverage

    First, with regard to the coverage of international human rights mechanisms on states, traditional mechanisms usually reach particular states, which take part at international organizations and international treaties. Generally, these mechanisms deal with the monitoring of the human rights situation on an individual basis, that is, on a single country or a specific human rights topic in each procedure.

    At the universal human rights system, non-conventional mechanisms cover all UN member states, while conventional mechanisms are limited to State parties that have ratified international human rights treaties and their protocols. In the regional human rights systems, the coverage of international human rights mechanisms has additional limitations, due to geographic restrictions that condition the participation of member states. In addition, in some regional systems, there are differences in the modality of states’ participation, depending on the adoption of their main normative instruments1.

    Before the UPR, there was no mechanism in the international architecture for the protection of human rights proposing the monitoring of human rights situation with universal and simultaneous reach over all UN member states, including in this process observer states, which also are entitled to make recommendations to the SuR in the UPR.

    Frequency

    When it comes to the frequency of monitoring human rights situation around the globe, traditional international mechanisms – although following some routines and pre-established agendas – do not always follow a specific schedule, which gives an element of randomness to these processes. Sessions and hearings can take place at more or less defined periods throughout the year and the working schedule varies significantly. Thus, the sequence of procedures established for a given State or at a human rights situation is susceptible to interruptions in the sequencing of procedural acts.

    In the UPR, the situation is quite different: the monitoring of the human rights situation takes place according to a predetermined agenda (list of SuR) and periodically (every five years) so that the procedural acts are repeated sequentially with each new cycle, as described above in section 2.

    Approach

    With regard to the approach given to the human rights situation within states, traditional international mechanisms have historically been structured on the basis of segmentation and specialization of human rights among themes and agendas. Conventional human rights mechanisms and their respective monitoring committees are typical examples of the topical approach given by traditional mechanisms to the human rights situation under monitoring. The existence of these specific mechanisms is important for the advancement of certain thematic human rights agendas, since in addition to giving prominence and specificity, and contributing to the consolidation of international human rights standards, this topical approach can meet the typical human rights demands of certain regions or vulnerable groups around the globe.

    However, the UPR reverses this logic, making it possible to holistically think, promote and protect human rights in the same mechanism. The range of rights monitored in the UPR is not restricted to the topics addressed by the treaties to which the SuR takes part. On the contrary, every human rights issue can be addressed, whether or not the state is bound to regional or universal instruments for the protection of human rights. In the Review, for example, there is room to work simultaneously on various human rights issues, from LGBTQIA+ agenda, gender, HIV-AIDS, public security, incarceration, education, indigenous peoples, among others. The importance of this holistic feature of the UPR is that monitoring takes an integrative look at competing themes, which contributes to the identification of violence intersections and more efficient solutions to multiple violence.

    Dynamics

    Regarding the dynamics observed in traditional mechanisms, these relationships are “triangulated” between international bodies, states and civil society organizations. In fact, civil society organizations play roles that are as fundamental as varied in these mechanisms, such as filing complaints, intervening in the judgment of cases (amicus curiae) and, in some cases, even mobilizing actions to finance international bodies (Haddad 2018). Also noteworthy is the participation of independent experts, especially in the work carried out by the Working Groups and within the Special Procedures of the UN Human Rights Council.

    On the other hand, at the UPR, interactions among states are reinforced (peer review), although the dynamics are formally in charge of international bodies (Human Rights Council and the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights). What also stands out are the formal limitations on the participation of civil society, such as: the restriction on sending reports (individual reports restricted to five pages and collective reports to ten pages); the SuR’s discretion regarding requests for consultations made by civil society organizations; and participation with observer status in the sabbatina (A/HRC/RES/5/1).

    Legal Basis

    As for legal bases, in the traditional mechanisms, decisions are affirmed through jus cogens, that is, on imperative norms, which materialize the highest human values solidified at the heart of the international community. Thus, the jurisprudence of regional human rights courts (in Africa, Europe or America) contributes to the identification of the most fundamental matters in international human rights law. For example, in the case of Villagrán Morales et al. Guatemala (2001) the Inter-American Court of Human Rights identified that forced disappearances, torture practices, summary and extrajudicial executions constitute violations of the jus cogens, therefore generating obligations to protect human beings destined to all those directly or indirectly responsible for the experienced human suffering. Decisions at the traditional mechanisms are mandatory so that compliance does not depend on the acquiescence or political will of the states.

    In the case of the recommendations made to the SuR in the UPR, these appointments are based on opinio juris communis, that is, on the expression of a universal legal conscience or of common “law ideas” about the protection of humanity that are externalized through customs established by the international community. The UPR is a custom of the international community, as it is a frequent and cyclical practice, with universal coverage over states, also involving the participation of several international actors. Therefore, by combining values from multiple visions of the international community on humanity, the UPR’s recommendations constitute a considerable normative production in terms of ideas often expressly invoked in this modality of international human rights monitoring.

    The UPR reports’ unique feature of providing, in a broad and voluntary way, the appointment of objective recommendations to the SuR promotes a spontaneous and regular flow of legal ideas in a dynamic that potentially diminishes the unilateral influence of more powerful states. In this environment, the process of international normative construction is guided by the emergence and identification of human needs that, above all, need to be expressed. The UPR’s recommendations would result from a common awareness of what is morally necessary to protect superior human values. In addition, its frequent updating reflects the complex evolution of the international community. Therefore, the repeated practice of expressing the universal legal conscience (opinio juris communis) facilitates the emergence of imperative norms (jus cogens) (Cançado Trindade 2010). From this perspective, UPR recommendations should also be considered mandatory, although compliance still depends on the acquiescence or political will of states.

    These five comparative parameters make the case that an international human rights mechanism cannot be considered “new”, “unprecedented” or “innovative” based solely on its nomenclature or contemporaneity. The designation of originality needs to be demonstrated through objective parameters regarding the distinctive elements of the new international mechanism. The five comparative parameters raised above elaborate, with greater precision and analytical density, the widely albeit fuzzily mentioned idea regarding the UPR’s originality in the international architecture of human rights.

    In this logic, the UPR is an unprecedented mechanism in the international protection of human rights because (i) it universally and simultaneously encompasses the entire international community; (ii) it occurs according to a predetermined and cyclical frequency; (iii) it promotes a holistic approach to the entire human rights corpus; (iv) it establishes predominantly international dynamics, with formal limitations to the participation of civil society; and (v) its recommendations are based on opinio juris communis arising from the practice of the UPR. It is precisely these unique characteristics of the UPR that define it as an instrument not only new, but, more specifically, unprecedented in the international human rights apparatus. We then proceed to analyze the presence and analytical weight of the attributes of contemporaneity and originality in the main research agendas on the subject.

    Research Agendas: assumptions and directions for examining the Universal Periodic Review

    The establishment of the UPR inaugurated new dynamics around the international architecture of human rights. In response, a growing body of literature gradually started to focus on the “new” object. This literature combines studies ranging from International Relations, International Law and Political Science. The innovations, particularities and possible impacts of the most recent mechanism justified the need, interest and relevance of the observations, research and evaluations ever produced. The development of knowledge on the UPR has contributed to boosting a growing and highly specialized literature which dialogues with the International Relation’s field.

    In this section, we present a systematization of this specific UPR literature through the presentation of the main research agendas on the subject. We carried out the survey of the state of the art by searching for keywords in digital repositories of scientific articles and books (Portal de Periódicos CAPES/MEC, Scielo and JSTOR). The result was the mapping of 43 scientific articles, published between 2006 and 2023. We classified these academic works into the following research agendas: (i) impact; (ii) comparison and best practices; and (iii) institutional policy, as organized in the table below.

    Thumbnail
    Table 3
    Systematization of Research Agendas on the UPR

    Impact Agenda

    The first research agenda analyzes the impacts produced by the UPR. In this agenda, there follows two research strands: compliance and effectiveness. In terms of compliance, studies on the impact of the UPR focus on the verification of compliance with the recommendations made to states. Studies within this strand aim to identify patterns of compliance with recommendations by country (Khoo 2014; Komanovics 2012) or by types of recommendations (Dominguez-Redondo 2012; Milewicz and Goodin 2018).

    Based on the assumptions of the normative paradigm of consent, researchers in the compliance strand tend to be guided by the restrictive idea that the key actors responsible for compliance with UPR recommendations are the states only. Following these assumptions, the impact of this international human rights mechanism is evaluated in terms of objective compliance with the recommendations recorded in the final report of the UPR. As a logical result, studies on compliance tend to conclude that the UPR has low impact, given the lack of capacity and/or political will on the part of states to comply with the recommendations. In this line, Davies (2010) argues that the political dynamics between states negatively impacts the result of the Review, making it possible for the SuR to avoid commitment and compliance, for example, through friendly backers. Komanovics (2012) points that the absence of enforcement mechanisms suggests shaming as the single impact of UPR. Similarly, Khoo (2014) points out the UPR’s deficiencies at encouraging domestic reforms in human rights. These works generically introduce the idea that the UPR specificities should be observed. However, these claims worked more as cautioning than as thick description of the UPR attributes.

    On the other hand, the adjustment of the UPR assumptions to the normative paradigm of consensus allows researchers to contemplate other actors to examine the impact of UPR recommendations, such as international bodies, CSOs, domestic institutions and the media. Following these assumptions, its impact is analyzed in broader terms beyond state compliance. Within this strand, it is possible to reach the UPR’s capacity to facilitate the formation and maintenance of pro-human rights coalitions, to promote awareness of human rights through the media, to instigate debates and domestic mobilization for the use of international mechanisms for the protection of human rights, among other possibilities.

    Studies guided by such assumptions delve into the aspect of effectiveness, focusing on examining the broader effects of the mechanism not captured by models of objective compliance with a particular recommendation. As a logical result, studies in the effectiveness strand tend to conclude on the diffuse impact of the UPR, given the numerous factors capable of establishing (more or less) favorable conditions for meeting international obligations. In this line, Alston (2006) highlights the guarantee of space for the participation of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights and experts as a condition of effectiveness for the UPR.

    Regarding the UPR’s originality factors for assessing impact, Domínguez-Redondo (2012) gives analytical weight to the legal basis of the UPR recommendations. According to the author, the commitments assumed in the UPR, based on non-conflictive dynamics, enhance the formation, definition, clarification and consolidation of customary human rights rules, which may result in new ways of building binding obligations. Milewicz and Goodin (2018) add that the deliberative process of the UPR also evokes cooperative responses from states, even those with poor human rights performance. These works are interesting for two reasons. First, because they challenge the notion that non-conflicting international mechanisms parallel low impact on international politics. Second, because they allow us to visualize potential ways to explore, in the practical field, how recommendatory decisions resulting from the UPR can constitute, through legal argumentation, new sources of legal obligations (opinio juris communis) for states, moving away from the theses of consent, capacity or political will to change state behavior on human rights.

    Likewise, McNeilly (2021) gives analytical weight to the frequency of the UPR recommendations. Factoring time as an external constraint active in the Review, the author argues that the UPR constitutes a rich and multifaceted temporal artifact by combining apparently incompatible notions of time, such as cyclicity, linearity, discontinuity and duration. In this sense, cyclicality, seen as the main frequency characteristic in the UPR, is just a starting point for deeper investigations into the notions of time that guide the Review process. This work contributes to offering new avenues of analysis for researchers in the area and, as for practitioners, it expands the understanding of the UPR functionality and process as a whole.

    Comparison and Best Practices Agenda

    The second agenda explores contrasts between the UPR and other international mechanisms for monitoring human rights. It explores points of duplicity or practical convergence and potential for disseminating lessons learned among international human rights mechanisms. Also, in this agenda, there are studies that start from thematic, state or regional cutouts to evaluate specific behaviors and identify good practices based on them. Three strands follow in the comparison and best practices agenda: the direct comparison of the UPR with international mechanisms, the thematic analysis in the UPR and the comparison by countries or regions.

    Within the strand of direct comparison between the UPR and other international mechanisms, Alencar (2010) highlights the potential for cooperation between the UPR and the Inter-American human rights system. Similarly, Gaer (2007) considers complementarity and duplicity between the UPR and the UN Treaty Bodies. Carraro (2017) compares the effects of politicization at the Review and in the UN Treaty Bodies, concluding that although the UPR is perceived as more politicized, the mechanism is also seen as potentially more able to generate compliance with the recommendations than the UN Treaty Bodies. Carraro and Jongen (2018) comparatively assess how transparency in UPR processes and the Implementation Review Mechanism (IRM) of the United Nations Convention against Corruption (UNCAC) has the capacity to generate peer and public pressure to implement the recommendations of international organizations, concluding that the transparency of both international mechanisms positively affects perceptions of peer and public pressure, although it appears to undermine mutual learning and does not guarantee equal treatment between the SuR in the case of the UPR (Carraro and Jongen 2018). Carraro et al. (2019) compare the UPR to the UN IRM, the Working Group on Bribery (WGB), the OECD Economic and Development Review Committee (EDRC) and the Trade Policy Review Mechanism (TPRM) of the WTO, concluding that the WGB and the UPR are perceived as the most capable of organizing pressure against SuR. Finally, Shah and Sivakumaran (2021) analyze 57,685 recommendations from the first two Review cycles to compare citations of international treaties. Results indicate that states employ sources other than the UPR’s (listed in Resolution 5/1) in the elaboration of recommendations, largely rejecting the distinction between soft and hard law.

    Within the strand of thematic analysis, a variety of themes are explored, such as those describing debates and recommendations in the UPR regarding laws criminalizing sexual orientation (Cowell and Milon 2012), right to health (Mesquita 2019), universal and relativists approaches to address polygamy (Patel 2017a) and women’s right to access health services (Patel 2017b). Still in this vein, there are studies that analyze the treatment of a topic by different cycles of the UPR, such as Hernandez and Rosa (2019) regarding the performance of the United States on the topic of migration, Matias (2014) evaluates the changes in Brazil in the theme of the prison system after the first two cycles of the UPR. Sousa et al. (2015) compare the issue of organized crime in the UPR, focusing on Brazil and Indonesia. In the scope of regionalized studies, Rhona Smith (2012) and Damien Etone (2018) mapped emerging themes in the UPR related to the Pacific Island and African states, respectively.

    Research like those, defining a range of states or social actors in specific themes in the UPR, can significantly bring inputs into the functioning of the mechanism or onto the improvement of actors’ performance. This is so because the focus on a particular theme allows detailed description of critical points in the UPR procedures and routines, like the appointment of experts to participate in the processes, the identification of support or resistance networks to the recommendations, among others. The gathering of such information helps with the formulation of solutions for deficiencies, distortions and failures at the UPR.

    Within the strand of comparison by country or regions, the studies aim to compare the performance of countries or regions at different UPR cycles. Chow (2017) compares the first two North Korean reviews and proposes an assessment of general aspects of the UPR that inadvertently contribute to masking atrocities committed by the Kim Jong Un government. Abashidze and Adu (2013) consider the second review on Russia, Cochrane and McNeilly (2013) the first review on the United Kingdom, Hernandez and Rosa (2018) compare the first two reviews on the United States. In the case of Brazil, Annoni and Santos (2015) examine the revisions that the country went through in the first UPR cycle. Similarly, Pereira (2020) considers Brazil’s role as a SuR in the first three UPR cycles and as reviewer in the first two, in order to relate the State’s performance in the UPR with the country’s foreign policy. In the scope of regionalized studies, Pereira and Espósito Neto (2019) analyze the performance of European Union states in the UPR on the issue of migration. Smith (2011) considers the level of economic development of Asia-Pacific countries to identify differences in the treatment of more or less developed states in the UPR, while Abebe (2009) examines the participation of African states in the first two sessions of the Review, aiming to identify actions that support the UPR to fulfill its mandate.

    These last two strands, notably, give greater analytical weight to the attribute of the cyclical frequency of the UPR. As the monitoring of the human rights situation in this international mechanism takes place through a predetermined schedule and periodicity, the acts are repeated sequentially in each new cycle, so that recommendations not fulfilled in a previous cycle are again the object of monitoring in a new one. This repetition is important, as it generates a cumulative effect on peer pressure (and on the part of civil society) for certain changes in state behavior. The extension in time of unfulfilled recommendations produces a recidivism effect, capable of forming an image of recalcitrance in the SuR with regard to systematic practice in certain types of human rights violations. This factor alone drives shaming among peers (and on the part of civil society), contributing to the stigmatization of the country as a violator of human rights.

    This research agenda regards importance in the face of the hyper-specialization of academic and policy/advocacy communities, allowing issues of mutual concern among international human rights mechanisms to benefit from cross dialogues and joint responses. In addition, studies with a comparative focus and good practices in the UPR contribute to understanding the singularities of a state or region, for example, the greater or lesser propensity to accept recommendations, to assume commitments and to comply with them; the availability to provide information on advances, setbacks and politically sensitive agendas in the domestic sphere; and the opening of channels for dialogue and participation of civil society. However, while some of these studies contribute with detailed explanations of state behavior, the conclusions obtained from such research designs do not always allow for generalization to other states or to the general human rights situation around the world.

    Institutional Policy Agenda

    The third agenda examines the dynamics around the UPR and the strategies to increase social participation in this mechanism. In this agenda – as in the impact research agenda – depending on the assumptions taken, there follows two strands: international and transnational dynamics.

    Within the international dynamics strand, the assumptions are: (i) prioritization of peer states interaction and (ii) formal limitation on the participation of civil society, pursuant to Resolution 5/1. Following these assumptions, Terman and Voeten (2018) analyze inter-state policy in the UPR by the creation of four types of relationship between Reviewer State and Review State (geopolitical affinity, formal military alliance, arms trade and humanitarian aid). The authors demonstrate that preexisting relationships between Review-State and SuR influences the shaming exercise in the UPR. In this line, pre-existing political relationship between states determines how shaming will be practiced and received in the Review. The relevance of this work lies in the demonstration that state dynamics have a direct implication on the effectiveness of the UPR (Terman and Voeten 2018).

    In another study on the international dynamics in the UPR, Burger et al. (2021) consider the geographic distance between states (measured between capitals) as a determining factor of behavior in the Review. When considering the practices of states as reviewers and reviewed, the authors conclude that the closer the states, the greater the number of recommendations issued and the less likely acceptation of them. In the case of states that share borders, the logic is reversed, with a smaller number of recommendations issued and a greater number of recommendations accepted. Both these studies contribute to the literature by testing hypotheses that generalize the understanding about states behavior in the UPR. The results of such research are also relevant to guide states’ and other actors’ practices in the UPR, as they bring some predictability on the willingness of states to develop or accept these recommendations.

    Within the transnational dynamics strand, the assumptions of the primacy of peer relationships and the limitation of social participation are challenged. Moss (2010) identifies possible ways for social actors to participate in the UPR, such as: mobilizing and participating in national preparatory consultations for the UPR; organizing visits and providing information on special procedures; advocating for the ratification of human rights treaties and sending information to conventional bodies; exerting pressure for the acceptance and implementation of recommendations made in the UPR; and monitoring implementation of the recommendations. Therefore, this literature describes forms of direct action (providing information, participation and advocacy strategies with other organizations and states); and indirect actions (sending reports to other interested international monitoring bodies) throughout the UPR process.

    Lilliebjerg (2008) identifies that the main limitations for social participation in the UPR lies in the fact that this international mechanism holds a high level of state control, with the participation of domestic NGOs and independent human rights experts restricted by force of normative provisions. Despite this, Landolt and Woo (2017) recall that, in practice, the UPR allows domestic social organizations to participate in the submission of reports, when associated with other social entities. This possibility of including new domestic actors has prompted further studies on the participation of civil society in some states in the UPR, as Tainsh (2019) did with regard to the participation of civil society actors of Canadian origin, McGaughey (2017) in the case of Australian-born civil society actors and Baird (2015) and Gomez and Ramcharan (2018) concerning civil society actors in Southeast Asia.

    Investigations into the transnational dynamics in the UPR are relevant because the experience of civil society in international mechanisms is representative of the functioning of an international society organized around the values of human dignity. Analysis focused on social participation in the UPR allows us not only to identify which states, regions or groups of states admit, to a greater or lesser extent, the participation of social actors in international human rights monitoring processes. More than that, studies focused on the participation of civil society allow us to understand how these social actors outline different strategies to mobilize presence and voice in intergovernmental environments, even when states/regions/groups of states or the international mechanisms themselves create formal obstacles to social participation.

    The participation of civil society organizations in international human rights mechanisms is fundamental because these actors play a critical role in registering, framing and sending alternative information to official sources of information from states/governments. In this sense, Chaney (2020) offers an important contribution to the literature on civil society in the UPR insofar as he considers the contrast between the information contained in civil society and state reports. The research points to a disconnection between the information contained in these two sources of the UPR and suggests that, in the case of India (object of his study), the state approach seems to encompass civil society participation and the promotion of rights. However, as long as the state promotes its political legitimacy in the UPR, it is at odds with its domestic practices.

    In the case of the UPR, the importance of understanding the strategies of social actors to guarantee participation in the UPR is even more justified as a way of diversifying perceptions and official voices regarding human rights abuses and violations. In this mechanism, a new category of actors becomes relevant: the international organizations themselves, responsible for the selection of information submitted by NGOs, as well as for the routine work of international human rights mechanisms. Nie (2023) considered around 7,000 civil society reports submitted to the UPR between 2008 and 2016 to conclude that international organizations tend to accept more information depending on the reputation of the reporting organizations, the language used in the reports and the support of other organizations or coalitions.

    Thus, the predominance of NGOs (domestic or international) in a state or group of SuR can generate inferences on the predominance of external voices and the silencing of domestic voices in the UPR (Baird 2015). Situations like this can be a source for assessing the systematic opening or closing of dialogue channels in a given state/government or in the institutional procedures of human rights mechanisms. The identification of domestic scenarios that are more or less likely to receive UPR recommendations suggest appropriate ways for the UPR to achieve its objective of contributing to the improvement of the human rights situation.

    Despite the importance of these studies, this is one of the least developed strands in the specific literature on UPR. One of the factors that explain the transnational dynamics’ gap in the literature is the false assumption that civil society organizations strictly act within the formal limits imposed by international human rights bodies. In practice, however, transnational dynamics in the UPR drive intense networking among social actors on shared strategies and innovative arrangements. This finding strengthens the impact of civil society organizations of different capacities and international expression. Hence, exploring this research gap seems one of the most promising venues for future research on the UPR.

    Conclusion

    Functioning for over fifteen years, the UPR is usually identified by the characteristics of contemporaneity and originality in the international protection of human rights. These attributes have not merited specific consideration in the literature beyond the trivial characterization of the mechanism. However, an international mechanism cannot be qualified as “current”, “unprecedented” or “innovative” based solely on its temporality or nomenclature.

    In this article, we provided a precise and analytically dense elaboration on these attributes. Since these elements constitute underlying assumptions with direct repercussions on the production of analytical and practical knowledge in the area, we gave perspective to the contemporaneity attribute and systematized the originality of the UPR as a way of demonstrating the unique characteristics of the UPR. By establishing critical points of difference between the UPR and other mechanisms, this article contributes to making an adjustment between research assumptions and possible logical conclusions derived from these choices. We applied these conceptual understandings to the main research agendas in the UPR to verify the analytical weight given to its unique characteristics in this literature.

    The accumulation of research on the subject and the continued accumulation of data over UPR cycles have promoted contradictory effects. If, on the one hand, the availability of online access to primary sources offers incentives to the research on the UPR; on the other, the accumulated knowledge has lifted the bar for new researchers. Contrasting and comparing the UPR with other international human rights mechanisms can offer a venue for renewed contributions to both academic and practical fields. The novel assortment of the main research agendas on the UPR, as proposed here, makes it more feasible for newcomers to identify gaps and research potentials, in addition to indicating more promising paths, including the exploration of less apparent global dynamics around the Review.
