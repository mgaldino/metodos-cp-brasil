Addressing ‘Maritime Aphasia’ in International Relations

Abordando a “afasia marítima” em Relações Internacionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article introduces the notion of ‘maritime aphasia’ in International Relations (IR) to foreground the ocean ontologically and conceptually in the discipline. Maritime aphasia relates to IR’s theoretical limitation whereby the political significance of the sea is acknowledged but filtered through non-oceanic concepts, thereby lacking its positivity as a constitutive element of the international. To investigate why and how maritime aphasia developed, we build on the philosophy of Deleuze and Guattari. We propose an ontology of the maritime domain beyond its material constitution and in terms of desiring-production. We theorise on the ocean as a desiring-machine and claim that maritime aphasia has an emotional dimension that relates to the repression/perversion of the unbearably abundant flows of desire enabled by oceanic machines. This generates an affective attachment to transcendent signs that totalise and elusively deprive the sea of its ontology through mechanisms of subjectification. We illustrate this by tracing the origins of IR back to repressions of desiring-production in the early views of the international. These we investigate by critically engaging with Carl Schmitt’s concept of nomos, which, as we suggest, indicated the emotionally charged Eurocentric discourses of the international from which IR as a discipline emerged.

    Key words
    maritime aphasia; oceanic machines; maritime domain; Critical Security Studies; Critical Maritime Studies; Deleuze and Guattari

    Resumo

    Este artigo introduz a noção de “afasia marítima” nas Relações Internacionais (RI) para colocar o oceano em primeiro plano ontológica e conceitualmente na disciplina. A afasia marítima está relacionada à limitação teórica das RI em que o significado político do mar é reconhecido, mas filtrado por meio de conceitos não oceânicos, faltando assim sua positividade como elemento constitutivo do internacional. Para investigar por que e como a afasia marítima se desenvolveu, nos baseamos na filosofia de Deleuze e Guattari. Propomos uma ontologia do domínio marítimo além de sua constituição material e em termos de desejo-produção. Teorizamos sobre o oceano como uma máquina desejante e afirmamos que a afasia marítima tem uma dimensão emocional relacionada à repressão/perversão dos fluxos de desejo insuportavelmente abundantes possibilitados pelas máquinas oceânicas. Isso gera um apego afetivo a sinais transcendentes que totalizam e privam o mar de sua ontologia por meio de mecanismos de subjetivação. Ilustramos isso rastreando as origens das RI até as repressões do desejo-produção nas primeiras visões do internacional. Investigamos essas repressões por meio de um envolvimento crítico com o conceito de nomos de Carl Schmitt, que, como sugerimos, indicou os discursos eurocêntricos emocionalmente carregados do internacional a partir dos quais as RI como disciplina surgiram.

    Palavras-chave
    Afasia marítima; máquinas oceânicas; domínio marítimo; estudos críticos de segurança; estudos marítimos críticos; Deleuze e Guattari

    Introduction

    This article introduces the notion of ‘maritime aphasia’ in International Relations (IR) to foreground the ocean both ontologically and conceptually in the discipline. In The Sea and International Relations (2022), Carvalho and Leira accurately captured the overall features of such a problem, suggesting that ‘IR has been a pathological “landlubber discipline”: either refusing to deal with the sea or treating it as land’ (Carvalho and Leira 2022: 1). They echo Hester Blum’s prescient insight that the very notion of planetary time is an abstraction projected onto the sea in a ‘land-based perspective that emerges from an understanding of the planet as subdivided into political categories’ (Blum 2015: 25). Maritime aphasia comes from such a notion and relates to IR’s theoretical limitation whereby the political significance of the sea is acknowledged but filtered through non-oceanic concepts, thereby lacking its positivity as a constitutive element of the international.

    Thanks to the valuable efforts put forward by Carvalho and Leira and the burgeoning literature that has been focusing on the ocean in IR (Carvalho and Mattos 2022; Valença and Duarte 2022; Levaggi 2020), we do not need to duplicate the exercise of justifying the obvious importance of the sea in international politics. From pricing whales’ poo to control the production of phytoplankton and assess climate change (Chapman 2023) to the promising UN Decade of the Ocean Science inaugurated in 2020 and the anxiety over a worldwide conflict escalation between NATO and Russia over the latter’s alleged sabotage ofn undersea pipelines in the Baltic, the international is increasingly oceanic.

    However, maritime aphasia still poses a more fundamental challenge for IR. Unlike other disciplines – like History, which has recently reinstated the idea of a maritime world history (Armitage, Bashford and Sivasundaram 2018; Bashford 2017; Paine 2014) or indeed Geography, through the pioneering works of Philip Steinberg and Kimberly Peters (2019; 2015) – in IR, foregrounding the sea has not necessarily produced a way to understand the international from an oceanic perspective. Historically, ocean-related works in IR are mostly related to the subfield of strategic studies, whereby the sea is essentially an instrument of foreign policy. As Till remarks, the maritime domain is nothing more than a medium of resources, transportation, exchange of ideas and dominion (Till 2018: 06). The very idea of sea power that governs this approach is born out of a Mahanian adaptation of how armies would conduct warfare ashore (Carvalho and Mattos 2022). Lambert’s astute reinvention of the term sea power does not go too far in this aspect, as it places the ocean only as a secondary element, having to be represented by nation-states to be of relevance (Lambert 2018).

    Indeed, important contributions have moved away from this realist approach. Mälksoo, for instance, explores the symbolic role of the Baltic Sea for Estonia’s myths and NATO’s maritime deterrence posture (Mälksoo 2022). Other critical approaches emphasise the inherent connectivity of the sea, like Shilliam (2015), who argues that the ocean can serve as space for connecting resistance across multiple localities around the globe. However, as insightful as these approaches can be, they still indicate that IR has given a ‘voice’ to the sea, but this voice still cannot be affirmatively expressed in its oceanic nature. Put another way, the sea has been filtered through non-maritime concepts to fit into the discipline of IR, such is the current stage of maritime aphasia. Hence, rather than foregrounding the ocean, IR still needs to ‘oceanise’ its understanding. We are rather interested in the possibility of a radical oceanic view, whereby its productive and affective role would shape the notion of the international.

    The first step for this is to understand why and how maritime aphasia took place, the primary purpose of this piece. For this, we build on Deleuze and Guattari’s philosophy (1987; 1983) to propose an ontology of the maritime domain beyond its material constitution and in terms of desiring-production. We theorise on the ocean as a desiring-machine and claim that maritime aphasia has an emotional dimension related to the repression/perversion of the unbearably abundant flows of desire enabled by oceanic machines. This generates an affective attachment to transcendent signs that totalise and elusively deprive the sea from its ontology through mechanisms of subjectification. We illustrate this by tracing the origins of IR back to repressions of desiring-production in the early views of the international. These we investigate by critically engaging with Carl Schmitt’s concept of nomos, which, we suggest, indicated the emotionally charged Eurocentric discourses of the international from which IR as a discipline emerged.

    This piece contributes to the burgeoning literature on the ocean in IR while indirectly adding value to incorporating Deleuze and Guattari’s philosophy into the discipline (Lenco 2014; Reid 2010; 2003). As far as we are aware, there has been no use of the concept of desire to indicate the affective role of non-human elements, such as the ocean, in world politics. In this aspect, this piece presents a significant contribution.

    We unpack our argument in three sections. First, we refer to the historical development of IR to investigate how the maritime domain was incorporated into the discipline. We non-exhaustively review the literature mapping the traditional ways IR is developed to qualify what we mean by maritime aphasia. In the second section, we present our ontology of desire and desiring-machines to create the conditions for the ocean to come ‘first’ when analysing the international. In the third section, we illustrate this feature by critically assessing Schmitt’s work on the nomos of the earth.

    Maritime aphasia in International Relations: a century of maritime appropriation

    We begin by qualifying the meaning of maritime aphasia and how it has been expressed in IR. We broadly divide the discipline into three interdisciplinary epistemological phases, following Ashworth (2009): a transdisciplinary first age (1919-1940s), followed by a realist age (1950s-1989) and a multidisciplinary age (1989 onwards). This schematic organisation, though only partially exhaustive, of course, provides an outline of how IR has evolved. To be sure, we are not suggesting that Ashworth’s perspective is the only correct one, but his division mirrors the main course of IR’s development well.1

    IR’s transdisciplinary phase and the ‘sovereigntisation’ of the maritime: affirming sovereignty at sea

    IR’s first age runs across the late 19th century. It is called ‘transdisciplinary’ because there was no attempt to build a unified theoretical framework but to adapt distinct methods and disciplines to forge a common language to orient policymaking and practical problems (Ashworth 2009: 18). It reflects the British political position as a great power. It was formally born with the first Department of International Politics at the University of Aberystwyth in 1919. In this age, IR drew on several disciplines, utilising different methods and approaches to addressing common empirical problems such as war, peaceful political change, international government, etc.

    The political emergence of the maritime domain in the late 19th century followed a similar trajectory of IR’s discipline. Britain was also mobilised as the great paradigm of what came to be called naval/maritime strategy, and the idea of commanding/controlling the sea started to legitimise states’ policies (Richmond 1947; Mackinder 1919; Mahan 1987 [1890]; Corbett 1911 [2004]). Mahan’s notion of ‘Sea Power’ widely spread. The much-commented elements of Sea Power – namely, geographical position, physical conformation, the extent of the territory, number of populations, national character, and character of the government (1987, chap I) – influenced nations as diverse as Imperial Germany, Italy, Russia, and Japan (Gat 1992: 186). Just like with the empirical purpose of IR, the ocean becomes an instrument to solve the problem of war and was also assessed through various disciplinary approaches. For instance: Sir Julian Corbett was a barrister, Sir John Knox Laughton a mathematician, and Alfred Mahan a US Navy officer.

    The worldwide influence of Mahan’s series was visibly present in the first age of IR. For instance, Alfred Zimmern, when discussing the political role of the League of Nations, spares one chapter to discuss sea power. He criticises Mahan but echoes his core assumptions. Referring to a ‘benevolent’ Britannia who ruled the waves, he places the sea as a ‘national instrument’ concerned with the carrying out of national policies which inevitably fell to discharge international duties (Zimmern 1936: 89). Hence, the transdisciplinary age of IR was met with a ‘sovereigntisation’ of the ocean, the latter perceived as a space where states affirmed their sovereignty to protect themselves from other state-related threats when pursuing their foreign policy.

    IR’s realist age and the ‘nuclearisation’ of the maritime: projecting power from the sea

    After the Second World War, the USA emerged as a great power, marking the beginning of IR’s second age. ‘IR’s capture by Political Sciences’ closed off links with other disciplines (Ashworth 2009: 23), and a realist theoretical approach shaped the field. IR was dedicated to mediating/reproducing the Cold War’s ideological battle in a theoretical analysis based on the ‘language of power’ (Ashworth 2009: 18). This meant a strong emphasis on the national interest; the use of power to preserve the so-called bipolar international order and the imperative of problem-solving approaches to strategic dilemmas faced by the US government. History was often mobilised to construct and justify abstract systemic models simplistically (Ashworth 2009: 19).

    The maritime domain reappears now fully enmeshed with this realist approach in the subfield of strategic studies. The ocean was channelled into the realist notion of national interest, becoming a paramount space for states to exercise their sovereignty and project power. This mainly relates to the technological developments of nuclear weapons operating for the national interest. The ‘second-strike strategy’ (Jervis 1989; Schelling 1967) was essentially enabled by nuclear-powered submarines carrying and launching ballistic missiles armed with nuclear weapons (SSBN). SSBN’s main objectives became almost exclusively tailored to hit targets ashore, neither directly affecting the war at sea nor attempting to secure the sea lines of communication (SLOC). SSBN constantly avoided contact with all other vessels precisely because of its strategic advantage (Till 1984: 217).

    The evolution of technology allowed the ocean to function as a platform from where states would guarantee their national interest and keep the balance of power against other states, and just like with IR, reproducing the East-West Cold War ideological dispute. By this time, Mahan’s concept of sea power was internationally consolidated, shaping how policymakers and theorists viewed the maritime. Admiral Gorshkov, writing in the 1970s and defending a Soviet view on the ocean, discussed the idea of the Sea Power of the State, highlighting that ‘sea power emerges as one of the important factors for strengthening its [the Soviet Union’s] economy, accelerating scientific and technical developments’ (Gorshkov 1979: 2). The use of SSBN was a problem of naval art, whereby striking using missiles ‘from a submarine on land targets, can immediately bring strategic results’, this being ‘a new quality in a category as customary as the strike’ (Gorshkov 1979: 224). When it comes to IR theorists, Hedley Bull, for example, defended Mahan’s notion of sea power, defining it as a ‘military power that is brought to bear at sea: on the surface of the sea, underneath it or in the air above it’ (Bull 1980: 3). Bull explicitly agreed with Mahan’s thesis that the control or the mastery of the sea should be the way states use sea power and that such was an ‘accurate description of the position the United States won for herself in the first two decades after World War II’ (1980: 6). When discussing the role of SSBN, Bull agreed that nuclear threat had been what maintained the balance between the two super-powers of his time (1980: 8).

    As Ashworth suggests, the ‘language of power’ is verified in various concepts expressing the Cold War’s ideological dispute. Deterrence was explored through the diplomatic use of navies (Booth 1977), the idea of gunboat diplomacy was widespread (Cable 1994), and other correlated themes such as navies’ constabulary roles became consolidated (Gray 1992; Grove 1990). Cutting across these examples is an understanding of the maritime now fully contained by states’ sovereignty, accentuating the previous phase and formally mobilising the ocean to harness the national interest through strategic defence papers.

    IR’s multidisciplinary phase and the securitisation of the maritime: stabilising the international system through the sea

    The third phase is what Ashworth calls the ‘reopening’ of International Relations and represents a ‘multidisciplinary’ age. This phase enabled the creation of an epistemic framework to accommodate critical views, and alternative agendas were innovatively put forward to understand the international system. Ashworth calls it multidisciplinary because it largely neglects previous liberal (first age) and realist (second age) contributions and draws from a variety of theoretical inspirations, such as Marxism, feminist, and post-colonial theories (Ashworth 2009: 20). Emerging at the end of the 1980s, this phase reflected changes in the international system, consequently widening IR’s former Anglo-Saxon predominance towards what came to be called the meta category of the ‘Global South’ (Haug, Brave-Boy and Maigold 2021). However, as Ashworth maintains, even in this phase, the discipline of IR is still clearly located within political science (Ashworth 2009: 20).

    IR’s multidisciplinary age paved the way for debates on maritime security, a fast-growing subfield which has innovatively suggested some level of understanding of the ocean’s ontology. Overall, maritime security interrogates the meaning of security when applied to the ocean and the corresponding policies/practices to allow for an internationally stable, economically fluid, and environmentally sustainable maritime domain (Boşilcă, Ferreira and Ryan 2022; Bueger, Edmunds and Edwards 2021; Bueger, Edmunds and Ryan 2019; Bueger and Edmunds 2017; Bueger 2015; Germond 2019; 2016; 2015). For this, it innovatively incorporated new voices from the Global South (Siebels 2020; Duarte and Kenkel 2019; Kamal-Deen 2015) and alternative post-structuralist and critical views (Ryan 2019; 2015; Shilliam 2015; Lobo-Guerrero 2012; 2008).     For an inattentive observer, the oceans were fundamental to International Relations. Like Forrest Gump, oceans are present in many of the field’s relevant events, overtly infused in theories, strategies, and policies. However, this ‘natural attitude’ has restricted a more in-depth examination of its constitution beyond a space to be appropriated or used by agents. Cutting across different theoretical and ontological approaches, the oceans are not a significant part of IR traditions they are ‘just there.’ Why has this been the case?

    Outlining a viable ontology for the ocean: beyond the subject, towards desire and desiring-machines

    To understand why maritime aphasia occurred, we propose an ontology allowing the ocean to express itself. In this section, we turn to Deleuze and Guattari’s ontology of desire (Deleuze and Guattari 1987, 1983), demonstrating how the ocean is productive rather than produced by subjects.

    There are few uses of Deleuze and Guattari in IR that build on the concept of desire2. Most of Deleuzian and Deleuze-Guattarian views focus respectively on the recent affective turn in IR (see Clément and Sangar 2018; Ariffin, Coicaud and Popovski 2016; Hutchison 2016; Greg and Seigworth 2010; Ahmed 2004) and in the concept of assemblage (Acuto and Curtis 2014; De Landa 2006). An important exception is Gammon (2010), who interrogates the problem of sovereignty in IR, questioning its capitalist foundations from a schizoanalytical approach as developed by Deleuze and Guattari in Anti Oedipus. Gammon’s critique is powerful, but still grounds desire on a human-related perspective. He depicts Deleuze and Guattari’s critique of capitalism as introducing a ‘fictive configuration of human desire’ (Gammon 2010: 360, our emphasis), placing their understanding of subjectivity along with that of Lacan’s. This Lacanian, human-related perspective is also the line of the approach undertaken by Zevnik (2016). Although there are many overlapping dimensions between Lacan’s psychoanalysis and Deleuze and Guattari’s philosophy, we consider Lacan’s approach limited as it still fails to incorporate non-human elements into the psychic apparatus of subjects3.

    The concept of assemblage captures this last feature, but, in our view, by the cost of overlooking the affective dynamics that give rise to assemblages in the first place and, with it, desire. Ian Buchanan depicts this trend accurately when observing both the Latourian Actor-Network Theory and the New Materialism that arose from the work of De Landa and others, when putting forward the concept of assemblage, ignore the role of desire in the way that Deleuze and Guattari developed. As he states, ‘[a]ll references to and considerations of desire are consciously excluded from the discussion as either unnecessary or simply too messy. Assemblages are thereby reduced to mere apparatuses, which is precisely not what Deleuze and Guattari intended’ (Buchanan 2020: 6).

    Unlike Buchanan, however, we do not turn back to the notion of assemblage to develop our ontology of desire because we believe that the translation of the original term into English from ‘agencement’ obfuscates its uses as a concept – and concepts are crucial for Deleuze and Guattari because they define what philosophy is (1994), as singularities that impact the flows of ordinary thinking (Deleuze 2006: 176). Assemblage in English comes precisely from the homonymous word in French (that Deleuze and Guattari do not use as a concept), ‘a collection of individuals’ or the gathering of similar parts, whereby the singularity between the act of gathering and that which is gathered is undermined. This is not the space to delve into the philosophical implications of such a move – for this, see Phillips (2006) – but in our view the system of knowledge built upon this (arguably new) concept insufficiently captures the affective in-betweenness of humans and non-humans suggested by Deleuze and Guattari’s concept of desire4. Such transindividual character of desire is crucial for us as we are interested in creating the concepts that allow for the articulation of the ‘voice’ of the ocean in IR.

    Desire goes way beyond a link assembling the parts of a whole or a human ‘need’ that goes from one subject to another (object). Simply put, it is the pure immanent cause of the auto-production of life within which humans, nature and history are entwined (Sibertin-Blanc 2010: 17). To be purely immanent means that desire does not need anything outside itself to be; it produces the attributes that constitute reality while multiplying its own existence (Deleuze 1990: 164), going back to itself to be several, avoiding, however being an ‘other’ (Deleuze 1997: 43). It all happens in a process of repetition of radically conceived differences. If only difference comes back to be repeated, then there is no case of original identities identical to themselves to start the process or subjects that are masters of their will and free to make their choices (Deleuze 1983: 48-52).

    This does not deny the objective existence of individual subjects but replaces their ontological position in the fabric of reality. Indeed, as Deleuze famously put, ‘all identities are only simulated, produced as an optical “effect” by the more profound game of difference and repetition’ (Deleuze 1994: xix). Desire must be understood as a sheer nomadic force, a principle of individuation that produces this optical effect through difference and repetition. It conveys a process of constant desubjectivation, where the individual is always blending with the environment, that is, being individuated and dissolved in a limitrophe zone of proximity with other beings, co-functioning among them by contagion (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 267). In brief, desire is a multiplicity without a unity (n-1), molecular and rhizomatic par excellence, continuously producing individuations (Deleuze 1994: 38; Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 19).

    This is important for us to understand the ocean. From this ontological position, there is no case of the ocean being ‘appropriated’ in principle: reality is composed of beings that are ‘naturally’ and immanently enmeshed. Moreover, if there is no transcendence in principle – that is, no domination of one ‘Being’ over another – then there is no ontological distinction between humans and nature. They are expressions of one and the same essential reality, ‘just as white includes various intensities, while remaining essentially the same white’ (Deleuze 1994: 36). This is what Deleuze calls a ‘univocity of being’: ‘Climate, wind, season, hour is not of another nature than the things, animals or people that populate then, follow them, sleep, and awaken within them. This should be read without a pause’ (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 290). The lack of pause that Deleuze and Guattari mention highlights the productive and incessant character of desire or desiring-production. In this chaotic, or, as Deleuze and Guattari put, schizophrenic rhythm, the subject, or the self, is only a momentary pause, a ‘concentration, accumulation, coincidence of a certain number of converging pre-individual singularities’ (Deleuze 1993: 63). To be clear, this horizontal ontology means that humans and non-humans are distinct in practice but composed of a singular substance in principle: desire and desiring-production.

    Desiring-production is invested in what Deleuze and Guattari call machines. ‘Desire only exists when is machined’, when operated through machines (Deleuze 1986: 96). Machines are the complex set of configurations coupling the heterogenous elements produced by desire and its individuating zone of proximity. In doing so, machines convert desire into other forms of existence – such as emotions if human subjects stratify desire. The concept of machines was inspired by Lewis Mumford’s work (1934; 1967) but originally stems from Guattari’s early work with psychoanalysis (Guattari 2011; Genosko 2015; Sauvagnargues 2012). It expresses the non-subjective role of the unconscious, which operates ‘out there’ like a factory, producing subjectivity without the mediation of dreams, myths, and the theatrical figure of the analyst (Deleuze and Guattari 1983: 14-15). Machines are always desiring as ‘desire is a machine, and the object of desire is another machine connected to it’ (Deleuze and Guattari 1983: 26), thus the notion of desiring-machines – again, the coupling of desiring-production ‘without a pause.’

    What is distinctive about desiring-machines is that they enable an understanding of reality without a subject, without a structure, but as a relational correlation of forces and intensities produced by desire. It suggests an experimentation of reality through its excessiveness abundancy, and endless dynamism, always becoming, ‘becoming is the process of desire’ (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 301). How is it, then, that the subject becomes a concentration of forces in such conditions, as Deleuze suggests? How is it even possible for social organisations to endure in space and time under this non-subjective and pre-personal schizophrenic machinisim? Although desire cannot be ontologically contained or forever arrested, it can be perverted by repressing its circulation. This defines the regime of functioning of social machines5, which qualifies the flows of desire through a semiotic system of coordinates contained in the language (Lapoujade 2017:167). By coding the flows of desire, social machines converge their circulation towards a particular direction.

    Deleuze and Guattari argue that language is a ‘syntagm of power’ (Guattari 2011: 38), as it imposes order through a set of totalising binary coordinates (such as right-wrong, masculine-feminine, good-bad, subject-object). Such a regime of signs operates abstractly by endlessly referring to one another rather than meshing with other elements. The ‘law of the signifier’ aims to subject, to recoil the subject of enouncement into the subject of the statement and convolute desire into seemingly universal symbols, repressing it into discursive practices that modulate affects and emotions in the name of a given identity, a self, or the ego (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 142-144). Ultimately, this leads desire to work for its own repression as subjects feel emotionally ‘safe’ within their own representation (Lapoujade 2017: 175). Social machines, thus, produce the emotional conditions through which non-human elements are secondarily expressed in human subjectivity.

    The crucial example Deleuze and Guattari give is, of course, the Oedipus complex. For them, Oedipus is not the consequence of the psychic repression generated by the invasion of the Name-of-the-Father, resolving the conflict between the Imaginary and Real and marking the subject’s entering the Symbolic order – as Lacanianism would suggest (Stavrakakis 1999: 27). Rather, by coding the schizophrenic flows of desire as ‘Oedipus’, under the ‘daddy-mommy-me triangle’, desire is perverted and repressed. ‘The family is the agent delegated to psychic repression; the incestuous drives are the disfigured image of the repressed.’ To put it simply: repression does not cause Oedipus; Oedipus is what causes repression (Deleuze and Guattari 1983: 119). By taking Oedipus as universal, subjects want to ‘solve’ it, they want to enter the Symbolic order to be able to live in society. Social repression causes psychic repression and not the other way around, this being the mechanism through which social machines operate. As Lapoujade puts it, Deleuze and Guattari do not deny the objective reality of Oedipus, but ‘that the unconscious is oedipalised in fact, that is “universal” in fact – does this authorise us to think that it is universal in principle?’ (Lapoujade 2017: 156).

    Oedipalised subjects invest desire into emotionally charged discourses, producing collective representations in the name of which they act in the world. This, however, is not a static process. As we explained above, desire is a positive, creative force, always circulating in an affirmative fashion exceeding and translating itself into different corporeality. Repression is not a neutral operation but a relational process. It cannot act without displacing desire, without giving rise to a consequent desire (Deleuze and Guattari 1983: 116). In other words, there is always a quantum of desire that escapes the grasp of the State apparatus, pushing forward further dessubjetifications, eroding the ego and driving new processes of individuation. Subjects are produced and dissolved in their environment, along with and through the affective configurations enabled by desire.

    As Buchanan summarises, the process of subject formation essentially means the movement of molecular entities which combine and blend to produce macroscopic, molar entities, which in turn react on those same molecular entities, forcing them to adapt and change again the molar aggregates they created (Buchanan 2008: 91). If there is no ontological discreteness between human and nature; if there is indeed an ontological immanent continuity underlying reality, then all transcendent and putative cleavage placing the self, the subject or ‘man’ above and beyond everything else by social machines is a political move artificially crafted. This is what the concept of desiring-machines allows us to investigate: what comes ‘before’ subjectivity.

    In short, from a Deleuze-Guattarian perspective, desiring-production does not entail subjects, as these are just a perversion of desiring-machines into molar entities. Desire does not need a representative unconscious to be mediated. It already produces reality (Sibertin-Blanc 2010: 26), so much so that if a particular representation of desire takes place (say in the form of the nation-state), it is because desire is already displaced, stratified, and repressed in a process of subjectification (Goodchild 1996: 147). Maritime aphasia relates to that: the ‘voice of the ocean’, its regime of sign, was repressed and cast into subjects’ language in such a way that IR only reproduced that in the form of subjects. We can now understand how the ocean can be considered a desiring machine.

    Oceanic machines and the nomos of the earth: the conditions of possibility for maritime aphasia

    Desiring machines are real because they are the conceptual instruments to investigate the disruptive continuities in desiring-production. To say that something ‘is’ a desiring-machine is a misleading denomination. For one, because everything is (in and through) a machine (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 283). The point, therefore, is neither to define nor judge what is (not) a desiring-machine, but to understand how desiring-production composes multiplicities, and the way in which these coalesce into temporary open totalities. There are no points or positions in this radical multiplicity, only lines. This rhizomatic multiplicity, however, exposes arborescent pseudo multiplicities for what they are (Deleuze and Guattari 1987: 8), allowing only a retrospective analysis.

    The ocean is an expression of this. When Roland Barthes says that the sea is a ‘non-signifying field’ that nonetheless provides semiological material through the flags, slogans, sign-boards, and clothes shown at the beach, he is highlighting the need for social machines to signify the rhizomatic flows of desire conveyed by the sea into arborescent structures (Barthes 1972: 160). This is what we will do in this section. We investigate how desire circulates through and by the ocean and is invested in social machines due to its excessive (more than) wet ontology (Steinberg and Peters 2015; 2019). By doing so, we seek to explain why maritime aphasia occurred through the repression of oceanic desiring-production.

    What we call oceanic desiring-production can be related to the plane of immanence that allowed life to emerge from the sea. A growing scientific consensus has suggested that the alkaline hydrothermal conditions produced by hydrothermal vents, first discovered in 1977 in the seafloor of the East Pacific, not only permitted protocell formation at the origin of life but actively favoured it (Martin et al. 2008; Jordan et al. 2019). These structures function like underwater volcanoes. Expelling geothermally heated water (at the temperature of 400C) creates a chemical reaction that results in the abundance of hydrogen, seemingly responsible for the origin of life. The functioning of the ocean, with its desiring-production, and regime of movement and rest, produced zones of proximity bringing together heterogeneous elements. These machinic agencements we call oceanic machines. The ocean, then, is an expression of desiring-production, being constantly actualised in oceanic machines.

    Oceanic machines are everywhere, but they are not the everything. They are a everything, ‘one’ among many perspectives of reality. Take, for instance, the complexity involved in the cycles of weather and rain. The oceanic water constantly evaporates and later falls back into the land as rain. As the water passes through rock and soil, it dissolves numerous mineral and organic nutrients, which eventually return to the ocean via river flow feeding the growth of plankton that is the base of diverse marine biology (Zalasiewicz and Williams 2014: 39). This marine biology travels across the world through oceanic currents, in a process of endless circulation affected by the regimes of winds, rotation of the earth and heat from the sun. As Rachel Carson once admitted, all the cosmic forces that contribute to the spinning of the planet are linked with the ‘majestic phenomena’ of the permanent currents of the ocean (Carson 1964: 128). In brief, the weather itself is an abstract machine, composed of other agencements that affect and are affected by planetary phenomena. Oceanic machines are one of them.

    Because of this, to be linked to the ‘spinning of the planet’ does not mean to cause it, nor does it exclude the role of other ‘smooth spaces’, such as the desert and the steppe, in the process (Deleuze and Guattari 1987). Oceanic machines are a relational component that creates compositions rather than judgements (Deleuze and Guattari 1994). They are a force amongst the myriad of intensities that compose reality. We could go on in expanding the role of the ocean as the pervasive totality in our lives and its social aspects, but that would be spilling more ink into well-charted waters (see, for instance, Blum 2019; 2010; Peters 2010; Connery 2010; Ingold 2008). As we are concerned with the political implications of such a feature, without losing sight of the ocean, we turn to how oceanic machines produced subjectivity and, thus, maritime aphasia.

    We can see the genealogy of this process in Carl Schmitt’s concept of nomos (2003). Indeed, Schmitt had a vision of the sea as an empty space, subordinating it to land. However, we are not concerned with the ‘validity’ of his theory as much as with the extent to which it voiced the mechanisms through which social machines pervert the oceanic flows of desire. Schmitt conceptualises nomos as ‘the immediate form in which the political and social order of a people becomes spatially visible.’ (Hooker 2009: 72, our emphasis). It is the ‘coherence of multiple, simultaneous understandings of political space’ (Schmitt 2003: 70). Externally it creates collective subjects, it produces the alterity necessary to differentiate an understanding of ‘our’ space and ‘their space.’ Internally, a nomos allows political units (subjects) to understand the zones of interactions through which they act (Schmitt 2003: 78; Hooker 2009).

    The land is the essential space that conditions such a mechanism. Schmitt suggests that land rewards human labour (with crops and mining, for instance); it can be visibly manifested through boundaries and publicly sustained signs of order. ‘Law is bound to the earth and related to the earth’ (Schmitt 2003: 43). Steinberg reads this as forms of investments, ways of organising society and establishing palpable structures, that is, in our view, affective investments or (over)coding procedures that social machines undertake to qualify desiring-production, and pervert the functioning of desiring-machines. ‘Social actors on land construct fences, buildings, and neighbourhoods that convey class divisions and other power relations, drawing lines and producing [for us, ethological] territories’ (Steinberg 2012: 269).

    For Schmitt, several nomoi existed, but it was only when a planetary consciousness of space emerged that the condition of possibility for a truly global nomos could become feasible (Schmitt 2003: 86). This was enabled by the ocean, through a process of worldwide sea appropriation, giving shape to the first nomos of the earth. It all started with the Spanish and Portuguese ‘discoveries’ in the late 15th century, acquired consistency with British maritime supremacy and crumbled after the end of the First World War and the emergence of the USA as a great power. For us, the nomos of the earth is an international oedipalising process. It perverted the flows of oceanic desiring-production towards a pre-given notion of the subject who gradually owned the sea and felt emotionally entitled to it. Understanding its trajectory means understanding why maritime aphasia occurred.

    The international subjectification of the ocean started with the rayas, lines demarcated on the map – such as the Treaty of Tordesillas – that defined the rights of the Portuguese and Spanish on the ‘free’ space of the ‘New World.’ If the oceanic machines produced the agencements that led to the ‘discovery’ of Brazil and America by expeditions led by Pedro Álvares Cabral, Americo Vespucci and several others, the rayas perverted this process further. It did so by being tuned into a sign through which the representation of the ocean produced subjects, subordinating sea/land ‘territories’ to a transcendent entity, a Christian God who invested in the Pope, sanctioned the missionary mandate for the Christian princes to explore their new colonies (Schmitt 2003: 91). The affective investments on these rayas generated the emotional discourse, the subjectifying grid, through which the Portuguese and Spanish further exploited their territory in Latin America in the name of religion missions (Steinberg 1999; Benton 2005).

    The amity lines followed these, signified now by ‘the category of truce.’ They aimed to bracket off a war between European states (mainly Dutch, Spanish, and French) in regions outside their demarcation6 (Schmitt 2003: 92). The emotional attachment to the terms of these lines meant that any conflicts over land, piracy, or violence that occurred beyond the west of the Canary Islands and south of the Tropic of Cancer did not violate any agreements or harm amity relationships between European countries (Netzloff 2019: 54; Mattingly 1962). As a result, the ocean was gradually striated, and the Atlantic was structured by slave trade (Milazzo 2023; Evans 2009; Hofmeyr 2008; Gilroy 2010; 1993). Human beings were treated like cargoes to abide by the discourse governing the oedipalised Atlantic with its lines of communications and ports. Rayas and amity lines created the semiotic coordinates that convoluted oceanic desiring-production into the signs of race and colonialism. The ocean was not speaking for itself but was ‘meaning something’ to social machines ashore.

    For Schmitt, however, such a colonialist striation of the sea allowed for a gradual pacification of war in Europe since the territorial logic of the home territory was intact (Hooker 2009: 93). Put differently, maritime aphasia guaranteed the foundations of the jus publicum Europaeum. The first nomos of the earth, however, would only emerge after the British consolidated their truly maritime existence (Schmitt 2003: 174 -180). Out of the flows of desire that escaped from the rayas and amity lines, English privateers enriched the Tudors and the Stuarts with the plundered treasures of Spanish and Portuguese ships (Schmitt 2003: 177). Neither pirates nor public officials, these private citizens were gradually appropriated by social machines and their institutions. Drake was knighted after circumnavigating the globe and played a significant role in the Elizabethan slave trade, along with his cousin Sir John Hawkins (Paine 2014: 397; Kelsey 2003); in 1599, the English India Company was created, and on May 1617 Sir Walter Raleigh created the first fight instructions regulating the naval operation of English warships (Corbett 1905: 30).

    By mastering the ocean, a balance between sea and land would define a system of sovereignty and state mutual recognition (Schmitt 2003: 180-185), and the Eurocentric, Westphalian state structure would become ‘international.’ The word ‘international’ comes from such a notion to qualify what lies beyond/between European states. As Suganami shows, the neologism invented by Jeremy Bentham in the late 18th century served to designate persons ‘whose conduct is the subject of law’ when members of ‘different states’ (Suganami 1978: 231, our emphasis). The ‘law of the signifier’, thus, became global, defining what it is and what is it not a state or a subject under the laws of the international. New ‘countries’ emerged from within this perspective, aspiring to be subjects, and emotionally investing themselves in such an enterprise – take the wave of independence taking place in Latin America in the 1800s, whereby Brazil auto-denominated itself an ‘Empire.’ The international is an outcome of the repression of the oceanic flows of desire created to subjectify social machines; it is a consequence of maritime aphasia; it is international relations.

    Schmitt’s nomos of the earth would coincide with a so-called Pax Britannica, that is, a highly fluid system of global control, developed to avoid any international activity that threatened British and European interests to guarantee cheap security and expanding trade (Lambert 2018: 4). The repression of the flows of oceanic desire undertaken by the British was made possible through massive investments in the Navy, an effort inextricable linked to the control of the circulation of (until 1807 slave7) trade, through a sophisticated financial, technological, legal, and logistical system (see Frei 2020; Kinkel 2018; Morris 2011). With the end of the Napoleonic wars, Trafalgar oedipalised the meaning of naval battles, being translated into a variety of signs that mythologised Nelson and subjectified how naval engagements should be pursued, shaping Navies around the world until the present day. By the middle and late 1800s, the Royal Navy was charting the ocean, policing the sea in the name of free trade and liberal values, producing the infrastructure for undersea telegraphic cable, and helping the UK to acquire colonial outposts to function as choke points to regulate the worlds’ sea routes (Kennedy 1991: 197-200).

    By perverting the flows of oceanic desire, Britain’s naval power and maritime supremacy contributed to the affective investments that defined what the international was constituted of. This generated an emotional attachment to a sense of international liberalism centred on the order in international politics and paving the way for progress and symbols of law and morality (Sylvester 2007: 34). In the late 1800s, the height of the British Empire also meant the apex of the nomos of the earth. The ocean ‘had already been there’ for centuries but muted or filtered not through signs of imperialism. The nomos of the earth ended after the First World War, as the later effect of another line produced earlier by a non-European actor: the United States. The Western Hemispheric line, traced in 1823 by President Monroe, emerges for Schmitt as a protest against European land appropriation. It was a line of self-isolation. If the perversion of the oceanic flows of desire conditioned the European subjectification of the sea into a colonialist and racist kind of international law, it was also the ocean that set the stage for another kind of machinic agencements that overcoded the latter. The US ‘isolationism’ was rather the effect of another oceanic machine, an affective investment on the ocean, abstracting the US Western position from a material, spatial geographical position to an emotional sentiment of belonging. ‘Western Hemisphere was used in a sense that the US identified itself with all countries that morally, culturally or politically belong to the substance of this hemisphere’ (Schmitt 2003: 288).

    After the First World War, such isolationism would turn into universalism, whereby the USA would shape a non-European, spaceless notion of the international founded on the spread of its own principles. Kissinger summarised that the US international posture ‘has reflected the conviction that its domestic principles were always self-evidently universal and their application salutary’ (Kissinger 2014: 234). There would be no more nomos, spatial appropriation, demarcation of rights, or distribution of capacities, but universal principles applied ad hoc. The USA would be free to make the decision to intervene everywhere while territorially needing to be nowhere other than the USA itself (Legg 2012: 111).

    Britain’s liberalism, repressing the oceanic flows of desire through its Pax Britannica, was then the seed for the displacement of desire contributing to the end of the nomos of the earth. Liberalism challenges the logic of spatial appropriation Schmitt reveres. It frees productive powers by increasing production and mass consumption and ending appropriation (Schmitt 2003: 331). The USA perverted the sense of liberalism promulgated by Britain. It is telling that an American best represented the ocean’s subjectification in a tale of British historic mastery: Mahan. In his best-seller, ‘The Influence of Sea Power Upon History’, Mahan built on the English and British historical trajectory to demonstrate how sea power should be pursued. If Nelson had oedipalised naval battles, Mahan overcoded Nelson’s signifiers into the notion of sea power, ‘The Embodiment of Great Britain’s Sea Power.’

    IR as a discipline arrives precisely at this stage, attempting to signify these flows of desire that escaped from the last nomos of the earth. IR appeared when the ocean was already overcoded but in the very transition between regimes of functioning of the social machines. Maritime aphasia in IR only reinforces this with its different expressions or phases.

    Concluding thoughts

    How and why maritime aphasia emerged in IR? In this piece, we demonstrated that IR has consistently portrayed the sea as a space to be appropriated. Across the three different phases of the discipline, the ocean ‘was there’ to be of political importance but lacked a concept that expressed its own constitution. It rather needed to be shaped by sovereign-related ideas, projection of power or, more recently, to be cast as another securitising element. If this was ‘how’ a maritime voice was heard, we needed a concept to make it understandable. We departed from an alternative ontology that radically decentred the subject and allowed for the ocean to come first.

    In Deleuze and Guattari’s theoretical approach, desire and desiring-machines we found a notion of reality capable of accommodating the ocean per se. We crafted the concept of oceanic machines to understand the process of repression and perversion that creates subjects. Carl Schmitt’s notion of nomos illustrates such a feature, not without expressing itself as a mechanism of repression of desire. We found that IR did not create maritime aphasia; IR was the consequence of maritime aphasia. Maritime aphasia in IR emerged because the international apparatus to repress the flows of desire was crumbling. It was a set of knowledge that signified what the international meant with what eventually became the US-led ‘Liberal International Order.’

    The concept of oceanic machines can, thus, shift the focus away from subjects and allow for a holistic analysis of the sea. It combines emotional and ‘material’ components that express the current excessiveness of the international system. This excess takes various forms, such as an overflow of data; the tendency of individuals to cling to their own version of the truth and reject others; an abundance of waste being dumped into the sea; the emergence of a virus that has led to a worldwide pandemic. Oceanic machines can indicate how the international transmutes its repressions into becomings through the nomadic lines constitutive of desiring-production.
