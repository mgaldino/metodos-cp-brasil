“Towards an Inclusive Capitalism”: A Neo-Gramscian Analysis of the IMF’s Feminist Turn During Christine Lagarde’s Term (2011-2019)

“Rumo a um Capitalismo Inclusivo”: Uma Análise Neo-Gramsciana da Virada Feminista do FMI Durante a Gestão de Christine Lagarde (2011–2019)

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article aims to analyse, based on the theoretical contributions of neo-Gramscian authors, the narrative mechanisms mobilized in the construction of the feminist turn of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) during Christine Lagarde’s tenure. Through network analysis, discourse analysis and content analysis, we conclude that although Lagarde, besides being the first female director, did not represent a turning point compared to her predecessors. However, she did use her authority from being a woman in a position of power to legitimize free market values as part of a feminist agenda. The director’s speeches and documents from the period emphasize free market and economic insertion as solutions for gender equality. Thus, the IMF’s feminist turn mobilizes concepts such as empowerment and leadership in an effort to link greater market participation to the promotion of gender equality. Therefore, there is an adoption of narrative elements related to feminist ideas in an instrumental way, aiming to legitimize the institution’s traditional neoliberal policies.

    Key words
    IMF; feminist turn; Christine Lagarde; neoliberal consensus

    Resumo

    Este artigo tem como objetivo analisar, com base nas contribuições teóricas de autores neo-gramscianos, os mecanismos narrativos mobilizados na construção da virada feminista do Fundo Monetário Internacional (FMI) durante a gestão de Christine Lagarde. Por meio de análise de redes, análise de discurso e análise de conteúdo, concluímos que, embora Lagarde, além de ser a primeira diretora mulher, não tenha representado uma ruptura em relação aos seus antecessores, ela utilizou sua autoridade enquanto mulher em uma posição de poder para legitimar valores de livre mercado como parte de uma agenda feminista. Os discursos da diretora e os documentos do período enfatizam o livre mercado e a inserção econômica como soluções para a igualdade de gênero. Assim, a virada feminista do FMI mobiliza conceitos como empoderamento e liderança, numa tentativa de associar maior participação no mercado à promoção da igualdade de gênero. Portanto, há uma adoção instrumental de elementos narrativos relacionados a ideias feministas, com o objetivo de legitimar as tradicionais políticas neoliberais da instituição.

    Palavras-chave
    FMI; virada feminista; Christine Lagarde; consenso neoliberal

    Introduction

    Christine Lagarde’s tenure as Managing Director of the International Monetary Fund (IMF) from 2011 to 2019 was marked by the so-called feminist turn of the organisation. The lawyer, named by Forbes in 2020 as the second most powerful woman in the world, is renowned for her advocacy of feminizing international institutions and increasing women’s representation in positions of power. Her leadership of the IMF was characterised by a shift in the institution’s internal policies, aimed at integrating a gender agenda into the Fund’s operations.

    The concept of a feminist turn refers to the adoption of a perspective that integrates gender into international institutions’ policies. It is closely linked to the promotion of the so-called gender mainstreaming, a policy aimed at encouraging the adoption of gender as an analytical category within international organisations (Miller and Razavi 1995). This movement began to take shape and gain momentum during the 1990s, particularly within the United Nations (UN). However, international financial institutions, due to their traditionally conservative nature, only began to recognise gender issues in the late 2000s. The IMF first incorporated gender analysis into its documents in 2013, primarily associating gender equity with macroeconomic stability - one of the Fund’s central policy goals. Between 2013 and 2019, over 2,900 documents were published mentioning the term gender, alongside a range of mechanisms promoting feminist discourse within the institution, such as online hashtags and public speeches (Coburn 2019; Duran 2021).

    But what did this feminist turn mean for the IMF? And, more importantly, what interests drove this change? This article analyses the narrative shift within the IMF’s towards a feminist turn and how feminist elements are mobilised by the institution. By adopting a neo-Gramscian perspective, we aim to highlight the actors and interests that shape the IMF’s agenda, as well as the intentionality behind the feminist turn within the institution. In this regard, the central question of this research is: ‘How has the IMF integrated a gender perspective into the institution’s agenda?’ Complementarily, we explore which aspects characterise this feminist turn and the motivation behind the mobilisation of feminist elements within a traditionally neoliberal and conservative institution.

    The evident failure of neoliberal solutions to crises in countries of the Global South led to a growing disillusionment with the Washington Consensus and more traditional neoliberal policies. In this regard, the IMF operates within a post-Washington Consensus model, which seeks to integrate categories of social development in order to lend a new facade to the neoliberal policies (Bergeron 2003; Prugl 2017). It is within this context that the institution’s feminist turn must be critically examined. We argue that the IMF’s intention in integrating gender perspectives is to legitimise the institution’s traditional neoliberal agenda. This is achieved through a narrative construction of a feminist turn, incorporating elements of feminist language and movement, but under neoliberal guidelines and values, in order to legitimise the IMF’s market-driven solutions as promoters of gender equality. In this way, the IMF mobilises feminist discursive elements alongside neoliberal values to promote economic integration and development policies that are already entrenched within the institution. As such, the IMF’s feminist turn assumes a merely discursive, instrumentalist, and highly individualised character, contributing to the maintenance of the institution’s hegemony and its neoliberal perspective, even in face of the crisis of this project.

    The literature analysing the feminist turn of international financial institutions is typically divided into two orientations: the first focuses on how gender has been assimilated by these institutions, while the second questions the very nature of the institutions, the objectives, and the meanings that the adoption of a gender perspective by them assumes. The first tends to concentrate on the instrumentalist form and the micro perspective that the feminist turn has taken within international institutions, seeking to question how the adoption of gender mainstreaming occurs – see Duran (2021), Detraz and Peksen (2016), Bohoslavski and Rulli (2020; 2021), and Berik (2017). The second, beyond this, questions what the adoption of these narratives represents, particularly in terms of their contribution to the systemic maintenance of a sexual division of labour and gender roles, which structure the adoption of a gender perspective within these institutions – see Coburn (2019) and Hickel (2014).

    This article contributes to the field of studies analysing the discursive construction of a gender perspective by international institutions. Specifically, we aim to analyse the relationship between the adoption of feminist agendas by international institutions and their role in maintaining the neoliberal hegemony of these institutions. The contribution of the neo-Gramscian lens allows us to analyse the institution’s discourses and symbolic mobilisations, alongside the actors and interests that shape its political agenda.

    The essay by Weinhold and Brodtmann (2017) paves the way for our study, staring the question of what a gender focus means for the IMF. The authors analyse the institution’s budgetary recommendations, searching for the gender elements proposed by the feminist turn. It seems evident to us that the narrative construction of the IMF’s feminist turn is a field that still warrants further exploration, particularly regarding its motivations and ideological elements. Our argument also engages with the debate initiated by Elisabeth Prugl (2017), who suggests that international financial institutions seek to construct a ‘neoliberalism with a feminist face’ to legitimise, or reaffirm, the consensus surrounding their policies. However, while the author focuses on the World Bank, we aim to analyse this construction within the context of the IMF during Christine Lagarde’s tenure (2011-2019).

    In examining the construction of the feminist narrative at the IMF and the way in which feminist ideas are appropriated and redefined by the institution, we also propose to explore how this unfolds within the broader context of International Relations. In the field of International Relations, we experience a silencing of the neoliberal agenda, which underpins a range of elements related to rational choice, self-interest, self-help, and individualism —elements that have become so naturalised in theorisation of international relations. Understanding how the neoliberal agenda reinvents itself and maintains its hegemony is one of the concerns that drives our research. Feminist theorists have challenged and transcended many of the hegemonic assumptions that underpin our discipline. It is crucial to pay attention to the relationships between feminist thought and the creation of neoliberal consensus in order to avoid falling into a narrative that legitimises historical structures of oppression.

    To undertake this effort, the present article is divided into five sections, in addition to this introduction. First, the theoretical framework of the study will be presented, followed by the methodology and a detailed description of the analytical methods. Next, we will present the results of the analyses and the discussion of these results, concluding with the final considerations of the work.

    Theoretical Framework

    The IMF has historically promoted an economic development agenda based on austerity and privatisation policies. These guidelines have been imposed to varying degrees through loan conditionalities, exemplified by debt crises in Mexico, Argentina and Eastern European countries. The relationship between indebtedness and the promotion of neoliberalism in these countries is largely attributed to the actions of the IMF and the US Federal Reserve in promoting these agendas as solutions to the problems of underdevelopment. Today, neoliberalism has acquired hegemonic status as a political project and forms the basis of the agendas of numerous institutions that constitute the international system (Harvey 2007).

    For Gramsci, the exercise of hegemony is characterised by ‘a combination of force and consensus that achieve a balance, without force overlapping consensus or otherwise appear fully supported by majorities consensus’ (Gramsci 2007: 95, translated by the authors1). The emergence of international organisations constitutes a polysemic understanding of hegemony and represents an institutional space for the legitimisation of an established hegemonic order. In this way, international institutions provide stability to hegemony and minimise the need for the use of force in its maintenance (Pereira 2016).

    In this regard, the inclusion and participation of peripheral countries in these institutions do not imply the possibility of modifying the system (Cox 1983). On the contrary, aiming to preserve the prevailing historical structure, these institutions incorporate peripheral countries and groups into their hierarchy and absorb ideas with counter-hegemonic potential into their discourses, seeking to strengthen their legitimacy in the face of these actors (Gill 1998; 2000). We argue that this is the case with the IMF’s feminist turn, which mobilises and integrates feminist elements in an effort to safeguard the institution’s legitimacy in face of the crisis of their neoliberal agenda.

    William Robinson (1996) analyses how feminist discourses are co-opted by traditionally conservative institutions, arguing that such institutions do not aim to nullify feminist mobilizations per se but rather to neutralize its more radical and anti-systemic elements, subjecting them to the hegemony of the elite and female representatives of transnational corporations. In this way, international institutions adopt gender approaches to control the narrative and the claims that will actually be addressed and/or implemented, ensuring that they do not challenge the order defended by the institution.

    This debate is also related to that proposed by Nancy Fraser (2007; 2013; 2017), who highlights that neoliberal institutions have captured feminist critiques, particularly those of the second wave2. This mobilisation occurs through the adoption and promotion of identity and recognition policies, while denying the more structural and redistributive aspects of the feminist project. In this way, the language and narrative are maintained, yet redirected towards individualism and private, micro experiences, negating the anti-hegemonic nature of the feminist struggle. The adoption of ideas such as representational equality and recognition as ends in themselves has led to a shift in the feminist imagination from an emancipatory and collective project to policies that integrate women into capitalist relations and power structures without fundamentally altering them (Fraser 2007; 2013; 2017). In this sense, we argue that the IMF has appropriated gender debates and ressignified them through the institution’s traditional lens, co-opting narrative elements and associating them with neoliberal values, with the aim of legitimising its traditional policies.

    Central to the process of constructing and/or maintaining hegemony is the existence of organic intellectuals, that is, the ‘commissars’ of a dominant group for the exercise of hegemony and the legitimisation of prevailing economic structure (Gramsci 1982). These intellectuals determine which viewpoints and opinions should be disseminated, which facts are significant enough to be consolidated, and how and from which angle they should be presented (Hayek 2017).

    From a Gramscian perspective, the dominant class legitimises itself by persuading society to adopt a specific form of thought, typically one that maintains the status quo and perpetuates economic and social inequalities (Gramsci 1971). For Gramsci, ideas and culture do not arise spontaneously, but are disseminated in an organised manner by the dominant class through intellectuals who play a central role in society. Each class creates its own intellectuals, who assist in the development of class consciousness and the legitimisation of its economic interests. Thus, the domination of the ruling class is not sustained solely by coercion, but also by consensus, operating through various social institutions that justify and legitimise the dominant system (Cox 1981).

    Institutions such as the IMF are therefore created to disseminate the neoliberal capitalist ideal of a ‘market civilisation’ (Gill 1995), which promotes deregulation and the free market under a narrative of the common good and universality. This functions as means of legitimising the project that, in reality, serves to perpetuate class domination. In this way, a complex network of institutions is established, serving the construction and maintenance of hegemony, promoting consensus around the hegemonic project and obscuring inequalities of power, influence, and class (Brzezinski 1998).

    Methodology

    Power is intrinsically relational; therefore, to understand how an individual holds and exercises power, it is essential to comprehend their relational patterns (Lazer 2011). To pursue this endeavour, we employ Social Network Analysis (SNA) to profile Christine Lagarde and compare her profile with that of other IMF directors. We analyse the following aspects: nationality, academic background, professional trajectory, and gender. SNA enables the investigation and measurement of persistent patterns between agents, which can define, enable, and constrain them (Hafner-Burton, Kahler and Montgomery 2009). Thus, our aim is to analyse the relationships and context that shape the director, as well as traits she exhibits, influenced by various variables—temporal, political, geographical, social, and economic—within a social structure.

    We begin with the assumption that individuals are ‘inseparably strategic locations, places to defend and conquer in a field of struggles marked by the effort to maintain the neoliberal consensus in society’ (Brum 2022: 135, translated by the authors)3. In this regard, SNA enables us to understand the environments in which the directors were socialised, the class interests they represent, and to identify which of these elements are most prominent in Christine Lagarde’s trajectory. We analyse a total of 12 directors of IMF, listed in Table 1. To this end, we rely on a database compiled by the authors concerning each category analysed. The sources include information available on the IMF website, as well as data from other institutions to which these individuals are or have been affiliated.

    Next, to understand the discursive dimension of the feminist turn, we conduct a Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) based on the contributions of Teun A. Van Dijk (1993; 1999; 2000; 2015). CDA enables us to understand how certain discursive structures are constructed, validated, and used to reproduce power relations, reinforcing domination and inequalities between the groups that exercise control and those that are subordinated (Van Dijk 1993; 1999; 2015). Our aim is to analyse how gender permeates the discourses of Christine Lagarde and how she connects it to the IMF’s policies. We analyse the speeches of Lagarde selected by the IMF, published in 2019 in the volume titled ‘The Power of Partnership: Selected Speeches by Christine Lagarde 2011–2019’. The book comprises 18 speeches, from which we selected those in which the gender agenda is mentioned, resulting in 12 speeches, as shown in Table 2.

    The speeches were delivered on various occasions between 2011 and 2019. Most of them address topics such as economic growth, development, economic cooperation, and globalisation, among others. Only one of the speeches specifically addresses women, namely, the speech ‘Daring the Difference: The Three L’s o Women’s Empowerment’. We conducted the discourse analysis based on the identification of the categories gender and women/woman, alongside four associative categories: development and economic growth—these two being concepts related to the typical IMF agenda—and empowerment and leadership—these two linked to the feminist agenda. Following the discourse analysis, with the assistance of the Iramuteq software, we performed a similarity analysis to identify and systematise co-occurrences between words.

    Finally, to analyse the relationship between narrative and policy formulation, we employed a Critical Content Analysis, based on the model proposed by Bardin (2011), to examine the IMF’s Policy Documents and Policy Papers from 2013 to 2019. The aim of the analysis is to identify how the IMF has addressed gender issues and how narrative elements are integrated into the institution’s political agenda. In doing so, we seek to analyse how feminist elements have been mobilised, and, in summary, how the feminist turn has materialised in the institution’s policy. Thousands of documents published during this period mention the word gender; given the impossibility of working with all of them, the selection of the documents analysed was based on the institution’s Gender Strategy, published in 2022.     Results

    Social Network Analysis (SNA) of the Directors

    Drawing on the conception that intellectuals and international organisations tend to express interests, political projects, and worldviews capable of forming a specific consensus aimed at maintaining a hegemonic class (Cox 1983), we conducted a Network Analysis focusing on the trajectories of the organic intellectuals who comprise the administrative elite of the IMF.

    Analysing the category of Academic Trajectory, and considering the overlap among those with multiple qualifications, we identified that the majority of former IMF directors are trained in Economics (8 individuals), Law (6 individuals), and Political Science (5 individuals), as shown in Figure 1. Lagarde’s academic background follows a similar trajectory to that of the other directors, having been trained in both Economics and Law.

    The prevalence of training in Economics and Law among the directors reflects the technocratic culture of the institution. These professions underpin the scientific and expert legitimacy that is mobilised to support the IMF’s agenda. The institution’s economic perspectives advocate for neoliberalism as a universal model, through the use of indicators and free-market models. This narrative is also applied to the tools for promoting gender equality endorsed by the institution, which are integrated into this agenda.

    Moreover, all of these directors attended predominantly European universities, with the single exception of a former director who studied in the United States, as shown in Figure 2. The majority of the universities attended by former IMF directors are French, with particular emphasis on the Paris Institute of Political Studies (Sciences Po), the National School of Administration, the University of Paris, and the institutions that succeeded its fragmentation, such as the Université Paris X-Nanterre.

    As they are all European and have careers built in renowned European institutions, the education of these individuals stems from an exclusively Global North perspective. However, their work at the IMF primarily takes place in Global South countries. Thus, endowed with the intellectual legitimacy conferred by their academic credentials, grounded in institutions recognised by this elite, these organic intellectuals are responsible for exporting ideas and worldviews that are predominantly Eurocentric. This perspective is also linked to the technocratic and universalising culture of the IMF, aligning with its class interests (Gramsci 1982).

    The State positions held by the former directors, as shown in Figure 3, complement this scenario. The majority of them held positions in economic ministries or central banks, which is also a result of their background in economics. However, this further reinforces the idea that the perspective they bring to the IMF is one rooted in an economic and technocratic standpoint.

They have also represented or been part of the boards of transnational companies, such as Ford, Unilever, and General Motors, as well as being affiliated with investment firms listed on the stock exchange, such as NYSE Euronext.     Similar to most IMF directors, Lagarde also worked in the private sector, being affiliated with the international law firm Baker & McKenzie. It is important to note that the firm was prominently mentioned in the Pandora Papers documents published by the International Consortium of Investigative Journalists, which revealed that the firm helped organise shell companies in Cyprus for RJR Nabisco, created a tax shelter in the Netherlands for Nike, and assisted Facebook in moving billions of dollars in profits to Ireland, where taxation is low. This ‘revolving door’ between the public and private sectors raises ethical concerns for institutions, and the case of Baker & McKenzie serves as yet another example of the political effects of powerful multinational corporations (LaPira and Thomas 2014).

    Discussion of Results

    As presented in the Network Analysis, the IMF directors share similar geographical backgrounds, with the majority hailing from France. They also possess comparable academic qualifications, primarily in Economics and Law from French universities. Furthermore, their professional trajectories are strikingly similar, having held State positions such as Ministers of Economics and Finance, as well as roles in the private sector, representing banks and multinational corporations.

    The analysis of the IMF board’s profile shows that its directors represent a specific, dominant economic and political class. They move between the political and economic sectors, both public and private, operating internationally and disseminating – through a private apparatus of hegemony – an economic and social ideology (Dardot and Laval 2016) that sustains their symbolic and material privileges. In this regard, Christine Lagarde does not represent a departure from the other IMF directors. Although she was the first woman to lead the institution, Lagarde is also part of the dominant economic and political class, with a trajectory and interests similar to those of her predecessors. Lagarde uses the authority of representativeness to advocate for the IMF’s agenda; however, this is a limited and hollow form of representativeness. The former director does not signify a departure from the dominant group or the interests that shape the institution.

    Therefore, we argue that she stands out as a figure in the instrumentalization of the gender agenda to legitimise the IMF’s neoliberal policies, as she draws upon the notion of representativeness, which grants her the authority to address the issue. The instrumentalization of gender mainstreaming here refers to the mobilisation of elements of gender and feminist narratives by the institution for a purpose other than their original project. In this context, the objective is to legitimise the IMF’s policies in response to the decline in the legitimacy of the neoliberal project.

    The significance of Christine Lagarde in this context lies precisely in her being the first female director at the institution, a fact that points to the integration of a female perspective within the institution and the adoption of representative policies. The notion of representativeness and the empowerment of women reduced to the presence of women in positions of power, without necessarily implying a change in the policies or the system of the institutions in which they are placed, is a reflection of the mobilisation of feminist narratives by neoliberal institutions. Representativeness and empowerment, when viewed as an individual and isolated experiences, do not challenge gender relations or question the gender structure (Fraser 2013; 2017; Prugl 2017).

    Thus, Lagarde’s appointment to the IMF as a form of representation, without altering or questioning its traditional policies, exemplifies the attempt by the dominant forces of global political economy to incorporate progressive forms of opposition. This is manifested in the rearticulation of feminist discourses on empowerment by international financial institutions such as the IMF seeking to expand their social support base through the incorporation of opposition (Gill 2000).

    In relation to the discourses analysed, we observe a strong presence of themes related to empowerment, participation, and economic integration. By linking these three elements, the director consolidates the institution’s gender narrative, which is grounded in the idea of economic inclusion as a form of women’s empowerment and promotion of gender equality. In the director’s speeches, financial integration, participation in capitalist relations, and involvement in the workforce are the only proposals associated with women’s freedom and empowerment. This narrative emerges from an opposition between participation in the market and the home. Participation in the market is presented as the means to overcome the inequality that oppresses and excludes women within the domestic sphere. This perspective also presupposes a superiority of ‘economically integrated’ women over those who are not. More profoundly, the relationship established in this opposition and implicit in this narrative suggests a supremacy of the market, which is seen as having the attribute of empowering individuals. This narrative simultaneously reflects the primacy of market solutions and a binary, polarised worldview, constantly evolving towards a fully market-driven society—both perspectives that are traditional in neoliberal thought.

    Moreover, we highlight that the perspective adopted by the director when discussing empowerment is merely formal. When addressing gender inequality in her speech ‘Forging a Stronger Social Contract: The IMF’s Approach to Social Spending’, Lagarde frequently attributes the solution to the integration of female labour into the workforce, advocating for the reduction of barriers to women’s participation in the labour market and emphasising the potential of this participation for economic growth. In the speech ‘Daring the Difference: The Three L’s of Women’s Empowerment’, the former director argues that women represent half of the global population but do not participate in even half of economic activities. In ‘Brothers and Sisters, There Is Much to Do’, Lagarde asserts that empowering women is essential for their active integration in the economy.

    The way in which gender equality is reduced to economic inclusion also reflects the institution’s economistic culture, which chooses to measure equality in purely economic terms. Indicators such as ‘gender equality’ create impressions of precision and objectivity but simplify complex social phenomena by transforming them into comparable numerical representations. This process reduces context and meaning, presenting reality in a dichotomous manner, eliminating nuances and combining incomplete data to create an illusion of coherence (Davis, Kingsbury and Merry 2012). In this way, such indicators often depoliticise potentially political issues, naturalise certain interpretations of reality, and exclude alternative perspectives, obscuring the sociopolitical conceptions embedded in their construction (Erkkilä and Piironen 2018; Davis, Kingsbury and Merry 2012). For example, gender equality indicators tend to correlate a country’s gender gap with its economic performance. By suggesting that the solution to inequality lies in a greater female participation in the labour market and public-private cooperation, these indicators reinforce a pursuit of gender equality focused on economic efficiency and global competitiveness, rather than promoting structural change (Marques 2022).

    In reference to economic development and growth, Lagarde states in ‘Demographic Change and Economic Well-Being: The Role of Fiscal Policy’ that one way to ‘grow the economic pie’ is by increasing the employed workforce, with a particular focus on women. In this regard, the former director argues in her speech ‘A Time to Repair the Roof’ that when women contribute more, the economy performs better. Citing several examples, she asserts that if women participated in the workforce at the same rate as men, GDP could increase by up to 5% in the US, 27% in India, and 34% in Egypt. By not allowing women to ‘contribute more to the economy’, we limit living standards for all, as increasing women’s participation in the workforce would lead to a rise in per capita income. The increase in per capita income from integrating women into the market on equal terms with men would be 27% in the Middle East and North Africa, 23% in South Asia, 17% in Latin America, 15% in East Asia, and 14% in Europe and Central Asia, as Lagarde addresses in the speech ‘A New Multilateralism for the 21st Century’. She concludes by stating that ‘we simply cannot afford to throw away these gains’ (Lagarde 2019: 179), which would be provided by women’s integration into the economy.

    Thus, by arguing that the inclusion of women in the workforce would lead to better living conditions for all, Lagarde suggests, as Prugl (2017) asserts, an understanding that markets do not produce inequality, but rather equality. In this way, the pursuit of profit and gender equality would go hand in hand. Furthermore, the increase in the number of women in the workforce is treated as an indicator of gender equality in itself, irrespective of wage disparities and the quality of these jobs (Prugl 2017). In this regard, Lagarde also argues that women should occupy more leadership positions, as ‘when women get the chance to lead, they actually lead better’ (Lagarde 2019: 166), since ‘women are also far less likely to engage in the kind of reckless risk-taking behaviour that sparked the global financial crisis’ (Lagarde 2019: 166).

    In her speech ‘The Financial Sector Defining a Broader Sense of Purpose’, Lagarde references the 2018 research ‘Women in Finance: A Case for Closing Gaps’ to assert that a higher percentage of women on the boards of banks and financial supervisory agencies promotes greater financial stability, which, in turn, supports stronger and more sustained growth (Lagarde 2019). Thus, ‘[c]learly, more female leadership is critical’ (Lagarde 2019: 19). More female leadership would not only prevent crises, but Lagarde also asserts that, for instance, the fight against corruption would be better led by women. According to her, in the speech ‘Age of Ingenuity Reimagining 21st Century International Cooperation’, ‘[c]orruption is a cancer that does not recognize borders. […] If we take on the challenge of corruption […] this time, women will play a starring role!’ (Lagarde 2019: 166). Therefore, the end of gender inequality would bring about a global economic change that is both broader and accompanied diverse and abstract economic and political benefits (Lagarde 2019).

    In ‘Lifting the Small Boats’, when addressing excessive income inequality, Lagarde asserts that she will not focus ‘on the gorgeous yachts of the super-rich, who have become the face of a new Gilded Age. It is not immoral to enjoy one’s financial success’ (Lagarde 2019: 166). Lagarde does not challenge the capitalist system – which particularly penalises women – nor the extreme concentration of wealth. On the contrary, in her speech, the former IMF Director proposes an ‘inclusive capitalism’ (Lagarde 2019: 151). By making capitalism more inclusive, it would be more effective and sustainable (Lagarde 2019). There is no questioning of structural gender inequality. In this regard, her concern does not lie with the problem of inequality itself, but rather with excessive inequality, which, according to her, makes capitalism less inclusive and could undermine the principles of meritocracy and democracy (Lagarde 2019).

    In her speeches ‘Forging Social Contract’ and ‘Making Globalization Work for All’, Lagarde asserts that the IMF has an obligation to the poor and vulnerable, including women, and she uses this obligation to argue that economic freedom must be encouraged, as ‘emerging and developing economies have been the primary beneficiaries of economic openness’ (Lagarde 2019: 80). Similarly, she claims that open trade has helped to reduce the proportion of the global population living in extreme poverty (Lagarde 2019). Therefore, this would be the path to making ‘[g]lobalization work for all’ (Lagarde 2019: 77).

    In contrast to what is argued by Lagarde, David Harvey (2006) points to a gap in neoliberal discourse between the rhetoric of benefiting everyone and the reality of ongoing inequality, with the benefit accruing to a small dominant class, which grows over time. In practice, neoliberal strategies and reforms have had a significant impact on the economies, societies, and institutions of countries on the periphery of the capitalist system, such as those in Latin America, leading to an increase in poverty and regional and class-based inequalities (Harvey 2006). Moreover, José Francisco Puello-Socarrás (2013) highlights the relationship between neoliberalism and authoritarianism. In Latin America, for instance, in many countries of the Southern Cone, the neoliberal model proposed by the Washington Consensus was implemented through coups d’état and civil-military dictatorships, often supported by foreign intervention (Puello-Socarrás 2013). In the same vein, Harvey (2006) emphasises the increasingly authoritarian use of State power to sustain the neoliberal hegemony and the undemocratic nature of neoliberalism. For the author, the lack of accountability and democratic control over institutions such as the IMF, the WTO, and the World Bank renders discussions of democratisation unfeasible (Harvey 2006).

    This relationship supports that the gender agenda proposed by Christine Lagarde does not break with the neoliberal guidelines set forth by the IMF. In practice, there is an instrumentalization of feminist discourse to create a consensus around the values proposed by the IMF and to legitimise them. In effect, a correlation is constructed between elements of feminist narratives and neoliberal values, such as the individualisation of empowerment and representation, as well as the reduction of gender equality to economic integration. Through these associations, which can be considered co-optations of feminist discourses (Prugl 2017), the director links feminist objectives to the promotion of neoliberal ideals as solutions and means to achieve greater gender equality.

    This internationalisation and naturalisation of neoliberalism is a central dimension of the strategy of hegemonic control by a dominant class on an international scale. By presenting neoliberal assumptions in the virtuous guise of promoting gender equality, the dominant class legitimises its interests as ‘common goods’ (Gramsci 1971). Lagarde’s feminist discourse, in this sense, veils neoliberalism and promotes it in a disguised manner, but does not break from it; on the contrary, it reinforces it.

    Finally, through Critical Content Analysis, we examine how gender elements are mobilised by the institution and the characteristics that the institution’s gender perspective assumes. Content analysis, in conjunction with discourse analysis, allows us to explore what type of inclusion and gender agenda the IMF has developed. Our argument is that the integration of the gender category by the IMF rests on three elements that underpin the institution’s traditional neoliberal agenda. These are: (i) the adoption of a micro perspective on gender, which ignores the structural dimension of gender inequalities and their intersection with other power structures; (ii) an instrumentalist perspective on gender equality, which links the reduction of the frequently mentioned gender gaps to economic growth; and (iii) a supremacy of market-based solutions to gender inequality. Thus, the mobilisation of the gender narrative by the institution does not challenge its hegemonic project. On the contrary, it integrates the gender perspective as means to legitimise the institution’s traditional agenda.

    First, we observe an increase in the use of the term gender throughout Lagarde’s term, which is partly due to the fact that two of these documents specifically address gender-related issues. However, it is also notable that the terms empowerment and leadership only began to appear in documents from the last two years. This suggests that, over the course of her mandate, there has been an increasing appropriation of terms and expressions associated with feminist discourse.

    The IMF, like the World Bank, promotes the idea that integrating women into existing production and financial relations would foster greater female empowerment (Coburn 2019). However, the perspective promoted by the IMF is essentially compensatory, viewing gender as a factor that affects only women on an individual basis (Duran 2021). The greater prevalence of the term women compared to gender reflects this approach, as does the strong association with terms such as labour market, financial inclusion, and leadership.

    This micro-level perspective overlooks the possibility of structural gender factors, such as the existence of a sexual division of labour that is fundamental to the economic system and the maintenance of work (Weinhold and Broadtamann 2017). Similarly, it disregards other intersectional power structures, such as race, which also condition economic relations (Bohoslavski and Rulli 2020; Duran 2021). As Elaine Coburn (2019) points out, the Fund focuses solely on the actor’s contribution to economic growth, without offering a relational analysis of the structure.

    In all these documents, we identify a strongly instrumentalist perspective on gender equality, where the promotion of women’s rights is consistently linked to the justification that it will enhance economic growth, make it more stable, and even more inclusive. The associations with the terms growth and economic development exemplify this relationship. By linking greater female participation in productive and financial relations to higher and more sustainable economic growth, the institution justifies the gender-focused approach in its policies. At the same time, by associating development with an improvement in women’s condition (in a broad and abstract sense), the institution legitimises its development agenda. This instrumental perspective, which does not recognise gender equality as an end in itself, highlights how the discourse of the feminist turn is manipulated to justify neoliberal agendas.

    Furthermore, there is a clear perspective that gender is an individual condition, an identity category, and that it affects only women (Duran 2021). In this regard, it is interesting to note the constant use of the term gender gap, which frequently appears in relation to wages, education, access to services, and even financial inclusion. In the document ‘Jobs and Growth: Analytical and Operational Considerations for the Fund’, the gender gap is mentioned on four occasions; this number rises to 28 times in ‘Pursuing Women’s Economic Empowerment’. In ‘World Economic Outlook’, it appears in 8 of the 25 instances where the term gender is mentioned, and finally, in ‘Gender Budgeting in G7 Countries’, gender gaps are mentioned twice.

    The document on Gender Budgeting addresses one of the key initiatives of Lagarde’s term: the promotion of gender-sensitive budgets. This campaign, with the management’s strong focus on gendering national budgets, employs feminist language and narrative in a manner that is palatable to decision-makers, without implying a more substantial transformation of the neoliberal economic policies promoted by the IMF.

    There is also a clear association with the terms equality and inclusion, which are consistently mobilised in the narrative that integrating women into a system and programmes already consolidated by the IMF—essentially neoliberal in nature—will promote greater gender equality and, consequently, ‘better’ economic growth. Here, we can clearly perceive how the IMF has instrumentalised the idea of gender equality, economic integration, and female participation to further the institution’s traditional economic growth agenda. Women are viewed as a means to increase the labour supply, productivity, and employment, serving as ways to expand growth and make it more stable. Rather than questioning how we can change economic relations to exploit and harm women less, the IMF asks how we can integrate women into economic relations to boost growth (Weinhold and Brodtmann 2017). This inversion of causal logic is not incidental; the IMF’s goal remains greater economic growth, and the integration of a gender perspective will always be conditioned by and in service of this development agenda.

    In a similar manner, only improvements regarding women’s condition that directly promote economic growth are endorsed by the IMF (Bohoslavsky and Rulli 2020). Issues related to the care economy and motherhood rarely feature in the institution’s perspective, as they are traditionally excluded from the economic sphere and attributed to exogenous factors. Since the IMF does not adopt a structural perspective, there is no correlation between reproductive labour and the gender gaps it presents, nor between a greater infrastructure of support for social reproduction and enhanced gender equality (Bohoslavsky and Rulli 2020; Coburn, 2019; Weinhold and Brodtmann, 2017).

    The IMF imposes its own definition of human rights – particularly gender equality – and legitimises its policies by invoking the term (Guilhot 2005). Above all, the instrumentalization of gender equality is easily associated with the perception of female labour, and women as a whole, as commodities. The idea that women are an underutilised resource objectifies and essentialises them. Furthermore, the IMF promotes, as is customary within the fund, an extremely depoliticised debate on the issue (Duran 2021).

    Nancy Fraser (2017) addresses the cultural transformations arising from neoliberalism, highlighting the capacity of the neoliberal agenda to adopt ideas and language from social movements in order to promote its anti-state, capitalist, and anti-democratic ideology. It is essential to acknowledge here that one of the main strengths of neoliberalism lies in its appropriation of ideas and the re-signification of popular concepts, such as the anti-statism of the 1960s movements and the idea of representativeness, to build consensus around the neoliberal agenda (Guilhot 2005). Of particular relevance is the way in which the concept of gender equality is transformed into a frivolous notion, referring only to formal equity, measurable through indicators. Similarly, empowerment is stripped of its meaning and redefined as an individualistic concept, associated with the idea of self-made women who have overcome gender limitations and now occupy positions equal to or superior to those of men. Without its collective and structural characteristics, the concept loses all critical value, becoming merely a decorative element for the IMF’s neoliberal agenda.

    Thus, the human rights agenda, like the gender agenda, which was once central to critiquing power, now becomes part of the very arsenal and language of power itself (Guilhot 2005). The mobilisation of the human rights agenda by institutions such as the IMF reflects a pursuit of maintaining private interests, rather than adhering to the progressive agenda of the movement, in a conservative and even counter-revolutionary manner (Guilhot 2005). This process of mobilising feminist imagery and language is more than mere co-optation; it empties and re-signifies feminist terms, and distorts the agenda through a neoliberal doctrine. This is why the adoption of an instrumental and micro-level perspective of analysis becomes so significant (Prugl 2017). The IMF must navigate numerous obstacles and mask a vast array of factors contributing to gender inequality in order to promote its neoliberal agenda. This situation is further exacerbated by the fact that much of the deterioration in women’s conditions in recent decades, both in the labour market and in relation to reproductive labour, is a result of the neoliberal policies promoted by the fund (Bohoslavsky and Rulli 2020).

    The IMF’s perspective that women are the solution to the crises and limitations of the current economic order functions as a mechanism for denying the problems arising from its own political project. More than that, the idea of gender mainstreaming has camouflaged, under a new and polished narrative, the fact that the IMF remains grounded in its more traditional and conservative foundations. The neoliberal agenda of austerity, privatisation, and market supremacy remains unchanged, what occurs is the adoption of feminist language and gendered adjectives to promote the perception of an agenda that is inclusive, innovative, and progressive (Prugl 2017). The moral appeal of the human rights agenda, like the gender struggle, becomes an ideal instrument for organising a broader national and international consensus for the IMF’s policies (Guilhot 2005). As Elisabeth Prugl states:

    It is a blueprint for a new ideology that preserves core commitments to market rationality and squares them with demands for more equality. Neoliberalism with a feminist face defines the problem to be solved not only as underdevelopment, but also as inequality. Its logic goes as follows: Neoliberal economics and liberal feminist values of equality co-constitute each other. (Prugl 2017: 47)

    Final Considerations

    Throughout this work, we have analysed the construction of the IMF’s feminist turn through the relationship between ideas, actors, and institutions. The focus on Christine Lagarde’s mandate (2011-2019) allowed us to examine how representativity and empowerment, the main feminist narrative mechanisms adopted by institutions, are mobilised in support of a neoliberal agenda. The IMF’s feminist turn under Lagarde’s leadership is grounded in the relationship between increased market participation and financial inclusion, alongside the promotion of gender equality. Additionally, as observed, the adoption of a micro-level perspective on the gender category invisibilises structural power relations and the sexual division of labour, leading to an analysis of gender inequality as a series of gaps. The instrumentalist perspective on gender equality, viewed as a means for economic growth and more stable development, complements the institution’s neoliberal viewpoint. Thus, gender equality becomes yet another mechanism for integrating peripheral countries into the dynamics of international neoliberal capitalism.

    The efforts of international financial institutions are part of an ongoing political project aimed at the subjugation of populations in the Global South to ensure the continuous accumulation and maintenance of profitability standards for the ruling classes at the centre of the system (Federici 2018). In this process, women are a key element, as their unpaid reproductive labour is the essential link that sustains the working class and the existence of communal goods today. They are the primary target of neoliberalism’s new initiatives, as they represent the main force of resistance to the advance of capitalist relations (Federici 2018). The defence of inclusive capitalism for all, by actors such as Christine Lagarde, obscures the true objective of neoliberalism in its ‘feminist’ phase: to advance further and dispossess more of the peripheral communities that still maintain some degree of independence from this relationship.
