MATURITY MODEL FOR EVALUATING INTEGRITY MANAGEMENT IN PUBLIC PROCUREMENT

Modelo de maturidade para avaliar a gestão de integridade nas contratações públicas

Modelo de madurez para evaluar la gestión de integridad en las contrataciones públicas

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    ABSTRACT

    This article presents the maturity model for evaluating integrity management in public procurement developed from national and international practices for identifying, assessing, and mitigating integrity risks. The model consists of 30 assessment questions weighed across four maturity levels. It was formulated through a comprehensive literature review and validated in four Brazilian public agencies at municipal, state, and federal levels. This model allows for self-assessment of key weaknesses and strengths in contractual risk management and supports the continuous improvement process of internal controls to preserve public value.

    Palavras-chave:
    public integrity; public bidding; risk management; public procurement; maturity model

    RESUMO

    Este artigo apresenta o modelo de maturidade para avaliar a gestão de integridade nas contratações públicas, desenvolvido a partir das práticas, nacionais e internacionais, de identificação, avaliação e tratamento de riscos de integridade nas contratações públicas. O modelo é composto por 30 quesitos, avaliados segundo quatro níveis de maturidade. Foi formulado a partir de uma ampla revisão da literatura e validado em quatro órgãos públicos brasileiros, de níveis municipal, estadual e federal. Esse modelo permite realizar uma autoavaliação das principais fragilidades e fortalezas da gestão de riscos contratuais, além de apoiar o processo contínuo de aprimoramento dos controles internos que visam preservar valor público.

    Keywords:
    integridade pública; licitações; gestão de riscos; contratações públicas; modelo de maturidade

    RESUMEN

    Este artículo presenta un modelo de madurez para evaluar la gestión de integridad de las contrataciones públicas, desarrollado a partir de prácticas nacionales e internacionales para identificar, evaluar y mitigar riesgos de integridad en la contratación pública. El modelo consta de 30 criterios, evaluados en cuatro niveles de madurez. Fue formulado mediante una amplia revisión de la literatura y validado en cuatro agencias públicas brasileñas a nivel municipal, estadual y federal. Este modelo permite la autoevaluación de las principales debilidades y fortalezas en la gestión del riesgo contractual, además de apoyar el proceso de mejora continua de los controles internos destinados a preservar el valor público.

    Palabras Clave:
    integridad pública; licitación pública; gestión de riesgos; contratación pública; modelo de madurez

    INTRODUCTION

    Corruption in public contracts is an ongoing challenge within public management. Negative events such as object manipulation, bidder collusion, overpricing, document fraud, and bribery of public officials undermine the creation and preservation of public value (Transparency International, 2022). Given the abundant resources available (opportunity) and the technical intricacies involved (capacity), public procurement processes stand out as highly susceptible to integrity risks (American Institute of Certified Public Accountants, 2022; Bono, 2021; Cingolani & Fazekas, 2017). As a result, both internal and external oversight bodies (including internal audits, courts of accounts, and civil society) have emphasized the importance of effectively managing risks that may engender corruption and compromise the efficacy of public procurement (Europa, 2024; Tribunal de Contas da União [TCU], 2020). Despite certain criticisms and limitations, ensuring a comprehensive evaluation of integrity risks and the implementation of robust internal controls throughout the public procurement process are crucial measures for forestalling corruption within public entities (Oulasvirta & Anttiroiko, 2017; Souza et al., 2020).

    The Maturity Model for Evaluating Integrity Management in Public Procurement (MGIC) seeks to bolster this initiative for Brazilian public entities by evaluating the effectiveness of internal controls in mitigating, detecting, addressing, and remedying integrity risks across the public procurement process. Through its application, public organizations can conduct self-assessments and enhance their performance in public procurement. Furthermore, it can serve as a benchmark for external evaluations, fostering a targeted interaction between social oversight and public entities, communicating assessment criteria, and facilitating learning and improvement.

    The following sections elaborate on the model’s assumptions and theoretical and legal foundations and specify its methodological features. The revised model and an analysis of its potentialities and limitations, informed by insights gleaned from practical application, are presented.

    LEGAL AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORKS

    Establishing risk-based controls is a fundamental feature of corporate governance for Brazilian public entities (Decreto nº 9.203, 2017; TCU, 2014). Corporate governance entails the direction and control of organizations to fulfill their purpose, ensuring that internal controls are based on proper risk assessment. The standard risk management system comprises a set of coordinated activities to identify, analyze, evaluate, treat, and monitor risks recently adopted by various nations as part of a new wave of public corporate governance reforms (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission, 2007; International Organization for Standardization [ISO], 2018; Maijoor, 2000). From this recent modernization movement, Brazilian internal and external control bodies have published practical risk management manuals to guide public managers (Controladoria-Geral da União [CGU], 2018; TCU, 2020).

    In this context, new laws on bidding and administrative contracts have clearly established the obligation to adopt risk-based internal controls in public contracts. In verbatim:

    Article 11. [...] Sole Paragraph. The senior management of the agency or entity is responsible for the governance of contracts and must implement processes and structures, including risk management and internal controls, to assess, direct, and monitor bidding processes and their respective contracts, with the aim of achieving the objectives established in the main clause of this article, promoting an integral and reliable environment, ensuring alignment of contracts with strategic planning and budgetary laws, and promoting efficiency, effectiveness, and efficacy in their contracts. (Lei de Licitações e Contratos Administrativos, 2021, emphasis added)

    Although the Brazilian legal framework considers risk management systems and compliance programs an integral part of all organizational processes (and must be regarded as a critical component of the decision-making process), the scant empirical evidence available suggests that its implementation remains highly limited among public bodies (Araújo & Vieira, 2020; Azevedo et al., 2019; Barreto & Vieira, 2021; Souza et al., 2020). In an administrative context marked by instability, low technical capacity, weak public leadership, and a limited culture of result-oriented public service, managing integrity risks in public procurement remains an exceedingly ambitious goal for most Brazilian’ governance structures within public entities (Gomide et al., 2022; Oliveira, 2023; TCU, 2018, 2021c; Vieira, 2019).

    Despite this limited context for most Brazilian public entities (at all federative levels), few with developed governance mechanisms can benefit from such a maturity model. Since the 1980s, when the first maturity model was introduced for the United States Department of Defense, it has become a significant tool for predicting and enhancing organizational outcomes (Reis et al., 2017; Stoiber et al., 2023). Some models have even been tailored to refine public procurement processes, yet none have ever specifically addressed integrity risk management (Aulia & Isvara, 2021; Rendon, 2015). Despite its originality, our model is inspired by others, as described in Table 1.

    Maturity models define sequential levels representing an evolutionary path, advancing from initial stages to an ideal maximum level for a specific process (Campos, 2013 apud Becker et al., 2009). Acknowledged as significant tools, they are designed to systematically evaluate and enhance organizational capabilities, and their demand in private and public organizations is considerable (Costa & Piana, 2020; Looy et al., 2017). However, the epistemological and methodological flaws in these models seem limitless (Mettle & Ballester, 2021; Wendler, 2012). There are well-known criticisms aimed at validity, applicability, relevance, flexibility, and so on (Mettle & Ballester, 2021; Pöppelbuß & Röglinger, 2011). To address them, guidelines have been proposed for designing maturity models (Becker et al., 2009; Lasrado et al., 2016). In the following section, we outline the selected procedures to circumvent these pitfalls in our model.

    METHODOLOGY

    This study employs an observational research design, using a qualitative approach to benchmark integrity risk management initiatives (Bryman et al., 2021). The integrity risks are defined as the “likelihood of an instance of corruption, fraud, irregularity, or ethical misconduct occurring, potentially hindering the attainment of institutional goals,” as outlined in Article 2, Section II of Decree 10.756 (2021). Data were gathered from primary sources (expert panels) and secondary sources (official documents) and then analyzed using content analysis (Blaikie, 2000). The methodological procedures were structured into five stages: 1) identifying (integrity risk events in the procurement process); 2) mapping (risk treatment measures for integrity); 3) prototyping (construction of the maturity model); 4) implementing (model’s validation at four Brazilian public entities of all federative levels); 5) reviewing (model’s adjustment).

    Figure 1
    Research Stages

    A desk review was conducted in the first and second stages to identify integrity risks and their recommended treatments in the public procurement process. Norms, guides, national and international manuals, and integrity program reports from the Brazilian administration were examined. Moreover, a total of184 integrity programs registered in the federal public integrity panel were examined (CGU, 2022). In their reports, keywords such as “licitaç,” “contrat,” and “compra” were searched to identify integrity risk events in the public procurement process. These data enabled us to classify these programs according to their approach to managing integrity risks in public procurement. These preliminary findings provided insights into the issue. In the second stage, the document analysis was expanded to identify treatments for these integrity risk events.

    In the third stage, for each evaluation item, four maturity levels were established: basic (1), limited (2), sustained (3), and optimized (4). Evaluation questions were categorized based on their relevance to different stages of the procurement process, considering planning (procurement planning and enactment), tendering (bidding, award, and contracting process), and management (contract management and evaluation). Each evaluation question is assessed using four statements for classification purposes. Therefore, each statement corresponds to a different treatment for an integrity risk in a public procurement process.

    In the fourth stage, the model’s prototype was submitted to four public entities expert panels (two ministries, one state secretary, and one municipality secretary). The sample was selected for convenience but represents public entities from three regions of Brazil (Southeast, Northeast, and Central west) and from all three government levels (federal, state, and municipal). All meetings were previously scheduled with managers responsible for the 1st, 2nd, and/or 3rd lines of defense in each of the sampled public entities (expert panel technique) (Institute of Internal Auditors [IIA], 2020; Nogueira & Fuscaldi, 2018).

    The maturity model was sent in advance to the participants. All meetings were recorded and preceded by documentary research on the organization’s website for a preliminary assessment. The preliminary model consisted of 30 assessment questions, which were classified into 03 stages (planning, tendering, and management) and thematically arranged into 9 topics (integrity unit, supplier selection, procurement agents, accountability, conduct of agents, procurement oversight, monitoring, participation, and transparency).     In the fifth and sixth stages, the maturity model was fully revised according to the public agencies’ inputs. Then, evaluation questions were properly adjusted. Additionally, the model’s limitations and potentialities were documented, assessing its contribution to improving the integrity of the public procurement processes. Summary charts of risk events, treatment insights, field observation notes (on the model and evaluation results), and assessment results are available for consultation in the GitHub repository (Paiva & Vieira, 2024).

    RESULTS

    A preliminary classification of federal public integrity programs was conducted based on the level of their integrity risk management systems for public procurement (phase 1). Based on data obtained from the Public Integrity Panel, it was possible to classify the 187 programs into 5 levels (Painel de integridade pública, 2022). It is shown that approximately 85% of these programs lack a risk management system, according to ISO or COSO standards. Furthermore, approximately 42% did not identify any integrity risks associated with the public procurement process. This highlights that most federal agencies lack adequate measures in their Integrity Programs to address integrity risks in public procurement.

    Following a preliminary validation process with public agencies, the Maturity Model for Evaluating Integrity Management in Public Procurement has been refined to encompass 27 assessment questions. According to the expert panel, assessment questions 22, 23, and 24 are deemed unsuitable for a practical maturity model. Furthermore, the order of the remaining questions has been adjusted to streamline the administration of the questionnaire. The fully revised model is available from the GitHub repository (Paiva & Vieira, 2024). In summary, a feasible maturity model for managing integrity risks in the public procurement process was developed and preliminarily validated in different Brazilian public entities. This preliminary validation process enabled its revision and underscored its value as a tool for learning and managerial enhancement.

    CONCLUSION

    The study delivers the first maturity model for managing integrity risks in the public procurement process, tailored for Brazilian public entities operating under the new public procurement law. Informed by national and international best practices, this model fully aligns with the Brazilian legal framework. Initial validation findings demonstrate its effectiveness as a managerial instrument, providing significant insights for public managers, practical utility in disseminating best practices, bolstering learning initiatives, and improving management procedures.

    Our findings highlight the maturity model’s potential for public entities. However, further development is needed to ensure broader adoption and successful implementation. A comprehensive validation process involving a diverse range of experts and public entities is crucial to enhance adaptability. Additionally, developing a technical manual that clearly explains the underlying concepts and theories will be essential, particularly for municipalities and states with limited administrative capacity. While broad integrity risk mitigation processes may offer significant benefits, a cost-benefit analysis should be conducted to determine their suitability for each kind of public organization.

    Future studies could enhance the validation process and further refine the evaluation questions, thereby improving the maturity model’s theoretical coherence and managerial utility for public entities. These efforts represent a crucial step toward preserving public value in public procurement, a fundamental aspect of upholding integrity within the Brazilian public administration.
