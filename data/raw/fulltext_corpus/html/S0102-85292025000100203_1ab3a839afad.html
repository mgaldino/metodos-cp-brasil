The Capability Approach: Multidimensional Poverty, Global Justice, and Global South Perspectives

A abordagem das capacidades: pobreza multidimensional, justiça global e perspectivas do Sul Global

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    The present work aims to analyse the capability approach, in the terms established by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum, and its consequences in studying poverty. The theoretical assumptions articulated by these authors not only show the complexity of that multidimensional phenomenon but guide the contours of what should be considered essential for a dignified life. Thus, the capability approach presents itself as the basis of a global understanding of justice. However, such a generalising claim invariably ignores nuances and particularities not properly valued a priori. In this sense, it is necessary to reveal neglected structural aspects that are decisive for the perpetuation of poverty and inequalities in those places where they are more forceful. To this end, peripheral perspectives will be highlighted as an important tool in identifying the limits of the capability approach as the basis of a conception of global justice.

    Keywords
    multidimensional poverty; capability approach; human rights; global justice; Global South

    Resumo

    O presente trabalho tem como objetivo analisar a abordagem das capacidades, nos termos estabelecidos por Amartya Sen e Martha Nussbaum, e suas consequências para o estudo da pobreza. As premissas teóricas articuladas por esses autores não apenas revelam a complexidade desse fenômeno multidimensional, mas também orientam os contornos do que deve ser considerado essencial para uma vida digna. Dessa forma, a abordagem das capacidades se apresenta como base para uma compreensão global da justiça. No entanto, tal afirmação generalizante invariavelmente ignora nuances e particularidades que não são devidamente valorizadas a priori. Nesse sentido, é necessário revelar aspectos estruturais negligenciados, que são decisivos para a perpetuação da pobreza e das desigualdades nos locais onde são mais marcantes. Para isso, perspectivas periféricas serão destacadas como uma ferramenta importante na identificação dos limites da abordagem das capacidades como base para uma concepção de justiça global.

    Palavras-chave
    pobreza multidimensional; abordagem das capacidades; direitos humanos; justiça global; Sul Global

    Introduction

    The capability approach, formulated by Amartya Sen (1999) and Martha Nussbaum (2000), has been internationally recognised for its broad theoretical and practical reach, inspiring debates ranging from justice theory to the conception of development, and influencing the agendas and strategies of state governments and international organisations. Its relevance also encompasses discussions on addressing poverty as a decisive factor in the deprivation of human rights.

    It is within this context that the present work is developed. The aim is, on the one hand, to highlight the contribution of this theoretical perspective in understanding poverty as a multidimensional phenomenon and its direct relationship with challenges to ensure a dignified life. On the other hand, the goal is to identify the limitations of that approach as it is considered a fertile basis for a global justice theory.

    Firstly, the foundational theoretical premises of Sen and Nussbaum regarding capabilities will be presented along with their contribution to the comprehension of poverty, considering its entire complexity. Secondly, reflections will be brought forth to question what is silent and omitted within this perspective. In this sense, the marginalisation of the role of dynamics between the Global North and South,1 both as a cause and a perpetuating element of poverty in peripheral countries, emerges as a flaw in the efforts to generalise the capability approach as the sole foundation of a global justice theory. The objective, therefore, is not to invalidate the assumptions of the capability approach but to emphasise the indispensability of a plurality of perspectives, especially those marginalised and directly affected, in addressing complex problems.

    Multidimensional poverty and the capability approach

    The theoretical framework that understands poverty as a deprivation of capabilities is propelled by the economist Amartya Sen in the elaboration of his analysis of human development. Sen’s capability approach focuses on the freedoms of being and doing that individuals possess and the opportunities they can access in their daily lives. In this sense, poverty can be understood as a deprivation of capabilities, as it hinders the possibility of individuals’ development according to their freedoms, thereby representing an obstacle to the potential flourishing of human capabilities. Therefore, the study of poverty requires an understanding of the deprivation of life that people can actually lead and the freedoms they actually have (Sen 1999).

    Complementing the one-dimensional conceptions of poverty, this perspective introduced by Sen focuses on the features of contingency and heterogeneity of poverty. While one-sided and objective conceptions view poverty solely as income deprivation and rely on resource distribution for its resolution, the capability approach analyses the entire spectrum of deprivations to which a poor person is subjected, understanding the multidimensional aspect of this phenomenon. Through this, it becomes possible to contextualise the understanding of poverty based on the reality of socially constructed power structures.

    In this context, the capability approach has gained prominence as a theoretical and practical tool of great value for the promotion of dignity and human development. By proposing an alternative way of understanding justice, this theory is based on the real living conditions of each individual and the substantive freedoms that each can effectively enjoy.

    The distinctive focus that this approach brings to justice theories is to start from the diagnosis of the existence of various injustices in the world and direct its attention to improving justice and removing injustice, instead of seeking perfect justice or perfectly just institutions. (Santos 2018: 23)

    Indeed, to question the limits of the capability approach, it is important to clarify the key concepts that surround it. In the same perspective as economist Amartya Sen, theorist Martha Nussbaum is also a leading figure in the development of this theory. With distinct purposes2 but starting from the same inquiry—focusing on what each individual is effectively capable of being and doing in a given context—the authors’ vision of human development is grounded in the definition of two central concepts: capabilities and functionings.

    In this sense, capabilities are the realms of substantive freedoms that each person possesses, enabling them the opportunities to live a life that they value and have reason to value. Functionings, in turn, are the states of accomplishment of capabilities, while capabilities correspond to opportunities for choice—the zones of freedom for each person that allow them to achieve specific vectors of functioning (Sen 1992, 1999; Nussbaum 2011).

    Within this perspective, Martha Nussbaum begins to advocate for the use of the concept of combined capabilities. According to Nussbaum (2000), the capabilities mentioned by Sen are better understood when seen as the combination of personal abilities with the social, economic, and political context at hand. Thus, an individual only has their areas of freedom protected if they possess, in addition to the potential for developing their internal capabilities, the viability of the effective exercise of these freedoms in a given environment. In this sense:

    To secure a capability to a person, it is not sufficient to produce good internal states of readiness to act. It is also necessary to prepare the material and institutional environment so that people are actually able to function. (Nussbaum 2000: 235)

    From this, Nussbaum argues that her capability approach is built upon a conception that views each person as an end in themselves. She thus emphasises that the well-being of individuals should not be observed through a general levelling of the population but rather through the observation of the reality of each member of society and the extent to which each one can exercise their freedoms and develop them into functionings that she deems valuable.

    It should be noted that, despite the focus on individual freedoms invoking occasional criticisms that link the approach to an essentially individualistic perspective, the core of the theory rests on the notion of expanding social opportunities as a way to enable human development and increase freedoms. In this sense, the emphasis on what each individual is effectively capable of being and doing in a given situation includes considerations about the context in which the individual is placed—and consequently, about the social relationships established with their surroundings that facilitate or hinder the achievement of capabilities. This notion is even invoked by Nussbaum in the construction of the concept of combined capabilities. Similarly, Drèzes and Sen observe:

    The crucial role of social opportunities is to expand the realm of human agency and freedom, both as an end in itself and as a means of further expansion of freedom. The word ‘social’ in the expression ‘social opportunity’ [...] is a useful reminder not to view individuals and their opportunities in isolated terms. The options that a person has depend greatly on relations with others and on what the state and other institutions do. We shall be particularly concerned with those opportunities that are strongly influenced by social circumstances and public policy [...]. (Drèze and Sen 2002: 06)

    The use of the concepts of ‘capabilities’ and ‘functionings’ aims to enable a contextualised analysis of the freedoms and options for accessing opportunities that each individual possesses—making them aware of the patterns of discrimination and oppression that organise reality and how such patterns affect the possibilities of being and existing for an individual. This is why the capability approach has gained international relevance and recognition in addressing issues related to injustices present in the world today. Nevertheless, it is necessary to grasp more than just the situations of discrimination themselves, including ‘the systemic or structural basis that produces them’ (Gallardo 2010: 68). These are the result of socio-historical production. In other words, it is an expression of a confrontation of social forces that involves the defence or change of certain systems of power’ (Gallardo 2010: 69).

    The mentioned approach departs from the premise that it is possible to identify, a priori, the capabilities necessary for a dignified life, aiming to give concreteness to the theoretical assumptions. Nussbaum argues that the promotion of substantive freedoms and the power of self-determination of individuals is of central importance and translates these concepts into the principle of each person’s capability—which is based on another principle: each person is an end, not merely a means to the will of others. Therefore, human dignity plays a central role in the construction of what the author refers to as a partial theory of justice, guided by pluralistic values and political objectives.

    Within her conception, each species has a way of living that provides it with appropriate dignity, and in the case of women and men, a truly human life would correspond to one that unfolds in conditions of dignity that enable the flourishing of a range of capabilities. For a person to have their human dignity respected, they need to have the opportunity to develop areas of freedom that are considered essential (Nussbaum 2011).

    In listing the capabilities that should integrate her conception, Martha Nussbaum (2000, 2011) questions what a life deserving of human dignity require. To answer this question, the author elaborates a list3 of ten capabilities that must be ensured at a minimum level, namely: (1) life, (2) bodily health, (3) bodily integrity, (4) senses, imagination, and thought, (5) emotions, (6) practical reason, (7) affiliation, (8) relation with other species, (9) leisure, and (10) control over the environment—both political and material.4

    Within this perspective of human dignity, the author begins to construct her ‘partial theory of justice.’ She argues that ensuring a minimum level of each of the ten combined capabilities to all individuals is a condition for achieving social justice. It should then allow humans to develop their abilities and, more than that, exercise them in the context in which they are inserted so that they can achieve the set of functionings they desire within the parameters of human dignity. At this point, the State is seen as the primary responsible for promoting capabilities and has the duty to ensure that all citizens live properly human lives—meaning lives consistent with the respect for their dignity.

    The ten capabilities, then, are goals that fulfil or correspond to people’s prepolitical entitlements: thus we say of people that they are entitled to the ten capabilities on the list. In the context of a nation, it then becomes the job of government to secure them, if that government is to be even minimally just. In effect, then, the presence of entitlements gives governments a job to do, and a central job of government will be to secure the capabilities to people. (Nussbaum 2011: 169)

    The problem, in this sense, is that by assigning centrality to the state apparatus, Nussbaum neglects the true agency of the individual, who is relegated to the role of a ‘client’ in such a way that their local reality is transcended and conceived by the state itself. The individual ends up ‘managed and controlled, compelled to manoeuvre within the limits set by the institutions’ (Escobar 1988: 435). In the words of Grosfoguel (2008: 133), the ‘rational planning and control of the nation-state contribute to the developmentalist illusion of eliminating inequalities within the capitalist world-system at the level of the nation-state.’

    Nussbaum (2011) argues that each nation should have as a legitimate political goal the promotion of the zones of freedom of individuals in the ten highlighted areas—so that this goal is not interpreted as functionalities in themselves, but rather from the notion of promoting the possibility of access to opportunities—that is, capabilities. On the other hand, the author does not limit herself to defining standards of social justice within the internal scope of states. More than that, in her work ‘Frontiers of Justice,’ Nussbaum aims to establish parameters for international justice. In this regard, the author concludes that: ‘there is no barrier of principle or argument against pursuing the central human capabilities as goals for every nation, and also for international society’ (Nussbaum 2006: 305). Thus, the discussion of the possibilities of using the capability approach for the formulation of political principles guiding international justice is brought to the fore.

    Considering the debates on global justice outlined by Nussbaum, this paper aims to unveil some omissions that limit the approach to materialise real opportunities for the exercise of combined capabilities. This is because, as analysed more closely in the second section, the ideas developed within the author’s partial theory of justice may end up being silent about international power dynamics and the socio-historical structural elements that perpetuate global inequality.

    It is noted that Nussbaum’s theoretical construction of capabilities can find correspondence in the very concept of human rights. Despite making some reservations, Martha Nussbaum (2011) acknowledges the relevance and determines the proximity of the human rights movement to the capability approach, emphasizing that human rights—much like the human development advocated by capabilities—are allies of the weakest, serving as tools to advocate for the guarantee of dignity.

    Similarly, the author argues that the list of capabilities outlined maintains a close content relationship with the fundamental rights safeguarded by international declarations of human rights. According to her, both movements ‘emphasises the idea of a fundamental entitlement grounded in the notion of basic justice. It reminds us that people have justified and urgent claims to certain types of treatment, no matter what the world around them has done about that’ (Nussbaum 2011: 68).

    The facets of similarity between the two approaches can be highlighted when human rights are understood as combined capabilities. This is because, for these rights to be guaranteed—much like Nussbaum’s combined capabilities—it is necessary not only to formally recognise them but also to provide real opportunities for their exercise under the factual conditions in which citizens find themselves (Nussbaum 2000). Thus, by substantiating living conditions contrary to human dignity, poverty, understood as a deprivation of combined capabilities, can also be analysed as a violation of human rights.

    It is from the relationship between human rights and capabilities that it becomes possible to analyse poverty as a phenomenon of deprivation of substantive freedoms and, thus, as an obstacle to the materialisation of parameters for social justice. It is worth noting that the constructed analysis of poverty as a hindrance to the enjoyment of fundamental freedoms allows for its understanding as a heterogeneous, multidimensional, and complex phenomenon. This is because the extent of deprivation to which a poor person is subjected is inherently variable due to their personal characteristics, diversities in the physical environment, social conditions, means of distribution within the family, and the different relational perspectives of the community in which the individual is embedded (Sen 1999). It will also vary depending on the accumulation of corrosive disadvantages to which an individual may be subjected (Wolff and De-Shalit 2007).

    Corrosive disadvantages are those whose incidence leads to other types of deprivation, so that the life of an individual with a corrosive disadvantage is impaired in other facets of capability, generating an accumulation of negative effects on their development. Fertile functionings, on the other hand, are the opposite: they refer to those characteristics whose development positively influences various areas of life, leading to the flourishing of different capabilities (Wolff and De-Shalit 2007).

    A significant example of a corrosive disadvantage is precisely poverty. Analysed through the capability approach, poverty reveals itself as a complex phenomenon and, as previously mentioned, inherently multidimensional. This is because, represented by the deprivation of capabilities, it can be understood as a factor that hinders the development of internal capabilities and the exercise of combined capabilities. It is possible, then, to be framed as a corrosive disadvantage, given that the situation of deprivation to which individuals are subjected radiates negative effects of insecurity, instability, and exposure to risks in various areas of their experiences. Furthermore, poverty, like many corrosive disadvantages in the terms advocated by the authors, is characterised by a dynamic and intergenerational nature.

    Regarding the aspect of dynamism, it is observed, once again, that poverty manifests itself in variable patterns and forms, depending on the context to be analysed—so that the deprivation extends across multiple dimensions of life. For this reason, unidimensional and financial approaches to poverty prove insufficient: in addition to not considering the different forms of deprivation, these approaches ignore the problem of converting income into capabilities and overlook the entire discriminatory power distribution structure in contemporary society.

    Indeed, the capability approach has the merit of deconstructing conceptions of poverty that rely solely on income and wealth variables. It also distances itself from those understandings that tend to render poor people invisible, treating the elimination of poverty as merely a humanitarian issue and a charitable obligation. Overcoming such simplistic notions is essential to rejecting common arguments that blame the poor for their own conditions of deprivation of capabilities to which they are subjected. Instead, it is recognised that ‘[t]he poor reveal the weaknesses and inconsistencies of our democratic system. Ignored and underestimated, they are living proof of the disregard for indivisible human dignity’ (Pettiti and Meyer-Bisch 2003: 209).

    Thus, the use of the capability approach, especially in the framework provided by Nussbaum in her partial theory of social justice, contributes to shifting the perception of poverty from being seen solely as an economic and charitable issue to being effectively analysed as a demand for social justice. This is because it becomes apparent that the freedoms violated by conditions of poverty are so essential that they are considered central to the very possibility of living a dignified human life. Nussbaum’s experience is applied in tackling ambiguous cases, i.e. situations where the elimination of the violation of essential capabilities has not yet been recognised as a legitimate political objective and a prerequisite for achieving justice. According to the author: ‘What must happen here is that the debate must take place, and each must make arguments attempting to show that a given liberty is implicated in the idea of human dignity’ (Nussbaum 2011: 32).

    As Sen (1999) aptly outlines, the focus on the distribution of substantive freedoms—rather than the distribution of income—elevates the scientific discourse to a crucial question for inequality thinking: the disadvantages that may exist in converting income into capabilities. This factor is central to the analysis of poverty, as it adds a new perspective to the unilateral view, enabling the study of personal circumstances that may be contributing to the perpetuation of this phenomenon of deprivation.5

    The multidimensionality and dynamism of poverty also stand out in the understanding of the United Nations High Commissioner for Human Rights, according to whom:

    For instance, people with different biological characteristics may require different amounts of food and health care in order to acquire the same degree of freedom to live a healthy life. Similarly, people living in different cultural environments might feel that they need different amounts of clothing in order to have the capability to be clothed at a minimally acceptable level. In other words, the degree of command over resources that may be adequate for one person may not be adequate for another. It would, therefore, be a mistake to define and measure poverty in terms of a uniformly low level of command over economic resources, when the fundamental concern is with a person’s capabilities. (OHCHR 2002: 8-9)

    At this point, the importance of freedom is emphasised through different perspectives: (1) the number of existing options; (2) the number of options effectively accessible to the individual; (3) the attractiveness of the presented options. From this, it is understood that poverty imposes material limits on the human development of poor individuals, causing a significant part of their choices—and the possibility of defining which functionings they wish to achieve—to be merely a simulacrum of the exercise of substantive freedoms to which they are entitled, given that ‘[t]he various liberties of choice have material preconditions’ (Nussbaum 2000: 227) that are not fully met in the degrading conditions of poverty.

    From this, it can be understood that poverty manifests as a form of oppression, as it leads vulnerable individuals to accept development conditions below the standards of human dignity and nullifies the bargaining power of the disadvantaged party (Fleurbaey 2007). By depriving individuals of a development opportunity that would be more beneficial to them, poverty violates integrity. By placing them at the mercy of the wills and choices of others, it affects not only the principle of each person’s capability but also the principle of each person as an end in themselves.

    The deprivation of capabilities caused by poverty represents the existence of living conditions that do not align with human dignity, making it plausible to frame it as a social justice issue. Thus, it can be argued that the essential freedoms attacked by poverty and the concept of human dignity are inseparable, just as the removal of undignified conditions of poverty is interconnected with the demand for social justice.

    The dilemma, therefore, lies not in the theoretical coherence or positive implications of the capability approach for understanding poverty in a multidimensional way. What is intended to be questioned is that, despite its merits, when adopted as a reference in global development efforts, international institutions contribute to the perpetuation of unequal structures supported by what the approach is silent about and conceals. In this sense, the next section of the article is constructed to deepen a critical perspective of the capability approach.

    The capability approach, global justice, and perspectives from the Global South

    The approach presented in the previous section, relevant to understanding poverty as a violation of human rights and a matter of social justice, carries aspirations that extend beyond state borders, seeking to uphold the ‘extending justice to all world citizens, showing theoretically how we might realise a world that is just as a whole’ (Nussbaum 2006: 18). Consequently, Nussbaum argues for the need to address issues of justice arising from inequalities between countries without, however, acknowledging the fact that they are embedded in a global economic dynamic that reinforces and deepens such disparities. The question that arises in this scenario is: to what extent does the relative silence regarding international power dynamics contribute to the neglect of the needs of ‘peripheral’ countries or to frame them as a matter of charity rather than basic justice?

    Indeed, the theoretical effort that follows is valid and relevant insofar as it seeks to unveil omissions that limit Nussbaum’s approach in achieving its inherent goal: to go beyond formal recognition, conceiving real opportunities for the exercise of combined capabilities, considering the actual conditions in which people find themselves. Nussbaum recognises the fragility and inadequacy of prevailing arrangements, rooted in the practices of ‘rich’ countries and international law itself, which restrict the control of the current economic system to a small group of states but impact the international society as a whole. In her words, ‘[w]e live in a world in which it is simply not true that cooperating with others on fair terms will be advantageous to all’ (Nussbaum 2006: 273). For the author, the capability approach provides a more compatible instrument within the complexities of the problems at hand.

    The capabilities approach is an outcome-oriented approach that supplies a partial account of basic social justice. In other words, it says that a world in which people have all the capabilities on the list is a minimally just and decent world. Domestically, it holds that one central purpose of social cooperation is to establish principles and institutions that guarantee that all human beings have the capabilities on the list or can effectively claim them if they do not. It thus has a close relationship to institutional and constitutional design. (Nussbaum 2006: 274)

    The problem, however, is that in the world-system we live in, to use the expression coined by Immanuel Wallerstein (2004), even if it is possible for a peripheral state to undergo transformations in its form of incorporation into the global economy, some of them even moving to a semi-peripheral position, ‘breaking with or transforming the entire system at the level of the nation-state is completely beyond the range of their possibilities’ (Grosfoguel 2008: 133). The consideration proposed in the face of this generalisation applied to international society in all its complexity and diversity is that, although it recognises the structural foundations of existing challenges, it does not reveal the essence of the imbalance between countries. By only marginally addressing crucial issues that, on the one hand, constitute the historical causes of poverty in countries of the Global South and, on the other hand, perpetuate social injustices to the people of these states, such an approach proves insufficient to guide genuinely transformative policies that ensure a dignified life for all, as its author envisioned.

    Nussbaum argues that there is a collective obligation to ensure what is due to all individuals. Nevertheless, even before resolving the issue of assigning responsibility for these duties, it would be possible to delineate the rights that everyone in the world should have, conferred by the notion of dignity inherent to each human being. This involves a critical examination of the global economy, a possible redistribution of wealth, as well as other social and economic rights. It is possible, as seen in the previous section, following the capability approach, to identify a set of rights without which life would not be compatible with the concept of human dignity.

    Such delineation is based on what Nussbaum calls ‘reflective intuition,’ moving away from a foundation in existing preferences, as these are malleable and adaptable to expectations and possibilities. In other words, the definition of the essential capabilities necessary for a dignified life is not inspired by a recognition of preferences within a particular society, especially since such preferences can be conditioned by the social dynamics in which those individuals are situated. ‘So far, things are very definite, albeit at a high level of abstraction and generality: the idea of what human beings need for fully human living is among the most vivid intuitive ideas we share’ (Nussbaum 2006: 279).

    Martti Koskenniemi (2003) problematises the very concept of global justice based on the inherent omission in its abstract delimitation. Although the idea of justice is unquestionably desirable as a universal goal, once its content begins to be defined, disagreements will emerge as the distributive preferences of international actors diverge. In order to remain as a purpose shared by all, the conception of ‘justice’ (as well as ‘peace’ or ‘security’) has to remain abstract enough to cover different political choices and distributive priorities. ‘And because those preferences differ, [such conceptions] can only either remain controversial or be formulated in such broad terms as to contain the controversy within itself-in which case it is hard to see how it could be used to resolve it’ (Koskenniemi 2003: 90).

    The extent to which these abstract discussions on global justice contribute to identifying effective mechanisms to combat poverty must be questioned here. Herrera Flores, in his critical theory of human rights, clarifies the importance of working with the ‘category of self-imposed duties in social struggles for dignity, and not with abstract rights or passive duties imposed from outside our struggles and commitments,’ as this is the only way to enable ‘equal access to the necessary goods for a dignified life’ (Herrera Flores 2009: 21).

    Furthermore, as human rights linked to capabilities are viewed by Nussbaum as pre-political, any country that does not recognise or ensure them to its population can be considered an unjust state. With that said, she infers that the capability approach, like human rights, would be state-centred, serving as a benchmark for internal social justice within that society. Thus, Nussbaum (2006) presupposes what Gallardo (2010: 163) asserts to be an objective order of the world that contains obligations. It focuses on the subjection to a ‘natural’ order at the expense of people’s capacity to decide for themselves regarding the demands of these systems. Nussbaum acknowledges this aspect of her approach, stating that it is a version of the old perspective of natural law, where requirements at the global level are moral requirements, not fully encompassed by any set of coercive political structures (Nussbaum 2006: 315).

    The reflective scrutiny proposed in this work aims to question ‘universality accorded to moral and legal perspectives which reflect and reproduce the power relations characteristic of the colonial encounter, and which are thus far from being universal’ (Seth 2011: 168). According to Sanjay Seth, the appropriate approach would need to be post-colonial, as it highlights the centrality of colonialism in contemporary reality.

    The ‘post’ in postcolonialism, let it be noted, is not a periodisation that signals the beginning of an era where colonialism is part of the past; on the contrary, it signifies the claim that conquest, colonialism and empire are not a footnote or episode in a larger story, such as that of capitalism, modernity or the expansion of international society, but are in fact a central part of that story and are constitutive of it. The ‘post’ does not mark the period after the colonial era, but rather the effects of this era in shaping the world that is ours. (Seth 2011: 174)

    There is indeed a paradox in the demand for universality that tends to reproduce an imperialistic impulse, as Sundhya Pahuja explains. The formation of the identity of the ‘West’ depends on the construction of the ‘other,’ the stranger. The constructed oppositions are evident, taking on different forms throughout history: the civilised and the barbarian or savage, the First World and the Third World, developed countries and developing countries. However, in the face of the moral universal claims of countries from the Global North, the exclusion of these ‘others’ has become inherently unethical. This implies the need to include the ‘others.’ Consequently, the peoples of the Global South have found themselves subjected to this process of exclusion, being different from the European and, at the same time, compelled to become equals. This dynamic encompasses both the ‘liberating promise’ and the ‘imperialist threat’ of universalist claims (Pahuja 2005).

    The perspective of those who are truly in peripheral and marginalised spaces is disregarded, remaining indifferent to the construction of globalised precepts. ‘[T]he conquered and dominated peoples were placed in a natural position of inferiority, and consequently, so were their phenotypic traits, as well as their mental and cultural discoveries’ (Quijano 2000: 2003). Mbembe (2006) demonstrates that post-colonial thought is responsible for highlighting the issue of humanity in formation—arguing that the colonised person is a living individual whose identity emerges from the tripartite movement of violation, erasure, and self-rewriting. According to the author, the political force of post-colonial thought arises from its involvement in the historical social struggles of colonised societies. In this sense, the author observes that post-colonialism aims to pave the way for an inquiry into the possibility of a politics of the similar, whose prerequisite is the recognition of the ‘other’ and their difference.

    In this context, Kohn (2019) asserts that contemporary global justice theories are built on questioning what ‘we owe to distant others,’ when the starting point should be ‘what others (wealthy countries) owe to us’ (Kohn 2019: 175). It is not about ‘speaking for others but listening to others’ (Kohn 2019: 181). This does not imply that the sensitivity of scholars or citizens from the Global South qualifies them as the sole individuals capable of accessing the truth or unveiling dominant ideology. The argument revolves around the fact that political and normative issues are extremely complex, and thus, the best explanation for these phenomena needs to be based on multiple perspectives. As readings from ‘dominant actors’—including researchers and scholars from the Global North—tend to obscure alternative explanations and views, perspectives from peripheral countries need to be highlighted.

    The capability approach aims to serve ‘the goals of the international community as a whole, of humanity as a whole’ (Nussbaum 2006: 291). However, in his critiques of global justice theories, Kohn (2019) insists that normative and socio-political issues be primarily appreciated by people who are not in a position of privilege—not because it means they will have a more accurate understanding or even contribute to progress in terms of justice, but because their perspectives tend to be ignored or disregarded due to established power relations. The goal is to combat narratives that are common in global justice analyses that reaffirm roles of ‘victims’ and ‘saviours,’ dehumanising the poorest, obscuring their autonomy, and legitimising international institutions (Kohn 2019: 164).

    It is important, at this point, to revisit the question of the allocation of responsibility for ensuring capabilities. Although, in Nussbaum’s view, it is a shared duty, institutions hold special significance. According to the author, it is possible to identify four reasons justifying this institutional centrality. First, since what is at stake are dilemmas of collective action, individualised attempts to solve them based on particular readings would not only be chaotic but also ineffective. Second, institutions would be better equipped to deal with problems more equitably regarding the distribution of burdens, limiting the possibility of ‘free-riding behavior.’ The third point refers to institutional capacity, both cognitive and causal, which generally surpasses any individual potential. The fourth issue raised involves the personal aspects of individuals, seen as obstacles to effective action in achieving capabilities promptly for the entire society. ‘Institutions impose on all, in a suitable fair way, the responsibility to support the capabilities of all, up to a minimum threshold’ (Nussbaum 2006: 310).

    This argument ‘legitimizes global institutions by depicting them as instruments of global justice and overlooking the way they have secured rather than dismantled global inequalities’ (Kohn 2019: 164). It is necessary, therefore, to unveil the historical role of these institutions, which act more as instruments to reaffirm established power dynamics internationally than as engines of transformation and capacity assurance for the peoples of peripheral countries. And there is nothing to indicate that they will take on this role in the near future. The example of the League of Nations is illustrative.

    The League is usually studied with a focus on its failed attempts to prevent international aggression. However, for non-European countries, the organisation’s effort to conceive dynamics and procedures specifically aimed at transforming non-European societies into modern societies is certainly more relevant. The first major international institution, it should be noted, was dedicated to creating sovereignties and promoting self-government through legal, administrative, and institutional mechanisms, with peripheral countries as its focus, while Western states remained immune to international scrutiny from the League. This allowed the development of management and control mechanisms that remain ingrained in the modus operandi of international institutions. ‘In essence, while the Mandate System worked towards the creation of sovereign states, or at least politically developed, ‘self-governing’ societies, both the ‘sovereignty’ and ‘government’ of the non-European society were created with a view to furthering Western interests’ (Anghie 2006: 747).

    Anghie demonstrates that countries in the Global South adopted development and progress models that were first articulated in the League of Nations Mandate system and later refined by development theories. International financial institutions, such as the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank, played an intrusive role in the economies of these countries and used their authority to reform the political and social structures of these nations under the pretext of promoting ‘good governance.’

    The virtues of good governance are apparently self-evident. But the meaning of the terms remains open and contestable and these institutions attempted to use an amended version of human rights law to further their neoliberal policies in the guise of ‘good governance’, rather than enabling real empowerment of Third World citizens. (Anghie 2006: 749)

    In this vein, Arturo Escobar (1998) argues that the discourse of ‘development,’ as a way of thinking and a source of practices, placed poor countries at the centre of various programs and interventions presented as indispensable. These were led by experts and implemented by global institutions created for this purpose, maintaining control over the imposed dynamics and their meanings. Consequently, in Latin America, after 1950, there was a complete reorganisation of state policies to align with the needs of development-oriented countries. ‘The production and circulation of discourses are an integral component of the exercise of power’ (Escobar 1988: 430).

    Nussbaum proposes certain principles regarding the international framework to ensure the prerogatives of all individuals. The author contends that prosperous nations need to commit to significant wealth redistribution, thus having an obligation to donate a substantial portion of their GDP to the poorer states. This narrative not only supports the idealisation of international institutions but also stigmatises peripheral countries, portraying them as victims, needy, incapable, or predatory, while the so-called ‘developed’ countries assume a commendable and deified role as ‘saviours.’

    Therefore, although the capability approach presents itself as an important theoretical alternative for expanding the understanding of poverty in its multidimensionality, its embodiment as a foundational proposal for achieving global justice ends up undermining structural elements that are at the origin and perpetuation of global inequality and the woes of countries in the Global South. Any approach that aims to be transformative in the international society needs to denounce and unveil power structures, not only representing or speaking for the subjugated parties but giving them centrality in the process of deconstructing hierarchised global systems of subjugation.

    It is worth reiterating that this is not a premise of the superiority of readings from the Global South—in other words, the article does not claim that perspectives from the Global South are inherently correct or theoretically superior. Instead, it addresses the necessity of incorporating such viewpoints into debates on global justice and international structures. By reassuring the protagonism of individuals that have been subjected to processes of marginalisation, the article proposes a course that promotes pluralism as a more consistent alternative for addressing complex dilemmas. Otherwise, core aspects of the problems will remain hidden or downplayed in these debates. What is questioned, therefore, is the risk of proximity to views (typically Eurocentric) that ‘the relationships between the components of a social structure are given, ahistorical, that is, they are the product of the actions of some agent prior to the history of relations between people’ (Quijano 2020: 330).

    Is it possible to talk about global justice, poverty alleviation, without emphasising the fact that the exploitation of material resources (wealth) from colonised countries largely facilitated the processes that led to the characterisation of what are now called ‘rich’ and ‘poor’ countries? Or without considering approaches that argue that colonial domination relations have contemporaneously taken on a new imperialistic guise, replacing the use of force with economic strategies that rely on theoretical fallacies and directly impact the consolidation of hunger and poverty in peripheral countries? Or how the presumption of linear evolution of societies, where all must go through the stages of ‘development,’ namely savagery, barbarism, and civilisation, imposes a predetermined and unavoidable path for ‘poor’ countries, conceived from a specific (and unequal) understanding of development? Or even without facing the simple fact that states exist within the structure of an interstate system, and ‘their relative strength is not merely the degree to which they can effectively exercise authority internally but the degree to which they can hold their own in the competitive environment of the world-system?’ (Wallerstein 2004: 55).

    Certainly, the effort to raise these issues does not necessarily invalidate or diminish the value of the capability approach for understanding the challenge of poverty alleviation and as inspiration for the struggle for a dignified life for all. Here, Herrera Flores’s lesson is once again fitting:

    We cannot analyse human rights outside of their Western contexts. However, we must also not forget their enormous capacity to generate hopes in the struggle against the injustices and exploitations that much of humanity faces. It is these struggles that, in reality, allow such a concept to ‘universalize’ as an ethical and legal foundation for all social practices aimed at creating and ensuring useful instruments when it comes to accessing the material and immaterial goods required to live with dignity. (Herrera Flores 2009: 36-37)

    However, problematising the predominant development discourse is not an effort to ‘reach the truth’ but rather a construction of an object of criticism for academic and political debate and action. It is a manifestation of resistance to the pretension of dominant approaches to represent the real, a logical truth, ignoring the perspective that theoretical choices are always ‘a political process with consequences for the real world’ (Escobar 2005: 23). The intention of criticising such a widespread perspective as the capability approach is to reflect on the symbolic role, in maintaining existing power structures, of generalising narratives whose transformative potential dissipates into abstractions that neglect the centrality and unfolding of historical dynamics in the interstate system.

    Conclusion

    From the ideas developed here, we sought to demonstrate, initially, the foundations of the capability approach for understanding the phenomenon of poverty and framing it as an issue of social justice. Building on this, the second section of the article introduced a reflection on the perspectives of the Global South and their contributions to thinking about capabilities and their potential limitations regarding a global justice project.

    It is observed that the capability approach—led by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum—enables the analysis of poverty through a range of interconnected and multidimensional deprivations, emphasising the fundamental importance of freedoms for the existence of a human life with dignity. Thus, following the partial theory of social justice presented, one notices the necessary connection between the freedoms undermined by poverty and the central value of human dignity—which is the core and intersection between capabilities and human rights.

    Understanding the multidimensionality of the phenomenon through the lens of capabilities allows, thus, a distancing from positions that categorise the fight against poverty as a philanthropic or charitable agenda. The theoretical impact of the capability approach enables framing poverty—and the resulting deprivations of freedoms—as a matter of social justice.

    Nevertheless, despite the valuable contributions of the approach to understanding the aforementioned phenomenon and its inherent complexity, Nussbaum’s theory cannot be uncritically embraced, especially when reflecting on justice at the global level. It is essential to consider the structural foundations that unveil the historical causes of poverty in peripheral countries. As proposed, discussions about poverty and global justice should start from a place of listening and with who occupies peripheral and marginalised spaces.

    This article proposes a reflection on the limitations of theories addressing poverty in the context of global justice. Drawing from the arguments developed here, the goal is to present the significance of the capability approach for reflecting on multidimensional poverty while simultaneously questioning to what extent abstract discussions on the matter can effectively contribute to identifying efficient mechanisms to combat poverty and, furthermore, to structuring transformative policies that have an emancipatory focus for citizens of the Global South.

    Those considerations open up space for future developments. Rethinking the approach to capabilities from the Global South implies advancing in defining responsibilities. There is indeed a collective obligation, as Nussbaum (2006) asserts, but responsibility needs to be differentiated and objectively assigned. In a context of historical discrepancy and institutionalised imbalance, these delineations need to be prioritised in any theoretical effort advocating global justice.

    As the author states, the pursuit of human capabilities can be the goal of each state and also of the international society. The problem lies in understanding an international system that is ‘not designed to realize ultimate ends but to coordinate practical action to further the objectives of existing communities’ (Koskenniemi 2003:100). Delegating decision-making on distributive preferences to existing institutions perpetuates inequalities ingrained in the international structure. There are big challenges, moving forward, that must be faced to achieve an approach that aspires to be universal. It is necessary, therefore, to address the matter of global justice through the dilemma of accountability beyond state obligations to their internal community. Future research can be done to figure out in what ways this must be done without supressing historical dynamics that pervades the interstate system. Although this isn’t a small effort, recognising the path is certainly an important first step.
