To leave or stay? Unpacking the aspiration and capability of (im)mobility of young people from Brazil

Sair ou ficar? Desvendando a aspiração e a capacidade de (i)mobilidade dos jovens do Brasil

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Abstract  Following a long period of focus on mobility as a primary research interest, academic attention started to look into immobile populations. This paper explores ethnographically the subject of immobility in a transnational region of Brazil’s countryside with high rates of outmigration. The research mainly reviews how young people are (unadjusting) their aspirations and capacities of (im)mobility. By doing so, the study demonstrates that it is essential to understand why so many people do not migrate and debunks the static notions of migration. The findings highlight that to leave or stay entails different reasons, meanings, and motivations. They also reveal how within a “field of possibilities”, the young people make decisions over time, employing their capabilities to look for alternative routes and destinations, but also adjusting the desirable conditions for (im)mobility. Moreover, the paper provides insights to help the problematizations of the mobility/immobility binary and adds nuance to conceptualizations of classified typologies. The conclusion is that immobility is a meaningful concept for future research in the context of migration studies and that addressing this gap is crucial for developing a holistic understanding of human mobility patterns. Furthermore, the paper also opens research questions for future study.

    Keywords:
    aspirations; capability; mobility; Migration Theory; young people´s

    Resumo

    Resumo  Após um longo período de foco na mobilidade como principal interesse de pesquisa, a atenção acadêmica começou a se voltar para as populações imóveis. Este artigo explora etnograficamente o tema da imobilidade em uma região transnacional do interior do Brasil com altas taxas de emigração. A pesquisa analisa principalmente como os jovens estão (des)ajustando seus desejos e capacidades de (i)mobilidade. Ao fazê-lo, o estudo evidencia que é imprescindível entender por que tantas pessoas não migram e desmascara as noções estáticas de migração. Os achados destacam que partir ou ficar comportam diferentes motivos, significados e revelam como dentro de um “campo de possibilidades”, os jovens tomam decisões ao longo do tempo, empregando suas capacidades para buscar rotas e destinos alternativos, mas também ajustando as condições desejáveis para a (i)mobilidade. O artigo fornece insights para ajudar nas problematizações do binarismo mobilidade/imobilidade e adiciona nuances às conceitualizações de tipologias de classificações. A conclusão é que a imobilidade é um conceito significativo para pesquisas futuras no contexto dos estudos de migração e que abordar essa lacuna é crucial para o desenvolvimento de uma compreensão holística dos padrões de mobilidade humana. Além disso, o artigo abre questões de pesquisa para estudos futuros.

    Palavras-chave:
    aspirações; capacidade; mobilidade; Teoria da Migração; jovens

    1. Introduction

    The subject of immobility has been an often-neglected dimension in migration studies. The simple question animating much of migration research today is, “Why do people migrate?” However, the migration theory will never explain human mobility while maintaining a myopic focus on migration alone (Debray et al., 2022). Therefore, the main argument of the mobilities’ turn’ reflects how the social sciences have not thoroughly dealt with mobility, which also applies to migration studies (Wiegel et al., 2019). Nevertheless, an increasing body of literature addressing immobility is shedding new light on why people do not migrate. The research has been focused on the drivers of immobility (Schewel, 2015), on how people remain in place (Mata-Codesal, 2015), and on the ‘adaptive preferences’ that follow unfulfilled aspirations (Carling and Schewel, 2018).

    Although immobility has gained status as a proper research object, researchers have called for more significant research on immobility. They argue that answering why people stay can enhance our understanding of why people leave (Schewel, 2015). In this vein, Mata-Codesal (2018, p. 2) accurately observes that one of the problems with immobility is that “the label . . . is imposed on heterogeneous situations, obscuring the many different ways of staying put and the wide variety of reasons, degrees of (in)voluntariness and lived experiences of immobility”. Furthermore, while current transnational perspectives on migration studies often emphasize cultural adaptation in destination countries, there has been limited focus on the experiences of those who remain. Few studies explore the actual impacts of varying constellations of (im)mobility on youth experiences. This includes an examination of how contemporary processes of youth mobility are influenced by transnational connections across specific locations, thereby shaping their aspirations, identities, and opportunities (Collins and Carling, 2018).

    Starting from an ethnographic perspective, this paper intends to increase the understanding of the practices and experiences of mobility among young people from a region in which movement is self-evident. Based on the mobilities paradigm, which presents novel viewpoints for examining young people’ engaged in motion (Sheller and Urry, 2006), this article contributes to the field of migration studies by approaching the significance of immobility as both an analytical concept and a lived experience (Salazar, 2021). The analyses review a number of possible explanations for staying, highlighting the often structural factors that constrain or encourage (im)mobility (Schiller and Salazar 2013), the instrumental rationales behind the aspirations to move or to stay put (Haas, 2021), and the changes in aspirations and capabilities of decision-making over time (Rodríguez-Peña, 2023), and the different dynamics of mobility and immobility that are embodied in a transnational place (Salazar, 2021).

    The paper begins by exploring the existing literature on “immobility” to shed light on the importance of this concept in contemporary migration studies, followed by an introduction of research context and methodological aspects. Afterwards, it presents various types of immobility and the influencing factors that have shaped migration aspirations and capabilities among interviewees. It concludes by arguing for the importance of focusing on practices and motives related to (im)mobility in migration research and providing insights for future studies.

    2. Developing a conceptual background approach to immobility

    Although often linked, migration and mobility represent separate concepts, with different historical trajectories, and subject to distinct intellectual discussions. Migration refers to the physical relocation of individuals, driven by factors such as poverty, work opportunities, or the pursuit of improved living conditions (Mejía and Scapin, 2023; Salazar, 2021). Mobility, however, implies a broader view, encompassing not only the movement of people but also the exchange of materials, financial resources, knowledge, ideas, and technologies. It recognizes that such movements are shaped by personal desires, obligations, cultural traditions, and unpredictable life paths, highlighting the balance between movement and immobility in societal contexts. (Mejía and Scapin, 2023; Salazar, 2021).

    This paradigm of mobility moves away from focusing on rigid boundaries and instead highlights the experiences and significance of crossing said boundaries. It shows how the act of moving beyond limits shapes ideas of belonging and exclusion. Rather than placing stability in opposition to mobility, it explores how movement and permanence coexist in social contexts. It underscores that mobility encompasses both physical and social aspects, linking migration to the potential for social mobility (Sheller and Urry, 2006). This view provides a deeper understanding of the complexities of mobility and immobility in contemporary societies, encouraging researchers to consider the power dynamics and diverse meanings attached to migration processes (Schapendonk and Steel, 2014).

    Whilst researching frustrated migration aspirations in Cape Verde, Carling (2002), introduces the idea of distinguishing between ability to migrate and aspirations of migration. This led to the concepts of “involuntary immobility” for those who aspire to migrate but lack the ability to do so, and “voluntary non-migrants”, those who choose not to migrate. However, defining “ability” proved complex, which later led the author to suggest “capability” as a way to broaden the concept of “ability” (Carling and Schewel, 2018). That framework (Figure 1) includes four (im)mobility categories: mobility, voluntary immobility, involuntary immobility, and acquiescent immobility1 (Schewel, 2019).

    Aspiration-capability framework. Source: Schewel, 2019

    However, Berg (2020) argues that an individual’s decision to stay or to leave is influenced by both well-being and place attachments. Attachment is a part of well-being, and consists of four dimensions: social relations, material aspects, past experiences, and emotions. These dimensions are intricately intertwined and challenging to differentiate. Therefore, in an era marked by increased mobility, migration should be viewed as an integral component of the broader rhythms of young peoples’ lives and as a response to people’s aspirations to either sustain or enhance their overall quality of life, including their everyday sense of well-being. In this sense, Hjälm (2014, p. 579), proclaims that the staying “does not occur in isolation but it is connected to other life projects and people”.

    Vezzoli (2023) highlights the importance of considering a range of factors, both economic and non-economic, to understand individuals’ desires to stay and how their past experiences, social connections, and perceptions of time and space shape their desires to migrate. The author introduces the notion of “relative endowment”, which denotes a favourable evaluation of what individuals’ value in their current location, even if they are cognizant of social disparities. This concept entails not only a feeling of contentment but also a sense of advantage in comparison to others, such as migrants.

    In the same direction, Robins (2022) describes how immobility can be motivated by a sense of duty to small-scale imagined social objects such as “family”. Analyzing data collected from residents of São Paulo, Brazil who had decided not to emigrate despite having the capability to do so, he proposes the term “active immobility”. He argues that immobility is motivated by a duty to stay and cannot easily be classified as either voluntary or involuntary. In this regard, Harris and Raffaetà (2021) argue for expanding the mobilities framework to include young people who do not fit traditional migration or immigrant-generation paradigms. They introduce the concept of “sessility” to describe how young people can still exercise agency and engage with global flows despite disruptions to their mobility plans. Derived from plant biology, “sessility” is metaphorically used to challenge the binary understanding of mobility and immobility, suggesting that there is a spectrum of agency and adaptation between those two extremes, and it emphasizes the complexity of challenges and opportunities faced by youth in a mobile world. It underscores the importance of recognizing and valuing agency in situations where physical movement is not possible or chosen.

    According to Ortiga and Macabasag (2021), there is a research gap in understanding the temporal subjectivity of individuals aspiring to migrate or to remaining in their home countries. In the same way, Rodríiguez-Pena (2022), argues that the framework of mobility itself does not explain how people move between these those analytical categories. She also claims that studies on the transition between different states of (im)mobility) remain scarce. This highlights the importance of studying (im)mobility comprehensively, considering the evolution of aspirations of migration and of staying aspirations throughout a young peoples’ life course. Both authors argue that aspirations are shaped by relational practices and influence decision-making processes, ultimately leading to actual migration or immobility outcomes. By emphasizing individual agency, this perspective adds a temporal dimension to the aspiration-/capability model of international migration. However, empirical research using this model has yet to fully explore the role of temporality and the subjective experience of time in explaining the transition between different forms of immobility among migrants (Ortiga and Macabasag, 2021). Thus, further investigation is needed to understand how migrants actively evaluate their immobility, especially when confronted with structural barriers to future emigration.

    Besides that, such as noted by Sheller and Urry (2006), the (im) mobility is differential because people have uneven aspirations and capabilities for (im)mobility practices, which are grounded in both personal and structural factors, such as mobility control, technological, regulatory, and commercial infrastructure, as well as institutions and actors that facilitate and condition mobility (Lin Weiqiang et al., 2017). As a result, the experiences of (im)mobility are highly diverse and uneven, varying in timing, circumstances, and on the nature of the movement itself. These asymmetries reflect and reinforce factors such as personal networks, available resources, and varying levels of external control, and these factorswhich can create disparities in young peoples’ capability to engage in particular forms of movement (Wiegel et al., 2019). Consequently, certain young peoples’ may find it harder to pursue certain moves while others may find it easier. It is important to acknowledge that infrastructures not only provide the physical and organizational frameworks for mobility but they also contribute to the differential experiences of mobility.

    In this regard, Mata-Codesal (2018) claims that the lack of means to migrate internationally has been generating a growing pool of immobilized people in the current mobility regime and, thus, “the age of migration” is simultaneously “the age of involuntary immobility”. In a similar vein, Karell (2022) looking into involuntary immobility, shows that poorer households more often lose the most in financing overseas employment attempts that go awry. He also uses the findings to introduce the term “capability conversion” to specify a dynamic of immobility, or the process by which migrants’ capability to migrate at one point in time “crashes up against unexpected constraints, thereby affecting their future capability to migrate” (Karell, 2022). He argues that “capability conversion helps explain how migratory attempts can lead individuals to become more “stuck” over time and, more generally, helps develop the aspiration/mobility framework for analyzing (im)mobility” (Karell, 2022).

    Additionally, there is a need to pay attention to “the unequal distribution of (im)mobility rights which means that mobility and immobility are—depending on the context—valuable assets that are asymmetrically distributed” (Mata-Codesal, 2015, p. 2277). Furthermore, legal irregularity does not entirely hinder migration but increases the vulnerability of migrants pushed to migrate this way. Hence, restrictive immigration policies are only suitable to discipline an underclass of irregular migrant workers whose physical presence and fundamental rights are denied. In addition, migration regulation causes some migrants to build transnational family lives. In particular, research on global care chains, livelihood approaches, and transnational family analyses have highlighted that the actions of migrants can significantly curtail the mobility prospects of their family members, primarily as a result of altered caregiving responsibilities (Schapendonk and Steel, 2014). Theses research’s highlight how someone’s mobility often depends on someone else’s immobility, and that outcome shows one among many different mobility–immobility articulations (Mata-Codesal, 2015).

    From this perspective, the notion of “motilities” (Kaufmann, 2002) becomes helpful in improving the discussion about migration decisions and capacity and aspiration of (im)mobility, as it broadens the scope of the analysis by encompassing the various factors that influence the practices of young people’. “Motilities” refers to the analysis of the multiple dimensions of movement, including not only physical migration but also the flow of information, ideas, goods and resources. It encompasses the different forms of mobility and immobility that people experience. It is made up of factors relating to access (the conditions under which available options can be used), to skills (required in order to use these options), and to cognitive appropriation (the evaluation of the available options vis‐à‐vis one’s projects). Thus, “motilities” is defined as the field of possible actions around the development of a project, or, in other words, as the way in which an actor builds mobility potential and makes use of it (Flamm and Kaufmann, 2006). This idea helps to understand how (im)mobility could be an additional factor of social inequality and how the (im)mobility capital joins cultural and relational capital in a young person’s decisions to leave or to stay.

    3. Rethinking international migration and (im)mobility: The Governador Valadares region in Brazil as a focal point

    The Governador Valadares Region of Minas Gerais (MGV) in Southeastern Brazil has a long history of emigration, which began in the late 1970s and early 1980s due to economic hardships. This trend has continued over the years, with many young and economically active individuals (between 20-40 years old) from MGV migrating irregularly, often risking their lives on dangerous crossings over the Mexico-United States border. The migration networks in MGV are complex, span over multiple countries, such as the United States and Portugal, and provide various alternatives for young people to move (Siqueira, 2018). Its perpetuation in the region is due to the association of a migratory culture with a globalized migration market that maintains the stimulus to the migratory project (Sousa and Fazito, 2017).

    The presence of a “migration culture” in the region is based primarily on social representations formed through daily interactions among community members, migrants, non-migrants, nationals, and foreigners. These representations involve objects and symbols characteristic of the so-called “American way of life” (Margolis, 1994). Migration culture in areas where migration is viewed as an economically viable and preferable option can significantly shape the values, attitudes, and lifestyles of individuals. In MGV, emigration is deeply entrenched as a primary path to personal success solidified into an implicit societal norm for a substantial segment of the population (Soares, 2002; Silva et al., 2025). International migration is so deeply woven into the fabric of the region that transnational mobility among young people is not merely a possibility, but an expectation. Many young people’ anticipate living and working abroad as an inevitable milestone in their lives (Reis and Machado, 2008).

    The formation of a collective perception which values expectations and life opportunities in American society was facilitated by social networks established by various actors (individuals, families, organizations, and the community itself) in countries of origin and destiny. Thus, by the existence of personal networks and direct access to mechanisms that help carry out the migration project. Fusco (2005) posits that the “cordial capital”, built on relationships of kinship, friendship, and mutual support, is instrumental in understanding the clustering of MGV immigrants in areas like Boston. However, these networks have also facilitated the rise of an organized “illegal migration industry” (Fazito and Soares, 2015).

    The social perception of international migration and its associated opportunities for a desirable way of life have spread over time to neighbouring municipalities, contributing to the construction of a distinct identity and the expansion of the existing migration system. For that reason, several studies have focused on MGV to operationalize concepts such as “migratory culture” or “migratory networks” and to investigate various aspects of migration, including transnational family dynamics (Jorgensen et al, 2019), return migration (Siqueira and Santos, 2012), gender dynamics (Assis and Siqueira, 2009), and remittances and local development (Martes and Soares, 2006).

    At the same time, there is a completely under-researched specific empirical gap in the mobility of young Brazilians from the MGV. Neither the transnational youth mobility, nor the mediation process of the immobility of those who stay hasve been studied. HoweverRegardless, same MGV is still a relevant place to examine the common dichotomous picture of mobility-/immobility in migrant studies. The MGV allows for a the reflection consideration of young people’s (im)mobility through the structures (family, cultural and institutional) that govern living and the way in which the desire, the aspiration, the possibilities and the outcomes of mobility are often differentiated between different social groups and spatial contexts.

    4. Methodology

    This paper is based on a larger ethnographic study investigating international emigration among young people from the MGV region of Brazil. The empirical data was gathered over ten months of fieldwork, from March to December 2022, through participant observation and 14 semi-structured interviews with youth aged 22 to 29 who lived in MGV at the time (Table 1). The biographical nature of the interviews explored their migration preferences, practices, and capacities, alongside factors that either hinder or support their aspirations. A phenomenological approach was used to understand how participants’ lived experiences and perspectives influence their decisions regarding mobility. The biographical approach is proved effective in migration studies because, while focusing on young peoples’ narratives, it does not ignore the role of structural forces (Robins 2022). To capture the nuances of young peoples’ experiences, selected excerpts from the interviews are integrated into the paper as vignettes, emphasizing particular micro-level insights. All interviewees’ names have been replaced with pseudonyms for confidentiality.

    5. Migration aspirations and capabilities: The narratives of the young about international migration.

    In Valadares2 it is usual for people to leave quietly. I know various cases of youths who have left for the United States. For example, I saw my neighbour on the street last week, on Monday. On Thursday, I saw on Facebook he was already living in the US. Sometimes I do not see someone I know for a week, and when I ask about them, they tell me they have gone to “America”. Some of my friends did it and they have moved to the US in the last months. (Fábio, 2022)

    Fabio’s account of people’s practice of leaving the region quietly reflects the idea that mobility can be a common feature of the local culture. Although Fabio recognizes the normality of this practice in MGV, he and three young interviewees say that they do not intend to participate in this movement, despite believing themselves able to do it. That is also the case for Julia, who, in a perspective similar to Fabio´s, sees a “good enough” future in MGV. From their narratives, it is possible to understand how the structure of perceived opportunities depicts individual interactions within fields of production and reproduction, including parenting, life satisfaction, belonging, and economic activity. They do not subscribe to the “American dream”; thus, the capability to remain is seen as an attractive option since they value the cultural (education and skills) and relational (family and friends) capital that can be maintained and improved in Brazil. Look at what they said:

    I do not have that “American dream” that many here hold. It does not make sense for me to leave a place I belong to go to a place that is not mine to have a better life. It does not make sense to leave my country to go to a place that is harsh on people just to make money. Before thinking about leaving the country, I think it is necessary to exhaust all opportunities locally. In Brazil, you also have the opportunity to have a better life. For example, I have a job and an economically satisfying life, and I am close to my family. I do not believe there are better opportunities for me outside (Fabio, 2022).

    I thought about going to the US when I was about eighteen, but then I thought better of it and gave up because I realized that I have more significant opportunities to have a life that makes sense living in my country, close to my family and friends. I looked into the job market for immigrants there and saw that most of the jobs were insecure. My job here allowed me to continue my studies and build a professional career in Brazil. I am taking advantage of the opportunities in my country to create a comfortable life (Julia, 2022).

    The other two youths said they have an aversion to outmigration due to the challenges and risks associated with it. Gabriel, in particular, provides a compelling example. Over two years, his mother, employed as a housekeeper alongside some other family members, diligently worked to support him in enrolling in an English course with the ultimate goal of securing a US visa. However, Gabriel’s decision not to migrate to the US, despite having the ability to move legally (mobility capital), is rooted in his awareness of the health and psychological risks associated with living there as an undocumented individual. He believes that migration cannot provide quality of life and that one’s emotional wellbeing can be harmed. That led him to choose to stay in MGV, where he has significant cultural and relational connections, despite economic challenges. He said:

    I can go there [to the US]) more “easily” than those who do not have a visa, but I cannot remain quietly living there, as many of my peers believe. They do not understand why I do not go to America since I have a visa. Life here is not easy at all, but it is your place. Here, you can come and go, there, you have to live like an animal, enclosed. There is no respect for you, and you are there irregularly. They can do whatever they want to you. They pay well but mistreat you, so a person only works and does not do other things because he is afraid of everything. Over time, this messes with your head, and when you realize it, you are already sick, with depression and other things. My uncles who are there talk about the bad side of living in another country. I know many people who come back, they come back old and sick and spend all their money on doctors and medication. I do not want to sell my health as they did (Gabriel, 2022).

    “I do not want to sell my health”. That statement is also used by Laura to explain her lack of desire for international migration, despite having the opportunity to claim US citizenship due to her father having citizenship. Their reasons for staying are based on the experiences of their family members and their own experiences of internal mobility in Brazil. Laura’s speech highlights how international mobility may not be the most desirable choice for everyone and how factors beyond economic opportunities can influence the decision to stay in a place. Concern for health, wellbeing, and quality of life play a fundamental role in her choices. Laura explained her desire to stay:

    I moved to Vitória, capital of the neighboring state of Espírito Santo. I lived there for two years. It was not perfect, and I did not like the experience of living in a big place. You do not know anyone, and people are indifferent. I found it very difficult to live. You feel emptiness and loneliness. (...) I think moving here is difficult; imagine going to another country. (...) I see this emptiness among my relatives who live outside Brazil. They talk about how hard it is to be away. They say they work a lot, do not have time, and are always tired. I do not want to sell my health. Many are doing well financially but very poorly regarding their physical and mental health. Why leave and die working? I do not want this for my life (Laura, 2022).

    These discourses show how these young people prioritize health and wellbeing over potential economic gains, challenging the idealized notion of the “American dream”. They consider that mobility does not always ensure an improved quality of life. Moreover, the situation illustrates how they carefully ponder between the potential advantages of mobility and the accompanying difficulties and sacrifices associated with migration. In this aspect, several studies have revealed how social, emotional, spiritual, and cultural bonds lead to attachment to places and how young people’ may perceive and accept the risks of migration differently (Rodríguez-Peña, 2023). Simultaneously, they reveal how personal networks tend to be more reliable sources of information about conditions elsewhere. In this regard, Schewel (2019) observes how the communication of challenges and risks across migrant networks creates “negative feedback mechanisms” for mobility practices. As a result, some people are discouraged from migrating, and there is a positive relationship between staying and risk aversion.

    These stories highlight how the (im)mobility decision-making process is complex and multifaceted, involving factors such as young people’s aspirations, perceptions of the structure of opportunities, family support, legal strategies, as well as considerations related to health, wellbeing, and overall quality of life. They reveal the dynamic nature of these decisions and the need to consider various factors when navigating the complexities of (im)mobility. Likewise, they underscore the intricate social and family aspects intertwined with migration, showing how mobility frequently operates within a context of broader social networks. Additionally, some of these young people who prefer to stay contested the scarcity narratives that drive emigration among their peers and positioned themselves as active agents of their (im)mobility decisions. They stated:

    People believe that they need to leave here to have things, like a house or a car. People believe that in America you will live a “dream”. But, you can work here as much as there and conquer your stuff. That is what I am doing, working and investing in my future here to fulfil my dreams (Laura, 2022).

    Here in the region, they have this belief... this obsession... of talking about leaving Brazil because there is nothing here. This is not true. If you want it, you can have a quiet life here. I can see it for myself, I have what my cousins have there, like a nice car, for example. Here, if you work, you can buy it too (Fábio, 2022).

    On the other hand, five young people do not share the perspectives and immobility aspirations of the previous interviewees; they believe migration ensures an improved quality of life. For them, there are more employment opportunities and the potential to save money outside of Brazil. This is the case for Nanda and Paulo, who want to move safely and are trying to obtain a US tourist visa to enter the country regularly. Both participate in local WhatsApp groups formed by young people sharing information, experiences, and strategies to help them surpass undesired mobility status. These examples prove that ICTs have been crucial for mobilizing migrants’ networks. Thus, in MGV, digital networks emerge as a contemporary space where the cordial capital (Fusco, 2005) that values interpersonal relationships and sociability in Brazilian culture finds its modern parallels. They explained their strategies to surpass the structural barriers that constrain safe mobility:

    I tried it once, two years ago, but it did not work out. But now I am going to do it differently, I am going to do it as one of the guys in the [WhatsApp] group did. He opened a MEI [micro company] – I already did this - and after a year applied for a visa with the whole family. (...)His parents, like mine, are government workers, which can help us; it is like we are asking for a visa to go on vacation/visit the US as a family. In WhatsApp groups, they still say that it is easier for us here in MGV to get a visa at the Consulate in Recife instead of at the one in Rio de Janeiro, because we are less targeted there. We are thinking of trying there [Recife] (Nanda, 2022).

    I am thinking about a way to get a visa to move. Some people on WhatsApp said that the best thing is for me to travel to other countries first. Like I am going to Portugal for a few days, then I am going to Spain and when I have some stamps from other countries, I will ask for an American visa. But they also say that there are some young people who get a visa when they enrol in a local University to do something like a master’s degree. I still have doubts about what I am actually going to do, but I have already saved up to try to move. If I cannot go there, maybe I will go to Portugal (Paulo, 2022).

    These examples demonstrate how they are exploring various strategies to achieve mobility. This involves the mobilization of financial and social resources, which indicates that the ability to move (mobility) is linked to social position and available resources. They illustrate how (im)mobility can be a factor of social inequality, as not everyone has equal opportunities to engage in particular forms of movement. That is the case for Lena, Rafael, and Tulio, who differently from the previous youths mentioned, realized that they have no chance of obtaining a US visa to enter the country as a regular tourist and that the only way to move there is by crossing the Mexican border irregularly. They know the risks and disadvantages of international emigration but believe some people are luckier than others. Lena had attempted to do so four times and was caught by the border police while entering the country every time. She intends to try to cross the Mexican border for the fifth time soon and explains how she has been able to pay for her travels. Tulio explains that he had only recently started to think about moving abroad, motivated by his brother’s previous experience and financial support. However, Rafael, who has a ten-month-old daughter, says that his biggest dream since his childhood has been to move to the US. This is what they said:

    I will try for the fifth time to cross again. It is not easy, I know that better than anyone. But it looks like I will be able to go again (...) I do not pay anything, I get about 3 or 4 people who want to go and who have money to pay the coyote, who is my friend. I make a bridge between them so that my trip is free. (...) I am talking to a family of three adults and three children who want to move to the US and they already have almost all of the money [or guarantees that they will pay] (Lena, 2022).

    My brother went a year ago with the family using the “cai-cai” scheme3. He suggested that I find a woman who has a child and wants to go to the US. And that is what I am trying to do, find a woman with a child who wants to go. I pay for their trip and she registers the child under my name, as if I were the father. A colleague of my brother’s moved there with this scheme (Tulio, 2022).

    I have dreamt of living in the US since I was little. I never got a chance to do that. It is expensive, you know? I have never been able to get enough money. You earn little, and life is tough here. My wife is feeling it now that we have a baby. Each doctor’s appointment means a lot of money that we have to leave there. Before the baby, she never talked seriously about moving there, but now she started to think more about it. She has relatives there who have offered us financial help to do this. They are always telling her to go, that they will help. (…) She, like me, sees how our families’ lives are better there. So why stay? I have been talking with her a lot, and I always say that the best thing we can do for our family is to go to the US. We have to take the chance to do “cai-cai” (Rafael, 2022).

    For these young people who want to migrate, the “American dream” outweighs some risks, and they are eager to do it. Mobility is seen as a way of accessing a better future. They are adapting their aspirations and mobilizing family informational, emotional, and economic resources to move. In this regard, the narratives highlight how family connections and social networks are essential as they help to overcome mobility barriers. They also illuminate the complex capability constraints potential migrants face, revealing how those inequalities in the access to and the control over mobility are key features of globalization (Schewel, 2019). Those with mobility capital can explore opportunities elsewhere, while those who do not have this capital may face limitations in their life prospects. These stories show the diversity of paths that contemporary migrants explore as they assess risks, consider consular policies, educational and financial opportunities to achieve their mobility goals. It also reveals how these young people employ their capabilities to surpass spatial and infrastructural moorings that differentially enable (im)mobility (Hannam et al., 2006).

    Furthermore, Rafael’s narrative clearly illustrates how households can pressure certain members to emigrate, even when it may be undesirable. However, our findings emphasize that family networks shape both migration aspirations and decisions to remain immobile (Mata-Codesal, 2018). The experiences of João and Paula further reveal how (im)mobility is deeply intertwined with family responsibilities and a sense of duty towards aging parents. As the only children still living in Brazil, while their siblings have already emigrated to countries like the United States and Portugal, their stories demonstrate how the mobility of some family members can directly contribute to the immobility of others. Their accounts highlight the weight of caring for elderly parents and the social pressure to meet this family obligation.

    I am the only one living here. All eight of my brothers have left. Two live in Portugal, and the others in the US. I have never considered the possibility of leaving. It is as if I had a duty to stay. I am the youngest. I do not have much choice. They left, and I, as the youngest, stayed behind. They always said “you stay there taking care of mother for us” and have always helped to have an organized and financially peaceful life here. I do not dare to leave my mother alone, and I know my brothers are calm there because I am here (João, 2022).

    My older brother went to the US, and I had no choice of staying or leaving. He had the right to leave, and I was obligated to stay because someone needed to be here to care for our older parents. Of course, it is not that open, but my parents always joke, “you will never leave us here alone, right?”. Like I must care for them. And this sense of duty does not allow me to think about a future in another country or far from my parents (Paula, 2022).

    Contrasting the costs and benefits of staying with the costs and benefits of moving can be complex and dependent on the spatial configuration of family networks. These stories reveal how life trajectories are configured by a person’s ties and individual choices, exchanges, obligations, and relationships with other people in their family, household, and social networks, and how it is not always easy to disentangle individual aspirations from concerns of kinship. In this regard, the decision to stay out of a sense of duty cannot be easily classified as voluntary since it stems from a sense of obligation or “obedience”. Carling and Schewel (2018, p. 958) recently argued that “when such commitments discourage migration, it is not obvious whether the effect is about aspirations to stay or constraints on the ability to move.”

    Constraints on moving, immigration restrictions, and structural inequalities can make emigration dreams turn sour. Such is the case for Pedro and Maria, who had experienced a constraint in their migration journey after they tried to immigrate to the US irregularly, were arrested and deported to Brazil. The experience forces them to consider whether such aspirations are still worth pursuing. They are exercising agency by advancing their education and building careers in Brazil with the support of family members abroad. This highlights how cultural (education, qualifications) and relational (family support) capital are also important factors in (im)mobility decisions. In their narratives, it is possible to see how these young people are coming to terms with the results of constraints and how they are navigating (im)mobility, adapting their strategies and dreams to the local place. In this regard, Ortiga and Macabasag (2021), argue that the decision to stay is mainly borne out of subjective views of ideal future selves. Pedro’s and Maria’s cases are as following:

    I have always wanted to go there (US), but it did not work out. (...) After being deported, I thought I would try again, but it takes work. It is financially and psychologically costly. (…) I realized that it was better to think about my future here and not there. I am engaging with my goals here. So I took an electrician technical course and invested in this area of expertise. I have had my company for almost three years, and it is growing daily. I chose a good market niche here in the region, construction. It is good both because migrants from there (US) invest a lot here and because here there is a lack of specialized labor. So, I am well here, I have a stable life, and I do not feel like leaving anymore (Pedro, 2022).

    I had just finished university when I decided to go to the US to try to live the “American dream”, but it did not last long. (...) I remember that while I was still on the flight back to Brazil, I was thinking about going back to the US. I did like living there. But, when I arrived in MGV, I thought better of it. I started to “weigh” all the good things I had there but all the constraints too, you know? It was a lot of effort crossing the border, fearing being arrested and sent back here with nothing. I preferred to focus my effort in a future here, with the help of my relatives from there. I went to college for a second time. I graduated in Dentistry a year ago. I am building my things here and do not think about leaving anymore. My dreams are built and realized here (Maria, 2022).

    Another young person adjusting his strategies and dreams while moving across (im)mobilities is José. In his case, the idea of a future had played a fundamental role in his strategies. After living in the US for eight years carrying fake documents, he decided to go back to live in MGV. He revealed how his (im)mobility capital (his ability to wait), combined with his network of family relationships (working with his sister) and his knowledge of opportunities in the US (investing in property recovery):

    I was uncomfortable there using fake documents, so I decided to go back to MGV right after my sister got her papers, and then do the right thing. I knew this could take up to ten years. But waiting here for it was the best path I had because I could work on my land and help my aging parents. Additionally, every month I receive a salary from the US, because before returning to MGV, I lent money to my sister and my brother-in-law so that they could invest in a rental property recovery business, and I became their partner. All of this helped me decide to come back and stay here while waiting on the green card process so that I can return to the US and be able to move there in a regular manner. And that is why I am staying here, waiting to get back into the US through the front door, not jumping over a wall (José, 2022).

    These narratives demonstrate how the aspirations and capabilities of young people in MGV are shaped by a complex interplay of factors, including their visions of an ideal future, their present circumstances, and past experiences. It is evident from these stories that young people’ are making strategic and informed decisions based on aspirations, available resources, and the support of their social networks. As a result, mobility and immobility are fluid and dynamic states that can coexist and shift over time, shaped by a combination of individual circumstances and broader structural factors. Thus, “capabilities and aspirations are not fixed; they exist along a spectrum” (Schewel, 2020, p. 336). Consequently, understanding the complexity of these factors and how they interact is essential to fully grasp the region’s dynamics of mobility and immobility.

    Moreover, these accounts highlight how young people actively exercise agency in navigating (im)mobility and adapting to shifting opportunities and challenges. Even when their mobility plans are disrupted or rerouted, or when they choose to stay for positive and negative reasons, they continue to engage with the benefits of movement and global flows (Harris and Raffaetà, 2021). However, a key limitation of this analysis is the inability to fully assess whether respondents genuinely have feasible opportunities for international migration and how the lack of such capabilities may shape the development of adaptive preferences (Debray et al., 2022).

    6. Conclusion

    This paper follows recent appeals to position (im)mobility at the core of migration research (Mata-Codesal, 2018; Schewel, 2019), demonstrating the need to explore young peoples’ mobility aspirations and capacities to understand why many choose not to migrate while also questioning static migration concepts. By introducing the aspiration/capability framework, we uncover power imbalances within (im)mobility experiences and we sharpen our understanding of the fluid and dynamic nature of immobility and mobility, showing how young peoples’ actively navigate between these states. It pushes the boundaries of this framework further, delving into how migration and staying aspirations evolve throughout one’s life course, how relational dynamics decisively shape decision-making processes, and how these forces ultimately drive migration or (im)mobility outcomes.

    In this sense, the notion of “motilities” helps to expand the aspiration/capability framework as it reveals the influence of the different variables, such as conditions like a desire to migrate, whether the infrastructures are facilitative or not, the valuation of the available options, of social ties and of family duty. All these factors shape the decision-making of (im)mobility among the young people in MGV. Thus, they grant a “more holistic apprehensions of the factors important to people when they decide to migrate or to stay” (Schewel and Fransen, 2022, p. 18). In other words, this perspective allows for an understanding of how young people navigate a “field of possibilities”, making decisions and utilizing their capabilities as they seek new routes and destinations or refine the conditions they consider ideal for (im)mobility (Silva, 2019). It highlights that immobility, far from being just a lack of movement, can be seen as a purposeful choice made by many young people in this region

    Despite the narratives only providing snapshots of young people’s lives, this approach yields valuable insights to help with problematizations of the binarism “mobility and immobility”. The examples provided elucidate the engagement of these young people with structural factors and their exercise of agency in adapting migration aspirations and capabilities. They underscore the imperative consideration of the local context and of young peoples’ choices when analyzing mobility and immobility. Their decisions manifest the intricate interplay between aspirations, capabilities, opportunities, and constraints, which defies oversimplification to a single formula.

    In this regard, the stories of young people from MGV show that mobility and immobility “are not mutually exclusive categories but, rather, two dynamic sides of the same coin” (Salazar, 2021, p. 3). This study challenges the entrenched “mobility bias” in migration research, proving that immobility is just as intricate and deserving of rigorous investigation as mobility (Mata-Codesal, 2015). It highlights the critical need to assess both mobility aspirations and opportunities available to young people, which may either flourish or be constrained. Moreover, how their ability to act on those opportunities, to stay or to leave are shaped by the extent to which their environments support their desires.

    Building on this, the paper advocates for future research to focus on a longitudinal study tracking young people over time to explore how their aspirations and capabilities for (im)mobility evolve in response to various social, economic, and cultural factors. Such research could adopt the “motilities” framework to analyze the multifaceted dimensions of mobility, investigating how experiences of immobility and opportunities for movement influence young people’s decision-making regarding their futures. It would also emphasize the interplay between individual aspirations, structural conditions, and dynamics of (im)mobility throughout the participants’ life course.
