Too Big to Dock: Brazil-China Iron Trade and the Valemax Vessels Crisis

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article analyzes the Valemax crisis to explore how regulatory barriers and domestic interests shape trade. When the Valemax, vessels owned by Brazil’s giant mining company Vale, were barred from Chinese ports, allegedly due to safety concerns, a diplomatic and commercial impasse emerged. Drawing on the Bureaucratic Politics Model, the study shows how Brazilian state agencies coordinated to defend Vale’s interests, while China protected its shipping sector. The eventual resolution, by transferring ship ownership to Chinese State Owned Enterprises (SOEs), illustrates how foreign firms must adapt to China’s political economy. The case highlights the influence of large companies on foreign policy and the complexities of commercial diplomacy in asymmetric economic relationships.

    Keywords
    Brazil–China relations; Bureaucratic politics; Commercial diplomacy; Iron ore trade; State-owned enterprises; Valemax

    Introduction

    In 2024, Brazil and the People’s Republic of China celebrated 50 years of diplomatic relations, a partnership that can be broadly divided into two distinct phases. During the first 30 years (1974-2004), the bilateral relationship was primarily driven by political interests and cooperation, with economic exchanges being initially minimal. However, in the last 20 years (2004-2024), economic exchanges have skyrocketed. Trade volumes surged in the 2000s, and Chinese investments in Brazil grew substantially in the 2010s. While political and diplomatic cooperation remains relevant, the last two decades have seen economic ties become the dominant feature of Sino-Brazilian relations.

    In effect, Brazil-China trade flows represent the most significant commercial relationship between any two Global South nations, consistently setting new records each year. Since the early 2000s, bilateral trade has grown exponentially, soaring from USD 4 billion in 2001 to over USD 158 billion by 2024 (Ministério do Desenvolvimento, Indústria, Comércio e Serviços do Brasil 2025). For comparison, China-India trade in the fiscal year of 2024 amounted to USD 118 billion (Ministry of External Affairs of India 2024), which is approximately 25% less than Sino-Brazilian trade. This is particularly notable given that India’s economy is roughly 80% larger than Brazil’s (International Monetary Fund 2025). Additionally, while China and India share a common border, Brazil is geographically distant, situated on the opposite side of the globe, which makes trade more difficult.

    This article explores a key feature of the deepening economic ties between Brazil and China: iron trade. It examines the Valemax vessels crisis in the early 2010s, which underscored the complexities and occasional friction in bilateral trade relations. Commissioned by Vale, Brazil’s giant mining company, these massive vessels were designed to transport large quantities of iron ore to China efficiently, reducing shipping costs and improving Brazil’s competitive position. However, despite their logistical advantages, the vessels encountered regulatory opposition from Chinese port authorities, barring them from China for several years on grounds of safety and infrastructure limitations.

    In this context, this article investigates how Brazil’s foreign policy apparatus responds to commercial crises involving strategic interests abroad, using the Bureaucratic Politics Model (BPM) as its analytical lens. The central research question is: What role did high-level diplomacy play in overcoming regulatory barriers in the Valemax case, and how does this illustrate the interdependence between commercial and political channels in high-level economic negotiations? The Valemax case is especially relevant for testing the BPM framework because it exemplifies an episode of bureaucratic alignment, where multiple ministries and the executive branch acted in concert to defend the interests of a major Brazilian multinational. Unlike traditional BPM applications that emphasize bureaucratic conflict, this case highlights the conditions under which bureaucratic consensus can emerge, and how it shapes foreign policy outcomes.

    The article analyzes the technical, regulatory, and political factors contributing to the impasse and how they illustrate the broader challenges Brazil faces in exporting high-volume raw materials to China. Additionally, it explores how the eventual resolution of the issue, which involved diplomatic, logistical and economic adjustments, reflects the complex interplay between regulatory bodies and political-economic imperatives in high-stakes international trade. By analyzing this case, the article advances the understanding of how major Latin American private actors engage with Chinese regulatory systems, how the Brazilian state defends strategic commercial interests in China, and how China’s domestic priorities condition market access for foreign partners. The case provides empirical insight into the evolving structure of Sino-Brazilian economic relations, which, while highly complementary, are increasingly shaped by strategic asymmetries and regulatory constraints. Moreover, the Valemax case illustrates the broader challenges faced by Brazilian companies in accessing China’s tightly regulated market and underscores the necessity of state involvement and adaptive strategies in overcoming regulatory impasses.

    Valemax vessels are a type of very large ore carriers (VLOCs) owned or chartered by Vale. VLOCs are a class of bulk cargo ships designed to transport large quantities of raw materials, primarily iron ore, with deadweight tonnage exceeding 200,000 tons. They play a critical role in global trade, particularly in facilitating the movement of iron ore from major exporters such as Brazil and Australia to industrial hubs such as China. With a capacity of up to 400,000 tons, the Valemax were the largest dry bulk carriers ever constructed at the time of their launch in the early 2010s. These ships offered cost advantages by consuming less fuel and reducing carbon emissions compared to smaller vessels (Góes 2014).

    The Valemax crisis leads to a few secondary questions concerning the international economic strategies of both Brazil and China: How was the crisis resolved, and what does this outcome suggest about China’s “win-win” discourse in practice? What were the institutional and strategic asymmetries revealed by the Valemax case, and how do they affect Brazil’s capacity to act as a sovereign commercial actor in its relations with China?

    To shed light on the aforementioned questions, this article adopts a qualitative historical analysis, seeking to reconstruct the sequence of events and decision-making processes surrounding the Valemax vessels crisis. Primary sources include interviews with high-level officials involved in the negotiations, diplomatic cables exchanged between the Brazilian Ministry of Foreign Affairs and its Embassy in Beijing, official statements from Brazilian and Chinese government bodies, and public statements from company executives. These are complemented by secondary sources such as news articles from specialized economic outlets and academic literature on foreign policy analysis and Brazil-China relations. The analytical framework draws on the Bureaucratic Politics Model (BPM) within Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA) to identify the key actors, action channels, and institutional dynamics involved in Brazil’s response to the Valemax ban. This model allows for an examination of how inter-organizational coordination and bureaucratic preferences influenced the formulation and implementation of Brazil’s China policy in this specific case.

    The remainder of this article is organized as follows. The next section outlines the analytical framework employed in the study, drawing on the Bureaucratic Politics Model to situate the Valemax crisis within Brazil’s foreign policy decision-making structure. The third section, Too Big to Dock, provides a detailed account of the commercial and logistical motivations behind Vale’s investment in the Valemax fleet and the subsequent resistance from Chinese authorities. The fourth section, Rolling Out the Red Carpet, traces the progressive involvement of the Brazilian government and the diplomatic channels activated in response to the crisis. The fifth section, From Tit for Tat to Quid Pro Quo, analyzes the negotiations and commercial compromises that led to the eventual resolution of the impasse. The sixth section, Beijing’s Win-Win Strategy, critically contrasts the strategic outcomes of the case from a Chinese and a Brazilian perspective. Finally, the concluding section, Anchored Ambitions, reflects on the implications of the Valemax episode for bilateral economic relations and the lessons it offers for Brazilian firms engaging with China’s regulated and politically sensitive market.

    The Bureaucratic Politics Model

    To understand Brazil’s China policy during the Valemax crisis, the Bureaucratic Politics Model (BPM), within the framework of Foreign Policy Analysis (FPA), offers valuable insights. FPA emphasizes that foreign policy stems from decisions made by individuals or groups, and includes both implemented and unimplemented actions (Salomón and Pinheiro 2013; Hudson 2005). It considers multiple levels of analysis, including individual psychology, domestic pressures, systemic factors, and, crucially, bureaucratic politics (Hudson and Day 2019). Since states act through organizations, FPA rejects the notion of the state as a unitary, rational actor, highlighting the political nature of bureaucracies. As Frederickson et al. (2018, 41) argue, bureaucratic politics bridges the divide between administration and politics. Although non-state actors influence policy, their inputs are mediated by state institutions. Thus, analyzing the characteristics of the bureaucracies and the “authoritative decision unit” – individuals or groups with the power to make binding, non-reversible decisions (Hermann 2001, 48) – is essential. In this case, these include ministers, the president, and Parliament, but may also include other entities, such as Vale.

    According to the Bureaucratic Politics Model, government leaders are not a unified group but individual players engaged in a bargaining game within a hierarchical structure (Allison 1969, 707). Unlike the Rational Actor Model, which assumes a unitary decision-maker, BPM sees foreign policy as the result of negotiation among actors pursuing different national, organizational, and personal goals. Decisions emerge from political “pulling and hauling,” not rational calculation, as power is decentralized across agencies facing complex and contested issues. Policy outcomes reflect compromise, competition, and even confusion among officials focused on different facets of the same problem. These actors operate within established action channels, respond to deadlines, and define problems based on their bureaucracy’s priorities and perceptions (Allison 1969, 709).

    Policy games often begin in response to deadlines or events, with players’ goals shaped by national, organizational, domestic, and personal concerns. Bureaucracies compete for influence, and while strategies may exist, most players focus on persuading others. Action channels structure these interactions by defining who participates, when, and with what advantages. Government actions do not necessarily reflect clear intentions, but rather the aggregate of diverse inputs, often producing outcomes no single actor intended. Problems may be addressed through decisions, policies, or even inaction (Allison 1969, 711).

    Despite its analytical utility, the Bureaucratic Politics Model also presents certain limitations. Its focus on internal state dynamics tends to underplay the role of external structural factors, such as asymmetries in the international economic order, or the agency of private actors like multinational firms. In the Valemax case, the alignment of bureaucratic actors limits the model’s ability to account for variation in policy preferences. Other approaches, such as the Two-Level Game framework (Putnam, 1988) or Principal-Agent models (Jensen and Meckling 1976), might offer complementary insights, particularly regarding the interaction between domestic and international incentives or the delegation of authority to state representatives. Nevertheless, BPM remains especially useful for identifying how intra-state coordination (or lack thereof) influences the formulation of foreign policy in complex trade disputes.

    The Valemax case is especially relevant for applying the BPM because it diverges from the model’s conventional emphasis on bureaucratic rivalry and policy fragmentation. Classical applications of BPM typically illustrate how foreign policy outcomes result from negotiation, conflict, and compromise among agencies with diverging institutional interests, worldviews, and bureaucratic mandates. In contrast, the Valemax episode offers an empirical example of bureaucratic alignment: a scenario in which multiple branches of Brazil’s executive, including the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Itamaraty), the Ministry of Development, Industry and Foreign Trade (MDIC), and the Ministry of Mines and Energy (MME), converged around a unified position in support of Vale, a major Brazilian multinational. Rather than exhibiting internal contestation or coordination failures, the Brazilian response to China’s port restrictions on Valemax vessels was marked by swift inter-ministerial coordination, consistent diplomatic messaging, and the absence of significant dissent within the executive apparatus. This bureaucratic cohesion significantly shaped the contours and efficacy of Brazil’s diplomatic strategy. Thus, the case highlights not only how consensus can emerge within the state apparatus, but also how such unity – albeit atypical in BPM analyses – can condition the state’s ability to protect strategic commercial interests abroad. In this sense, the Valemax case serves as a valuable counterpoint that expands the explanatory reach of BPM by illustrating the model’s capacity to capture both conflictual and cooperative bureaucratic dynamics in foreign policy decision-making.

    The following section examines how these tensions materialized in the Valemax dispute, highlighting the fragmented and contested nature of economic and foreign policy outcomes.

    Too Big to Dock: Freight Politics and the Valemax Ban in China

    In 2008, during a period of high international freight rates, Vale ordered 19 Valemax vessels from Chinese and Korean shipyards, at a cost of USD2.1 billion (Durão 2011a), and contracted another 16, totaling 35 super vessels, capable of transporting 60 million tons of ore per year (Durão 2011b). Initially, Beijing embraced the idea of constructing Valemax ships, not least because the Valemax project would generate business for Chinese shipyards (Murphy 2012). Two Chinese state-owned banks – the Export-Import Bank of China and the Bank of China – provided USD 1.3 billion to finance half of the ships built in China (Murphy 2012). This also marked a significant shift for Vale, which had traditionally relied on shipping companies for transporting iron ore. The strategy of owning its fleet was introduced by the company prior to the market crisis that erupted at the end of 2008 when freight rates to China were around USD 100 per ton (Durão 2011c). At that time, Vale was forced to halt the production and shipment of 70 million tons of ore due to a shortage of available ships for hire. This shortage prompted the company to decide on purchasing supercarriers (Ibid). However, by the early 2010s, when Valemax vessels started to be delivered, the price of international iron ore freight had already plummeted, weakened by an oversupply of ships and the global economic slowdown (Durão 2011d). Freight rates from Brazil to China fell from over USD 100 per ton before the 2008 financial crisis to around USD 27 per ton in the early 2010s. The freight rate from Australia, Brazil’s main iron competitor, to China was about USD 10 per ton, down from USD 40 per ton in 2008. In the early 2010s, the freight rate differential between Brazil and Australia on the China route was USD 15 per ton, a significant drop from the USD 60 per ton differential in 2008. Vale’s Cost, Insurance, and Freight (CIF) sales policy was implemented to stabilize freight rates (Ibid). In turn, Vale’s vessels faced strong pressure from Chinese shipowners and major state-owned steelmakers, who feared that Vale’s large fleet would further depress iron ore freight prices in the international market.

    By acquiring its own fleet, Vale positioned itself as a potential competitor to dry bulk cargo transport companies, especially China Ocean Shipping Company (COSCO), a Chinese state-owned enterprise (SOE) and the largest maritime transport company in China (Cosco Shipping Lines Co 2025). In June 2011, Chinese authorities denied the Valemax named Vale Brasil permission to dock, forcing the vessel to redirect to Taranto, Italy (Wright 2011). Subsequently, the vessel unloaded in Malaysia and the Philippines, with smaller ships transporting the ore onward to China. In December 2011, Berge Everest was the first Valemax to dock at a Chinese port, in the city of Dalian, setting a world record by unloading 350,000 tons of iron ore in 55 hours1. Controversy surrounding the Valemax intensified after the Vale Beijing, the third carrier in the series, developed cracks in its ballast tank during loading for its maiden voyage in northern Brazil also in December 2011 (Wright 2011). This incident raised concerns about the viability of the quick-loading method used for these ships. A month later, the Chinese Ministry of Transport (MoT) issued Circular no. 13 effectively banning Valemax ships from Chinese ports due to safety concerns1. Although Beijing did not specifically mention Vale, China announced stricter restrictions for all large vessels, including the Valemax fleet. “We are not optimistic about the safety situation of port operations for large vessels, especially the docking operations of super-large ships, whose dimensions exceed permitted design standards”, stated Chinese transport authorities. “Considering the significant hidden dangers, we have decided to adjust the port management system regarding the docking of large ships”. For its part, the Chinese Shipowners’ Association labeled the Valemax as a “monopoly and unfair competition issue”, claiming it harmed the interests of the shipping industry in China and other regions1. The Association contended that the new vessels were unsafe and would exacerbate the global oversupply of dry bulk ships, a surplus driven by the substantial orders placed during the 2003 to 2008 shipping boom (Wright 2011). “We pay very special attention to this matter, and the association has been firmly opposed [to the Valemax fleet],” the Shipowners’ Association stated at the time (Wright 2011).

    The obstacles China posed to the Valemax fleet were part of longstanding tensions between China and the world’s largest iron ore mining companies – BHP Billiton, Rio Tinto, and Vale – collectively known as the “Big Three” (Hook 2012). They were frequently accused by Chinese steel industry organizations of monopolistic practices or conspiring to raise iron ore prices. Wei Jiafu, then COSCO’s president, held a significant role within the Chinese business community. Referred to as “Captain Wei”, he also occupied a prominent position in the Communist Party’s Central Commission for Discipline Inspection. Wei was vocal about the issue of overcapacity in the maritime sector. In 2011, the China COSCO division reported a substantial loss of 10.5 billion yuan (USD 1.69 billion) due to sector overcapacity and a downturn in global trade (Murphy 2012).

    In response to the challenges faced with Chinese ports refusing to accept Valemax ships, Vale developed an alternative strategy to ensure the continued entry of their iron ore into the Chinese market. José Carlos Martins, then Vale’s Executive Director of Integrated Operations, attributed the refusal to “technical problems”, noting that most Chinese ports could not accommodate the 400,000-ton vessels (Durão 2011b). As an interim solution, Vale implemented a parallel scheme to distribute its iron ore without directly docking the Valemax ships in China. By 2013, Vale’s 35 super ships had a capacity to transport 60 million tons of ore annually. Vale constructed distribution centers in Asia and the Middle East and adapted Brazilian ports, such as Tubarão in Vitória (Durão 2011b), to anchor these massive ships. Ore distribution centers were inaugurated in Oman and Malaysia. These centers transferred the ore to smaller vessels, such as Capesize and Panamax, destined for China. The Brazilian mining company’s tactics ensured that its ships docked at ports capable of handling them, including Ponta da Madeira (Brazil), Rotterdam (Netherlands), Sohar (Oman), and Taranto (Italy), while negotiations were ongoing with other Asian ports outside China (Durão 2011d). This allowed Vale to maintain stable freight rates and long-term contracts, with the flexibility to sell some ships if needed.

    To avoid upsetting its main export market, Vale adopted a conciliatory stance. Martins denied that the refusal to receive the Valemax ships was due to a power struggle between the mining company and Chinese freight companies, highlighting the announcement by China’s NDRC that the issues were solely related to port equipment and infrastructure (Rosas 2011). He emphasized that Vale had to comply with Chinese regulations and that port preparations take time. He noted that the six operational Valemax ships were docking at ports capable of receiving them and mentioned the development of other ports in Asia. Martins also stated that the company’s decisions were based on financial considerations, downplaying any potential conflict with Chinese freight companies (Rosas 2011).

    Rolling Out The Red Carpet: Brazil’s Diplomatic Push for Vale

    Initially the Brazilian government had not intervened with the Chinese government on behalf of Vale. However, during the visit of Chinese Vice Premier Wang Qishan to Brazil, where he led the delegation for COSBAN’s 2nd Plenary Session in February 2012, the problem was discussed with then Vice President Michel Temer (Durão 2012). Prior to the Chinese mission’s arrival, the Brazilian government had reached out to Vale to understand the situation and offer assistance. Vale requested that, if the issue were to be addressed, it should be part of a positive agenda to foster closer relations and increase business between the two countries, rather than being seen as part of a negative trade dispute (Durão 2012). Vale’s CEO Murilo Ferreira stated that the issue with the super ships was not political. He emphasized that the discussions were technical and highlighted the significant involvement of Chinese shipyards in constructing the Valemax vessels. Ferreira expressed optimism about the negotiations, noting that the restrictions were being addressed at the port level (Durão 2012).

    On July 4, 2012, the State Secretariat (Itamaraty’s headquarters in Brasilia) reported that Ambassador Carneiro Leão, then Itamaraty’s Secretary for Economic Affairs, met with the Chinese Ambassador in Brasilia, Mr. Li Jinzhang, to discuss preparations for the upcoming visit to Beijing by the Brazilian Minister of Development, Industry, and Foreign Trade, Fernando Pimentel, scheduled for July 112. Ambassador Carneiro Leão highlighted that President Dilma Rousseff recently had a bilateral meeting with Prime Minister Wen Jiabao on June 26, on the sidelines of the Rio+20 conference, during which they discussed the Valemax issue. Following the Chinese leader’s proposal to establish a working group to address Valemax-related issues and bilateral iron ore trade, President Rousseff instructed Minister Pimentel to undertake an official visit to China. Ambassador Carneiro Leão clarified that the visit was a courtesy call with a political, rather than a negotiatory, nature2. He requested the Chinese Ambassador’s assistance in arranging meetings with Vice Premier Wang Qishan, Minister of Commerce Chen Deming, Minister of Transport Li Shenglin, and Chairman of the NDRC Zhang Ping. Carneiro Leão also presented a draft Terms of Reference for the working group to the Chinese Ambassador for feedback, expressing Brazil’s interest in finalizing the document promptly for signing during Minister Pimentel’s visit2. In response, the Chinese Ambassador committed to providing full support in scheduling the meetings2.

    On July 5, 2012, a meeting was held at MDIC with representatives from Vale, MDIC’s Secretariat for Foreign Trade, and diplomats from Itamaraty’s economic units to address the challenges faced by Valemax ships in docking at Chinese ports due to new regulations imposed by China’s MoT1. The objective of the meeting was to review and update information in preparation for Minister Fernando Pimentel’s visit to China. During the meeting, Claudio Alves, Vale’s International Marketing Director for iron ore, and Rafael Benke, the company’s Director of Corporate Affairs, provided a detailed presentation on the issue1. They highlighted Vale’s efforts to understand China’s political and economic sensitivities, including inviting COSCO to participate in the Valemax project. However, COSCO had declined the offer due to its strong position in the maritime cargo transport sector at the time1. Vale had also negotiated memoranda of understanding with major Chinese ports to make the necessary technical adjustments for Valemax docking. Despite these efforts, the introduction of Circular 13 in January 2012 transferred the authority to grant docking licenses to the MoT, complicating the process1. According to its representatives, Vale had never received a formal refusal from the Ministry regarding the docking of Valemax ships. Instead, they only received “signals” from their Chinese partners suggesting that it would not be advisable to submit the request1. According to Vale, only Valemax ships had been prevented from docking at Chinese ports, while even larger and more complex vessels, such as supertankers, had not faced such problems1. Vale representatives emphasized that Chinese ports and steel mills would benefit from Valemax ships due to increased productivity in iron ore unloading1. They also noted that while Vale had not received formal refusals from the MoT, there were indications from partners that it was not opportune to submit docking requests1. The meeting concluded with the acknowledgment that the financial situation of COSCO was indeed critical, but that Valemax operations posed no significant threat to COSCO1. Some developments indicated a potential shift, with Dalian port authorities showing openness to continuing Valemax operations, possibly influenced by the high-level meeting between President Rousseff and Prime Minister Wen Jiabao on the sidelines of the Rio+20 summit1.

    On July 6, 2012, a cable from the Embassy in Beijing to Brasilia reported that a consultation with MOFCOM had revealed that the NDRC had been designated to coordinate the Valemax issue within the Chinese government and was responsible for scheduling the requested meetings3. The Embassy maintained continuous contact with the NDRC to secure confirmations for these meetings, which included Vice Premier Wang Qishan, the Minister of Commerce, and the Minister of Transport3. The NDRC, for its part, requested the composition of Minister Fernando Pimentel’s delegation3. Ten days later another cable from the Embassy in Beijing to Brasilia reported that the NDRC had confirmed its Vice Minister Zhang Xiaoqiang’s intention to visit Brasilia on August 34. Vice Minister Zhang was scheduled to hold a working meeting with MDIC. The agenda included the establishment of the working group comprising representatives from both governments and enterprises to discuss issues related to the iron ore trade and processing, as well as the berthing of Valemax vessels in Chinese ports4. This initiative aimed to address and find solutions for the logistical challenges and enhance bilateral cooperation in the iron ore sector.

    On November 9, 2012, a cable from Brasilia to Beijing highlighted the need to reschedule a meeting between Brazilian and Chinese officials aimed to present a pilot project prepared by Vale to address the Valemax issue5. The cable emphasized the importance of this preliminary meeting as preparation for a subsequent high-level discussion requested by MDIC with the NDRC, scheduled between November 19 and 22, with the participation of the MDIC Executive Secretary, Alessandro Teixeira. Further communication on November 14, 2012, reaffirmed the Brazilian side’s interest in holding the proposed meeting despite the absence of the NDRC Vice-Minister6. For her part, the MDIC’s Secretary of Foreign Trade, Tatiana Prazeres, reiterated the significance of this meeting for discussing iron ore and Valemax issues6. It was noted that the presence of the Deputy Executive Secretary of the Ministry of Mines and Energy, Francisco Romário Wojcicki, would be beneficial, as his involvement would better integrate his ministry into the Valemax discussions6. The Brazilian delegation was prepared to adjust the level of its representation depending on the Chinese side’s participation. The cable also mentioned the delivery of a pilot project to the Chinese authorities, underscoring Brazil’s commitment to resolving the Valemax docking issue and enhancing bilateral cooperation in the iron ore sector6.

    According to Vale, the Valemax bilateral task force created in the context of Chinese Premier Wen Jiabao’s visit to Brazil in June 2012 resulted in little progress (Murphy 2012). Vale expected that the 2013 presidential transition in China from Hu Jintao to Xi Jinping could lead to some advancements. Despite its efforts, Vale did not succeed in securing a direct meeting with the Chinese MoT without the intermediation of the Brazilian government (Murphy 2012). This underscores the importance of state-to-state relations, particularly high-level visits, in resolving bilateral issues with the Chinese government.

    From Tit For Tat to Quid Pro Quo: Vale’s Rebuke and the Compromise Solution

    As a protest against the Valemax ban, Vale boycotted COSCO, refusing to use its vessels as of early 2012 (“Vale boicota navios chineses, diz operadora.” 2012). The SOE anticipated a significant operational impact from the boycott and considered filing a complaint in China’s MOFCOM. According to COSCO president Ma Zehua, Vale had avoided using COSCO’s fleet for a couple of months, opting for more expensive alternatives. Zehua suggested that Vale’s actions were driven by the belief that COSCO had lobbied to prevent the Valemax ships from docking at Chinese ports due to safety concerns. Vale did not comment on the boycott or the safety concerns, stating only its hope to gain permission to use its ships in China.

    On April 19, 2013, a representative from Vale in China informed the Brazilian government that a Valemax vessel had successfully docked and unloaded 220,000 tons of iron ore at the port of Lianyungang in Jiangsu Province earlier that week7. According to Vale, the ship docked with a load significantly below its maximum capacity of 400,000 tons, adhering to the limits set by Chinese port regulations (Góes 2013a). This docking occurred despite the regulatory measure issued by China’s MoT which had removed the discretion of ports to authorize the berthing of ships exceeding certain size limits (Hook 2013). The Vale representative speculated that the port might not have consulted the central government since the load did not violate national regulations7. Press reports seemed to confirm this version, with a MoT official, He Jianzhong, stating that he was unaware of the docking and reaffirming that the prohibition on mega vessels remained unchanged7. Zhang Shouguo, Secretary-General of the China Shipowners Association, reacted angrily, insisting that Valemax ships continued to violate national legislation regardless of their load size and asserting that future approvals for such dockings would be “absolutely impossible” for Vale7. Despite this, Vale indicated the possibility of additional dockings in the coming weeks7.

    In September 2013, Vale expressed optimism about an improvement in the Chinese economy, which could boost demand for iron ore and facilitate the direct supply of Valemax vessels to the Chinese market (Murphy 2013). The Chinese government, persuaded by steel companies and other businesses that believed these ships could lead to more abundant and cheaper iron ore, considered delegating the decision on ship size to individual ports. While Chinese ports could handle ships up to 300,000 tons, some had already begun upgrading facilities to accommodate larger vessels. Chinese transport authorities’ preliminary proposal suggested allowing ports to accept larger ships if they met safety and other requirements (Murphy 2013). Nevertheless, the proposal implied that part of the cargo might need to be transferred to smaller vessels to comply with quay safety limits. Vale, eager to see the final outcome of these regulatory changes, noted the positive business environment under Xi Jinping’s new leadership (Murphy 2013).

    In a strategic move to solve the crisis, Vale entered into a USD 500 million agreement with Shandong Shipping Corporation (SDSC), a large SOE under the Shandong Provincial People’s Government, transferring the operation of four Valemax vessels to the SDSC (Góes 2013b). José Carlos Martins indicated that the company planned to transfer its fleet to shipping companies, prioritizing Chinese ones. Shandong Shipping operated the vessels within the existing regulatory framework, with the possibility of future permissions allowing Valemax operations in Chinese ports. In September 2014, Vale reached two similar agreements with Chinese shipowners. One with COSCO and the other with China Merchants Energy Shipping (CMES), both Chinese SOEs among the world’s largest shipping companies (Góes 2015).

    Vale sold four Valemax vessels to COSCO, and another four to CMES. Both agreements were formalized during the visit of Chinese Premier Li Keqiang to Brazil in May 2015. The agreement with COSCO involved the sale of four Valemax ships for USD 445 million, with a lease-back arrangement for a period of 25 years (Góes 2014). Additionally, Vale signed charter contracts for ten new Valemax vessels, which were to be constructed by COSCO (Saraiva 2015). Vale’s agreements with COSCO and CMES aimed to facilitate the docking of Valemax ships in China. The additional capacity provided by the new Valemax ships would help maintain low freight rates, a critical factor for Vale given the low iron ore prices at the time (Eisenhammer and Goh 2014). This agreement raised expectations that Vale could pursue further deals to transfer ownership of the remaining 15 Valemax ships still owned by the company (Eisenhammer and Goh 2014).

    In February 2015, the Chinese Ministry of Transport lifted Circular 13 (Rostás 2015). Besides the agreement with COSCO, in May 2015 during Chinese Premier Li Keqiang’s visit to Brazil, Vale announced an expansion of its existing agreement with China Merchants Energy Shipping (CMES), a subsidiary of China Merchants Group (Saraiva 2015). This agreement focused on long-term strategic cooperation in the maritime transport of iron ore. As part of the expanded agreement, Vale planned to sell another four Valemax to CMES, totalling USD 445 million. In July 2015, the Chinese government fully lifted the three-year ban on large iron ore carriers (Mitchell 2015). The announcement from the MoT and the NDRC indicated that four ports would now be accessible to the 400,000-ton Valemax vessels. This decision followed the late-June authorization for the Valemax carrier Yuan Zhuo Hai to anchor off Qingdao port in eastern Shandong province. Qingdao, along with Dalian, Tangshan, and Ningbo, were the four ports designated to receive these massive vessels (Ibid).

    In March 2016, prominent Chinese maritime enterprises, including COSCO, CMES, and ICBC Financial Leasing Co., placed orders for 30 Valemax vessels, collectively valued at USD 2.5 billion (Paris 2016). This initiative was intended to enhance China’s control over Brazil’s iron ore exports and exert additional competitive pressure on Western maritime carriers. Each of these three Chinese shipping companies ordered ten vessels from four Chinese shipyards: Shanghai Waigaoqiao Shipbuilding, Beihai Shipbuilding, CIC Jiangsu, and Yangzijiang Shipbuilding. Vale’s last Valemax vessels were sold in December 2017 to China’s Bank of Communications Finance Leasing (Bocomm) (“Vale sells final Valemax bulkers to Bocomm.” 2017).

    Beijing’s Win-Win Strategy: When China Wins Twice

    The resolution of the Valemax crisis through the sale of the ships to Chinese shipowners was described as a “win-win situation that plays to their respective advantages”, by Zhang Shouguo, executive vice president of the China Shipowners Association (Eisenhammer and Goh 2014). The concept of win-win cooperation emerged as a central pillar of China’s diplomatic discourse in the early 2010s, reflecting a normative repositioning and a strategic response to global transformations (Danzhi 2019). Its origins can be traced to Xi’s speech at the Moscow Institute of International Relations on March 23, 2013, where he first proposed building a “new-type international relations” centered on mutual benefit and cooperation (Ibid). This was meant to mark a departure from Cold War mentalities and zero-sum logic, which Xi rejected as outdated in an increasingly interconnected and interdependent world. Rooted in traditional Chinese philosophical notions – such as the idea of benefiting oneself by benefiting others found in Confucian, Daoist, and Zhuangzian thought – the concept also sought to transcend the perceived “law of the jungle” that underpins Western international relations theories (Ibid). Framed as both a continuation of the PRC’s diplomatic tradition and an innovation in international relations theory, the concept sought to position China as a responsible major power advocating a more equitable and cooperative international order, with the ultimate goal of fostering a “community with a shared future for mankind.”

    Nevertheless, scholarship has challenged China’s “win-win” narrative, emphasizing the asymmetrical and utilitarian nature of Beijing’s economic engagements. Bernal-Meza (2016) argues that the so-called “win-win rhetoric” serves as a legitimizing discourse for a North-South type of relationship in which Latin America functions as a periphery integrated into China’s development model, providing raw materials while deepening dependency and structural asymmetry. Similarly, Crovella (2015), analyzing China’s strategy in Africa, contends that the win-win discourse obscures a “win-through strategy,” whereby China advances its own long-term interests by exporting its economic and social systems, gradually reshaping African societies to mirror its own and thus aligning their future needs with Chinese strategic goals.

    According to a seasoned Brazilian ambassador with experience in commercial negotiations with China8, when the Chinese speak of “win-win” cooperation, they often ensure that China benefits twice. In the Valemax case, Vale took into account China’s political and economic sensitivities from the outset. Initially, Vale invited COSCO to participate in the Valemax project, but COSCO declined. The construction of the ships by Chinese shipyards, financed by Chinese banks, was a strategic move by the enterprise to please the Chinese. However, upon the vessels’ completion, COSCO pressured Chinese transport authorities to veto their docking. Both COSCO and the MoT cited safety concerns regarding the Valemax ships. In retaliation, Vale stopped using COSCO’s vessels. Against this complicated backdrop, the Brazilian government came into play seeking to find a viable solution. Ultimately, a compromise was found through the sale of the Valemax ships to COSCO and other Chinese carriers. In the case at hand, the Chinese indeed “won” multiples times: Chinese shipyards benefited from the construction orders, Chinese banks profited from financing, and the reduced transportation costs lowered the final price of Brazilian iron ore to Chinese steel companies. Despite these gains, it was only after the Valemax ships were sold to Chinese transporters that Vale was permitted to operate them in China. After Vale transferred the vessels to Chinese SOEs, the ban was lifted, and soon after, COSCO, CMES, and ICBC Financial Leasing commissioned 30 new Valemax ships. Once the fleet was under Chinese control, Beijing not only allowed Valemax operations but actively expanded them, consolidating China’s grip over iron ore maritime logistics.

    Anchored Ambitions: Vale’s Lessons in Chinese Waters

    The Valemax case study exemplifies the complex interdependence characterizing Brazil-China trade relations, revealing how economic ambition is woven with strategic considerations and domestic regulatory mechanisms. Vale’s original goal with the Valemax vessels was to stabilize freight rates and ensure more predictable iron ore supply logistics to China. However, this strategy faced significant resistance from Chinese stakeholders, showing the dynamics that emerge when foreign economic initiatives intersect with national economic policy. China’s decision to restrict Valemax vessels from docking at its ports, justified by official statements citing safety and technical concerns, reflects a deliberate approach to safeguard its domestic maritime sector. By imposing these regulatory constraints, China effectively protected its state-owned shipping industry, primarily represented by COSCO, as well as the broader interests of its shipowners. The regulatory measure issued by China’s MoT banning the docking of mega-vessels, including the Valemax ships, signaled a broader economic strategy that preserved domestic stakeholders’ competitiveness against international pressures by maintaining control over national infrastructure.

    Vale’s approach, which involved diplomatic negotiations and operational restructuring, underscores the challenges that foreign enterprises encounter when operating within China’s regulated market environment. Initially, Vale had intended to align its investment with Chinese interests by commissioning the Valemax fleet from Chinese shipyards and securing financing from Chinese banks. Nevertheless, these economic overtures proved insufficient to overcome the regulatory barriers instituted by Chinese authorities, revealing the complex nature of the Chinese political economy, where strategic, regulatory, and domestic industry considerations converge to shape market access.

    The eventual compromise, involving the transfer of ownership of several Valemax vessels to Chinese state-owned shipping companies such as COSCO and CMES, proved essential for Vale to continue its operations within the Chinese market. This solution enabled Vale to indirectly utilize its own vessels to transport iron ore to China, while reinforcing Chinese control over the logistical chain of this significant commodity. By entering into sale-and-leaseback agreements with Chinese carriers, Vale reestablished access and engaged in a commercial arrangement that redistributed operational benefits across both Brazilian and Chinese entities. Consequently, the resolution of the Valemax issue exemplifies the importance of recognizing and adjusting to China’s approach to foreign economic activities, where concessions are often structured to align with Chinese economic interests.

    The role of Brazilian diplomacy in facilitating this compromise illustrates the essential function of high-level state engagement in addressing the regulatory and logistical challenges associated with the Chinese market. Diplomatic interventions, such as those undertaken by Brazil’s Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Itamaraty) and Ministry of Trade (MDIC), were instrumental in securing meetings with Chinese officials and establishing a bilateral working group to address the Valemax issue. This collaboration underscores the necessity for foreign enterprises operating in China to engage with both commercial and governmental channels, particularly in instances where regulatory barriers impact operational continuity. The coordinated efforts by Brazilian and Chinese officials to create avenues for dialogue and negotiation highlight the central role of state-to-state interaction in advancing complex bilateral trade issues.

    The lessons drawn from the Valemax case highlight the broader implications for Brazil-China economic relations, particularly in sectors where Brazilian exports play a substantial role in supporting China’s industrial base. In operating within China’s regulatory environment, companies must not only accommodate the technical and logistical requirements imposed by Chinese authorities but also anticipate the strategic dimensions that may influence policy decisions. This episode underscores the significance of adopting a flexible approach to international trade relations, to maintain a competitive edge in China’s critical import markets. By aligning business strategies with China’s regulatory expectations and strategic priorities, firms can position themselves to leverage their economic strengths while abiding by the institutional frameworks that govern China’s complex economic dynamics.

    The Valemax crisis illustrates that economic engagements with China demand an understanding of China’s approach to foreign competition and market access, where regulatory, political, and strategic factors intersect. By adjusting to these conditions and engaging through diplomatic and commercial channels, Brazil can work to secure its interests while fostering a stable trade relationship with China, based on economic pragmatism and diplomatic considerations.

    Considering the Bureaucratic Politics Model, the Valemax episode was an extraordinary crisis situation. There was no intra-executive conflict; Itamaraty, MDIC, and the Ministry of Mines and Energy defended the same position, which was the protection of Vale’s commercial interests, Brazil’s largest mining company. Consequently, policy formation occurred at the ministerial level without the need for arbitration by the President. When the issue was escalated to the level of government heads, President Dilma Rousseff defended the unified position of the Brazilian bureaucracies, forged at the ministerial level. Furthermore, there were no powerful actors with conflicting interests; on the contrary, Vale’s interests were fully defended by the Brazilian government, so the issue was not brought before Parliament. Hence, the course of action taken by the executive branch was directly implemented. This case illustrates the significant influence large non-state actors can have on foreign policy when their interests align with those of the government, and when bureaucratic unity is achieved at the ministerial level.

    Conclusion

    The Valemax case illustrates how high-level diplomacy, strategic concessions, and structural asymmetries converge to shape internationaleconomic relations. Initially, Vale attempted to resolve the ban on the Valemax vessels through technical and commercial negotiations, including outreach to Chinese ports and shipowners. However, these efforts proved insufficient in the face of China’s regulatory apparatus, particularly the Ministry of Transport’s Circular 13, which centralized decision-making and effectively barred the vessels. Only after President Dilma Rousseff raised the issue directly with Premier Wen Jiabao in 2012 did meaningful progress occur, leading to the creation of a bilateral working group coordinated by Brazil’s MDIC and China’s NDRC. This sequence of events highlights the central role of high-level diplomacy in overcoming regulatory barriers in China and underscores the interdependence between commercial initiatives and political channels in relations with China.

    The ultimate resolution of the impasse came through a strategic compromise: the sale of Valemax vessels to Chinese state-owned enterprises (SOEs), including COSCO and China Merchants Energy Shipping. This maneuver allowed Vale to regain access to Chinese ports, but only by relinquishing direct ownership and operational control over its fleet. While this solution was publicly framed as a “win-win” outcome, the distribution of benefits was notably asymmetrical. Chinese shipyards gained from the initial construction contracts, Chinese banks profited from financing, and Chinese SOEs secured logistical control over iron ore imports. In effect, the episode exemplifies how China’s “win-win” discourse often conceals a strategy of layered economic gain, in which market access is contingent upon structural concessions by foreign actors.

    The broader implications of the Valemax case point to significant institutional and strategic asymmetries in the relationship between Brasilia and Beijing. Brazil’s capacity to act as a sovereign commercial actor was curtailed by its limited influence over Chinese regulatory decisions and by the absence of institutional mechanisms to challenge such barriers. China’s party-state system, by contrast, enabled a coordinated response that aligned bureaucratic authority, state-owned enterprises, and strategic industrial policy. As a result, Vale’s efforts to assert control over freight logistics were ultimately subordinated to China’s domestic priorities. This asymmetry reveals the constraints firms face in accessing China’s tightly regulated market and underscores the necessity of state support and diplomatic engagement in defending strategic commercial interests. The case demonstrates the importance of aligning foreign economic policy with a clear understanding of China’s regulatory landscape, where access is mediated not only by market dynamics but also by political and institutional considerations.
