SPECIAL ISSUE: WHERE IS THE SEA IN IR? • Contexto int. 47 (2) • 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20244603e20220090 linkcopy

Fluid Boundaries: Reassessing Maritime Spaces and Nomadic Waves in International Relations Theory

Fronteiras Fluidas: Reavaliando os Espaços Marítimos e as Ondas Nómadas na Teoria das Relações Internacionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Covering more than seventy percent of the Earth’s surface, the sea is a pivotal element in International Relations. It has served as the stage for critical global interactions, conflicts, and shifts, from the ascendancy of the Iberian empires in the 16th century to the waning of British dominance in the 20th century. Today, it remains at the heart of contemporary geopolitical tensions, exemplified by disputes in the South China Sea and the opening of new maritime routes in the Arctic due to global warming. Yet, despite its undeniable importance, the sea poses a unique challenge to International Relations, stemming from its intrinsic complexity and the tendency to apply land-based frameworks to maritime contexts. In order to help the field to overcome some of these theoretical challenges, this article draws on psychoanalytic theories and current discussions in philosophy and anthropology to suggest that the discipline’s difficulty in addressing maritime issues arises from a rigid adherence to obsolete, binary views on nomadism and sedentarism. Therefore, the interplay between nomadic and sedentary drives is sublimated in the development of institutions and maritime technologies, enabling state territorial nomadism, contrasting with the suppression of dominance through the embrace of alterity.

    Key words
    International Relations theory; maritime geopolitics; nomadism and sedentarism; psychoanalytic theory; state territorial nomadism

    Resumo

    Cobrindo mais de setenta por cento da superfície da Terra, o mar é um elemento central nas Relações Internacionais. Tem servido de palco a interações, conflitos e mudanças globais cruciais, desde a ascendência dos impérios ibéricos no século XVI até ao declínio do domínio britânico no século XX. Atualmente, continua no centro das tensões geopolíticas contemporâneas, de que são exemplo as disputas no Mar do Sul da China e a abertura de novas rotas marítimas no Ártico devido ao aquecimento global. No entanto, apesar da sua inegável importância, o mar representa um desafio único para as Relações Internacionais, decorrente da sua complexidade intrínseca e da tendência para aplicar enquadramentos baseados em terra a contextos marítimos. A fim de ajudar o campo a ultrapassar alguns destes desafios teóricos, este artigo baseia-se em teorias psicanalíticas e em discussões atuais na filosofia e na antropologia para sugerir que a dificuldade da disciplina em abordar as questões marítimas resulta de uma adesão rígida a visões binárias e obsoletas sobre nomadismo e sedentarismo. Por conseguinte, a interação entre as pulsões nómadas e sedentárias é sublimada no desenvolvimento de instituições e tecnologias marítimas, permitindo o nomadismo territorial do Estado, contrastando com a supressão do domínio através do abraço da alteridade

    Palavras-chave
    teoria das Relações Internacionais; geopolítica marítima; nomadismo e sedentarismo; teoria psicanalítica; nomadismo territorial do Estado

    Introduction

    In cartographic representations, the Earth’s surface is delineated by lines demarcating national territories. Yet, the vast expanses of international waters constitute the most significant unclaimed areas. The seas and oceans, less subjected to human control compared to land, are predominantly seen as conduits for passage. They epitomise the ultimate challenge for expressing humanity’s nomadic impulses, yet they are far from being mere voids, as discussed by Carvalho and Leira (2022), Armitage, Bashford and Sivasundaram (2018), and Steinberg (2001).

    The discourse on nomadism, while established in International Relations literature, particularly concerning migrations, often harks back to the transition from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic era as a shift from nomadic to sedentary lifestyles. This perspective is considered outdated in this context. Firstly, Lévi-Strauss (1952) contends that categorising cultures as either nomadic or sedentary is both prejudiced and anachronistic, failing to acknowledge the breadth of human diversity. Secondly, we align with the views of Deleuze and Guattari (1995) and Bauman (2013), who argue that contemporary history is marked by the simultaneous existence of nomadic and sedentary modes of inhabiting spaces.

    The contention over land between nomadic and sedentary communities extends to the maritime realm. This dichotomy, intertwined with the violence of warfare, is also mitigated through the sublimation involved in establishing regulations and technologies that bring routine and predictability to maritime navigation. Consequently, while shipping is facilitated, the settlement of people on land is solidified.

    In contemporary international society, an understanding has emerged to alleviate this tension, conceptualising ships as mobile territories and ports as spaces that temporarily integrate these nomadic territories (ships) into national territories. Cólas (2022: 43), drawing on Bach (2011) and inspired by Bruno Latour, suggests that ships navigate space without forfeiting their role as sites of production, akin to immovable assets. However, the significance of ports extends beyond their operational functions; they are pivotal in facilitating the territorial nomadism of the Modern State, inherently predicated on sedentarism.

    From ancient Mediterranean trade routes to today’s global networks of freighters, bulk carriers, and oil tankers, it is primarily through maritime channels that significant movements occur, enabling humans to explore the unknown, satisfy their exploratory urges, and find respite from the mainland’s constraints. To view the process of homeostasis – unattainable through mere sedentary existence – through a solely ideological lens oversimplifies the complex and contradictory nature of human existence.

    Ideologies serve to imbue the existential void of human limitations – neither omnipresence nor omniscience – with meaning (Fiori 2021a; Ricœur 1994). Realist or liberal paradigms strive to reconcile with the European man’s incapacity to emulate divine perfection in mastery and creation. This historical trajectory is characterised by the cyclical creation and transformation of structures (institutions), impacting both maritime and terrestrial realms, where the dichotomy between nomadism and sedentarism persists. The concept of territory, extending beyond linguistic constructs, is intertwined with survival, resource access, and interpersonal interactions. Ideologies, transcending deterministic and binary perspectives, lend significance to actions that shape spatial configurations. The interplay between drives, while instrumental in the historical construction of structures marked by cyclicality, remains relational. Life and death, as antagonistic drives, are not dichotomous but coexist concurrently. Within this repetitive cycle, change occurs, and the sea has emerged as a conduit for the dissemination and broadening of cultural exchanges and (dis)encounters.

    The control of maritime routes, while driven by the quest for geostrategic positions to assert sovereignty, does not fully encapsulate the essence of human endeavour. This is because, beyond conflict, there exists a fascination with the unfamiliar. Similarly, the mobility facilitated by maritime transport, with its established protocols and technological advancements, transcends its role as a mere facilitator of capitalism, despite contributing to its perpetuation. Likewise, the reduction of maritime commerce to a mere expression of the natural inclination towards rational exchange, as posited by liberal theory, overlooks the inherent subjectivity and adventurous spirit of embarking on a vessel to explore the unknown.

    The sea and land beckon humanity to roam, while also urging settlement on terra firma. International waters enable navigation, yet they also pose a cautionary note to coastlines about potential discoveries or threats emanating from their depths. Conversely, the land offers sanctuary from external aggressions, whether from the sea or terrestrial sources. However, stagnation on land can lead to resource depletion. Humans have the capacity to adopt both nomadic and sedentary lifestyles, encapsulated in the adage: “To navigate is necessary; to live is not.”1

    This study aims to explore the concepts of nomadism and sedentarism through the lens of psychoanalysis, drawing on Freud’s and Lacan’s theories of drives, and their implications for geopolitics, particularly in the context of maritime flows. We propose to examine the critical and sufficient role of maritime transport in facilitating the nomadic tendencies of state territoriality in the contemporary international arena.

    To achieve this, we will initially delineate the concepts of the nomadic and sedentary drives. This section will trace the origins of these concepts from psychoanalytic theories of life and death drives and discuss their relevance to International Relations studies, especially in the context of maritime geopolitics. The interplay between nomadic and sedentary is sublimated in the development of institutions and maritime technologies, enabling state territorial nomadism, contrasting with the suppression of dominance through the embrace of alterity.

    The third section will explore how certain nations, grappling with the dynamics of nomadism and sedentarism, edge closer to a nuanced understanding of power by harmonising the continental defence of their territorial sovereignty with their maritime prowess.

    To be or not to be? The dilemma of nomadic vs sedentary states in international relations

    Traditionally, nomadism is depicted as a form of social organisation, surpassed by sedentarism, which heralded the transition from the Paleolithic to the Neolithic era, characterised by the domestication of animals and the advent of agriculture. The argument posited here is that nomadism, when interpreted as an instinctual drive, vies with the sedentary spatial organisation that human beings enact in international maritime transport.

    The emergence of the Modern State, an entity rooted in sedentary principles through the demarcation of territories, has been made feasible by the conventions, routines, and technologies that facilitate transport, allowing the nomadic drive to be sublimated. Without these mechanisms, the interstices for peace and relative predictability in international interactions would vanish, rendering the notion of ‘otherness’ elusive.

    Fiori (2021b: 450) contends that the transition from nomadic to sedentary living marked a pivotal era, where peoples, nations, and empires were embroiled in conflicts, acknowledging the futility of peaceful coexistence amidst diverse and intractable interests and “ethical truths”. Aligned with a sceptical view on the unattainability of perpetual peace, Fiori (2021b) echoes the foundational premise of this discussion: humanity is inherently divided and internally conflicted. This discord arises from the unfulfilled quest for completeness within oneself, which in turn affects human interactions with nature (Baumann and Dessal 2017).

    As the concept of the Modern State evolved, the intensity of inter-community conflicts escalated. However, the transition to sedentarism did not signify a complete departure from nomadism; rather, how could the inherent clash arising from the diversity of interests and beliefs occur in the absence of nomadic flux? It represents a continuous juxtaposition under constant strain. When unresolved by international diplomacy, it culminates in conflict.

    Fiori (2021b: 452) employs categories inherent to the human psyche to probe the origins of peace and war, rooted in the Western foundational myths of a deity sovereign over rules, juxtaposed with humanity’s attempts to usurp this dominion2. The question arises: why should nomadism, embedded in the evolutionary trajectory of the human species and the development of diverse cultures, be any different? Can the nomadic impulse, ingrained in the human psyche for millennia, simply vanish overnight? The crux of the matter lies in the tension between nomadic and sedentary lifestyles, representing the spatial manifestation of the internal struggle between the conscious and unconscious mind, oscillating between the desires for security and freedom, as discussed by Bauman and Dessal (2017). This dichotomy stems from the life and death drives, central themes in psychoanalysis.

    The notion of ‘drive’ encapsulates the subject’s motivating force (Laplanche and Pontalis 1991: 394; Roudinesco and Plon 1998: 628). Chemama (1995: 177) suggests that given its multifaceted nature, it is prudent to consider drives in the plural, perceived as antagonistic or dual forces. Engaging with this concept presents challenges, such as the propensity to conflate it with instinct, though drives are more deeply rooted in mythological symbolism than in biological terms. Thus, they do not present as tangible clinical phenomena or have direct implications for analytical techniques. Nonetheless, drives are pivotal in Freudian and Lacanian psychoanalysis, denoting the force propelling the subject towards fleeting satisfaction (jouissance).

    However, the fulfilment derived from drives is inherently temporary; complete satisfaction remains elusive, perpetually reigniting tension. The object of desire is invariably somewhat inadequate, never fully satiating its intended purpose (Chemama 1995: 181). Given this transient nature of pleasure, drives can lead to either repression or sublimation.

    This unresolvable split between drives, in the context of the dichotomy between settling and wandering, manifests in the creation of laws, the practice of diplomacy, and technological advancements in maritime transport. These elements perpetuate a cyclical movement between sublimation via alterity and repression through control. Consequently, both sedentary and nomadic drives encapsulate the life and death instincts. Roudinesco (2008: 102-104) posits that sublimation through politics is juxtaposed with the perversion manifested in the domination of others for pleasure. In this light, regulating the movement of populations to facilitate or restrict access embodies a tension with the establishment of treaties that permit the flow of nomadism and the embrace of alterity.

    In an environment perceived as anarchic, under the shadow of a deity meting out eternal punishment for our failure to conquer inner demons, leading to a perpetual dissatisfaction with oneself and others, the Western European narrative culminated in the establishment of the State. This creation aimed to shield us from our inherent mistrust, characterised by the centralisation of power within a defined territorial realm, perceived as property, and enveloped in an ethnocentric ethos. This ethos, akin to a social cohesion around these territories, sets them apart from others deemed inferior in a hierarchical schema (Dussel 1995)3.

    Beyond the State’s domain lies the unknown, the realm of the ‘barbarian’ traversing ambiguous, untamed spaces devoid of governance. Thus, the sedentary European State seeks to assert control over its territory and extend its influence over international, foreign terrains, dictating conditions for passage, monopolising routes, and annexing resources.

    However, this quest for security paradoxically begets insecurity as it encroaches upon realms beyond one’s jurisdiction. Yet, this dilemma transcends mere control; engaging with the unknown is vital for survival, beyond the confines of subjugating others. The nomadic impulse strains against the sedentary fabric of existence. Concurrently, the Modern State, oscillating between security and liberty, sustains itself by enabling its denizens to interact with external entities and vice versa. Thus, suppressing the nomadic impulse undermines the recognition that movement, the flux of arrivals and departures, is fundamental to the psychological equilibrium of the human psyche.

    It is crucial to distinguish nomadism from mere mobility. A ship facilitates mobility, serving a functional role, yet it also embodies the quintessential human endeavour to navigate the dichotomy of nomadism versus sedentarism, a subjective and contentious state.

    The nomadism inherent in the territoriality of States, enabled through infrastructures, technologies, and conventions, represents a form of sublimation addressing the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary impulses, themselves derivatives of the life and death instincts. Far from being a desolate expanse, the sea offers a unique realm where territories can traverse and anchor within others.

    The intrinsic human oscillation between nomadism and sedentarism is poignantly captured in Bauman’s reflections:

    Jacques Attali has recently suggested that it is the image of the labyrinth which nowadays comes to dominate, even if surreptitiously, our thinking about the future and our own part in it; that image becomes the principal mirror in which our civilization in its present stage contemplates its own likeness. The labyrinth as an allegory of the human condition was a message transmitted by the nomads to the settlers. Millennia have passed, and the settlers have finally acquired the self-confidence and courage to rise to the challenge of the labyrinthine fate. “In all European languages”, Attali points out, ‘the word labyrinth became a synonym of artificial complexity, useless darkness, tortuous system, impenetrable thicket. ‘Clarity’ became a synonym of logic. The settlers set about making the walls transparent, the devious passages straight and well signed, the corridors well lit. They also produced guidebooks and clear-cut, unambiguous instructions for the use of all future wanderers about which turns to take and which to avoid. They did all this only to discover in the end that the labyrinth is firmly in place; if anything, the labyrinth has become yet more treacherous and confusing owing to the illegible tangle of criss-crossing footprints, the cacophony of commands and the continuous addition of new twisting passages to the ones already left behind and new dead ends to the ones already blundered into. The settlers have become “involuntary nomads”, belatedly recalling the message they received at the beginning of their historical travels and trying desperately to recover its forgotten contents which – as they suspect – may well carry the “wisdom necessary for their future”. Once more, the labyrinth becomes the master image of the human condition – and it means “the opaque place where the layout of the roads may not obey any law”. Chance and surprise rule in the labyrinth, which signals the defeat of Pure Reason. (Baumann 2013: 149-150)

    Thus, escaping the pendulum swing between these impulses, embedded in the unconscious’s maze, is unattainable. The dichotomy of life and death is defining, yet the establishment of norms and agreements transcends mere domination. This interplay is often mistaken for an endeavour to secure reciprocity and self-determination as survival strategies.

    The oscillatory movement around the drives perpetuates in a cycle of repetition, with institutions being formed, altered, and dissolved in the neurotic process of division.

    International society has, notwithstanding, identified certain, albeit temporary and precarious, solutions to navigate the conflict between nomadism and sedentarism, extending beyond mere dominion by one state over others and over international waters. Maritime law, which will be examined in greater detail in the subsequent section, embodies these potential resolutions.

    In terms of commerce, various procedures and routines precede the supremacy of any particular international order and even the capitalist mode of production itself. The consensus that a ship represents a fragment of a nation’s territory, permitting its movement, has ancient roots. Historically, from city-states to nation-states, it has been acknowledged that a key site of international engagement (the port) must honour the sovereignty of these maritime territories (ships). Moreover, the practices involved in setting sail, navigating, and docking have been institutionalised well before the advent of the Modern State and the capitalist economy that persists to this day (Steinberg, 2001). Yet, these routines have undergone significant transformations in the dynamics of time and space with the intensification of capitalist production.

    The international regulations governing maritime transport are collective achievements, emerging from the interplay among individuals. The modern capitalist state, wielding control through institutions and technologies that facilitate the spatial mobility of people beyond its borders, is a manifestation of the European historical trajectory. Its utilitarian, goal-oriented approach has proven to be an effective means of domination. To overlook the enduring presence of the nomadic impulse is to ignore the existence of communities for whom nomadism is intrinsic to their identity and self-recognition.

    Lévi-Strauss (1976) contends that cultures cannot be ranked based on the nomadic-sedentary distinction. Yet, the post-Holocaust experiences of the Jewish people, alongside the diasporas of African nomadic civilizations, illustrate the challenges nomads face in a world dominated by sedentary states.

    While capitalism both relies on and amplifies nomadism, particularly with the establishment of the Modern State, not all manifestations of the nomadic impulse stem from capitalist dynamics. The deterritorialization of production, facilitated by mega-vessels and Special Economic Zones (SEZs), marks the current phase of capitalist globalization (Ong, 2006; Colás, 2022). Nonetheless, capitalism represents merely one model of production that thrives on the interplay between nomadism and territoriality, the latter being an attribute of the Modern State and synonymous with sedentarism.

    Furthermore, even in the feudal era European city-states hosted itinerant trade fairs amidst isolation and restricted currency circulation. In these bartering environments, with minimal surplus, the development of burgos and the ascendancy of merchant power were notably fostered. The autarkic production model of the feudal system, as termed by the physiocrats, did not alleviate the tension between nomadic and sedentary groups.

    Thus, the concept of “territorial nomadism of states” is employed as an analytical tool, denoting the ability of states to navigate the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary impulses. This dichotomy arises from the inherent human dissatisfaction in reconciling the desire for freedom with the need for security, a tension rooted in the life and death drives. Maritime transport, governed by regulatory conditions and productive forces, epitomizes the essential mechanism for accommodating state territorial nomadism, engendered by the conflict between anchoring and setting sail. This, in turn, supports the capitalist production system but is not confined to it. Naval activity serves as a means of both domination and fostering alterity (Steinberg, 2001).

    Cowen (2014: 10) offers an insightful perspective on the interplay between capitalism, the state, and territory, challenging the notion of deterritorialization. Cowen suggests, “The issue at hand is not the diminishing significance of territory but rather a nuanced shift: the unfolding of a specific historical-geographical manifestation of territory structured via nation-states–namely, territoriality”.

    Furthermore, Cowen (2014) posits that if territory is distinguished from territoriality, logistics emerges as a pivotal force in compressing time, space, and territory. This compression catalyses globalisation and reshapes jurisdictions (Cowen 2014). The simultaneous interplay of nomadic and sedentary impulses, often in conflict, fuels the forces underpinning globalisation. Hence, the inherent paradox within logistics – its capacity to engender both life and death – should not be surprising, as movement inherently carries the potential for both.

    The dynamics of nomadism and a sedentary lifestyle serve as spatial metaphors for the life and death drives; however, they are not directly analogous. The nomadic impulse does not solely embody the life drive, nor does sedentarism exclusively represent the death drive. Both nomadism and sedentarism encapsulate elements of life and death drives, embodying their complexities. Despite the antagonistic nature of these drives, they are not binary opposites and operate concurrently. The pursuit of pleasure, whether through repression or sublimation, offers a temporary reconciliation of this dichotomy, engaging with one drive or the other in a continuous cycle of repetition. This cycle fosters the creation and transformation of institutions around desires. The resolution of this dynamic between movement and stasis is provisional and fleeting, yet it imprints lasting effects.

    The ambiguity and uncertainty inherent in human subjectivity are precisely what endow it with profound creative potential in action.

    Territorial nomadism of states enabled by international maritime relations regime

    In ancient city-states, pivotal decisions revolved around opening ports to friendly nations and the quandaries of wartime involvement, illustrating the intrinsic role of external engagement in the concept of territoriality. This historical backdrop frames the ongoing dilemma between settlement and mobility. This section will outline how maritime transport serves as a conduit for the territorial nomadism of states, addressing the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary impulses discussed previously. The discourse will encompass the evolution of International Maritime Law and the technological advancements in the maritime transport sector.

    In modern times, nations wield exclusive legislative powers within their territories, imposing tariff and administrative barriers on imports and granting permissions for port construction and maritime operations. However, the essence of international goods transport, both maritime and aerial, lies in the traversal of international zones beyond any single state’s sovereignty (Felix 2019; Casella 2022). Instituting a semblance of order in the ostensibly unregulated expanse of international waters is pivotal for facilitating inter-country mobility, thereby enabling state territorial nomadism.

    Historically, the maritime domain has been fraught with piracy and contention over maritime route control. The Mediterranean, with its rich history of coastal conflicts in the Italian peninsulas and Macedonia, epitomises these struggles, particularly between the Roman and Ottoman empires (Fiori 2021c; Özsu 2016; Steinberg 2001). The blockade of the Silk Road propelled Europe to seek alternative maritime passages to India and China, extending its expropriatory and dominative practices to foreign coasts, straits, and seas. These ventures expanded beyond mere resource expropriation to include human trafficking, notably the slave trade, making piracy a lucrative endeavour aimed at intercepting vessels carrying enslaved individuals.

    The era of European maritime nomadism, marked by the fear it instilled along the coasts of Africa, the Americas, and Asia, persisted until the late 19th century (Dussel 1993). The contemporary post-industrial framework of navigation, as recognised today, emerged in the latter half of the 20th century, alongside the globalisation of capitalism (Steinberg 2001).

    Maritime stability was increasingly assured as the military capabilities of global powers were enhanced to safeguard the safety and navigability of vessels. Yet, it’s imperative to also consider the technological advancements and the establishment of the international maritime transport regime. This regime began to solidify throughout the 20th century, introducing key principles such as the extraterritoriality of ships based on ownership and flag; the carrier’s liability for cargo in international waters; the obligations under ship charter contracts and within shipboard spaces; and the passage rights of foreign vessels through foreign waters (Casella 2022). These elements are crucial for understanding what is referred to as the territorial nomadism of states, a concept integral to the contemporary landscape of capitalist globalisation.

    The international arena, particularly since the mid-20th century, has been characterised by a blend of competitive and cooperative state interactions, marked by a notable degree of predictability and the presence of multilateral forums for deliberation. Following the First World War, the League of Nations, established in 1920, represented the initial effort to create an international platform surpassing traditional treaties and conventions. This aimed at standardising rules among nations as a means to prevent armed conflict, heightened by the looming threat of nuclear warfare (Seintenfus 2018).

    The Second World War highlighted the limitations of this early attempt at establishing a generic international body. Nonetheless, the post-war period saw a renewed pursuit of multilateral forums to devise norms and regulatory standards governing state interactions across various domains, extending beyond mere security and international disputes (Seintenfus 2018).

    Consequently, the UN System emerged as a significant platform, accommodating a range of regulated topics while allowing for relative autonomy among member states (Colás 2022).     Even prior to the establishment of the UN, the 19th century witnessed the emergence of international organisations dedicated to maritime transport. The International Hydrographic Organization (IHO), founded in 1889, focuses on hydrographic and oceanographic conventions, significantly influencing nautical charting and maritime navigation safety (IHO 2018). Following this, the Comité Maritime International (CMI) was established in 1897 to standardise maritime laws across various facets, serving as an arbitration body on issues from flag registrations to sea freight insurance contracts (CMI 2018).

    The International Chamber of Commerce (ICC), initiated in 1919 by business professionals, established the International Court of Arbitration in 1923. It became a pivotal entity in private international law, offering standard contract templates for maritime cargo transportation, freight contracting, insurance, and payment methods in international trade. Throughout the interwar and post-war periods, the ICC played a significant role in the Bretton Woods system and garnered support within UN committees focused on International Maritime Trade. Despite being an international non-governmental organisation, its extensive institutional capacity has enabled it to establish representative chambers in numerous countries.

    The International Maritime Organization (IMO), under the UN’s institutional framework, was conceived in 1948, focusing on maritime safety in its broadest sense, from shipbuilding technical standards to maritime traffic regulations. The founding treaty of the organisation was activated in early 1959. The period from the late 1960s to the late 1970s saw an escalation in negotiation rounds following tanker accidents involving oil spills. Subsequently, environmental concerns have been integrated into maritime safety discussions, leading to stricter technical standards for ship construction and operational protocols for maritime vessels and ports (Fiorati 1997; Steinberg 2001).

    Security and defence discussions, particularly concerning maritime territorial disputes between nations, were addressed at the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS) and NATO (Fiorati 1997; Steinberg 2001). The sea, viewed as a territorial extension in geopolitical terms, reflects the defence capabilities of states. Moreover, the ocean has become a contested resource due to the surge in industrial production activities. The establishment of exclusive economic zones as a means of territorialising the sea exemplifies the sedentary impulse within an environment inherently resistant to such practices, achievable only with substantial naval military capabilities.

    In 1945, US President Harry Truman expanded the jurisdiction of the US Navy, a move swiftly emulated by other nations. This development, compounded by the tensions of the bipolar Cold War era, set the stage for significant international maritime law reform. In 1973, under the auspices of the United Nations Commission on the Law of the Sea, the inaugural UNCLOS was initiated in New York. The negotiation process spanned approximately nine years, culminating in its endorsement by the UN in 1982 (Fiorati 1997). However, the convention only came into definitive effect in 1994, following its ratification by the sixtieth nation, Guyana (Fiorati 1997). This milestone established a territorial sea boundary of 12 nautical miles and an exclusive economic zone of 200 nautical miles, setting forth guidelines for navigation, economic exploitation, and defence measures within territorial waters, among other aspects (UN 2018).

    The synergy between international multilateral entities and private arbitration and standardisation bodies, exemplified by the collaboration between the UN and the ICC, illustrates the construction of state territorial nomadism through the interplay of nomadic and sedentary impulses. Instruments such as letters of credit and other payment mechanisms have become universally accepted in private international commerce. Similarly, contract types (International Terms of Commerce – Inconterms) concerning cargo liability among exporters, importers and carriers, alongside the delineation of cargo ownership in international waters through bills of lading issued by private shipping companies, have become standard practice.

    These routine practices have been regimented and institutionalised through the establishment of the UN and other multilateral forums. Without such formalisation of customs and routines in international maritime trade, shaped by the oscillation between nomadic and sedentary drives, the level of predictability required to ensure the smooth flow of goods as seen today would likely be unattainable. The self-serving interests arising from commercial exchanges inherently conflict with entrenched habits and traditions.

    Krasner (2012) posits that customs and practices can give rise to regimes in their own right, reinforcing and perpetuating the dynamics of self-interest, political dominance, and prevalent values. However, alongside this utilitarian perspective, standardised customs evolve over time, establishing routine practices that underpin the global maritime trade framework.

    In the aftermath of the First World War, a comprehensive framework of rules was established to accommodate technological advancements, as evidenced by conventions on the maritime transport of goods: the Hague Rules (1924), the Hague-Visby Rules (1968 and 1979), the UN Convention on the Carriage of Goods by Sea (1978), the United Nations Commission on International Trade Law (UNCITRAL) Model Law (1996), and the Draft Convention spanning 13 sections between 2002 and 2008 (Tong-Jiang and Peng 2009). These conventions have been instrumental in shaping the regulatory landscape of maritime commerce.

    Additionally, various agreements and treaties have addressed the registration of ships under national flags. The advent of information technology has further streamlined financial transactions and the flow of information in commercial exchanges. The introduction of containerization and the expansion of ship capacities have revolutionised maritime transport rules, exerting pressure on national regulations governing port construction and operations (Ong 2006; Cowen 2014).

    Until the 1970s, protectionist policies dominated port activities and maritime transport (Slack and Starr 1999). The widespread adoption of containers heralded a new spatial and operational paradigm for ports, extending shipowners’ roles to encompass port operations. This shift allowed shipowners to achieve economies of scale by managing door-to-door cargo processes and encouraged market consolidation. The containerized transport system not only enhanced port profitability through increased cargo volumes but also facilitated the vertical integration of the maritime cargo transport chain (Oliveira and Ludolf 2017).

    Parallel to the trend of productive deterritorialization, transport has assumed a pivotal role in the global distribution of goods (Oliveira and Ludolf 2017). Companies have decentralised production to leverage competitive advantages specific to each country, thereby enhancing predictability in production and logistics. This development has placed demands on maritime transport to lower costs (Oliveira, You and Coelho 2021).

    Nations have subsequently had to revise their port operations and the interface between ports and urban areas, which were traditionally viewed as state monopolies providing public services (Verhetsel and Sel 2009). Many countries have centralised strategic decisions, such as regulating port relationships with the shipping industry and global value chains, while decentralising operational decisions, including intersectoral activities and concession plans.

    In this context, national authorities have sought to maintain port competitiveness within the global value chain.

    In the 1990s, increased trade liberalisation and productive interdependence between nations spurred investments and the growth of global terminal operators. Nations began to permit international shipowners to manage both long-haul and cabotage lines, as well as port operations (Wilmsmeier and Monios 2016). However, it is important to note that while the World Trade Organization (WTO) advocated for the liberalisation of service markets, port operations were exempt from this push due to public concessions being a recognised exception to free trade under the WTO’s regulations; this exemption did not extend to sea freight. Despite this, many countries advanced the liberalisation of their port and maritime sectors to attract foreign investment in infrastructure.     This leads to the question: how have the legal regime and productive processes of maritime transport influenced the territorial nomadism of the Modern State? This adaptation was crucial for the perpetuation of capitalism and its global expansion. Maritime transport customs and rituals have achieved enhanced predictability and stability, not only through technological advancements but also via the military might of global powers. Thus, while the nomadic impulse has paralleled the globalisation of capital, it should not be conflated with capitalism itself. The technologies and regulations shaping international maritime trade interact with the dichotomy of nomadic and sedentary impulses, in a continuous cycle marked by the tension between dominating repression and the sublimation towards otherness.

    What has been described here in this section on the Regime of International Maritime Relations converges with Steinberg’s theses on the different perspectives of the social construction of the Oceans. Organizations focused on the vision of the ocean as a provider, others on the theme of the void as a space for traffic, or as a battlefield, or more focused on the ocean as a set of natural physical phenomena and processes (Steinberg 2001: 5 and 11). These different perspectives of the social construction of the ocean compete and intertwine in a contemporary social construction that marks this post-industrial period. These sets of norms, inventions and routines from different perspectives have at their root the division between advancing over the sea while having the land as a reference, that is, it is not only traveling over the continent, but also over the water. In this analogy, there are a myriad of possibilities for how to recognize the ocean. The adventure on the sea resides in the discomfort between nomadism and sedentarism and leads human creativity to different ways of socially conceiving the ocean.

    Territorial nomadism in maritime transport: a dualistic force for state existence

    The Modern State, characterised by the centralisation of power, rulemaking, and the exertion of force within specific territories, emerged in Europe against a backdrop of migratory movements, particularly marked by the tension between sedentary kingdoms and nomadic groups. Drawing from the philosophical pendulum swinging between freedom and security, from Machiavelli to Hobbes, the state endeavours to mediate these intrinsic human impulses that fuel perpetual dissatisfaction with oneself, others, and the surroundings (Baumann and Dessal 2017). Thus, within the capitalist framework, the state wields power and domination as tools to affirm its position in an inherently anarchic global setting.

    This drive for self-preservation and the need to exert control over others underpin the formation of the modern international system, where state power is transient and requires constant assertion (Fiori 2015). In this context, state power – understood as the capacity to influence – needs to be actively exercised, transcending crisis moments to permeate the routine of international relations. As the Modern State evolves, so too do the routines and technological advancements that facilitate the flow of materials, information, and finances, all of which have significant geopolitical implications. The most influential states are those that successfully navigate the life and death drives, reconciling the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary impulses. This invokes the question of divine omnipotence, seemingly unbounded by such dualistic constraints.

    Prior to the advent of the modern European state and its practices of nomadic expropriation, Paul Kennedy highlights the exceptional navigational prowess of Ming Imperial China:

    [...] It is also not surprising to learn that the Chinese had turned to overseas exploration and trade. The magnetic compass was another Chinese invention, some of their junks were as large as later Spanish galleons, and commerce with the Indies and the Pacific islands was potentially as profitable as that along the caravan routes. Naval warfare had been conducted on the Yangtze many decades earlier–in order to subdue the vessels of Sung China in the 1260s, Kublai Khan had been compelled to build his own great fleet of fighting ships, equipped with projectile-throwing machines–and the coastal grain trade was booming in the early fourteenth century. In 1420, the Ming navy was recorded as possessing 1,350 combat vessels, including 400 large floating fortresses and 250 ships designed for long-range cruising. Such a force eclipsed, but did not include, the many privately managed vessels which were already trading with Korea, Japan, Southeast Asia, and even East Africa by that time, and bringing revenue to the Chinese State, which sought to tax this maritime commerce. (Kennedy 2010: 17)4

    In comparative terms, the contemporary naval capabilities of many powers bear resemblance to those seen during the transition from the Sung to the Ming dynasties. Kennedy (2010: 18) elaborates on the formidable Chinese navy by spotlighting Admiral Cheng Ho’s maritime expeditions between 1405 and 1433. These voyages involved hundreds of ships and tens of thousands of men traversing ports from Malacca and Ceylon to the Red Sea’s gateways and Zanzibar. During these naval ventures, they bestowed gifts upon local rulers who acknowledged the emperor’s supremacy, compelling the intransigent to follow suit.

    Yet, a crucial distinction arises. Unlike the Portuguese, Dutch, and other European invaders of the Indian Ocean expeditions, the Chinese, as Kennedy, a British historian, notes, neither plundered nor inflicted harm. Intriguingly, some historians and archaeologists suggest Cheng Ho’s nautical skills were advanced enough to circumnavigate Africa and ‘discover’ Portugal, predating Henry the Navigator’s expeditions south of Ceuta by several decades.

    The resurgence of Mongolian threats from the north led China to halt its maritime explorations to redirect military efforts towards its northern land borders. Adopting a more conservative stance, the Confucian bureaucracy viewed maritime nomadism as a risk, potentially compromising the continental territory’s integrity. Consequently, China began to withdraw from global engagement, confining foreign trade activities to the Pearl River Delta, particularly Macau (Kennedy 2010: 19). Decades later, European navigators would fill the void left by the Chinese junks, driven by a quest for domination through mercantile pursuits, laying the groundwork for global capitalism.

    Kennedy’s narratives reveal the evident nomadic drive in these Chinese maritime endeavours. Yet, he suggests that the outcomes of Chinese maritime nomadism diverged significantly from those of Europe. This perspective is echoed by Eric Tagliacozzo (2018), with additional instances of the nomad-sedentary dynamic beyond the Mediterranean documented by Armitage, Bashford, and Suvasundaram (2018).

    Nomadism is indeed a prerequisite for capitalism but does not equate to it, nor is it the sole pathway. For nomadism to fully enable the capitalist mode of production, it must be coupled with domination, effectively repressing the divide between nomadic and sedentary impulses. A pertinent example is the Lusitanian (Portuguese) State, which played a pivotal role in the emergence of the modern centralised sovereign state. Engaging in cultural exchanges with the Moors, Portugal was also at the forefront of maritime expeditions that facilitated colonial expropriation overseas from the late 15th to the early 16th century.

    Contrary to the Confucian bureaucratic stance, the Portuguese monarchy perceived maritime exploration as an opportunity to extend its dominion by appropriating resources and enslaving people beyond its borders. This strategy was particularly crucial when traditional trade routes were obstructed following the Ottoman Empire’s capture of Constantinople. Portugal’s maritime ventures were a strategic response to the dominance of the Habsburg Empire over the Iberian Peninsula, a dominance temporarily mitigated during the Iberian Union from 1580 to 1640, which marked the peak of Habsburg influence in the European balance of power.

    Portugal’s maritime endeavours were further bolstered by its longstanding alliance with the United Kingdom, formalised in the Treaty of Windsor in 1386. This alliance not only facilitated Portugal’s eventual independence from the Habsburgs but also countered Spanish colonial ambitions, particularly in the River Plate region of South America, until the late 19th century (Ricupero 2017). Moreover, this alliance secured the territorial integrity of what is now known as Brazil, based initially on mutual military support. The commercial dynamics of this relationship were later defined by the Treaty of Methuen in 1703. The strategic value of the Treaty of Windsor was underscored during the Napoleonic Wars, culminating in the Portuguese royal family’s relocation to Brazil, a move made feasible by maritime mobility.

    The Habsburgs capitalised on the nomadic potential of the seas to annex foreign territories across two distinct phases. Initially, through Spanish endeavours and subsequently, following the fragmentation of the Habsburg Empire post the Thirty Years’ War, via the Dutch. Spain’s territorial expansion was crucial, while the Netherlands, amidst the mercantile boom and the nascent stages of industrial capitalism, played a pivotal role in professionalising navigation and port operations (Arrighi 1996). The Hanseatic League further facilitated territorial nomadism in the Baltic region, enhancing maritime trade routines (North 2018). It pioneered a versatile financial system, introducing future value trading and interest collection on both state public debt and private maritime venture financing (Arrighi 1996). The shipowners and ports spanning Belgium to Germany, with their extensive river networks penetrating the European heartland, became vital nodes connecting the continent to its overseas expropriation networks through merchant navies (Kennedy 2010; Arrighi 1996).

    England, with its insular position, leveraged its maritime prowess alongside state organisational reforms. As a sizable island, England was sufficiently removed from the intense territorial rivalries of continental Europe, yet proximate enough to engage in mainland ventures and maintain a crucial trade hub (Kennedy 2010). Additionally, its network of extraterritorial conquests, augmented by treaties such as the one with Portugal, secured control over key trade routes. Notably, India emerged as a significant direct dominion, while Canada and the USA, owing to their distinct configurations, achieved greater autonomy, eventually severing colonial ties earlier than British holdings in Asia, Africa, and the Americas (Kennedy 2010).

    The United Kingdom navigated the dichotomy between sedentarism and nomadism through a utilitarian and pragmatic approach, centred on asymmetrical trade agreements. This strategy fostered relative political stability within its territories, paving the way for industrial advancement.

    Following its independence, the USA refined and expanded upon the British approach to managing sea and land. Initially formed as a confederation, the country later emerged from the Civil War as the first federalist republic. This form of territorial organisation provided the political stability needed for capitalist expansion across its vast continental lands, complemented by significant maritime capabilities, both commercial and military. These assets allowed the USA to project its power globally, eventually surpassing its former colonial master to lead the Western hegemonic bloc throughout the 20th century (Kennedy 2010; Arrighi 1996). Alfred Mahan (1890) underscored this synthesis of land settlement and maritime mobility, advocating for the construction of the Panama Canal in the mid-19th century to link the Pacific and Atlantic oceans. Mahan’s vision extended beyond military strategy to encompass the control of commercial routes, highlighting the importance of strategic naval capabilities (Mahan 1890; Kennedy 2010).

    While China remained isolated, the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR) emerged as another continental power after the Bolshevik Revolution. However, its geostrategic position was more precarious than that of the USA, which, on a global scale, mirrored England’s insular status relative to Europe in the 18th century (Kennedy 2010). Whether due to its vulnerable geostrategic stance or a doctrine prioritising border security, the USSR did not heed Mahan’s advice, focusing predominantly on military naval strength over commercial capacity.

    The shift in the global balance of power, no longer confined to Europe, became apparent in the first half of the 20th century with the World Wars. Concurrently, the international community, led by the Western bloc, sought to establish a maritime regime that would only achieve stability in the latter half of the 20th century, despite the deterrent effect of nuclear weapons in preventing direct conflicts between superpowers. As previously discussed, advancements in maritime law and technology have fostered a level of interconnectivity and interdependence that has reshaped the scope of maritime territorial nomadism in the contemporary era.

    Global powers have continually navigated the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary drives, often employing domineering repression. Presently, all the powers mentioned, with the exception of Portugal, are dominant forces in international maritime trade, playing significant roles in port operations and navigation, which are crucial for the functioning of transnational corporations. Furthermore, the naval prowess of these countries lends their diplomatic corps considerable leverage in negotiating maritime-related agreements.

    In the early 21st century, China re-emerged as a leading exporter of manufactured goods, reclaiming a position it lost post-Industrial Revolution. This revival can be attributed to its renewed embrace of maritime strategies, perceiving the sea as a conduit for its expansive nomadic drive. Following in the footsteps of Taiwan, Singapore, and Korea, China, under Deng Xiaoping’s leadership, reopened itself to global engagement through maritime initiatives. A notable development is the establishment of state-owned shipping companies, such as COSCO, capable of also functioning as port operators. On the continental front, the introduction of Special Economic Zones (SEZs) has been a successful strategy, balancing national sovereignty with global economic integration.

    SEZs, as implemented by various Asian nations, function similarly to ports but with expanded customs areas, welcoming foreign products and manufacturing operations without necessitating the nationalisation of production (Oliveira and Ludolf 2017). These zones effectively limit the influence of foreign productive forces within their port environs. They represent a nuanced approach to engaging with global capitalism’s deterritorialised production methods, offering an alternative to the European model of nomadic repression through domination, while simultaneously accommodating the unique form of nomadism inherent in today’s global capitalist framework.

    The present configuration of states, driven by the urge to channel their nomadic impulses within a framework constrained by military conflicts and technological advancements such as the internet, epitomises the nomadic-sedentary dichotomy. This has precipitated a critical phase in the maritime legal regime. On one side, there is an inability among nations to forge a consensus on customs and taxation procedures for imports and exports. On the other, amidst this regulatory ambiguity and the hegemonic bloc’s efforts to overturn its own established rules to preserve or regain its relative power advantage, an alternative trade network has emerged. This network is utilised by those sidelined, including countries like Iran, Russia, and Venezuela.

    Whether in the era of ancient maritime ventures or today’s era of complex, interconnected, and deterritorialized capitalist production, the divide between nomadic and sedentary tendencies persists. Yet, under the guise of territorial nomadism, global powers have devised varied approaches to engage with the ‘non-modern’, addressing this fundamental human dichotomy. The strategies employed by the Western hegemony and Eastern nations seeking alternativity to dominance do not escape the capitalist framework, nor do they sidestep neoliberal principles (Ong 2006).

    These approaches, whether through conflict or commerce, unveil contradictions and dilemmas in perpetuating the repressive European dynastic model, extending its logic to the seas – the quintessential domain of nomadism – as if imposing multilaterally agreed-upon rules were feasible. In maritime matters, the anthropocentric perspective, compounded by Eurocentric rational hubris, seeks to master the ‘unmasterable’, despite its own inability to master itself.

    Final considerations

    The sea, straddling continents and resistant to territorial control, remains a perennial source of contention. Simultaneously, it facilitates the movement between communities settled on land. The term ‘earth,’ etymologically stemming from ‘eorþe’ (meaning ‘dry’), used to denote the only planet in the solar system with a predominantly liquid surface, reflects an inherent desire for dominion – to achieve stability in the fluid expanse of the sea as on the solid ground. This dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary tendencies elicits a complex and contradictory experience of travel across both terrains, with true sedentarism seemingly feasible only on terra firma.

    Just as there is a divide between sea and land, humanity oscillates between nomadism and sedentarism. The contemporary resolution to this division, facilitated by the societal structures afforded by sedentarism, has enabled the Modern State – a cornerstone of the International System – to engage in territorial nomadism. The distinction between nomadic and sedentary states in International Relations is perceived as a drive, the impetus propelling entities towards their objectives amidst fleeting, oppositional forces. Continually grappling with the inability to find satisfaction in either settling or roaming, states resort to repression or sublimation in response to this enduring discontent.

    Repression involves diverting the object of desire to quell this restlessness through subjugation, leading to self-objectification. Sublimation, on the other hand, fosters the development of strategies for self-preservation and coexistence. Thus, the nomadic drive manifests when states strive to control passages and negotiate over international waters – endeavours to confront life and death in the uncharted. Conversely, the sedentary impulse has historically led to the establishment of the polis for protection against nomadic incursions and the vagaries of the external world. It has also ignited conflicts over imagined territorial boundaries, resulting in war and mortality. Therefore, the nomadic and sedentary drives intertwine with the concepts of life and death in a simultaneous, non-binary, yet conflicting manner.

    Territorial nomadism among states has been enabled by advancements in international maritime transport, underpinned by the solidification of legal regimes and technological enhancements in maritime navigation. These developments have lent greater predictability and stability to the practices and traditions inherent in maritime transport, while also taking into account the martial prowess of major powers in curbing piracy. Thus, while the nomadic drive has paralleled the global spread of capital, it should not be conflated with capitalism itself. The technologies and regulations shaping international maritime commerce reflect the ongoing dialectic between nomadic and sedentary impulses, perpetually oscillating between dominant repression and the pursuit of alterity.

    The use of maritime transport as a tool of domination by states, as a form of repression, is exemplified by modern powers that extend their influence over foreign territories through maritime projection from their terrestrial bases. However, states can also embrace alterity by structuring themselves in such a way as to accommodate the nomadic impulse, either through naval expansion or port development. This has been observed in recent times in Asian countries with the implementation of Special Economic Zones (SEZs), which engage with global capitalism without a complete departure from it. The historical example of Ming China illustrates that territorial nomadism can exist independently of capitalist production models or expropriatory relationships.

    This paper aims to provoke thought within the field of International Relations by incorporating psychoanalytic concepts, suggesting ample scope for further exploration in applying these ideas to the discipline’s phenomena. Consequently, the exploratory nature of this work may impart an impressionistic quality.

    As directions for future research, the following questions emerge: What broader geopolitical ramifications stem from the dichotomy between nomadic and sedentary impulses? How have other civilizations, beyond Europe and China and predating the modern era, navigated this divide? Conduct a study based on content analysis to identify actors and organizations in international maritime relations that are close to the archetypes presented by Steinberg (2001) of perspectives on the social construction of the ocean.
