The how, where, and what of organizational corruption: an integrative literature review

El cómo, dónde y qué de la corrupción organizacional: una revisión integradora de la literatura

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Research on organizational corruption has been approached from different perspectives. However, there is still a lack of a more comprehensive focus on the intersection of public and private management. This article presents a review of 589 academic studies. The predominance of quantitative research suggests the need for a more balanced approach that incorporates qualitative and mixed methods, enabling a deeper investigation of organizational corruption. Furthermore, studies on corruption published in top-ranked English-language journals are mainly concentrated in the northern hemisphere. This methodological dominance shows the need to broaden the scope of the investigations to include the southern hemisphere in these publications. Another aspect observed was the lack of empirical studies and the need to understand corruption as a multidimensional phenomenon. Future research must emphasize primary data analysis to generalize and validate corruption theories.

    Keywords:
    organizational corruption; integrative literature review; organizational studies; global south

    Resumen

    La investigación sobre la corrupción organizacional se ha abordado desde diferentes perspectivas, pero aún falta un enfoque más integral en la investigación organizacional en la intersección de la gestión pública y privada. En este artículo, realizamos una revisión de 589 artículos académicos. La predominancia de investigaciones cuantitativas sugiere la necesidad de un enfoque más equilibrado que incorpore métodos cualitativos y estudios mixtos para permitir una investigación más profunda de la corrupción organizacional. Además, los estudios sobre corrupción publicados en las principales revistas escritas en inglés están concentrados principalmente en el hemisferio norte. Esta predominancia metodológica muestra la necesidad de ampliar el alcance de las investigaciones para incluir el hemisferio sur en estos medios. Otro aspecto observado fue la falta de estudios empíricos y la necesidad de comprender la corrupción como un fenómeno multidimensional. Las investigaciones futuras deben enfatizar el uso de análisis de datos primarios para generalizar y validar teorías sobre la corrupción.

    Palabras clave:
    corrupción organizacional; revisión integradora de la literatura; estudios organizacionales; sur global

    1. INTRODUCTION

    Corruption is defined as the abuse of power for private benefit (Ashforth & Anand, 2003). It covers a range of unethical activities, from transactions between a private and a public agent to an enduring transnational scheme involving several agents. In this relation, it must be noted that the determinants of corruption vary between the individual and structural levels (Graaf, 2007). This variation in corruption is captured in several studies. For example, Jancsics (2019) defined different concepts of corruption by their type of resource transfer or the primary beneficiary. Other researchers shifted their focus on corporate corruption from public agents to private managers (Castro et al., 2020).

    Recent reviews have focused on different areas of corporate corruption (Castro et al., 2020) especially its articulations with international business (Bahoo et al., 2020). Corruption research commonly victimizes private organizations in front of public officials demands, however, the social phenomenon has intricate process from both sides that allows for its endurance over time (Arellano-Gault, 2019). Our research extends this literature by focusing on the intersection between public and private corruption and delineating the development of the corruption theory conjunctly in these fields. Specifically, we ask the following three questions to understand the landscape of corruption theory on public and private management fields:

    1. How have the researchers studied organizational corruption?

    2. Where are the studies conducted?

    3. What has been studied on organizational corruption?

    To answer the aforementioned questions, we conduct an integrative literature review (Callahan, 2010; Elsbach & van Knippenberg, 2020). We search for the keywords “corruption in organizations” and “organizational corruption.” This search and the subsequent filtration yielded 589 management and organization studies on public, non-profit, and private organizations (Arellano-Gault et al., 2013). From these articles, we extract their objectives, theories, concepts and definitions, method, empirical context, and findings. This approach integrates the micro and macro views of the organizing processes (Robichaud & Cooren, 2013).

    The review allowed us to present the main frameworks used to analyze corruption both in private and public management. Presenting the methods, theories, and state-of-the-art corruption research. Second, we examine the geographical distribution of corruption studies published in top-ranked international journals written in English and discuss the implications of this distribution. Methodologically, corruption has been researched from multiple perspectives and methods, allowing for its theory to expand but without enough empirical validation. Corruption can be considered a global phenomenon, but cross-cultural analysis has showed that different cultures and institutional environment affects its occurrence.

    This article is divided into the following five sections. First, we explain the need to study organizational corruption and present examples of corruption. Then we describe the review methodology. After that, we present the results of our review. Specifically, it shows the methods used to study corruption and the geographic location of the studies and provides some of the most interesting paths to develop corruption literature. Then we present a discussion section with the main results of the review and suggestions for future research. Finally, we present the concluding remarks and limitations this approach.

    2. WHY STUDY ORGANIZATIONAL CORRUPTION?

    Corruption is often defined as the misuse of power or position by a public agent for personal gain to the detriment of society (Darsareh & Bastanipour, 2016). However, the definition goes beyond misuse of public office as it varies greatly depending on its context. The overemphasis on public agents has led scholars to overlook the importance of private agents in corrupt relationship (Castro et al., 2020). Even though, scholars often study corruption as an exception, it is a collective and systemic phenomenon that captures the state and organizations for private interests and thereby harms society (Fazekas & Tóth, 2016).

    Research is often based on the idea that the private agent is just doing business and treating the perpetrators as bad apples (Nielsen, 2003). Recently, management studies have shown that corruption has turned into a systemic problem mainly because of organizational scandals such as the Enron scandal (Levine, 2005), the Volkswagen scandal (Hulpke, 2017), and the Brazilian Car Wash operation (Pinto et al., 2008; Rodrigues & Barros, 2022). These examples portray private companies as the main actors in systemic and transnational corruption schemes. Given this, it is impossible to find good apples or structures untouched by corruption (Pinto et al., 2008).

    Several studies have reviewed the corruption literature. Graaf (2007) classified corruption research according to their level of analysis, context, research methods, and the causal chain leading to corruption. Jancsics (2019) reviewed differentiated corruption concepts by their forms of resource transfer - a market exchange, reciprocal deal, or redistribution of resources - and the type of primary beneficiary - individuals, groups, or organizations.

    Graaf (2007) and Jancsics (2019) focused on the characteristics of public administration to understand the factors contributing to corruption. This interdisciplinary collaboration facilitates a conceptualization of corruption prevention by positioning prevention mechanisms in different disciplines along with their focus and scope (Bautista-Beauchesne & Garzon, 2019).

    In another review, Castro et al. (2020) focused on the antecedents of corruption. They found four research streams - rational action, institutionalized practice, cultural norms, and moral failure - that analyze corruption from a different perspective, focusing on either the individual or moral aspects of the phenomenon. Concerning the review methodology, Bahoo et al. (2020) used a quantitative citation analysis, while Castro et al. (2020) selected articles using publication rankings. Complementary to their review, we use an integrative and qualitative review approach making interdisciplinary research in public and private management literature. Moreover, we move on from those reviews that are mostly focused on the antecedents of corruption to understand the theories of corruption, the geographical location of empirical studies and the methods used to research corruption. We argue that, besides determining the antecedents of corruption, locating the theories used, these questions may help scholars to advance corruption theory. Our review focuses on the following contributions: providing a methodological approach to study corruption, presenting the geographical location of corruption studies published in top-ranked international and English written journals, and exploring what organizational corruption theories are been developed at the intersection on public and private management.

    3. METHODS

    An integrative literature review comprehensively searches, analyzes, critiques, and synthesizes the state-of-the-art literature on a topic (Fink, 2005). We conduct the literature review in line with the approach of Tranfield et al. (2003). First, we ascertain the contribution of the review to the corruption theory. Second, we establish a search protocol and select articles written in English, ranked by the Chartered Association of Business Schools (CABS) journal list; this ranking serves as a proxy for quality. Subsequently, we organize and analyze the data, summarizing their crucial aspects. Finally, we present a report and recommendations for future studies (Tranfield et al., 2003).

    3.1 Research protocols

    The protocols comprise the timeframe of the articles, databases, keywords, and article selection criteria (Callahan, 2010; Tranfield et al., 2003). We searched the Web of Science® and Scopus® databases because they provide the impact factor measures and cover various international journals of high quality in management and business studies. We filtered articles by research area and language. Specifically, we selected articles written in English and published in peer-reviewed journals of business, management, or public administration. These criteria limit the reach of our conclusions and portray the texts analyzed. We exclude relevant knowledge that can be presented through other meaningful criteria to provide an understanding of corruption research. We also emphasize that language fosters a parochial understanding of the issue. In this regard, it must be noted that internationally oriented manuscripts frame their arguments to the English-speaking community. Therefore, we recommend a focus on this knowledge and other approaches for future reviews.

    Concerning the search and filtration processes, we searched for the terms “organizational corruption” and “corruption in organizations” in the title, abstract, and keywords of manuscripts in both databases. By using the word “organization,” we studied the individual, group, and structural aspects, and thereby covered most of the approaches to studying corruption (Robichaud & Cooren, 2013). The search yielded 2,223 published articles. After excluding articles that fell outside the domains of business and management as well as public administration, we were left with 855 articles. Filtering based on the English language criterion further reduced the count to 810 articles. We omitted those that were books or otherwise did not meet the criteria for inclusion in the database and were therefore 778. The deletion of 189 duplicate articles yielded the final sample of 589 articles.

    After building the database, we identified titles, authors, areas, keywords, journals, and the year of publication. We then transferred the manuscripts to the qualitative analysis software Atlas.ti to establish codes and organize data. To read all the 589 manuscripts, we homogenize the labels used for all the papers. We developed code groups - objectives, theories, concepts, method, context, and findings - and registered this information for each article. Subsequently, we grouped the articles according to their similarity in the characteristics of methods, theories, and corruption definitions. We classified theories by individual, group, or structural phenomena. We divided concepts based on the definitions of corruption and other concept definitions and divided methods by their qualitative, quantitative, or theoretical nature. We divided the context based on the geographical location of the empirical research and on whether the articles tested hypotheses, made propositions, created or used models and frameworks. Box 1 presents a summary of the coding process, revealing the number of different elements for each code. The objectives, research questions were in vivo coded - derived from the actual phrasing from the text (Wicks, 2017). We used axial coding to create codes from theories, concepts, methods, context and findings, subsequently, we grouped these codes according to their characteristics (Charmaz, 2000). In other words, in vivo coding makes it possible to group 589 passages describing objectives and research questions, and the axial coding allows for a categorization of articles that analyzes corruption 148 article that analyze corruption from an individual approach, 147 group perspectives, 214 structural and 80 mixed approaches.

    3.2 Summary of the sampled articles

    To integrate data from the sampled articles, we explored other characteristics. For instance, we searched the publication date, sub-field and journal, based on the metadata of the databases. We do not limit the results to the initial publication date. Our database includes all articles published until November 2023, the date of the data collection.

    Other scientific fields have been focusing on corruption for a long time, management disciplines turned their attention toward corruption in the past 15 years. The sample grows from 7 manuscripts in 2006 to 20 manuscripts in 2008 and continues to grow to 44 in 2017 and 67 in 2023 (see Figure 1). The management and business scholars have been adopting more systemic approaches to understand the phenomenon of corruption (Castro et al., 2020; Wedel, 2012).

    We used the CABS list to classify the articles in their fields. The sub-fields of the journals in the sample vary from accounting to social sciences. With 159 (27%) articles, the most representative area is ethics, corporate social responsibility and management. There are 67 (11,4%) articles in journals in the area of economy and 62 (10,5%) in public sector, management and administration, followed by international business with 41 (7%) and Organizational Studies with 31 (5,3%).

    The sample has journals with an impact factor (Journal Citation Report - JCR) between 0,162 and 12,289. The journals with the most corruption-related articles are the Journal of Business Ethics (75 articles), Public integrity (15 articles) the Journal of Management Inquiry (12 articles), and the International Journal of Public Administration (10 articles). The leading position of the Journal of Business Ethics can be explained by the topic but also by the number of published editions per year.

    4. FINDINGS

    In this section, we explore the answer to the three proposed research questions of this research. With the analysis of the sample, it is possible to describe an overview of the methodological approaches to study corruption (How?), Where the studies of the sample are carried out empirically (Where?) and the main themes being researched (What?).

    4.1 How have the management and business scholars studied corruption?

    In corruption studies, the use of different epistemological, ontological, and methodological approaches adds complexity to the understanding of corruption (Lewis & Grimes, 1999). Despite the use of sophisticated approaches, difficulty in obtaining primary data has been affecting qualitative research on the subject. Hence, scholars mostly use secondary data, document analysis, and newspaper articles to study different aspects of corruption (Phiri & Guven-Uslu, 2019).

    In our sample, the methodologies utilized by the articles are as follows: 216 employed quantitative methods (37%), 135 (23%) were theoretical essays or literature reviews, 159 (27%) utilized qualitative approaches, and 79 (13%) adopted mixed-method approaches. After an expressive amount of theoretical articles in 2008 the number of quantitative and qualitative articles rose, which shows the commitment of the field with searching for empirical evidence. In 2023, there is an equilibrium between quantitative and qualitative research, which shows that scholars are finding ways to overcome the challenge of acquiring qualitative data about corruption.     Scholars need more reliable data on corruption to enhance its theory. These data can be extracted from public investigation documents (Rodrigues & Barros, 2022), ethnographies, and qualitative research (Castro et al., 2020). Although studies have theoretically investigated corruption rationalization and the mechanisms driving individuals to become corrupt actors, empirical studies on moral disengagement are still scarce (Klerk, 2017a; Zyglidopoulos & Fleming, 2008). Based on the findings of this review, this scarcity can be attributed to the obstacles of acquiring primary qualitative datasets on corruption. To overcome this challenge, the review suggests extracting data from newspaper articles, media coverage of scandals (Castro et al., 2020; O’Connell & Bligh, 2009), judicial documents, and plea bargains (Halpin & Dundon, 2017; Sarpong et al., 2019).

    In this context, it must be noted that studies on the institutional aspects often use empirical and secondary data. They also use the Corruption Perception Index (CPI) developed by Transparency International (TI) as the proxy for the quantitative measure. The CPI has been, which annually evaluates the perceived corruption level of a given country based on expert opinion and surveys (Transparency International, 2019). Despite TI’s clarifications that the CPI is not a measure of corruption, scholars have used the CPI index as a proxy (Sardžoska & Tang, 2015; Seleim & Bontis, 2009). Some researchers have even validated the CPI as a proper measure of corruption (Wilhelm, 2002). Concerning leadership studies, they have used data from surveys and the global leadership and organizational behavior effectiveness (GLOBE) research (Resick et al., 2009). The GLOBE research program provides cross-cultural data for other correlated variables on organizational behavior (e.g., performance orientation, individual collectivism and power distance (Seleim & Bontis, 2009).

    Both the institutional and leadership research conduct case studies and use secondary and primary data on huge scandals to construct a theory on corruption. However, it is necessary to revisit their use of the indexes. Both CPI and GLOBE oversimplify realities (Resick et al., 2009; Seleim & Bontis, 2009). The CPI has been criticized for using parameters established in developed countries to measure the perceived level of corruption in the global south (Couto, 2020; Maria, 2008). The CPI reinforces colonial hierarchies through its business-centric focus, which holds the developing countries responsible for transnational corruption (Couto, 2020; Maria, 2008). Although it measures perception, it neither considers the role of transnational agents nor differentiates between active and passive actors in a corrupt transaction (Sampson, 2010; Wedel, 2012). Considering the roles of public, private, local, and foreign agents, indexes can never fully capture country-level corruption, and hence they should be viewed skeptically. Studies can also use new indexes, for example, the capacity to combat corruption (CCC) to measure the institutional capacity of a country to combat corruption (Simon & Aalbers, 2020). These measurements can also benefit from technological advancements such as big data, machine learning and artificial intelligence (Hansen & Flyverbom, 2015). In this respect, quantitative studies must become more reflexive about the data they use to reach conclusions.

    Concerning the stream on the individual aspects, these studies are more complicated than those mentioned above. Most of the researchers have conducted theoretical studies owing to primary data limitations as a result of the unwillingness of individuals to share information on corrupt behavior during surveys and interviews. While researchers have conducted primary surveys and case studies of companies and corrupt organizations (Sardžoska & Tang, 2015), some qualitative research have suffered because of primary data limitations caused by the unwillingness of respondents to share information (Ananthram & Chan, 2016). Specifically, these respondents do not consent to interviews, and the statements of those who respond are treated with caution. The analysis of court cases (Tran, 2008) and law and public documents (Marwaha, 2017) has been effective in overcoming these primary data limitations. Few studies have echoed the voice of the corrupt agent (Moore, 2008). Some of the researchers get access to primary data because of the willingness of whistleblowers to share information (Gravley et al., 2015). Others rely on experiments or questionnaires to understand unethical decision-making in specific groups, not necessarily corrupt (Gunia et al., 2012; C. Murphy et al., 2017; P. R. Murphy, 2012).

    4.2 Where have organizational scholars conducted studies on corruption?

    Some studies have highlighted that an analysis of national cultures is critical to the understanding of corruption (Seleim & Bontis, 2009). It must be noted that a permissive culture can increase corruption and the number of lenient institutions (Colla-De-Robertis & Navarro-Castañeda, 2017). Seleim and Bontis (2009) suggests that a country’s culture impacts its level of corruption. Gelbrich et al. (2016) have highlighted the role of national wealth in influencing cultural values, practices, and corruption. The cultural values and practices of richer countries less significantly impact their corruption level. Still, several studies have claimed that poorer countries deviate from the rules to compete with wealthier countries (Nur-tegin & Jakee, 2020; Nye, 1967). From the transaction cost perspective, in a globalized world, the developing and poorer countries often witness a high level of corruption owing to their less stringent penalties and regulations (Husted, 1999; Li et al., 2012). Hearn (2015) has argued that permissive laws play a greater role than culture and other factors in facilitating systematic and international corruption. This argument is based on the multinational scandals that have revealed the presence of corruption in developed countries and fiscal paradises (Kulik et al., 2008). This debate has led to the development of cross-cultural analyses and variables revealing the determinants of corruption in specific locations. These analyses have overlooked that corruption is a systemic and multidimensional phenomenon (Lange, 2008; Warburton, 2013) dependent on contextual and individual factors (Zyglidopoulos & Fleming, 2008). These factors also include the cultural context. It must be understood that corruption can occur at any place. To contribute to the debate on how culture influences corruption, we identified the countries where the empirical studies in the sample were conducted.

    Figure 2 shows all continents represented in the reviewed corruption research. Most of the empirical studies are in the United States (51) and in China (45), the fact that other countries have less or any can be explained by our selection of articles that did not reach studies in other countries. Our methodology resulted in elements that show global south having considerably less studies than the global north in the database. This discrepancy can be attributed to the high scientific production of the countries published in the journals researched (Wilson & Knutsen, 2020). In the context of corruption, the sample studies cannot be used as a proxy for cases of corruption. Although the sample reflects the context of corruption theory, it also reveals the need for research on the topic across multiple locations.

    The main point that can be achieved through this analysis is that research on corruption about global south has not achieved English, mainstream and good ranked journals - according to the global north rankings. That entails that our current understanding of the corruption phenomenon may be biased towards the global north conception of corruption. As corruption definition and theories can change depending on culture (Seleim & Bontis, 2009), current corruption theories might ignore the contextual and global south understanding of the construct.

    Therefore, it will be important for future research to investigate the cases of organizational corruption in countries with fewer or no studies and to have journals fostering such research. It will also be critical to investigate transnational and international corruption schemes involving different countries and their specific settings (Bahoo et al., 2020).

    22 articles were classified as “cross-country” with more than ten countries as empirical settings. The empirical studies in various countries show that, in a globalized world, different institutional environments catalyze the perpetration of systemic corruption. Second, they call for cross-cultural analysis and institutional research to determine how different variables (e.g., permissive culture, regulations, and national wealth) increase the opportunities for corruption. Briefly, the lack of detailed studies within countries and cross-regional and cultural analyses makes it difficult to assess the influence of culture on corruption.

    To add to this discussion, Sampson (2010) described how the TI, the World Bank, other international institutions, and NGOs positioned corruption in an anti-corruption industry framework. In developing countries, this industry framework allows for the anti-corruption initiatives to co-exist with corruption, without critically analyzing the system surrounding the anti-corruption measures and discourse. Hence, the corruption studies using these measures fail to establish a relationship with culture, without being incidental or conjectural.

    Our sample comprises quantitative research based on data from developed countries. In recent years, these corruption studies have progressed from their focus on bad apples to a more structural and systemic view of corruption (Castro et al., 2020; Pinto et al., 2008). Future scholars can use mixed methods to analyze data that broaden perspectives on the topic. They must challenge the idea that corruption studies can relate to all realities. While each stream will have its idiosyncrasies, empirical research with data from the global south and cross-cultural studies will define the limits of parochial perspectives.

    4.3 What has been studied on organizational corruption?

    To explore the themes being studied about corruption it is possible to carry a network analysis of keywords, highlighting the centrality of corruption. Different disciplinary approaches to studying corruption are identified, divided into individual, group, and structural theories. The influence of unethical leadership and the processes of ethical and unethical decision-making at the individual level are also discussed. In summary, the section provides a panoramic view of the theoretical and methodological approaches adopted in the study of corruption.

    We described the articles’ content using a map of the co-occurrence of keywords between the articles. By using the software VOSviewer, we established a network of keywords occurring in more than one article; we divided these keywords into clusters. We tested different thresholds of the number of links in a network; we found that the network considering at least five links of keywords presents the clearest and important information. In other words, the nodes in the networks are keywords linked at least five times with other keywords. Figure 3 presents the network with 56 keywords in 8 clusters.

    Corruption occupies a central role in the network because it is the searched keyword; it is part of the cluster with individual aspects of corruption (green). This green portion represents the core aspects of corruption individual theories built over the past few years including terms such as business ethics, gender and leadership. Another notable cluster is the one focusing on the current forms of combating corruption with words such as Corporate Social responsibility and governance (red). The other clusters focus on unethical behavior (dark blue); whistleblowing and organizational culture (yellow); ethical leadership and integrity (purple). Interesting to notice the contexts of emerging markets or specific countries are dispersed between the various clusters, meaning that all branches of theory on corruption are being studied in specific contexts (yellow).

    Figure 3

    This map charts the keywords giving a superficial overview of the main themes of the articles in the literature review. An analysis reveals that these articles used several theories to study the corruption phenomenon. In this context, it must be noted that different disciplines adopt different approaches to studying corruption. Sociology investigates the social structures facilitating corruption; economics investigates the individual cost-benefit rationale to engage in corruption; anthropology discusses the group relations and meaning given to corruption; political science analyzes the impact emerging from the political and structural aspects of society (Castro et al., 2020). In these disciplines, studies use theories that approach corruption from an individual, group, or structural perspective. All these theories contribute to the understanding of this complex phenomenon.

    We divided the theories in the reviewed articles into three categories - theories addressing the individual and psychological aspects of corruption, theories investigating the group and organizational behaviors, and structural theories analyzing the impact of context on corruption. These categories are not closed groups, and the theories can fluctuate between levels.

    Concerning the category of structural theories, the most prominent stream of theories uses an institutional approach. Research employing this approach seeks to explain corruption in the institutional context. Under this umbrella, some studies adopt a legalistic view - how laws and rules prevent or allow corruption (Cumming et al., 2010; Tran, 2008); - and others discuss how the organizational environment and culture promote corruption. These studies focus less on the societal aspect; instead, they observe specific companies or sectors. The major focus area of empirical studies in this stream has been the fallout of corrupt practices from the construction sector’s relationship with various governments (Arewa & Farrell, 2015; Leitão & Domenico, 2015).

    Luo (2005) created an interesting model to investigate the aforementioned institutional and contextual factors contributing to organizational corruption. He also integrated the importance of environmental aspects with individual behaviors and procedures, corruption outcomes, and combat mechanisms. Several scholars have empirically used this model. For instance, Azim and Kluvers (2019) reconfigured the model to show the need for integrating the institutional and environmental aspects into the anti-corruption control system. They emphasized integrating the model architecture into the system, highlighting the need to use institutional efforts to alleviate organizational corruption. Given this, Luo’s (2005) model can also be linked with the next group of studies based on behavioral theory. In this context, some corruption theorists have focused on organizational behavior and culture to explain corruption. Unlike institutional studies, this group focuses on the intra-organizational aspects that promote corruption. These studies not only focus on the contextual and normative aspects but also the individual aspects influencing corrupt behavior.

    Using the behavioral theory and the theory of planned behavior some scholars have explained this corrupt behavior. Some individual behavioral constructs influencing corruption intention and action are avarice, stress, individual ethics, and values (Sardžoska & Tang, 2015; Tang et al., 2018; Tran, 2008). In this group, the models explaining corrupt behavior use the institutional aspects and the organizational and individual aspects as background and core constructs, respectively. Despite this conceptualization, this group considers corruption an institutional and context-based decision. In other words, the context of corrupt agents compels them to make the decision (Tran, 2008).

    Concerning such corrupt behavior in organizations, certain scandals serve as generalizable cases in organizational studies. One such case is the Enron scandal; it has contributed to advancements in systematic and networked studies on corruption. Specifically, Enron’s e-mail corpus has contributed to the development of corruption theories and methodologies to study similar cases (Hardin et al., 2015). Owing to its relevance for analyzing organizational culture and malpractices, the researchers have coined the term Enron effect. It refers to the effect of the avarice on managers as they advance in their careers, and it is strengthened by low corporate ethical values. Another case that gained much academic attention is the National Health Service of the United Kingdom (NHS). Studies on the NHS focus on whistleblowing and mismanagement in the organization (Pope, 2017; Pope & Burnes, 2013).

    Huge corruption scandals often provide empirical data and contribute to understanding specific aspects of a theory. These specific cases are often treated as singular and not normal in organizational research, they are studied as exceptions without a clear relation to the contemporary forms of organizing. While media information portrays corruption as abnormal, there may be several unreported cases in organizations normalizing corruption. In the latter case, corruption may not be an exception. In this regard, it must be understood that only the reported scandals garner public and academic attention. This may lead to a narrow understanding of the concept. Hence, researchers should also focus on the unreported or small scandals to truly understand the nature of corruption (Clemente & Gabbioneta, 2017). Corruption studies based on organizational behavior also discuss how organizations can restore legitimacy tarnished by a scandal (Pelletier & Bligh, 2006). There is also an interesting discussion on corporate death penalty for corrupt organizations (Hulpke, 2017).

    Besides the institutional environment and organizational factors, unethical leadership plays a crucial role in promoting structural corruption. Given the focus on ethical leadership, scholars have also been focusing on the role of an unethical leader in the construction of corruption networks. The recent corruption research has presented insightful studies on the relationship between leadership and corruption (Brown & Mitchell, 2010; Jurkiewicz & Giacalone, 2016). The leader serves as a link connecting organizational values and processes with the employees, and thereby allows the structural aspects of corporate culture to influence individual behaviors (Jurkiewicz & Giacalone, 2016). In this sense, unethical leadership contributes to corruption theory based on its role in constructing corruption networks and behavior, while ethical leadership provides tools to combat corruption.

    The last group of studies is based on the individual aspect. In this group, some corruption theorists have attempted to understand the process of ethical and unethical decision-making, which also leads to theories on morality and ethics. These studies are concerned with the ethical development and value construction of employees. Two streams of research have been developed to study this approach. One stream focuses on moral disengagement theories investigating human psychology to understand how certain individuals normalize and rationalize corruption activities (Anand et al., 2004). In this regard, some theorists have used the moral development theory in psychology (Lynch et al., 2002). Another stream uses behavioral ethics and rational decision-making to understand how individuals rationally take immoral decisions (Gunia et al., 2012; Moore, 2008).

    Despite this divisions, a unified idea and theory of corruption that understand it as a multidimensional phenomenon is needed. In this sample, there are only 80 articles that mix these perspectives. The idea that private companies are victims of public agents does not fit current understanding of corruption as endemic and systemic, which means that the groups of the network analysis could be integrated with more research that relates different aspects of corruption. This idea also improves the importance of the public-private relationship as the central theme of corruption research without only blaming one or the other side (Arellano-Gault, 2019; Arellano-Gault et al., 2013).

    5. DISCUSSION

    Based on how, where and what of organizational corruption, this literature review allows for progress in corruption theory at the intersection between private and public management field. It deconstructs the idea that private companies are victims of public officials. It also fosters a multidimensional approach towards to the phenomenon incentivizing the publication of different methods and contexts in top ranked English journals through a decolonial view on corruption (Couto, 2020).

    The growth of corruption research in these fields during the past decades reinforces the importance of the phenomenon and its theoretical advancements. However, scholars must pursue qualitative approaches built upon primary data sources to advance the corruption theory in its nuances and its contribution to the causes, consequences, and combat mechanisms of corruption (Bahoo et al., 2020). Most of corruption theories are created theoretically and without empirical validity. Despite the difficulties of acquire data on this sensible topic, the use of corruption scandals and cases has been an important way to advance corruption theory on all aspects. Also, the comparison between cases and even countries are useful to advance corruption theory.

    Quantitative corruption studies can also be enhanced by using new indexes such as the CCC index and by creating scales measuring the degree of corruption in a given country or other measures. The decision to use these indexes should be based on a careful and critical evaluation. This is important provided that the CPI has been criticized as an unreliable measure for corruption owing to its favoritism toward countries in the global north (Maria, 2008; Sampson, 2010). These proxies have failed to facilitate an in-depth description of the local realities. Cross-national and cultural studies can provide an understanding of how culture influences corruption and makes it a transnational phenomenon (Gelbrich et al., 2016; Khatri et al., 2006). Individual and organizational aspects of corruption also lack empirical studies, the use of corruption cases and scandals and the creation of academic measures might help to generalize corruption theories that were only theoretically proposed. To do that, scholars can also use social media and big data.

    Our database and protocol shows that research on corruption from global south cases has not reached English and mainstream internationally top tier journals. Which means that our current understanding of corruption theory is western and northern centric. It is imperative to allow for other non-central research to add to current notions of corruption. This path is important given that our current understanding of corruption English and international journals can be biased towards countries from the global north which can blame the global south for their corruption. This is demonstrated by the corruption indexes used as proxies in corruption research but also by how corruption is defined across the public and private management fields.

    Finally, considering the research streams, it was possible to understand that corruption theory divides the phenomenon between individual, organizational and structural characteristics. Research on corruption in the individual perspective lacks primary data sources to understand how individuals engage in corruption behavior, furthermore, why they rationalize that behavior and the means to combat it (Klerk, 2017b). This field has been theoretically proposed but was not empirically validated or generalized. Also, the same idea could be applied to public servants and how they rationalized and morally disengage with corruption.

    Organizational perspectives often use large cases and scandals of corruption to construct theory (Hardin et al., 2015). However, there are few examples of comparative cases or meta-analysis which hinders the progress of theoretical propositions. In the same way, the relationship between public and private organizations should be investigated closely, understanding that the passive-active definitions of corruption are not coherent with current forms of organizing corruption (Warburton, 2013). With companies gaining economic power to capture states (Fazekas & Tóth, 2016) but also with people amassing financial and influential power over international relationships (Hearn, 2015), researchers should also be attentive to systematic and endemic corruption that surpass the simple quid-pro-quo between public and private agents.

    Finally, structural contributions to the phenomenon often are based on the mainstream research of corruption, using global north indexes as proxies for corruption that does not explain local realities neither systemic and endemic corruption. Overall, this research proposes that corruption theorist should pay attention to global south research and conceptualization of corruption, should seek reliable data to understand the phenomenon and must continue to pursue validation to individual and organizational theories.

    6. FINAL REMARKS

    By conducting an integrative literature review on organizational corruption, we trace the concepts, methodologies, and geographical location of corruption studies. This approach has contributed to future avenues of research. First, future research should improve how corruption is measured and conduct extensive and quantitative data analysis. Second, research on corruption should focus on the idiosyncrasies of the global south, and thereby diversify the geographical scope of empirical corruption research. These studies should also understand corruption as a global systemic phenomenon. Third, corruption scholars should continue to overcome the qualitative data limitations by using judicial data, ethnographies, and other qualitative methods.

    We acknowledge some limitations to our study. Despite the comprehensive search and analysis of articles in the integrative literature review, the filtration criteria based on the use of the English language and the quality of the journals have led to the omission of additional contributions (Tietze & Dick, 2013).The use of different languages and quality parameters can reveal different and interesting results in similar research. This can be important given that most of the studies are concentrated in the global north, as per the results of the search on the Web of Science® and Scopus® databases. The use of different languages and quality parameters would also agree with a more decolonial view of corruption. This is unlike the colonial view that hopes to provide an answer to modern-day corruption by charging the developing countries with transnational corruption. It must be noted that the huge scandals of recently discovered corruption involve banks in fiscal paradises, offshores in developed countries, and multinationals mostly headquartered in the global north. Given this, a decolonial approach toward corruption is highly recommended for future research.
