RESEARCH ARTICLE • Contexto int. 47 (03) • 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20244603e20240023 linkcopy

War Memes and Brazilian National Politics

Memes de Guerra e a Política Nacional Brasileira

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article adopts the theoretical framework of memetic warfare and International Relations (IR), applying a qualitative, interpretive case study to a curated dataset of 192 memes circulated in pro-Bolsonaro WhatsApp groups in Brazil. These visual artefacts are examined as discursive tools that reframe global events - most notably the 2022 war in Ukraine - within national political cultures. The findings reveal how memes have been mobilised to construct political identities, reinforce authoritarian populist narratives, and disseminate disinformation. By analysing how international crises are appropriated to shape domestic discourse, the article contributes to IR by highlighting the role of digital media in political communication and opinion formation, particularly in the Global South.

    Key words
    Memetic warfare; Ukrainian conflict; social media narratives; political communication; Brazilian politics

    Resumo

    Este artigo adota o referencial teórico da guerra memética e das Relações Internacionais (RI), aplicando um estudo de caso interpretativo qualitativo a um conjunto de dados selecionados de 192 memes circulados em grupos pró-Bolsonaro no WhatsApp. Esses artefatos visuais são examinados como ferramentas discursivas que reformulam eventos globais — especialmente a guerra na Ucrânia em 2022 — dentro de culturas políticas nacionais. Os resultados revelam como os memes foram mobilizados para construir identidades políticas, reforçar narrativas populistas autoritárias e disseminar desinformação. Ao analisar como crises internacionais são apropriadas para moldar o discurso doméstico, o artigo contribui para as RI ao destacar o papel das mídias digitais na comunicação política e na formação da opinião pública, particularmente no Sul Global.

    Palavras-chave
    guerra memética; conflito na Ucrânia; narrativas em mídias sociais; comunicação política; política brasileira

    Introduction

    The ongoing war in Ukraine is not limited to tanks, missiles, aircraft, submarines, and drones. Parallel to the powerful resistance of javelin missiles against the Russian tanks, the world also witnessed a flood of memes taking over social media and setting the grounds for symbolic battles according to the grammars of the 21st century (The Economist 2022). The fast, ironic, and visual flows of communication pervading different platforms have drawn the world’s attention to the escalation of the conflict, its possible causes, and its potential consequences. Studies demonstrated the diplomatic skirmishes between Russia and Ukraine has spawned memes since the early 2010s. Some of these memes even take on a transnational character (Denisova 2019). They are shared by users from other countries, attesting to the significant circularity of digital humour and the ability to translate and adapt to local contexts (Shifman, Levy and Thelwall 2014).

    Both Russians and Ukrainians have invested time and effort to produce memes in the dispute of the narratives around the war. The support and broad sympathy of the West to Ukraine in this conflict have propelled an enormous volume of messages addressed to international audiences (Scott 2022). Although this case shows the relevance of memetic grammar in the battles around the meaning of a conflict, it remains unclear how different countries host diverse memetic disputes nurtured by a given conflict.

    This article discusses this issue through a case study focused on Brazil. By delving into the memes circulating in WhatsApp groups that support President Jair Bolsonaro in the weeks that preceded and followed the beginning of the invasion, we show how politically mobilised groups use international events to galvanize their positions and narratives. We also call attention to the manners these groups employ to provide a messianic interpretation of the controversial role played by Brazil – led by former president Bolsonaro – at the very beginning of this conflict. Such a personal cult to Bolsonaro provides valuable insights into the understanding of how these groups discuss and interpret international relations via biased information and conspiracy theories. As Gür-Şeker, Boonen and Wentker (2022) argue, memes are effective tools for propagating conspiracy theories as they insert themselves into broad discursive networks, and challenge official narratives.

    During Bolsonaro, Brazil assumed a low-profile diplomatic strategy to cope with the Ukrainian war. The hesitation and ambiguity in the statements made by the Brazilian diplomatic body in international arenas has been remarkable. Although the Brazilian government issued an official note demanding the immediate suspension of hostilities on the same day the war was declared, there was no explicit condemnation of Russian actions. Bolsonaro even suggested that Ukrainians should surrender. Zelensky criticized Brazilian diplomatic strategy. Nonetheless, Bolsonaro’s supporters shared memes claiming the opposite: Brazil would be actively operating to provide a peaceful resolution to the conflict. Even former ministers shared memes based on the fake news that the Brazilian president would have prevented the ‘World War III’.

    The then Brazilian president is morphed into a divine actor alongside Jesus Christ in the battle of Armageddon. Sometimes as a hero, sometimes as a martyr, Bolsonaro assumes archetypal representations in the memes spread by his supporters (Chagas 2021). The war in Ukraine also became a topic for these memes. Due to the highly controversial foreign policy adopted by the Brazilian government and the ostracism to which the country was relegated, the Ukrainian war was grasped as a rare opportunity to frame Bolsonaro as a player in global politics. A clear illustration was his visit to Russia on the brink of the war.

    Bolsonaro’s supporters have demonstrated strong engagement on WhatsApp and pro-government demonstrations in the streets. The president’s electoral base is composed of different segments of a conservative hue, including monarchists, militarists, neoliberal groups, certain neo-Pentecostal sectors, and others. The circumstantial alliance of these groups composed the so-called Bolsonarism. One of the most successful political communication strategies of Bolsonarism was the large-scale use of public political discussion groups in private mobile instant messaging services, notably WhatsApp, to bring these supporters together. These virtual groups are not simply a channel for disseminating campaign news. They are kind of informational menu for these users, who share links to far-right blogs, YouTube videos, and memes.

    We structured the article in three parts. Firstly, we introduce how the literature on International Relations (IR) is gradually shedding light on the importance of social media (and memes) to make sense of contemporary phenomena. In the second part, we present the methods of our case study and a brief context of the Brazilian politics. Lastly, we discuss Brazilian memes around the Russian invasion of Ukraine, which circulated in WhatsApp groups supporting former President Jair Bolsonaro for a period of 30 days. We show how the conflict is grasped as a discursive opportunity to strengthen some of the symbolic elements frequently linked to Bolsonaro, while also opening possibilities to reshape elements of his image. Usually seen as belligerent and aggressive, Bolsonaro was associated with pacifism in the weeks preceding the war and was later ridiculed by his opponents for his alleged ineptitude once the conflict arose.

    International Relations, Social Media, and Internet Memes

    There is a growing interest for digital media in the field of IR and Political Science (see Branagan 2007; López-Paredes and Carrillo-Andrade 2022; López-Paredes and Renó 2024). These platforms are at the very heart of contemporary international politics (Duncombe 2019; Brassett, Browning and Wedderburn 2021; Kreps 2020). IR literature on social media deals with a wide range of phenomena, covering issues such as terrorism (Chertoff, Bury and Richterova 2020; Heath-Kelly and Jarvis 2017), misinformation in conflicts (Crilley 2018), diplomacy (Adler-Nissen and Eggeling 2022), migration crises (Berents 2020; Hall 2019), riots (Makhortykh and González Aguilar 2020), and sanitary crises (Zhabotynska 2020). North American foreign policy analytical strands have incorporated digital platforms to delve into social debates sparked by White House’s decisions over immigration or interventions abroad – such as the strategies towards the war in Afghanistan and Iraq (Silvestri 2016). Likewise, social media became an arena analysed by studies about other great powers such as Russia and China. During the last decade, many studies about the use of digital platforms in China flourished due to mass protests in Hong Kong, oppositional movements to Xi Jinping’s government, and its censorship strategies (Postema and Melissen 2021) or the controversial employment of these mechanisms by diaspora groups and diplomacy to harass critics and influence other countries’ political decisions to augur in favour of Beijing (Shumba 2021).

    Some authors stressed that not only diplomatic and military bodies but also international organizations, multinational corporations, celebrities, terrorist groups, activists, or members of the general public have increasingly used visual narratives on social media to discuss global politics (Crilley, Manor and Bjola 2020). X/Twitter accounts of diplomatic institutions make a growing use of memes. Public diplomacy strategies can carve out space in social circles by using memes to explain a given episode. It facilitates agents to spread their partial perspectives (Bos and Melissen 2019).

    In addition, a literature over social media and public diplomacy (Sevin and Ingenhoff 2018) came up due to the growing disputes in the Indo-Pacific involving the United States, Japan, Australia, India and China (Byrne 2019). Social media ramped up the capabilities of states to address foreign publics over international issues. Pacific Islands (Burch 2021), for instance, make frequent use of social media to call attention to the consequences of global warming. There is a rising strand of foreign policy analysis related to the use of digital media by diplomatic bodies (Adler-Nissen and Eggeling 2022; Bjola 2016). Digital platforms, however, are often reduced to tools strategically used to influence political disputes. Many scholars depict social media as war arenas where actors employ their ‘weapons’ (Singer and Brooking 2018), such as memes.

    The advance of populism (Katja, Frank and Christine 2022) is also a trendy topic, driving the attention of IR scholarship to digital platforms (De Sá Guimarães and de Oliveira e Silva 2021). Groups can more easily spread conspiracy theories against the liberal world order and multilateral organizations (Adler-Nissen and Zarakol 2021). A widely known example is the association of left-wing parties with an imagined transnational plan to destroy Western values (Casarões and Farias 2021). There is also a growing literature about how official and non-state agents work on social media to cement a paranoid-minded worldview to legitimize political decisions. For instance, some works scrutinized social media profiles from security-affiliated Russian think tanks and media outlets. They inferred that these institutions diffuse alarmist perspectives about international menaces against Moscow (Pomerantsev 2015; Kragh, Andermo and Makashova 2022). In so being, social media is frequently assimilated as a way to get valuable insights into belief systems and standpoints of different actors in international relations.

    Within this broad agenda, visuals started to receive more attention due to their centrality to ground arguments in public debates (Mendonça, Ercan and Asenbaum 2022) - which also include their capacity to trigger emotions and set the contexts. Some studies analysed, for instance, how pictures of a dead refugee child triggered diplomatic reactions in Europe (Adler-Nissen, Andersen and Hansen 2020). As a specific type of visual expression, memes are also catching the attention of IR scholars. Memes are attractive and accessible messages that can travel easily in times of communicative abundance. They also serve as a space for expressing differences between expectations and actions taken by political actors and are strongly influenced by popular sentiment (López-Paredes and Carrillo-Andrade 2022).

    According to Phillips and Milner (2021), memes set up deep frames, which consolidate imaginaries around certain subjects. The origin of the concept is directly associated with social and cultural representations (Shifman, Levy and Thelwall 2014), and it has recently become central to some political approaches (Milner 2016; Chagas et al. 2019). Studies such as those by Bogerts and Fielitz (2019) argue that memetic warfare has been widely used to disseminate extremist propaganda, combining iconographic references to a glorious and mythical past and traditional moral values, such as family and ethnic supremacy, to cartoon remixes and futuristic aesthetics like those of the vaporwave genre. Moreno-Almeida and Gerbaudo (2021) argue that the memes of the Moroccan far right act as an entry point to nationalist, misogynistic and racist ideas. Making use of irony and sarcasm, memes often build visual representations that pave the way for specific interpretations of reality. They are key meaning-making elements in an era marked by complex mediatic ecosystems, and they reflect political and cultural landscapes from the ideational standpoints of groups where they pop up (Zidani 2021).

    Recent works analysed the role played by memes in the construction of the public image of certain political actors. Chatterjee (2020) discusses how memetic images of Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi combine politics and pop culture, with references to Bollywood films and cricket. Adegoju and Oyebode (2015) and Tella (2018) focus on the 2015 Nigerian elections, in which the candidates Muhammadu Buhari and Goodluck Jonathan were running. They draw attention to the use of memes to mistreat the adversary and to express socio-political criticisms and public concerns. Lamerichs et al. (2018) also highlight the use that supporters of populist authoritarian leaders, such as Trump and Putin, have made of memes to build an image of heroism and masculinity.

    In elections, political candidates and their supporters have used memes to frame political themes, but also to shut down conversations and distract audiences, or to mobilize identities in a call for support (Lankshear and Knobel 2019). The literature also emphasizes the role of humour in fostering a communal sense of belonging and highlights its capacity to sensitize, educate, or even ‘convert’ audiences (Branagan 2007).

    Jair Bolsonaro’s supporters used memes in the 2018 national elections to claim that their candidate was the only way to avoid Brazil would experience a Venezuelan-alike economic crisis (Chagas, Modesto and Magalhães 2019). Memes are often employed to delegitimize political adversaries, laughing at their attributes, actions and alleged contradictions (Ross and Rivers 2017), as much as to produce a common framework for political proselytism. However while they are characterized as discursive artifacts (Wiggins 2019), strategically activated by political actors or their supporters, they should not be read as isolated pieces. Instead, they must be faced as an ecological media arrangement that interacts directly with other similar content and produces communality, a practice within the repertoire of collective action. Political groups opportunistically employ them to render visibility to a given agenda or create a kind of lingua franca that allows complex concepts to be translated into reasonably simplified ways.

    In this article, we seek to investigate how the 2022 war in Ukraine was part of the Brazilian memetic disputes around the Bolsonaro administration. IR scholarship pays more attention to the role memes are playing in the public sphere, as they are employed by diverse actors to strengthen their discourses and deal with their public images. By shifting the observation to a domestic perspective, this study also proposes to recognize how memes can be employed as part of this repertoire of collective action to build master-frames that benefit and exalt authoritarian populist governments from a biased interpretation of global politics.

    We agree with Shifman’s (2013) recent definition. According to her, memes can be understood as groups of digital items - not just as ideas or behaviours in abstract terms. Coker (2008) claims that meme theory could provide a creative framework for understanding the multitude of competing interpretations that emerge among contenders in a war context. Milner (2016) similarly concludes that memes stimulate a dynamic of polyvocality, according to which there are multiple voices in dispute for the hegemonic narrative and, consequently, too much noise to discern the information. Memes works as discursive-convincing mechanisms aimed at the legitimation of a perspective and the formation of global awareness about an event.

    Despite the advances in this agenda, most studies in the field of IR focus on state-related issues, investigating disputes between countries or actions that directly influence foreign policy decision-making. Few studies focus on how domestic groups interpret global events via social media, mobilizing discursive assets and emotional perceptions to feed internal political struggles. In this sense, this kind of analysis provides an interesting contribution to the growing body of IR literature about emotional response to international phenomena (Sasley 2013; Van Rythoven 2021; Katja, Frank and Christine 2022).

    Methods and Brief Contextualization

    Bolsonaro’s presidential trip to Russia days before the conflict (15-18 February 2022) was significantly controversial. It happened when Moscow was on the brink of initiating the military invasion of Ukrainian soil. Due to global concerns and diplomatic pressures to avoid close ties with Russia, many Brazilians criticized the decision to proceed with a visit previously scheduled. Yet, Bolsonaro’s supporters tried to explore this event as an opportunity to claim the Brazilian president was traveling to Russia to convince Putin not to start a war against Ukraine (Werneck 2020). A myriad of memes surged throughout their groups in WhatsApp. Although many Brazilians faced these memes as jokes, they worked to convey a message of leadership among Bolsonaro’s supporters.

    As a media hub, WhatsApp integrates different platforms and achieves enormous capillarity, mainly due to three factors. The first is an economic factor. In the hope of attracting more users from the lower classes, telecom companies adopted zero-rating policies, allowing customers to use private messaging services even when their mobile plans expired. This measure greatly expanded the WhatsApp user base in Brazil, which currently exceeds 120 million active accounts. The second factor is a social one. WhatsApp chats replaced other social media and SMS as the main interpersonal contact form for mobile communication amongst the Brazilian population, including for professional purposes. Currently, about 99% of Brazilian smartphones have WhatsApp installed – a much larger market share than any competing application (Infobip 2020).

    The third factor is technological. Having adopted an end-to-end encryption system, which prevents messages exchanged between users from being lost or intercepted without authorization by third parties, WhatsApp has become a privileged arena for the dissemination of misinformation and attacks on political opponents. It is difficult to monitor the circulation of this type of message on the platform (Duque and Peres-Neto 2022). Hence, WhatsApp assumed a privileged role not only in Jair Bolsonaro’s electoral strategy but also throughout his presidency. In the discussion groups organized by his supporters, links, comments and memes were exchanged in defence of the government and against political opponents.

    The discursive contents range from criticism of the so-called gender ideology, supposedly propagated by feminists and LGBTQIA+ people, to the categorical defence of the government, including support for an eventual authoritarian regime and the denial of the existence of the pandemic (Massuchin et al. 2021). Specific episodes are quickly transformed into memes and pieces of political propaganda. These groups also addressed international events to reinforce their assumptions.

    To analyse the memes that circulated on WhatsApp, this article starts from a systematic collection of data in a set of approximately 200 public groups of discussion declared to support the Bolsonaro’s administration. The monitoring of these groups follows a covert research protocol since their users are far-right supporters hostile to scholarly research. The consent of the subjects was waived through approval by a national research ethics committee. All user data of a private nature has been anonymised, and results work only on an aggregated scale.

    We extracted all images that circulated in the groups between 8 February and 8 March 2022. This period includes the date when the first contents associated with the tension between Russia and Ukraine emerged in the monitored groups. Although the war only began on the 24 February, President Bolsonaro’s trip to meet Vladimir Putin in Moscow took place on the 15 February. There were, hence, a number of memes circulating in the week leading up to the meeting. On the 8 March, we have another important milestone, as then state representative for São Paulo Arthur Do Val (commonly known by his YouTube nickname, ‘Mamãe Falei’) gets involved in a controversy after visiting Ukraine at the beginning of the war to carry out alleged social actions.

    During the period, a total of 192 unique images mentioning the conflict between Russia and Ukraine were collected. Almost all of them contained humorous commentaries or jokes. WhatsApp memes are often less ‘clean’ than other social medias, and there are many screenshots or crude montages turned into jokes. For this reason, we employed the same analytical process observed in Denisova (2019) and Chagas et al. (2019). We worked with the whole dataset as Internet memes and selected only the exemplary cases for discussion. The analysis applied a qualitative interpretive method to highlight discursive patterns present in these contents. Next, we discuss the main findings of this analysis.

    Findings

    Humour about war or national tragedies, such as pandemics or natural disasters, is often controversial and arouses all kinds of reactions. Kuipers (2002), for example, recognizes three distinct ways to deal with tragic moments: the complete suspension of humour, the call for humour to cope with the circumstances, and the censorship of humour as inappropriate behaviour. Brazilian WhatsApp memes about the war in Ukraine, which circulated in political groups of supporters of Jair Bolsonaro, mostly use the conflict only as a backdrop to interpret domestic issues. Sometimes they recur solely to humour, although they often seek to trigger other types of feelings, such as indignation. The caustic irony is, however, persistent, serving different purposes.

    We found five main purposes or presumed intentions that helped us organize and understand our database: (1) praise of Jair Bolsonaro as a world leader; (2) attacks on adversaries and institutions; (3) dissemination of political agendas at the heart of Bolsonarism; (4) clarification of the ‘actual’ meanings of the war; (5) laughing at ourselves (e.g. Brazilians). Although these memes aim to address the domestic audience and do not represent war from a geopolitical perspective, they highlight the principles of Brazilian foreign policy during the Bolsonaro government. Therefore, they allow us to understand some of the positions assumed by Brazil on international affairs.

    (1) Praising Bolsonaro

    Many memes depict Bolsonaro as a strong and determined leader. Despite being frequently diminished by his adversaries, he is arguably well-intentioned and destined to do the best for Brazil according to God’s plans on Earth. This manner of representation became particularly strong in the days preceding the conflict, when Bolsonaro went to Russia on a very controversial official visit. Several memes depicted the former Brazilian president as a peacemaker. Often these images appealed to religious interpretations as if Bolsonaro had been chosen by Jesus Christ to circumvent war - thus deserving praise and recognition. Likewise, a meme shared the idea that Bolsonaro would be a peaceful leader such as John Lennon, Gautama Buddha, Mahatma Gandhi, or Martin Luther King due to his personal commitment to ‘save the world.’

    There are many memes with Christian religious references. For instance, we found images depicting Jesus Christ next to President Bolsonaro and captions such as ‘Go and stop the war, Jair’ or ‘Let’s go, Jair! We have a war to stop.’ The sentences refer to the alleged negotiation the president would have undertaken during his visit to Moscow. The memes praise Bolsonaro and employ religious motifs that are in tune with Christian audiences that support Bolsonaro. There is also some criticism of purported atheism or Satanism supposedly practiced by left-wing supporters. These memes evoke a series of conspiracies and paranoia, but they also seem to suggest that Bolsonaro’s diplomacy pursues a conservative agenda, with its own issues and perspectives.

    Unlike other BRICS nations, Brazil usually followed the Western powers in voting sections about the Ukrainian war at the United Nations Security Council (UNSC) and General Assembly (UNGA). Brasília sustained diplomatic mechanisms with Russia to safeguard trade ties and avoid skirmishes with Moscow, but there was no robust mobilization to offer to mediate the conflict. Russia is Brazil’s largest supplier of fertilizers - representing a quarter of Brazilian imports in the sector. Hence, it is a core issue for Brazil’s agribusiness, which historically constitutes one of the most influential sectors in the national economy. This sector mostly embraced Bolsonaro’s political campaign in 2018 and its conservative values. Bolsonaro’s trip to Russia revolved around farmers’ concerns about the side effects of Western sanctions against Russia on trade issues. A cut back on the purchase of Russian fertilizers could trigger logistic problems for their crops. Indeed, Bolsonaro’s administration avoided any frontal criticism against Moscow. Bolsonaro’s supporters shared memes cheering this initiative as a diplomatic accomplishment.

    These memes aimed to depict Bolsonaro as an ‘influent global player and a diplomacy mastermind’ who could negotiate even with iron-fist presidents like Putin. Some of the images compare the distance Putin took during official meetings with different presidents. The French President Emmanuel Macron refused to carry out RT-PCR tests to detect SARS-CoV-2—because this could imply the transfer of genetic material from the French leader to Moscow. Consequently, he was subjected to a caricatured situation during the official meeting, with a long table distancing him from Vladimir Putin. Bolsonaro, in his turn, submitted to all the demands of the Russian government and was able to have a meeting in a more informal-led style of diplomatic negotiation, sitting closer to the Russian president. For Bolsonaro supporters, this proximity is understood as a sign of respect, which suggests a given understanding of diplomatic autonomy.

    Bolsonaro’s volatile political style is widely considered as a cause for Brazil being sidelined in international discussions. Yet his supporters seek to convert this pariah status into an alternative form of leadership. His supporters often shared memes conveying fake news where traditional media outlets would have reported the alleged achievements of the ongoing administration. There was an initiative to manifest a purported global relevance of Bolsonaro. We came across memes where Bolsonaro was on the cover of a Time magazine edition that claimed he deserved a Nobel Prize. The fake cover of the magazine states that Bolsonaro was ‘the man who could define the world’s future’ due to his upcoming visit to Moscow. A similar joke, with a sexist and discriminatory undertone, stands out in the caption to another meme: ‘For a president who manages to reconcile an ex-wife and a current one without a fight or hair pulling, bringing peace to Russia and Ukraine is easy.’ Praising Bolsonaro has a metonymical character, in which the far-right leader represents the nationalist sentiment of his supporters. According to them, Bolsonaro would rescue Brazil’s greatness and its leading role in international arenas.

    (2) Attacking Adversaries and Institutions

    The second subset of memes circulating in pro-Bolsonaro WhatsApp groups serves the purpose of confronting and insulting those framed as political adversaries. This eclectic group includes not only international ‘opponents’, such as Biden and Macron, but also local ‘enemies’, such as the Workers’ Party (PT) and the mainstream media. Bolsonaro’s supporters considered these agents members of a conspiracy against traditional Christian values. International opponents are often gathered under the broad umbrella of a purported New Global Order, which would be liberal, progressive, and globalist, advocating the interests of the affluent elites of the world against those suffering the consequences of their actions.

    Bolsonaro is framed as a Western democratic bulwark. Many memes attacked Western leaders who are vocal critics of Bolsonaro - namely Angela Merkel, Joe Biden, Justin Trudeau, and Emmanuel Macron. According to these memetic narratives, these political leaders were portrayed as incapable of handling geopolitical crises. Thus, Bolsonaro’s supporters often share negative messages against these leaders - asserting they were weaker than the Brazilian president.

    Biden is repeatedly called ‘stupid,’ ‘incapable’ and ‘weak.’ This observation suggests Bolsonaro’s supporters also imported some theories circulating in digital conservative communities from the United States (Hyzen and Van den Bulck 2021). When Biden won, the alliance between Brazil and the United States was shaken, as Bolsonaro’s supporters took increasingly anti-American positions. In addition, Biden’s statements in favour of defending the environment triggered personal attacks and virulent messages in WhatsApp groups.

    Since Bolsonaro openly endorsed Trump during the last US elections in 2020, several memes compare Joe Biden with Donald Trump, assuring that the war could have been avoided by a ‘stronger’ voice. Biden is portrayed on different occasions in images with captions such as ‘strong men create easy times, but weak men create difficult times,’ a simplified version of the quote from G. Michael Hopf’s novel, ‘hard times create strong men, strong men create good times, good times create weak men, and weak men create hard times.’ Putin and Xi Jinping appear side by side in another meme with the caption ‘Strong from the East.’ Putin is also compared to Zelensky, in memes that bring a long curriculum of the former confronted with a one-word description of the latter: ‘comedian.’

    The insults to other world leaders are not random. They are perfectly in line with the ideological diplomacy adopted by the Bolsonaro government, which broke with a long-lasting tradition of universalism and multilateralism adopted by the Brazilian Foreign Ministry. Memes circulated by Bolsonaro’s supporters reflect previous attacks made or encouraged by the president against nations he believes are aligned with left-wing philosophies or are opposed to a conservative concert, as is the case of Ukraine vis-a-vis Russia.

    The attacks are not restricted to politicians in a rather strict sense. Environmental activist Greta Thunberg is an example of a public figure equally ridiculed by Bolsonaro’s supporters. Thunberg has voiced criticism of the Brazilian president’s environmental policy on several occasions, and Bolsonaro has responded by calling her a ‘brat’. Pro-Bolsonaro groups claim Thunberg represents movements that would be interested in controlling the Amazon. One of the memes claims: ‘[D]id somebody sees this little girl? She wanted to save the planet. Tell her that it is high time to do so’ – as if she did not care about the Ukrainian war.

    Domestic opponents are also represented in derogatory terms. The attacks on local politicians in the context of memes about the war in Ukraine have two fundamental purposes. Firstly, they perpetuate the agendas and stimulate the rivalries cultivated by Bolsonaro, keeping his bases mobilized from this antagonistic rhetoric. Secondly, these initiatives attempt to contain any paradiplomacy, as opponents gain some prominence in the political scene. To illustrate, Bolsonaro begins to systematically attack Governor João Doria, Jr., for instance, when Doria leads a vaccination campaign against Covid-19 and assumes a national role in the face of the Federal Government’s hesitation.

    Memes oscillate between ad hominem offenses through homophobic and misogynistic heavy-toned messages and accusations against third parties (e.g. depicting former representative Arthur do Val as a misogynistic person). Do Val claimed traveling to Ukraine during the war to endorse civilian movements against Russia. His trip ended with the revelation of audio recorded by him with sexist content against Ukrainian women. This episode triggered the spread of many memes joking about his audio.

    There are also many references to Bolsonaro’s main adversary: Lula da Silva. Some memes compared Lula to a weapon of mass destruction, suggesting Bolsonaro threatened Putin by sending his adversary to Russia. Other memes refer to his controversial arrest in 2018, making fun of the release of prisoners in Ukraine to fight the war with the following subtitles: ‘whereas Zelensky has freed prisoners to fight in a war, prisoners can run for President in Brazil.’

    Left-wing women are traditional targets of far-right groups such as Bolsonaro’s supporters (Heinemann and Stern 2022) For example, there are offenses against former President Dilma Rousseff that gravitated over sarcastic comments about her intelligence, making a wordplay in Portuguese with the expression ‘traumatismo craniano’ (head trauma) by changing it to ‘traumatismo ucraniano’ (Ukrainian trauma). Whereas these memes reinforced some prejudice against physical and cognitive characteristics, the same groups shared contents praising the controversial beauty of Bolsonaro’s wife, Michelle Bolsonaro. One of them depicts Putin staring at the first lady and claims he was looking for trouble against Brazil.

    The last important actor targeted by the Bolsonaro’s supporters war-related memes is the media. While the exposure Bolsonaro received on mainstream media talk shows helped bolster his public image and lent legitimacy to his controversial views (Chagas 2021), he increasingly came to regard the media as an adversary—except for the outlets aligned with his agenda. The memes that attack the media are part of this ecology and corroborate the strongly ideological and populist discursive strategy that the president propagates.

    The media is portrayed as an unreliable and malicious (communist) actor. For example, one meme claimed that CNN reported the death of a journalist during the war in Ukraine, stating that he had actually died in Afghanistan. Since Bolsonaro supporters’ groups generally disapproved of media outlets’ endorsement of sanitary measures and vaccination campaigns during the Covid-19 pandemic, some memes joked about the Russian invasion by drawing parallels with the pandemic period. A picture of a nationally known journalist has the following caption: ‘Russian troops invaded Ukraine neither wearing face masks nor carrying vaccination certificate.’

    (3) Spreading Political Agendas

    Bolsonaro’s supporters use the Ukrainian war to spread the interpretive frameworks that ground their arguments and worldviews. They shared memes using the Ukrainian war to justify far-right policies. The war clearly serves to reinforce their argument for allowing citizens to purchase firearms. Zelensky is ironically mocked for his previous anti-gun ideas, contrasting with his call for citizens to defend their country at the beginning of the Russian invasion.

    Anti-LGBTQIA+ discourse also serves to make fun of the presence of transgender personnel in the US army or suggest that adversaries are homosexuals. The photograph of the UK Pride Rally from 1995 shows protesters parading on top of a pink tank. Bolsonaro’s supporters employed this image to refer to the Western armies. The tank is used to challenge the virility (and capacity) of the American forces. Another photo of a pink tank mocks João Doria, Jr., saying that he himself would engage in the war with his tank.

    Some memes make fun of gender-neutral language. In one of the images, a female officer proposes replacing the masculine Portuguese term ‘soldado’ (soldier) with a neutral neologism: ‘soldade’. A final transphobic meme claimed: ‘Calm down! The USA is sending its gender-neutral troops’ while showing pictures of transgender people in military service in a derogatory way. The memes sought to make fun of their adversaries by depicting their alleged flawed masculinity. These frames hinged on recognized conservative agendas in Brazil: the opposition to the so-called gender ideology. Widely known fake news claimed that leftist governments were indoctrinating children in schools to become homosexuals through the distribution of booklets and absurd materials, such as a baby bottle with a penis-shaped nipple.

    Strengthening the framework of virility, a meme claims that the world quickly realized that men and guns are essential to protect women and children. Feminists are ironically questioned for an alleged request for women not to fight in Ukraine. On sexism, messages pro-Ukraine very often showed women sexually serving Kyiv’s army. We came across offensive memes comparing the ‘beauty’ of Ukrainian women and Bolsonaro’s rivals. Another meme exposed racist content where a poor afro-Brazilian girl was astonished by the alleged beauty of a blonde Ukrainian refugee. Another illustration of sexism is clear when women exerting the role of ministers of defence in several countries are contrasted to a male general in charge of the Russian forces. There are many derogatory references against women journalists who work for mainstream media outlets.

    The fundamentalist tone is complemented by messages arguably unveiling why the mainstream media criticizes Kremlin’s actions: Putin was fighting the New World Order. Nonetheless, there was also a meme linking Moscow to controversial theories diffused by Bolsonaro’s agenda. A message claimed Russian cyber warfare could pose a security risk to the electronic voting system in Brazil. Bolsonaro and his supporters have repeatedly put the Brazilian electoral system under suspicion. In Brazil, since the 1990s, elections employ electronic voting polls, regularly tested and internationally audited, to guarantee security against fraud. Bolsonaro was elected representative several times by the same system. He was elected president in 2018 through electronic polls. Despite this, he has questioned electoral results to destabilize the regime. Hence, the war in Ukraine works as another excuse to distrust democracy.

    Finally, another agenda strongly addressed by Bolsonaro’s supporters is that of scientific denialism. They have shared numerous anti-vax messages during the Covid-19 pandemic, for instance. The memes of the war in Ukraine do not let this topic go unnoticed. In some images, Putin is praised as a hero capable of restoring the world’s normality, as the media coverage had finally shifted away from the (allegedly fake) pandemic.

    (4) Hesitating of What is the War About

    Bolsonaro’s supporters had a hard time making sense of what was their side in the war. The ambiguous diplomatic behaviour adopted by Brazil during the Ukrainian war accentuated this philosophical challenge to interpret the contexts. Since Bolsonaro’s administration worked to sustain a neutral position toward this international episode, WhatsApp groups presented different messages trying to give sense to the war and classifying the parts involved into a good-versus-evil logic.

    On the one hand, they had what they usually see as the evil classic communists. On the other hand, they had what they tend to frame as the progressive globalists of the New World Order – e.g. the ‘new communists.’ There are affinities between the far-right groups of Ukraine and Brazil, but Moscow’s conservatism raised some doubt as to which would be the most strategic ally. This difficulty is noticed in some of the memes, although they quickly endorsed (and justified) Bolsonaro’s denial to take a stand against Russia. Putin, then, becomes an example of a strong leader, capable of showing what is wrong with the West’s soft politics. Yet the Holodomor is mentioned as a counterpoint to highlight the pernicious consequences of Soviet oppression. Socialist regimes in Latin America, such as Cuba and Venezuela, are presented as Russia’s allies in a clear nod to Ukrainian forces. Despite this, the pro-Ukraine media coverage was decisive for Bolsonaro’s supporters to take a favourable position toward the Russians.

    Putin and Bolsonaro are made equivalent by the treatment they allegedly received from the media. Bolsonaro’s supporters suggest media outlets persecuted the Brazilian president. A meme demonstrated, for example, that Putin would be at Bolsonaro’s neighbourhood. The messages refer to the fact that the gated community where Bolsonaro lives is the same one in which the main suspect accused of murdering councillor Marielle Franco (in an episode of political violence that became internationally famous) resided. Witnesses, such as the door attendant of the condominium, also linked the murderer with members of the Bolsonaro family. Because of this, Bolsonaro’s supporters claim that the president is unfairly linked to crimes. Some memes about the conflict in Ukraine suggest that Putin is also unfairly blamed by the media. One image shows a parrot, claiming that it was hired by a major news television station because it learned to say: ‘Putin is guilty.’

    Both Putin and Bolsonaro are interpreted as leaders who expose Western’s decadence. A meme urges people to endorse the Brazilian president because the war in Ukraine and the Covid-19 sanitary rules was supposedly part of a globalist plan. Another image claims Brazil would be the next target of the New World Order movement, due to its alleged interest in seizing control of the Amazon. NATO would be arguably working to invade Brazil because Western powers would have geopolitical ambitions to explore Amazon soil. Nationalist groups believe international agents plan an intervention in the Amazon region. Bolsonaro (Phillips 2019) shares this impression. Recently, Bolsonaro’s supporters praised Russia to veto a UNSC Resolution proposed by Niger and Ireland, in December 2021, to consider (Pannett 2021) climate change as a potential threat to global peace and stability. Bolsonaro’s supporters claimed Russia protected Brazilian territory against the use of multilateral mechanisms to justify a controversial invasion of Brazil to save the world. Fact-checking agencies in Brazil also denied information conveyed by memes from far-right WhatsApp groups framing this UNSC Resolution as an attempt to internationalize Amazon (Lima 2022).

    NATO’s ambiguous position in the conflict, refusing to intervene but promising to send supplies, is also ridiculed in the memetic narratives analysed. This is clear, for instance, in the meme that depicts Ukrainian soldiers expecting air support from NATO and simply seeing a plane with a motivational saying: ‘You are doing great.’ A series of memes in defence of the Russian president draws attention to how international public opinion was condescending to the wars in Afghanistan, Iraq, Libya and Syria - promoted by the United States - yet strongly condemns the conflict in Ukraine.

    (5) Mocking Ourselves

    The last subset of images we would like to draw attention to is not so clearly associated with Bolsonaro’s patriotic narratives or aspirations of greatness. These memes are intended to make people laugh, and they often do so by mocking ourselves and ‘who we are.’ There is, for instance, a meme that compares a trio elétrico (a truck equipped with a sound system and a stage for music performance typically used in Brazilian Carnival) with a Russian military truck with an explosive warhead.

    In another example, a meme makes fun of the Brazilian coercive forces, recalling a recent episode in which dozens of cops spent 20 days running after a single fugitive: ‘Brazil had trouble hunting a fugitive. Imagine a war!’ And a third one simulates an official decree of the Presidency of the Republic to claim that the Brazilian Army would be calling all the male reservists to present themselves for the war, with departure scheduled for the 25 February and return ‘only’ on Wednesday, 2 March, in case of survival. This timeframe corresponded to the Carnival.

    Another meme adapts to Portuguese a motif that also circulated in other countries. This meme compares the dangers of recent years, associating 2020 and 2021 with growing waves in the face of a helpless and naïve swimmer who cannot see the peril approaching him/her. Behind the two visible big waves, a Godzilla emerges, representing 2022. The menace refers to recent global events, including the war in Ukraine, emphasizing a sense of hopelessness.

    These examples suggest that even the memes that circulate in Pro-Bolsonaro groups are no exception to the rule when it comes to typical Brazilian humour. As Monteiro Lunardi (2018) argues, the self-deprecating jokes work as a kind of self-affirming action through irony in Brazilian culture. By laughing at themselves, the Brazilian people transform their suffering into resilience, embodying a picaresque spirit that refuses to be defeated. Although there is no direct inference in relation to foreign policy, these memes function as a kind of comic interval and serve to provoke public discussion around collectively constituted cultural identities.

    Concluding Remarks

    This paper sought to contribute to the growing interest about social media in IR. Although IR studies tend to emphasize state-related issues, we argue that the relevance of digital platforms to international relations is not limited to diplomatic and other official means. Social media discussions help one understands how national actors frame international affairs to strengthen their narratives and purposes. Digital platforms have become key arenas to understand social perceptions and narratives about international relations.

    The article focused on how supporters of the far-right former Brazilian President, Jair Bolsonaro, have dealt with the war in Ukraine in WhatsApp groups. We were particularly concerned with the memes circulating in these groups. Bolsonaro’s supporters saw the Ukrainian war as an opportunity to spread and revamp their ideological perspectives and political agendas via memetic grammars. In a community that shares a mistrust against media outlets, these easy-reading memes were able to convey ideas and frames that are central to Bolsonarism.

    We found five main purposes or presumed intentions in the use of memes in the investigated context: (1) praise of Jair Bolsonaro as a world leader; (2) attacks on adversaries and institutions; (3) dissemination of political agendas at the heart of Bolsonarism; (4) clarification of the actual meanings of the war; (5) laughing at ourselves. Across these categories, we observe the construction of an image of Bolsonaro as a strong leader, capable of aligning himself with other hardline politicians in shaping global affairs. We also found the constant use of a harsh humour to make fun of adversaries, framed as weak, incapable and ill-intended. Anti-LGBTQIA+, pro-gun ideas, Christianism, and chauvinism are recurrent topics in the analysed memes. A clear distinction between ‘Us’ and ‘Them’ works to remind Bolsonaro’s supporters that they would be standing in the right side, even if there is some ambivalence in the comprehension of what would be the ‘right side’ in the war. Since Brazilian diplomacy did not choose a side, Bolsonaro’s supporters became confused if the best option was condemning Russia as a former ‘communist’ country or standing with Putin against leaders considered to be progressive and ‘globalist.’

    Our conclusions reinforced that the political use of memes could open new avenues to IR studies. We aim to encourage further interpretative analyses about international relations that employ memes as data and understand their role in framing interpretations and narratives around key international affairs.
