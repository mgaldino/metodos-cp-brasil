FORUM - PUBLIC BUDGET AND FINANCE IN CONTEMPORARY BRAZIL: DIAGNOSES AND CHALLENGES • Cadernos Gestão Pública e Cidadania 30 • 2025 • https://doi.org/10.12660/cgpc.v30.93532 linkcopy

BUDGETING AND ACCOUNTABILITY IN LOCAL GOVERNMENTS ACROSS EVOLVING PUBLIC ADMINISTRATION PARADIGMS

Orçamento e controle nos governos locais sob os paradigmas em desenvolvimento no campo da administração pública

Presupuestación y rendición de cuentas en los gobiernos locales en los paradigmas de la administración pública en evolución

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    ABSTRACT

    This article explores the evolving dynamics between budgeting and accountability within local governments, emphasising the complex interplay of financial and non-financial accountability across different public administration paradigms - i.e, Traditional Public Administration, New Public Management, and New Public Governance. By critically examining how each paradigm shapes budgeting processes and accountability expectations, the paper highlights emerging tensions, particularly in contexts where these paradigms coexist. Financial accountability faces challenges from competing accounting methodologies, diverse expectations of solvency, and blurred jurisdictional boundaries due to collaborative governance and externalisation of services. Simultaneously, non-financial accountability calls for inclusive stakeholder engagement, participatory budgeting mechanisms, and comprehensive assessments of social and environmental outcomes. Digitalisation further complicates this landscape by offering innovative accountability tools yet potentially exacerbating existing inequalities. The paper advocates for an integrated, holistic approach that reconciles efficiency-driven financial practices with broader, stakeholder-centric governance demands, ultimately enhancing accountability, transparency, and the sustainable impact of local governments.

    Keywords:
    budgeting; accountability; local governments; public administration paradigms; stakeholder engagement.

    RESUMO

    O presente artigo aborda as dinâmicas de orçamento e controle que vêm se desenvolvendo nos governos locais, com especial atenção à complexa relação entre controle financeiro e não financeiro à luz de diferentes paradigmas no campo da administração pública, a saber: a Administração Pública Tradicional, a Nova Gestão Pública e a Nova Governança Pública. Por meio de um exame crítico de como cada paradigma molda os processos orçamentários e as expectativas em relação ao controle e à prestação de contas, o estudo revela as tensões que emergem, particularmente em contextos onde os paradigmas coexistem. Enquanto isso, controles não-financeiros pedem o envolvimento inclusivo das partes interessadas, mecanismos de orçamento participativo e avaliações abrangentes de resultados sociais e ambientais. Finalmente, o processo de digitalização acrescenta ainda mais complexidade a esse cenário, uma vez que oferece ferramentas de controle inovadoras, ao mesmo tempo em que potencialmente agrava desigualdades já existentes. Defendemos aqui uma abordagem holística e integrada, que concilie práticas financeiras orientadas à eficiência com demandas de governança centradas nas partes interessadas, visando aprimorar o controle, a transparência e o impacto sustentável dos governos locais.

    Palavras-chave
    orçamento; controle; governos locais; paradigmas da administração pública; envolvimento das partes interessadas.

    RESUMEN

    Este artículo explora la dinámica en evolución entre la presupuestación y la rendición de cuentas en los gobiernos locales, enfatizando la compleja interacción de la rendición de cuentas financiera y no financiera en diferentes paradigmas de la administración pública, es decir, la administración pública tradicional, la nueva gestión pública y la nueva gobernanza pública. Al examinar críticamente cómo cada paradigma configura los procesos presupuestarios y las expectativas de rendición de cuentas, el artículo destaca las tensiones emergentes, especialmente en contextos donde estos paradigmas coexisten. La rendición de cuentas financiera se enfrenta a los desafíos derivados de la competencia entre metodologías contables, las diversas expectativas de solvencia y la difuminación de los límites jurisdiccionales debido a la gobernanza colaborativa y la externalización de servicios. Simultáneamente, la rendición de cuentas no financiera exige la participación inclusiva de las partes interesadas, mecanismos de presupuestación participativa y evaluaciones exhaustivas de los resultados sociales y ambientales. La digitalización complica aún más este panorama al ofrecer herramientas innovadoras de rendición de cuentas, pero potencialmente exacerbar las desigualdades existentes. El artículo aboga por un enfoque integrado y holístico que concilie las prácticas financieras orientadas a la eficiencia con las demandas de gobernanza más amplias y centradas en las partes interesadas, mejorando en última instancia la rendición de cuentas, la transparencia y el impacto sostenible de los gobiernos locales.

    Palabras clave:
    presupuestación; rendición de cuentas; gobiernos locales; paradigmas de la administración pública; participación de las partes interesadas.

    INTRODUCTION

    Public budgeting and accountability are deeply connected and serve as foundational functions of government (Bovens et al., 2008; Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development [OECD], 2002; Sicilia & Steccolini, 2017). Budgeting is not only a technical process of allocating expenditures within the limits of available revenues in any given financial year (Anessi-Pessina et al., 2016) but it also plays a critical role in enabling accountability. According to Bovens (2007, p. 450), accountability is “a relationship between an actor and a forum, in which the actor has an obligation to explain and to justify his or her conduct, the forum can pose questions and pass judgment, and the actor may face consequences.” Through budgeting, local governments are evaluated by different forums on the extent to which they make decisions transparent, demonstrate responsible use of public resources, and uphold principles of fairness and equity (Anessi-Pessina et al., 2016; McDonald et al., 2024; Saliterer et al., 2018).

    Budgeting and accountability mechanisms, while critical to local governance, differ significantly across public administration paradigms and are fundamentally shaped by countrylevel regulatory structures, executive-legislative power dynamics, and politico-administrative traditions (McCarthy-Cotter & Flinders, 2017; Saliterer et al., 2018). It is well known that public sector management has been shaped by the tenets of Traditional Public Administration (TPA), New Public Management (NPM), and New Public Governance (NPG). Each paradigm embodies different assumptions about how public organisations should be run, how budgets should be formulated, and to whom and how local governments are accountable (Almquist et al., 2013; Bovens et al., 2014; Saliterer et al., 2018). While TPA focuses on bureaucratic accountability, based on rules, processes, and procedural compliance, NPM focuses on outputs, efficiency, and effectiveness - something criticised by NPG, which shifts the focus to outcomes rather than outputs, considering long-term public value (Almquist et al., 2013).

    TPA, followed by NPM, reinforced a narrow focus on financial accountability, emphasising adherence to fiscal rules and providing the fundamental framework for financial responsibility and oversight, which regulate budget approval, resource allocation, and expenditure control, leading to overreliance on cost-effectiveness and fiscal discipline (Almquist et al., 2013; Bracci et al., 2021; Broadbent & Guthrie, 2008). However, more recently, scholars and practitioners have recognised that financial oversight alone is insufficient to address the complex challenges of modern governance. Local governments must navigate increasingly dense accountability webs (Flinders, 2011), balancing fiscal responsibility with the broader expectations of diverse stakeholders - which reflects NPG’s emphasis on collaborative, networked governance. This evolution implies a transformation in budgeting from a mere technical exercise of financial planning1 to a process intertwined with strategic governance, transparency, and citizen engagement. Contemporary governance demands a broader conception of accountability, one that extends beyond financial probity to encompass social, ethical, and sustainability-oriented responsibilities (Grossi & Steccolini, 2015). The growing complexity of public administration and the increasing expectations of citizens means that local governments are not only accountable for financial discipline but also for the effectiveness, inclusivity, and long-term impact of their policies (Bracci et al., 2019; Carnegie & West, 2005; Helden & Steccolini, 2024).

    In this article, we explore the evolving nature of budgeting and accountability in local governments, addressing both financial and non-financial dimensions and the challenges they present. By critically examining these interwoven aspects, we highlight the tensions between economic efficiency and broader governance values - ultimately arguing for a more holistic approach to accountability in local government.

    BUDGETING AND ACCOUNTABILITY IN LOCAL GOVERNMENTS UNDER DIFFERENT PUBLIC ADMINISTRATION MODELS

    Traditionally, budgeting refers to the process through which public resources are allocated, managed, and controlled to meet governmental objectives - but more broadly, as tools for ensuring transparency and stakeholder engagement (Anessi-Pessina et al., 2016; Saliterer et al., 2018; Sicilia & Steccolini, 2017). Budgeting operates, at least, within a dual dimension, namely, technical and political (Saliterer et al., 2018). The technical dimension involves procedures, fiscal rules, financial classifications, and performance metrics, emphasising objectivity, predictability, and compliance. Conversely, the political dimension reflects the priorities of elected officials, involving negotiations, trade-offs, and power dynamics in resource allocation (Rubin, 2015; Wildavsky, 1964). Importantly, as highlighted by Saliterer et al (2018), budgetary documents and the budgeting processes are integral to external accountability, as governments are expected to be held accountable for their decisions regarding the allocation of funds raised from citizens, who ultimately pay the taxes that finance public services.

    Local governments operate within a complex and multifaceted environment often described as a “web of accountability,” where various forums, each with its own demands and expectations, may require different types of information presented in diverse formats and within different timeframes (Aleksovska & Schillemans, 2022; Schillemans, 2015). For instance, federal agencies may focus on compliance with fiscal rules, demanding detailed financial and budgetary reports, while audit organisations and citizens might prioritise information related to the performance and quality of public services. Citizens may specifically expect accessible and user-friendly information, known as popular reports (Cohen & Karatzimas, 2015). Additionally, audit bodies may require extensive evidence and documentation to assess the legality of expenditures (compliance auditing) and alignment to accounting standards (financial auditing). Moreover, the emergence of digitalisation in the public sector has further intensified the complexity of the accountability landscape for local governments, as different forums can increasingly require information at low cost (Lino et al., 2022; Lino et al., 2023), and digital tools and platforms, such as dashboards, e-government portals, and automated reporting systems, have transformed how non-financial accountability is performed and managed (Agostino et al., 2022).

    The multiplicity of accountability demands, often reflecting how budgeting processes are formulated (Saliterer et al., 2018), is further complicated by the coexistence of different public administration paradigms - i.e., TPA, NPM, and NPG - which operate simultaneously in many governance contexts. TPA prioritises hierarchical, bureaucratic control, emphasising procedural compliance and rule-based oversight (Almquist et al., 2013), meaning accountability is predominantly formalistic. Accordingly, budgeting under TPA stresses accuracy, procedural adherence, incrementalism, and line-item based input controls. NPM, in contrast, introduced performance-based accountability, focusing on efficiency, market-driven solutions, and measurable outputs, often leading to a managerial approach to accountability (Hood, 1995). Budgeting becomes more output and outcome-oriented, prioritising efficiency, managerial discretion, and measurable performance (Saliterer, 2018). NPG critiques both TPA and NPM, advocating for collaborative governance, outcome-oriented evaluation, and multi-stakeholder engagement (Osborne, 2010). Accountability becomes multi-dimensional and relational, involving multiple actors (citizens, civil society, private sector) through participatory mechanisms. Budgeting under NPG seeks broader engagement and transparency, as manifested by participatory budgeting exercises, and also embedding social equity and justice considerations (Azevedo et al., 2022; McDonald et al., 2024).

    However, rather than a linear transition from one paradigm to another, many governments today experience a paradoxical blend of these models, adding further complexity to accountability mechanisms (Pollit & Bouckaert, 2017). For example, fiscal rules and financial or compliance audit mechanisms remain grounded in TPA, requiring adherence to strict financial procedures and hierarchical oversight. Simultaneously, NPM-driven performance management demands measurable efficiency and cost-effectiveness, pushing local governments to implement privatesector-inspired reporting tools. At the same time, NPG encourages stakeholder participation, social accountability, and multi-actor governance, requiring transparency mechanisms that prioritise inclusivity and long-term impact. The interplay between these models may lead to “conflictual accountability” (Aleksovska & Schillemans, 2022), where fulfilling the requirements of one paradigm (e.g., financial solvency) may contradict or overshadow expectations from another (e.g., social or environmental accountability).

    Digitalisation also amplifies the challenges of managing multiple and potentially conflicting accountability forums. As digital tools become the primary medium for accountability, different stakeholders may demand that these systems be customised to meet their specific informational needs - including issues related to budgetary planning (Lino et al., 2023). For instance, an online portal designed to satisfy federal fiscal requirements may not be suitable for engaging local citizens, whose expectations might involve interactive and user-friendly platforms that provide real-time service updates.

    The pressure to balance these competing demands forces local governments to navigate a web where they must prioritise certain forms of accountability over others (Hwang & Han, 2017; Lino et al., 2022; Schillemans, 2015), potentially leading to selective transparency or strategic compliance, where they choose to disclose information that satisfies the most powerful or immediate stakeholders, often at the expense of broader, more inclusive accountability practices. Indeed, local accountability typically emphasises efficiency and financial performance, overlooking the diverse needs of local stakeholders (Bracci et al., 2021; Helden & Steccolini, 2024). This approach may sideline the needs and concerns of vulnerable communities, reinforcing existing power imbalances rather than addressing them (Biondi & Bracci, 2018). Moreover, digital transformation can lead to further fragmentation in accountability practices, as different systems operate in parallel, sometimes with limited integration or coordination (Lino et al., 2023). As local governments attempt to respond to these diverse digital demands, they may face increased pressure to harmonise conflicting requirements or, conversely, risk creating silos where certain aspects of accountability are prioritised over others. In this context, the emergence of digital accountability not only highlights the pre-existing tensions within the web of accountability but also raises new questions about interoperability, transparency, and the consistency of digital reporting standards across different forums.

    Against this background, namely, the coexistence of overlapping public administration paradigms with conflicting expectations and recent digital innovations, deeper tensions between financial and non-financial accountability may emerge. As local governments attempt to meet increasingly diverse demands, they face distinct yet interrelated challenges on both sides. The following sections unpack these dynamics by first examining the evolving challenges associated with financial accountability. This is followed by an exploration of issues related to non-financial accountability.

    CHALLENGES FOR FINANCIAL ACCOUNTABILITY IN LOCAL GOVERNMENTS

    We argue that the complexity of financial accountability in local governments can be attributed to the sedimentation (Pollit & Bouckaert, 2017) and interplay of public administration paradigms that distinctly shape budgeting and accountability practices - introducing unique challenges to public financial management.

    Under TPA, financial accountability is traditionally legalistic and procedural, requiring strict adherence to budgetary laws, expenditure ceilings, and legally mandated fiscal limits (Almquist et al., 2013). Consequently, many local governments continue to operate accounting systems based on cash (which records cash inflows and outflows) or modified cash/accrual (which tracks commitments to future cash inflows and outflows) basis. Also known as “budgetary accounting,” such systems are designed primarily to ensure budgetary compliance and facilitate hierarchical oversight. However, as described by Olson et al. (1998) and Guthrie et al. (2005), the traditional cash-based approach can limit the quality and usefulness of financial information for broader managerial and stakeholder purposes. Within this paradigm, budgeting remains largely incremental and input-based, prioritising predictability and procedural adherence. This incremental approach may restrict the capacity of local governments to respond flexibly and strategically to emerging governance challenges, such as sudden financial shocks or evolving policy priorities (Anessi-Pessina et al., 2016; Barbera et al., 2017, 2021; Saliterer et al., 2018).

    In contrast, the introduction of NPM paradigms shifted accountability toward efficiency, managerial discretion, and measurable outputs, demanding reforms in accounting systems toward accrual accounting (Hood, 1995) - which records expenses when they are incurred rather than when they are paid and revenues when they are earned rather than when they are received. Also known as “financial accounting,” this shift has significantly altered financial accountability, emphasising the assessment of long-term financial sustainability rather than mere compliance. Budgeting under NPM transitioned from incrementalism to performance-based. However, aligning budgets with performance outcomes has proved challenging. Driven by NPM principles, initiatives like the adoption of International Public Sector Accounting Standards (IPSAS), grounded in accrual accounting, were implemented in many local governments globally, aiming at improving transparency and decision-making information (Brusca et al., 2017; Pina et al., 2009). However, the transition is complicated by complex implementation processes, varied adoption across regions, and significant gaps between theoretical benefits and practical effectiveness (Lapsley et al., 2009; Pina et al., 2009). Different countries, including emerging economies, have taken varied approaches to adopting these systems, resulting in a lack of uniformity (Brusca et al., 2017; Salato et al., 2024). Despite its accrual foundation, the new systems have not always succeeded in enhancing management or accountability, largely because they often fail to meet the informational needs of managers and stakeholders (Bonollo, 2023). The disparity between traditional and accrual-based accounting systems can hinder effective local government management and degrade the quality of decision-making information (Guthrie et al., 2005; Olson et al., 1998). In Europe, for example, many municipal governments, particularly outside the UK and Nordic countries, have introduced accrual accounting without integrating it into decision-making processes, leading to poor data quality and limited crossborder comparability (Pina et al., 2009).

    The coexistence of competing accounting methodologies and information demands from different forums, previously described as the complex “web of accountability” (Aleksovska & Schillemans, 2022; Schillemans, 2015), further complicates financial accountability. For instance, local governments typically find themselves at the intersection between two distinct yet intertwined domains: “government financial statistics” (often referred to as “national statistics,” i.e., the fiscal world) and “government financial reporting” (i.e., the accounting world). The former is macroeconomic in nature, designed to represent the economy as a whole and its various sub-sectors through an aggregate approach. In contrast, government financial reporting focuses on recording and representing all economic events and transactions that affect each individual public sector entity, such as city governments, and is more microeconomic and detailed. These tensions are further exacerbated when financial accountability is assessed through international comparisons, where differences in institutional contexts and accounting frameworks complicate meaningful evaluation (Bosse et al., 2021). While accounting seeks technical objectivity, accumulating and reporting all available data so that, from these records, a detailed view of the financial reality can be constructed, financial transparency obtained, and managerial utility found (aligned with NPM ideals), fiscal rules are normative and oriented toward specific political and economic goals (reflecting the logic of TPA). These differing approaches highlight the interests and objectives of two distinct communities: national statistic offices on one side and private actors, professional standard setters, accountancy bodies, and audit firms on the other. This inherent conflict between macroeconomic-oriented fiscal accountability and microeconomic-oriented accounting transparency creates tensions that local governments must continually negotiate. It is noteworthy that when accounting techniques are transferred across these domains, they often undergo modification, leading to a hybridisation influenced by political power and governance dynamics (Kurunmäki et al., 2010; Mennicken & Miller, 2012), as it impacts their perceived solvency and financial sustainability. Budgeting practices become a key site of conflict as they must simultaneously meet macroeconomic fiscal discipline rules (TPA), microeconomic performance targets (NPM), and transparency demands from diverse stakeholders (NPG).

    Assessing financial sustainability, a central component of modern financial accountability, has become increasingly complex for local governments due to diverse, sometimes incompatible measurement methodologies (Gardini & Grossi, 2018; Padovani, 2016). While it is generally accepted that multiple measures can be applied to evaluate a local government’s financial sustainability (Andersen & Mortensen, 2010; Levine et al., 2013), these approaches range from basic accounting and financial reporting analyses to qualitative assessments derived from audit reviews and more sophisticated statistical models (Honadle et al., 2003; Kleine et al., 2003; Murray & Dollery, 2005). As mentioned by Padovani et al. (2018), there is no universally accepted set of measures for evaluating a local government’s financial sustainability, particularly when comparing local governments across different countries. Nevertheless, four key dimensions of municipal financial distress are widely recognised (Groves et al., 2003; Hendrick, 2011; Jacob & Hendrick, 2012). First, cash solvency, evaluates a local government’s liquidity and cash management, particularly its ability to meet immediate liabilities such as payroll and payments to suppliers. Ineffective cash management can result in payment delays and disruptions in service delivery. Second, budget solvency measures whether a local government’s annual revenues are sufficient to cover its expenditures without relying on borrowing or depleting reserves. Persistent deficits are a clear indicator of financial distress. Third, long-run solvency assesses the impact of longterm obligations on future resources, evaluating the local government’s financial sustainability over time. A lack of long-run solvency places a financial burden on future generations. Finally, service-level solvency gauges the local government’s ability to maintain and deliver the necessary public services. Reducing services to improve finances may lead to public dissatisfaction and result in services falling below acceptable standards. In sum, local governments must simultaneously manage immediate liquidity (cash solvency), annual budget balance (budget solvency), long-term obligations (long-run solvency), and acceptable levels of public service delivery (service-level solvency), each reflecting distinct accountability pressures from the TPA, NPM, and NPG paradigms. These dimensions must be explicitly integrated into budgeting practices, requiring local governments to balance immediate budgetary pressures with longer-term sustainability concerns. However, the diverse and often conflicting expectations of different accountability forums make budgeting decisions contentious, forcing local governments into difficult trade-offs between maintaining fiscal discipline, achieving service-level solvency, and ensuring broader social accountability (Bracci et al., 2021; Helden & Steccolini, 2024). Evidence highlights a complex interplay in financial management, whereby decisions that may support long-term financial sustainability can undermine short-term stability and vice versa (Iacuzzi et al., 2025in press). Thus, while financial accountability under NPM and NPG emphasises long-run and service-level solvency, the TPA model prioritises compliance-oriented indicators like cash and budget solvency, complicating integrated assessments of financial health across diverse governance settings.

    Additionally, the issue of “jurisdictional perimeters” adds another layer of complexity to local financial accountability. With increasing trends toward externalisation, corporatisation, publicprivate partnerships, and co-production at local levels - developments particularly encouraged by NPM and NPG approaches - the boundaries defining local government responsibilities have become blurred (Bovaird, 2004; Farneti et al., 2010; Rackwitz & Raffer, 2024). Financial accountability under these conditions requires consolidated accounts capturing not only the local government but its subsidiaries and related entities (Grossi, 2009; Rackwitz & Raffer, 2024), while also raising broader questions on the nature of accountability in participatory settings and how to evaluate accountability in this context (Osborne, 2009). In budgeting, this means resources must be allocated transparently across multiple organisational forms, reflecting complex governance networks rather than traditional hierarchical structures. Such complexity challenges traditional budgeting systems, demanding more sophisticated methods capable of consolidating budgetary information across jurisdictions, sectors, and governance structures (Azevedo et al., 2022; McDonald et al., 2024). Moreover, differing legal interpretations, operational perimeters, and accounting treatments complicate valid and transparent financial comparisons across jurisdictions, challenging the fundamental objectives of financial accountability and transparency under all paradigms. In short, the legal and operational perimeters of a local government may differ due to varying interpretations, complicating comparisons. Additionally, when accounting differences between the different entities are factored in, achieving valid comparisons, especially at the international level, becomes increasingly difficult (Padovani et al., 2018).

    In sum, financial accountability in local governments currently faces multi-layered challenges emerging from the sedimentation of TPA, NPM, and NPG paradigms. First, while financial accountability under TPA and NPM prioritises monetary resources, fiscal discipline, and measurable efficiency, NPG emphasises broader societal impacts, stakeholder participation, and sustainability, creating tensions in budgeting processes - both political and technical. Second, financial accountability is further complicated by the coexistence of competing accounting methodologies - namely, cash-based and accrual accounting - which reflect divergent information demands and governance objectives. Cash-based accounting is more closely aligned with the principles of TPA, whereas accrual accounting responds to the managerial logic and governance logic underpinning NPM and NPG. Third, defining and assessing financial sustainability across diverse institutional and territorial contexts presents significant methodological and practical challenges, particularly in balancing short-term fiscal compliance with a long-term perspective. These tensions reflect the differing emphases of TPA, which prioritises shortterm fiscal discipline, and NPM and NPG, which promote a longer-term strategic outlook. Finally, evolving models of public service delivery (such as corporatisation, associated with NPM, and especially co-production, central to NPG) further complicate financial reporting and blur accountability structures, primarily due to divergent definitions and interpretations of jurisdictional boundaries. These issues are even more pronounced under NPG, where the involvement of multiple autonomous actors in service delivery makes it particularly difficult to attribute financial responsibilities and assess overall sustainability (Table 1).

    These challenges manifest as conflicts between compliance and performance, tensions between different accounting and reporting approaches, complexities in measuring financial sustainability, and the blurred jurisdictional boundaries of local governance. Budgeting processes lie at the heart of these tensions, serving as a primary mechanism for negotiating between competing accountability demands. Thus, addressing financial accountability effectively demands recognising these interconnected challenges and pursuing integrated approaches that balance competing demands for procedural compliance, managerial efficiency, and stakeholder-driven transparency. The following section will build upon these insights by examining the evolving challenges associated with non-financial accountability, further emphasising the holistic nature of accountability practices in contemporary local governance.

    CHALLENGES FOR NON-FINANCIAL ACCOUNTABILITY IN LOCAL GOVERNMENTS

    Local non-financial accountability involves addressing a range of challenges that differ significantly from financial matters. However, these challenges can similarly be traced back to the sedimentation of different public administration paradigms.

    Traditional financial accountability in local governments primarily focuses on numerical data such as budgets, expenditures, and revenues. However, as mentioned in previous sections, local governments are increasingly expected to evaluate broader social and environmental effects (Grossi & Steccolini, 2015). NPM reforms have faced criticism for their heavy focus on efficiency, competition, and market-based principles, often at the expense of equity, social justice, and sustainability (Bracci et al., 2021; Helden & Steccolini, 2024). The emphasis on performance measurement through financial or output-oriented metrics may neglect the complex societal impacts and long-term consequences of public policies (Vollmer et al., 2024), and the challenge lies in transitioning from one-way financial reporting to inclusive, stakeholder-driven accountability systems (Grossi & Argento, 2022).

    NPG models advocate for a shift away from these market-oriented practices toward a more collaborative and participatory approach. For instance, associated with collaborative governance, social accountability enables local governments to “embrace broader accountability for their wider social impacts encompassing the accountability for the impacts their actions have on other organisations, individuals and the wider environment” (O’Dwyer & Unerman, 2007, p. 450). Consequently, local governments go beyond financial figures and integrate diverse forms of accountability that reflect community values and long-term sustainability goals (Helden & Steccolini, 2024), calling for accounting systems that recognise the relational nature of public sector work, moving beyond the traditional monological (one-way) reporting formats of NPM (Grossi & Argento, 2022; Manetti et al., 2021). Therefore, budgeting processes must evolve from incremental or output-based budgeting toward more participatory forms, such as participatory budgeting, to incorporate community priorities, equity considerations, and sustainability goals directly into resource allocation decisions (Azevedo et al., 2022; McDonald et al., 2024). Moreover, involving citizens directly, for instance, through co-assessment practices where citizens are actively engaged in determining what metrics are relevant to measure, may ensure that the reported outcomes resonate with community needs and values (Barbera et al., 2024).

    The NPG shift toward outcomes and long-term societal impacts (Vollmer et al., 2024) requires new methods to assess performance comprehensively, moving beyond conventional metrics that primarily measure efficiency and effectiveness. Consequently, budgeting practices under NPG must incorporate outcome-based measures that explicitly link resource allocation to broader societal objectives and long-term policy impacts, such as those related to sustainable development (Bisogno et al., 2024). However, aligning budgets with these non-financial outcomes poses methodological and practical difficulties, particularly because outcomes are often difficult to quantify, predict, or attribute directly to specific budgetary decisions, and the comparability of results is at risk (Padovani & Scorsone, 2009).

    Measuring outcomes and long-term impacts remains challenging due to the complexity and interconnectedness of public sector issues, where a plurality of external events or conditions can influence policy success beyond the direct control of local governments (Pollit & Bouckaert, 2017). For instance, assessing the impact of environmental sustainability policies must consider immediate outputs (such as emissions reductions) and also long-term outcomes (such as improved public health and ecosystem resilience). Given that multiple factors outside government control, such as economic trends or societal attitudes, affect these outcomes, local governments need multi-dimensional assessment frameworks that blend quantitative metrics with qualitative evaluations, stakeholder feedback, and context-specific indicators (Helden & Steccolini, 2024; Vollmer et al., 2024). Integrating such multi-dimensional assessments into budgeting processes is particularly challenging, as local governments must reconcile traditional budgeting frameworks, focused on short-term predictability and fiscal discipline, with newer approaches that demand flexibility, adaptive management, and responsiveness to shifting community priorities and external conditions.

    By adopting these broader perspectives, local governments can create a more comprehensive and inclusive approach to non-financial accountability, shifting from a purely economic logic to one that truly captures the complexity and diversity of public sector value. It involves transitioning from traditional, one-way forms of accountability - where governments report to the public - toward more dialogical and relational methods, where citizens and communities are directly involved in deciding what to measure and how to measure it (Barbera et al., 2024). TPA usually restricts stakeholder involvement, emphasising procedural compliance and hierarchical oversight. In contrast, NPG emphasises co-production, citizen engagement, and participatory governance, but balancing diverse stakeholder interests remains challenging (Vinnari & Dillard, 2016). Digital platforms have expanded public engagement opportunities but simultaneously risk exclusion through digital divides (Agostino et al., 2022).

    Thus, a major challenge for non-financial accountability in local governments is related to engaging stakeholders meaningfully in these processes (Helden and Steccolini, 2024). For instance, integrating the priorities of various stakeholders, who often have different and sometimes conflicting interests, must involve the adoption of participatory mechanisms that bring these groups together, ensuring that their perspectives shape public policies (Bebbington et al., 2007; Brown, 2009; Vinnari & Dillard, 2016). For example, citizen consultations on service delivery quality, such as public transport or healthcare facilities, allow communities to voice their needs and preferences directly. This feedback can guide improvements that align services with community expectations, thus enhancing transparency and trust between local governments and the public. Another challenge in promoting non-financial accountability is bridging the digital divide, which can prevent some stakeholders from participating fully in digital engagement platforms. While digital tools, such as online surveys and community apps, can facilitate broader engagement by gathering feedback on service quality or environmental projects, they may exclude those without access to technology or digital skills (Agostino et al., 2022; Mizrahi & Minchuk, 2019). Maintaining offline engagement methods, such as town hall meetings focused on discussing the quality of local services or community forums on environmental initiatives, also ensures that all stakeholders, including those who prefer faceto-face interactions, can engage in shaping public services.

    However, engaging stakeholders in non-financial accountability extends beyond simple consultation - a practice more aligned with the NPM perspective, which, compared to TPA, places greater emphasis on designing public services in response to user preferences. Through co-production, which is typical of NPG, citizens and community groups work alongside local governments to design, implement, and evaluate services (Loeffler & Bovaird, 2021). For instance, environmental sustainability initiatives might include partnerships with local NGOs and residents to create and maintain green spaces or develop community recycling programs. By involving stakeholders as active partners in these efforts, local governments can ensure that projects are effective, responsive, and tailored to the community’s environmental goals.

    Another critical aspect of non-financial accountability is ensuring that information about non-financial outcomes, such as service quality or sustainability impacts, is accessible and understandable to all stakeholders (Bracci et al., 2023; Cohen & Karatzimas, 2015). Traditional reports often use technical language and formats that may be difficult for the general public (and even politicians) to understand (Jordan et al., 2016; Raudla, 2022). Therefore, local governments must adopt communication strategies that present non-financial information in simplified, accessible formats. Simplified reporting not only enhances transparency but also empowers citizens to engage more meaningfully in discussions about community priorities and service outcomes. More engagement with citizens when reporting performance information may also lead to more trust in the government (Grosso & Ryzin, 2011).

    According to Sicilia et al. (2016), the provision of public services has changed drastically in the last few decades, including allowing managers greater autonomy to improve the efficiency of public organisations, the marketisation of public services, and attempts for cooperation with and the involvement of members of the community. However, this evolution brings challenges to non-financial accountability (Virtanen et al., 2018). From a budgeting perspective, these hybrid service-delivery models require local governments to move beyond traditional line-item budgeting toward more complex resource allocation mechanisms, which can transparently account for financial and non-financial contributions from multiple partners, including private firms, NGOs, and citizen groups. Regarding marketisation, typical of NPM, when local governments collaborate with external entities such as non-profits, private firms, or other government bodies, in a typical outsourcing setting, these partnerships, while beneficial for resource pooling and service enhancement, complicate accountability (Pollit, 2006). This is because the standards, expectations, and regulatory frameworks of different partners may conflict or overlap (Padovani & Young, 2003), making it challenging to establish a unified accountability structure.

    Moreover, involving citizens and communities also brings challenges to accountability. For instance, Verschuere et al. (2012, p. 1094) questioned: “[...] who can the users hold accountable when the users themselves are part of the production process?” highlighting the inherent complexity of accountability when citizens and communities co-produce services, particularly concerning the quality of public services in these collaborative settings. For instance, co-design and co-delivery, typical of NPG, are representative of such challenges, as citizens actively shape the service’s structure and implementation, making it harder to identify clear accountability lines when these services fall short of expectations. Budgeting, as a process, under such conditions must account for shared responsibilities, potentially adopting flexible, participatory budget frameworks to ensure transparency, fairness, and ensure accountability.

    Digitalisation plays a critical role in new modes of service delivery. On the one hand, digital tools such as mobile apps for service monitoring create opportunities for continuous feedback loops, making it easier to document and demonstrate accountability through open access to performance data and user feedback. On the other hand, digitalisation can also exacerbate accountability issues. As mentioned, the reliance on digital tools may exclude those who lack access or digital literacy, leading to digital divides that undermine the inclusivity and fairness of co-production efforts (Agostino et al., 2022). Budget transparency and accountability mechanisms relying primarily on digital platforms might unintentionally marginalise certain stakeholders, highlighting the need for inclusive budgeting practices that combine digital and traditional formats to ensure broad stakeholder participation.

    In sum, we discussed three challenges for local non-financial accountability. First, while financial accountability emphasises monetary resources, budgeting, and fiscal discipline, non-financial accountability reflects NPM and NPG approaches that incorporate broader perspectives extending beyond financial metrics. In particular, budgeting under NPG increasingly integrates qualitative indicators, social justice considerations, and sustainability objectives, thereby challenging traditional frameworks focused on economic efficiency and fiscal control. Second, while TPA limits stakeholder inclusion to hierarchical oversight, NPM introduces user consultation to align services with citizen preferences, and NPG advances this further by promoting participatory governance and co-production with diverse actors. Third, while TPA is rooted in direct public provision with clear hierarchical accountability, NPM introduces marketisation and managerial autonomy through outsourcing and performance contracts, adding a layer of complexity; with NPG, collaborative service models-such as co-production and co-design-that blur traditional accountability lines, demand more flexible, inclusive, and participatory budgeting frameworks.

    Thus, non-financial accountability under the most developed public administration paradigm, NPG, requires careful balancing between inclusivity, transparency, and clarity in accountability structures, particularly within co-produced or digitally mediated services. Local governments must develop innovative accountability and budgeting mechanisms that accurately reflect the complexities inherent in these new governance forms, thereby addressing both financial prudence and broader societal expectations.

    CONCLUSION

    This article has argued that local governments face increasingly complex accountability demands shaped by overlapping public administration paradigms (TPA, NPM, NPG). Each paradigm introduces distinct accountability expectations: TPA emphasises procedural compliance and hierarchical control; NPM prioritises efficiency and measurable performance; and NPG focuses on collaborative governance, inclusivity, and long-term outcomes. The coexistence of these paradigms creates tension in budgeting processes, as local governments must simultaneously address conflicting accountability demands related to financial discipline, performance metrics, stakeholder engagement, and sustainability.

    Financial accountability has grown more intricate due to conflicting accounting practices, varying interpretations of financial sustainability, and blurred jurisdictional boundaries arising from collaborative service delivery models. Similarly, non-financial accountability presents challenges related to stakeholder inclusion, digital divides, measurement of qualitative outcomes, and the complexity of co-produced services. Digital technologies both facilitate and complicate these accountability mechanisms by creating new avenues for transparency and participation while also amplifying risks of exclusion and fragmentation.

    Consequently, contemporary local governance demands a holistic, integrated approach to budgeting and accountability that recognises and balances these conflicting pressures. Achieving effective accountability requires going beyond narrow fiscal or managerial criteria, incorporating multi-dimensional performance assessments, meaningful stakeholder participation, and adaptive governance frameworks that accommodate diverse demands. Ultimately, reconciling financial prudence with social responsibility and long-term sustainability is essential for effective, inclusive, and responsive local governance.

    Against this background, future research and practice should focus on the development of budgeting and accountability mechanisms that are robust, inclusive, and capable of addressing complex societal challenges. Researchers can focus on studying integrated frameworks that mitigate the usual trade-offs between financial and social sustainability (Sinervo & Laihonen, 2024). For instance, exploring empirical cases in emerging areas like climate or gender budgeting, which demonstrate how sustainability and equity goals can be effectively merged with traditional budgeting criteria. Moreover, given the persistent coexistence of multiple governance paradigms, additional studies should investigate institutional and managerial strategies that help local governments navigate conflicting demands. Along the same lines, understanding how smaller, resource-constrained municipalities manage paradigm shifts can be explored by future studies - i.e., focusing on tailored implementation strategies suitable for different municipal contexts, potentially identifying capacity thresholds required for successfully adopting NPM or NPG practice. Finally, and more broadly, analysing how plural values are operationalised within budgets and who holds organisations accountable for achieving diverse outcomes, represents a critical forward-looking research agenda.
