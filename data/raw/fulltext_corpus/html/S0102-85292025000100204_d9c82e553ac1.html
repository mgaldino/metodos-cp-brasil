Bolsonaro’s Foreign Policy in Laerte Coutinho’s Cartoons

A política externa brasileira bolsonarista nas charges de Laerte

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article examines former President Jair Bolsonaro’s foreign policy through Laerte Coutinho’s cartoons, published in the Folha de São Paulo newspaper, contextualised within Brazilian opening speeches at the UN General Assembly’s Annual Debate from 2019 to 2021. The work is epistemologically tied to the aesthetic turn in International Relations, broadly in line with a post-structuralist approach and uses iconographic analysis (including the tools of particular meanings and each cartoon’s situational context) and iconological interpretation methods (applying area concepts to explain foreign policy elements revealed through the sensitivity of the chosen works). Consequently, we identified that the cartoons effectively convey former President Bolsonaro’s approach to Brazilian foreign policy (BFP). It is an alternative epistemological and methodological approach to understanding international affairs.

    Keywords
    Brazilian foreign policy; Jair Bolsonaro; cartoons; iconographic analysis; iconological interpretation

    Resumo

    Este artigo analisa a política externa do ex-presidente Jair Bolsonaro a partir de charges da cartunista Laerte Coutinho, publicadas no jornal Folha de São Paulo, no contexto dos discursos brasileiros de abertura do Debate Anual da Assembleia Geral da ONU, entre os anos de 2019 e 2021. O trabalho se vincula epistemologicamente à virada estética nas Relações Internacionais e emprega os métodos da análise iconográfica (e as ferramentas dos significados particulares e do contexto situacional de cada charge) e da interpretação iconológica (com aplicação de conceitos da área para explicar elementos da política externa que se revelam a partir da sensibilidade das obras selecionadas). Como resultado, identificamos que as charges revelam e comunicam adequadamente a forma de agir do ex-presidente Bolsonaro na condução da política externa brasileira. Trata-se de uma perspectiva epistemológica e metodológica alternativa para compreender os assuntos internacionais.

    Palavras-chave
    política externa brasileira; Jair Bolsonaro; charges; análise iconográfica; interpretação iconológica

    Introduction

    The academy has privileged the written word in its effort to understand the world and its phenomena. This is the primary medium for recording and circulating knowledge. The advent of the internet and digital media increasingly confronts people with images, whose utility is relevant for understanding the contemporary world.

    Among the fundamental ideas of those advocating for the use of aesthetic sources in understanding international matters is the concept that an inevitable gap exists between every represented object (that about which something is produced) and its representation (be it a text or a painting), which encompasses the author’s values and perspectives (Bleiker 2009). Bleiker (2001, 2009) distinguishes between mimetic and aesthetic representations. Mimetic representations aim for the utmost similarity between the represented and the representation—a form of imitative depiction. For the author, this is the main method of representing international politics-related themes. On the other hand, aesthetic representations acknowledge the gap between the represented and their representations, identifying it as the space where politics resides and can be understood. Therefore, analyses incorporating aesthetic criteria enable the assimilation of elements beyond the reach of mimetic forms.

    In theoretical terms, this work is broadly in line with a post-structuralist perspective, analysing how discourses socially construct meanings and form representations and ultimately enable subjects to understand themselves and others (Gregory 1989; Neumann 2008). Discourses, here, are understood in the Foucauldian sense, as systems situated in time and space, producing meanings and senses. Furthermore, images provoke thought, convey and spread ideas, and interact with other symbolic forms, thereby creating a field of meanings through their existence (Samain 2012: 22-23).

    The presentation of Jair Bolsonaro’s foreign policy through Laerte’s cartoons acknowledges the political weight carried by such representations and the understanding that discursive language is both social and political, thus positioning the work within a post-structuralist theoretical framework.

    Among aesthetic representations using images for world comprehension, cartoons hold a significant place in political science. This is because as a satirical portrayal of society, the cartoon offers a unique interpretation and stance (Maringoni 1996: 86). It serves to provide a critical perspective on a topic and circulate the content of neglected arts, discredited by a consumer culture that disregards non-commercial arts (Ventura 2016). Finally, it aims at being widely circulated and understood by the general public. In contrast to works from specific artistic schools requiring an understanding of unique conceptual frameworks, the illustrations discussed in this article were published in a widely circulated Brazilian newspaper and relate to recent events.

    In this context, this article uses an interdisciplinary methodology to analyse cartoons by Laerte Coutinho, revealing them as documents about Jair Bolsonaro’s foreign policy. Laerte is a renowned Brazilian cartoonist, famous for creating the ‘Piratas do Tietê’ in the 1980s, a bold satire of Brazilian urban chaos. This title also names her comic strip in Folha de São Paulo (FSP), which continues to publish her work. Its features provoke deep reflection on Brazil’s economic inequalities, political issues, and various forms of violence. Laerte’s work demonstrates a history of challenging predatory capitalism, advocating for democracy and popular movements, contributing to the struggle for redemocratisation, supporting workers’ rights, and recently, highlighting the violence of Brazilian society and institutions against LGBTQIA+ groups. During Jair Bolsonaro’s four-year term, Laerte used her cartoons to express denunciation and resistance. This article analyses cartoons from her repertoire of productions during this period, particularly those created on the eve of Brazil’s participation in the annual United Nations General Assembly (UNGA) meetings.

    The work uses interdisciplinary methods, combining iconographic analysis and iconological interpretation. We aim at perceiving more than graphic art in the images, viewing them as significant documents that require interpretation. This interpretation should be guided by the reconstruction of their creation context and application of concepts from the field of Foreign Policy. This analysis aids in broadening the current understanding and fostering future comprehension of the context and meaning of Brazilian foreign policy (BFP) in the considered four-year period.

    Iconography and iconology in International Relations

    Adapting iconographic and iconological procedures to International Relations involves both challenges and benefits and starts from a conception of a pluralist methodological framework suggested by Roland Bleiker, in particular, the second type mentioned: ‘the understanding of the images themselves’ (2015: 878). The fact that the Humanities have produced a substantial amount of literature on the subject (Burke 2017) contributes to the task. In the field of adversity, analysing artistic manifestations applied to a field of ‘hard politics’ is a complicated exercise because it requires unveiling the political position and understanding the artist’s representations and intentions, which are usually subjective, about complex moments or events. Add to this the difficulty of understanding the conversion of public speeches and actions into art and, given our specific interest, the need to endow them with a conceptual meaning that is particular to International Relations (IR). In this case, the goal is to articulate an aspect of the foreign policy of Jair Bolsonaro’s government, observed from the clipping of his discursive manifestations at meetings of the United Nations (UN), with the imagetic allegories constructed in the cartoons of Laerte. The aim is to contribute to an explanation of a peculiar historical moment in terms of form and content of Brazil’s international activities.

    In the views of Aby Warburg and Erwin Panofsky in the early 20th century, and more recently Rafael García Mahíques (2008), iconographic analysis involves studying image-text from both denotative and connotative perspectives. Denotation involves reporting the elements in a composition; connotation refers to the intentions and objectives in the forms of representation. Here, we focus on connotative analysis, explaining a specific object through the pictorial and textual elements in an image. Iconological interpretation is structured on image-textual analysis within its historical-cultural context, guided by concepts from a specific study field. Apart from describing and attributing meaning to a representation, it aims at contextualising it within a specific spatial, temporal, and cultural framework, mediated by the artist. It assigns a combined meaning to the work and artist, derived from the ‘spirit of the time.’ The author and work thus encapsulate an artistic allegory of their historical context, influenced by the author’s unique perceptions, whether apparent or internalised in the unconscious (Burke 2017; Penn 2008: 323-325). Thus, iconological interpretation combines conceptual parameters from various fields, unlike iconographic analysis, even in its connotative perspective.

    Criticisms of the formal iconographic-iconological image analysis method targeted its basic principles and conclusions about the nature of art and artists. The most relevant consideration is the contextual mechanical interpretation of image analysis. For this bibliography, the act of creation is not merely a product of its time (Burke 2017), and reducing the artist’s work to allegories of the context is at best incomplete (Gombrich 1999: 241). Factors such as the difficulty of pictorially representing reality’s complexity and the assignment of rationalities the author himself denies underpin the criticisms of this method (Ginzburg 1989; Rampley 2001; Burke 2017).

    This positivist or structuralist bias on the part of the pioneers in the field has been highlighted, among others, by cultural historians such as Carlo Ginzburg (1989) and Peter Burke (2017), as well as the aforementioned Ernst Gombrich (1999). Associated with the observation that there is no direct and obvious connection between context and artist/art, since the cultural collective of any era is very heterogeneous, other problems arise, such as: 1. The difficulty artists have in pictorially representing the complexity of reality; 2. The common practice of analysing a particular work of art disconnected from the artist’s serial production; 3. The attribution of a rationality denied by the author himself (consider, by way of example, an attempt to rationalize works by the Surrealists, who were fundamentally concerned with the representation of the unconscious); 4. The excessively Eurocentric bias of the iconographic-iconological method; 5. The problems in establishing the boundaries that separate iconographic analysis and iconological interpretation, referenced even in the bibliography of art historians who opt for one path or the other; and 6. A certain degree of imprecision in iconological considerations (Ginzburg 1989; Rampley 2001; Burke 2017).

    However, in the same vein as these authors who are critical of the more rigid method, the iconographic-iconological approach should not be discarded in order to understand the socio-political dynamics that surround us. As Rampley argues,

    [...] Panofsky’s method offered a crucial art historical insight, namely, recognition of the social mediation of pictorial meaning. Thus, the iconological interpretation attends to the presence of visual symbols and their conventionalized meanings, coupled with an examination of parallels in other cultural practices such as literature, philosophy, law and so forth. (Rampley 2001: 304)

    Therefore, in addition to the techniques and recommendations enshrined in the formal method, improvements must be made to make it operational and overcome the problems pointed out. Avoiding the mechanical association of author and work as mere results of an era, understanding the particularities of what was intended to be represented, outlining the artist’s personal-political choices and weighing up the wealth of specific circumstances are good recommendations for beginning the study of artistic expressions. In this case, the aim is to understand the meanings, the context (iconography) and to offer interpretations based on International Relations concepts (iconology) for the cartoons by the cartoonist Laerte published in the context of the speeches by the former Brazilian president at the opening of the annual meeting of the UN General Assembly in 2019, 2020 and 2021.

    For each component of the iconographic/iconological study, the procedural method unfolds in three phases. Firstly, we seek to understand the pictorial meanings of each of the cartoons - what the artist sought to convey on each occasion, a procedure here labelled as particular meanings. Next, we take a situational approach to each image, looking at the national context restricted to the period immediately prior to the production of the cartoons. This procedure adopts an intertextual perspective, in which the work is connected to contemporaneous publications from the media (Hansen 2015, 2016). We call this behaviour verification of the situational context. In short, understanding the specific meanings and the situational approach make up the iconographic procedure. Finally, concepts about foreign policy will be used to proceed with the iconological interpretation, in a complementary way, which will offer a connection between the artist’s art and intentions and the knowledge built from the field of International Relations. This dialogical and complementary movement, which begins with the convention of more immediate meanings - the work and the political context - and ends with the explanation of the Bolsonaro government’s foreign policy, aims to build a broad argument in which art can find explanations based on foreign policy and foreign policy can find expression in the artistic manifestation.

    Bolsonarist Discourses and BFP

    Bolsonaro’s speeches at the UNGA annual meetings from 2019 to 2021 mirrored his national statements, appealing to his government’s supporters (Lima and Albuquerque, 2019). To summarise, the speeches first criticised socialism and denounced the PT (Workers Party) governments’ foreign policy towards Cuba and Venezuela. They mentioned the government’s liberal economic bias, social advancements, intended free trade agreements, and infrastructure projects. In the three statements, the Amazon was prominently featured. Bolsonaro defended alleged protection efforts, contrasting with deforestation data from public institutions. He also presented a unique perspective on the objectives of indigenous peoples and the government’s protection policy for this group. Another highlight was the reiterated defence of the Christian faith, acknowledging religious support groups, particularly neo-Pentecostal evangelicals. Speeches also commonly expressed appreciation for the numerous military personnel in the government, mentioning Brazil’s participation in UN peacekeeping missions. In his 2020 and 2021 speeches, the president defended the government’s pandemic response, praised the alleged early treatment, and approved social aid programs. Finally, in the discourses, we found references to the priority external partnerships with right-wing conservative-led countries, particularly to ex-Republican President Donald Trump in 2019 and 2020 (no mention of Democrat Joe Biden in 2021); the intensified public security measures and repeated stances against corruption from previous administrations should be noted (Andrade 2020; Verdélio 2019, 2021).

    Despite the Brazilian tradition of opening the annual UNGA meeting with a speech conveying the defence of multilateralism, foreign policy supported by Brazil’s international position, and specific group agenda issues (Corrêa 2007: 16; Sardenberg 2013: 88-91), the analysed years’ statements contained evident untruths and targeted the presidential crowd (Marés, Moraes and Afonso 2019; Macário et al. 2020; Nomura et al. 2021). The break from the historical pattern of international action has serious consequences for Brazil’s global standing, which is limited in terms of conventional power resources. In this sense, the negotiating, conciliatory, and pacifist skills of foreign policy are its possible means of diplomatic articulation to establish itself as a significant global actor.

    To understand BFP, its organisation and meaning, it is crucial to first understand its analysis, classification, and historically constructed paradigms; next, the context of Bolsonarism’s rise and consolidation; then, the external insertion project based on a relatively new paradigm; and finally, the link of this political scheme to the analysed cartoons, arguing that they closely mirror the direction of Bolsonarist foreign policy.

    When assessing the General Objective (paradigm) of the BFP, it is evident that Bolsonaro has abandoned the development-focused guidelines, established since Getúlio Vargas, specifically, Americanism and Globalism. The first, a development orientation enabled by a strategic alliance with the major American power; the second, focused on autonomous development, steered by the formation of various partnerships, crucial for national interests. Although initially seeming to follow Ideological Americanism, with Brazil adopting a subordinate and supposedly identical ideological discourse to the United States, the path taken was different (Pinheiro 2000: 308-312; Cervo, 2008). The government demonstrated that it is guided by its reference, instead of previous paradigms.

    Foreign policy also has specific objectives. At this level, instrumental proposals require forms to achieve specific objectives. The specific objectives and execution methods of foreign policy reveal the intended internal and external sectors to be served or mobilised. Understanding the Bolsonarist BFP requires verifying these components.

    Bolsonarism, as a political organisation seeking power, stems from a crisis context marked by the end of the commodity-based growth cycle in 2012, social discontent during the ‘June Journeys’ of 2013, and institutional instability due to contested results of the 2014 elections and ensuing corruption scandals. This series of events led to the questioning of the ruling left and the rise of various conservative groups. Strategically leveraging dissatisfaction, Bolsonarist groups gradually became the main actors (Singer 2018; Lapper 2021).

    Regarding Brazil’s international role, initial government actions and speeches indicated a return to the Americanist foreign policy paradigm, confirmed by several measures showing subordination to the United States, thus characterising Ideological Americanism (Pinheiro 2000: 309; Lopes 2021: 232). During the coexistence of the Trump and Bolsonaro governments from 2019 to 2020, subordinate or ideological Americanism appeared to be the BFP’s keynote, subtly guiding other analyses (Vidigal 2019; Saraiva and Silva 2019; Gonçalves and Teixeira 2020). However, the true scope of Bolsonaro administration’s international operations was revealed with Trump’s defeat in the 2020 US presidential elections.

    During the 2020 American electoral period, the former Brazilian president and his children broke established protocols by openly expressing their preference for the re-election of the Republican candidate (BBC News Brasil 2020). Even after Democrat Joe Biden’s victory was declared and his presidential inauguration, the Bolsonaro family joined the far-right on social media, denouncing alleged American voting fraud—an unprecedented behaviour in the history of relations between the two countries.

    Other indicators confirm loyalty to the conservative ‘Trumpism’ agenda. In 2021, despite China being Brazil’s main trading partner, Bolsonaro directly attacked the Chinese by echoing the conspiracy theory that the COVID-19 virus was developed as a biological weapon in a lab, a claim previously spread by Trump (Jornal Nacional 2021). Regarding Amazon deforestation, faced with Biden’s threats of economic sanctions, he criticised the US for his ‘obsession’ with environmental protection. Addressing his supporters with words unthinkable in Brazilian diplomatic tradition and clearly misunderstanding the outcome of a potential confrontation, Bolsonaro stated, ‘When the saliva runs out, there has to be gunpowder’ (G1 2020). However, in a very pragmatic and measured manner, relations with the Biden administration were relaxed in mid-2022 due to the Brazilian elections (Bulla and Bronzatti 2022). The close alignment with Trump’s government and significant issues with Biden’s administration confirm that any form of Americanism does not explain the overall direction of Bolsonarist foreign policy.

    The text O Estilo Bolsonaro de Governar e a Política Externa (‘The Bolsonaro Style of Governing and Foreign Policy’) by Maria Regina Soares de Lima and Marianna Albuquerque (2019) provides a first attempt to understand the Bolsonaro BFP, introducing the term ‘strategy of chaos’. According to this concept, the government’s actions are guided solely by chaotic behaviour to maintain the internal public’s engagement with conservative agendas. In its external actions, the government prioritises non-traditional topics and adopts radical behaviours characteristic of its leadership.

    The chaos strategy disrupts historical patterns by turning external agendas into ‘reinforcement showcases’ for domestic sectarian mobilisation. This tactic conveys the leader’s stable behaviour and reinforces its normality through association with global institutions, thus legitimising it due to the importance of multilateral institutions. Such guidance, not exactly new, was inspired by Trump’s practices throughout his term.

    If the foreign policy of the Bolsonaro government is guided by a zeal to appeal to its domestic supporters, shaping its behaviour by discursive populism and byfake news, this is only part of the explanation. Furthermore, the chaotic option is more a method – or form – for organising Bolsonarist foreign policy to achieve its goals, rather than an explanation of its nature.

    In his work, A rebeldia tornou-se de direita? (‘Did rebellion become right-wing?’), Pablo Stefanoni describes the formation and character of the Alternative Right (or New Right), which unites governments and political groups worldwide. Although this unorganised and decentralised movement articulates through digital networks, it has some common agenda. The main one is the denunciation of left-wing globalism, transformed into specific adversaries for each nation. In the Brazilian context, anti-PTism (and its variations) appears to be the unifying factor. In parallel to combating a mythical unified global left, other factors include heightened nationalism, community-family moral conservatism, reliance on conspiracy theories, false or exaggerated information, and the millenarian promise of a better, redemptive future to embrace the ‘rebellion’ of system dissenters (Stefanoni 2022).

    In Bolsonaro’s UNGA speeches, the mentioned points, though varied in emphasis, are all present. Therefore, if the ruler’s message in international forums is primarily aimed at internal supporters, using a chaos strategy to unite diverse groups, it is plausible to consider an additional intent to signal identification and foster cooperation with social movements and governments of the Global Alternative Right. Guilherme Casarões explained the transnational aspect of Bolsonarist foreign policy, describing the ideological connections between internal extreme right groups and American political, religious, and liberal organisations. Brazilians adopted their flags and practices from these organisations. This author categorised the process as the ‘Americanization’ of Brazilian politics and stressed that Bolsonarism is a representative of transnational movement that is part of the extreme right-wing wave (Casarões 2022: 16).

    In summary, Bolsonarist foreign policy can be classified as a break from the paradigms or general objectives of its predecessors. Second, the chaotic strategy of its behaviour should not be confused with the nature of its international operations. Finally, and more assertively, Bolsonarist foreign policy aims at promoting a Global Alternative Right (or General Objective) and its key programs (or Specific Objectives) through actions and conduct (or Forms) that differ from previous Brazilian leadership.

    The overall goal was to position Brazil at the global epicentre of far-right governments. Specifically, this goal aimed at spreading its fundamental principles: opposition to communism, globalism, and leftism; chauvinist nationalism; religious, community, and family moral conservatism; neoliberal values such as entrepreneurship, individual freedom, and meritocracy; and the use of virtual media and social networks for communication, political articulation, and creation of its public sphere. In operational terms, it is organised into exaggerated demagogic speeches devoid of content, marked by informalism, and filled with wholly or partially false information. In parallel, these actions, driven by the pragmatic need for political survival, serve the interests groups that may not identify with Bolsonarist conservatism but see explicit gains from them (Moraes 2022).

    An iconographic and iconological analysis of Laerte’s cartoons on the UNGA speeches’ contexts from 2019 to 2021 represents essential aspects of the Form of the Bolsonarist foreign policy. Although they do not depict the paradigm’s constituent aspects or his foreign policy’s specific objectives, they symbolise the former president’s conduct at international and domestic fora. This is a crucial factor in retaining loyal conservative allies, international correspondents, and also unveils a pragmatic bias in his political action.

    Laerte’s cartoons and the representation of Bolsonarist BFP

    The emphasis on one aspect of populist rhetoric that is devoid of content, the dissemination of fake news, and the dubious agreements with traditional political sectors are themes depicted by Laerte in the selected cartoons, revealing the nature of Bolsonaro’s foreign policy. The works capture the president’s deviation from historical BFP behaviour patterns, by addressing topics appealing to his ideologically aligned audience in the main multilateral forum. This approach closely reflects the Bolsonaro government’s foreign policy, targeting its supporters and international ultra-conservative organisations and governments.

    The first editorial cartoon analysed was published in the Folha de S. Paulo on September 17, 2019, a week before Bolsonaro’s address at the opening of the General Debate of the 74th annual UNGA meeting. This event was particularly notable because it was the president’s first speech at the opening of the Assembly, a tradition Brazil has held since 1955 (Amorim 2007). The second cartoon was published on 22 September 2020, the same day as the Brazilian speech at the 75th UNGA General Debate. The latest one, from 2021, is dated 23 September, two days after the speech at the 76th UNGA. In 2022, Brazil also initiated debates, but owing to the imminent elections, Laerte did not publish a new cartoon. Instead, she reposted the 2019 design on her social networks. We emphasise that all indicated illustrations here hold prominent positions as FSP editorial drawings.

    Laerte’s cartoon in FSP on Bolsonaro’s 1st UNGA speech

    The cartoon refers to the ruler’s populist and discursive eschatology, contrasting with the formality of international politics and the ceremonial opening of the UNGA. Some considerations are important for iconographic analysis. The contrast between the space’s seriousness and formality and the utterance of a single word cocô (‘poop’ in Portuguese) is striking, underscoring its inappropriateness for the environment. Ceremonial gravity is represented by various elements, such as: 1. Light brown and grey shades are used to give the environment a serious character, as they refer to formal spaces, while vibrant colours are associated with festive moments and spaces; 2. The predominance of geometric shapes with straight lines indicates institutional formality; 3. The auditorium’s large size and monochromatic colour. By emphasising the landscape over people or actions, it underscores the space and its symbols, not individual leading role, and accentuates the word pronounced by the Brazilian representative; 4. The use of indistinct human figures as a technique to collectivise space and provide autonomy against the political dominance of specific characters, except for the speaker; 5. The Onusian emblem, referring to an icon somewhat known to the general public.; 6. Clothing indications suggest an official environment. (Tresidder 1985; Joly 1996;Arnheim 2005; Penn 2008); 7. The disclosure of a single word, presented as the possible summary of an expectedly complex speech, considering the complexity of global political challenges.

    Regarding this last point, the word ‘poop’ presents elements of contrast and reinforcement. Written this way, followed by a period, it suggested that the former president’s entire statement was encapsulated in the meaning of the word. This action, performed by a state representative, seems symbolically inappropriate and incongruous with the expected behaviour of public figures in international institutional environments. Considering the speeches’ properties, particularly their fallacious nature and ideological orientation for specific internal and external audiences, the contradiction between the speech content and audience space in the cartoon suggests the cartoonist’s intent to create an exaggerated representation of the ridiculous based on a consistent history.

    In the iconographic analysis of this image, we also aim at understanding its situational context. Previous episodes before the cartoon’s publication provide context and explain the artistic reference to governmental eschatology. According to a journalistic survey, Bolsonaro mentioned the term ‘poop’ in five public statements in August 2019 (Poder360 2019). The circumstances and meanings referencing the term varied, but the word was always present. The cartoonist constructed the cartoon based on the repeated mentions. Without delving into the specifics of other fields regarding the psychological need to assert dominance and build security linked to the abundance of faecal terms used in discourse, it can be argued that the president’s coproloquy 1 is an implicit political tactic, tied to a facet of populist rhetoric he wished to align with.

    In the iconological interpretation, the 2019 cartoon represents discursive populism used to bring the leader closer to ordinary people (Moraes 2022). By condensing the UN statement into a single grotesque word, Laerte demonstrated the chaos strategy referenced by Lima and Albuquerque (2019), which lacks content and is merely a performance for domestic and international audiences. Thus, the sole word ‘poop’ in the illustration simultaneously shows an intentional lack of meaning to engage a diverse group of supporters and implies the aim of linking the former president to the average person.

    In a chapter focused on populist leaders, Professors Cas Mudde and Cristóbal Rovira Kaltwasser (2017) reveal a characteristic of these leaders: they ‘(...) use simple and even vulgar language, known as Stammtisch speech (bar table talk). They (...) connect with the ‘common man’ through sexist stereotypes and coarse language’ (Mudde and Kaltwasser 2017: 64-66, translated by author).

    Although framing Bolsonaro and his government within the concept of classic populism (Ianni 1991; Weffort 2003) is controversial, the political figure does embody some of its elements. The frequent insults towards journalists and critics, bad jokes, and inappropriate terms for the position demonstrate this tendency. Strictly speaking, the frequent use of abject and rude language to project oneself as a charismatic ‘man of the people’ and an ‘anti-elite’ politician can backfire. Its repetition may convey an intention to hide cognitive limitations and administrative incompetence, especially in an economic situation where the people, with whom the leadership aims to identify, perceive no social advances.

    Apart from the abovementioned factors, comparing this rhetoric element with other components of contemporary populism concept is essential. For Ernesto Laclau, populist discourse is legitimised through divisions: a socially constructed populace versus an opposing elite or Other. In these cases, the discourse should appear to represent social groups with similar demands, through the establishment of a governing method and a program perceived as comprehensive — even if it is actually partial. In parallel to the totalising proposal, the denunciation against elites or outsider groups—opponents of the people—fosters the formation of a hegemonic populist government (Laclau 2013: 122-135).

    In addition to the vulgar, violent, and simplistic aspects of Bolsonaro’s speech, the other issues he raised during his term, such as those described here as specific objectives, did not have this all-encompassing nature. Uncritical militaristic nationalism, moral conservatism, neo-Pentecostal Christian radicalism, conspiracy theories and fake news, individual entrepreneurship valorisation, and attacks on traditional communication channels did not result in Laclau’s advocated all-encompassing instrument. Instead, they created various polarisations, often within grassroots groups, or caused dissatisfaction among sectors benefiting from government policies. Therefore, the ‘bar talk’, encapsulated by the word poop in Laerte’s cartoon, lost its fundamental foundation and became merely a tool to mobilise domestic and foreign groups already aligned with Bolsonarism.

    The 2019 cartoon symbolises the given situational context of presidential coproloquy in public statements and epitomises his numerous statements marked by stupidity. The failure to build a hegemonic consensus among the majority was offset by positioning the government and the Bolsonarist movement at the centre of the Global Alternative Right, thereby exciting the former president’s supporters.

    Regarding the 2020 cartoon and its particular meanings, the opposition between the government’s environmental care claims and the fire spreading in the Pantanal and Amazon at the time is evident. This first conclusion is unequivocal in much of the image, which also displays a UN emblem on the right, with smoke emanating from the Brazilian section. This second image complements the explanation of the contradiction between discourse and reality, highlighting the internationally recognised environmental destruction in Brazil at that time. The montage featuring the smoky onusian symbol on the Brazilian side, alongside a screen displaying the presidential speech, exemplifies the contradiction and general awareness of the environmental problem.

    It is still possible to infer the collective convention’s mobilisation on the ‘smoke screen’ idea guiding Bolsonaro government’s actions. The dark tones of the illustration convey a serious and sad situation. Additionally, another possible interpretation of the character’s cough could be seen in his response to the COVID-19 pandemic affecting Brazil and the world. This last association links the analysis of the image’s particular meanings with the study of the situational context.

    Before his UN speech, Bolsonaro criticised health measures, advocated for an ineffective ‘early treatment’, discouraged COVID-19 vaccination when it was still in development, and encouraged crowds at events and during his international travels (Andrade 2020; Verdélio 2020; Sandes 2020). In this case, the artist may have depicted the head of government coughing to represent not only his infection but also his role in spreading the virus, considering the internal gatherings and international trips he undertook.

    Regardless, the cartoon’s main contradiction was significant as Brazil was experiencing one of the largest fires in its history. In addition to the rising trend of Amazonian deforestation since 2015, with a drop in 2017 (INPE 2021), the Pantanal lost much of its native area and fauna due to fires in August and September 2020 (Jucá and Bronze 2021). In his UNGA speech, Bolsonaro argued that the Amazon, being a humid forest, did not allow fire spread, attributing the fires to ‘caboclos and Indigenous people’. He also blamed the temperature for the large Pantanal fires and claimed that Venezuela had caused severe damage to Brazil by spilling oil (Bolsonaro, cited in Barretto Jr. 2022: 174).

    The allegations mirror the cartoon and symbolise the government’s modus operandi. In fact, rainforests rarely catch fire naturally, but fires are commonly used to clear deforested areas. In this process, the felled and dried native forest is burned to make way for agriculture. The majority of the Pantanal fires in August and September 2020 were caused by human action (Ministério Público do Mato Grosso do Sul 2021). Another point is Venezuela’s connection and accountability, renewing attacks on the ‘left’-governed country. At that time, diplomatic relations with Venezuela were nearly severed after the Bolsonaro government ordered the closure of diplomatic posts and removal of their employees from the country in March.

    If the 2019 cartoon reflects the rhetorical-populist aspect of Bolsonaro’s government’s foreign policy aimed at domestic supporters and the Global Alternative Right, the following year’s illustration exposes another significant aspect of its political practice: the spread of fake news. This is an ‘umbrella’ term for diverse information processing practices used by the extreme right. Thus, rumours, misinformation, simple lies as shown in the cartoon, conspiracy theories (narratives exposing secret government or corporate plans against ordinary citizens), and propaganda are generally tools to ‘create confusion, mobilise an audience, or defame opponents’ (Butter and Knight 2020: 2). Nonetheless, it is important to note the obvious: the dissemination of false information is not a new practice. What sets the current wave apart is its increased intensity and the extensive use of virtual media, leading to another aspect—the decentralisation of emission sources.

    Thus, fake news, broadly understood here, is a common tool of the alternative right (Avramov, Gatov and Yablokov 2020: 514). Apart from promoting confusion, mobilising supporters, and detracting opponents, they aim at establishing an alternative, legitimate epistemic authority for both national and international public spheres. Collectively, they forge a lasting understanding of reality. It is not just about responding to scientific knowledge, but also about creating new narratives to underpin political organisations and governments in proposing public policies. This movement already influences audiovisual productions, music, books, podcasts, and other channels, reproducing conservative ideas to create a new perception of reality through redundancy.

    The proliferation of fake news, through constant repetition, has become the most effective tool for the Bolsonaro government’s foreign policy to maintain domestic loyalty by creating a unique interpretation of reality. Simultaneously, the use of the common resource of the alternative right’s transnational networks reinforced the transnational movement, positioning the Brazilian government and its affiliated organisations as a global conservatism pole. The cartoon captures this aspect of the Bolsonarist presidency by depicting the lies spread in international forums.

    In the third cartoon, from 2021, published after Bolsonaro’s speech at the UN, the first particular meaning refers to the figure of former president Michel Temer. A trait of the politician is his hand gestures during public speeches, typically delivered in a slow, monotone, professorial tone. Then, the goal was to establish a hierarchy between the two politicians, with Temer being far more versed in the jargon and practices behind the scenes of power.

    A second factor is Bolsonaro’s eyes which, unlike Temer’s didactic serenity, suggest anger and impulsiveness, abundantly demonstrated in his interactions with journalists and critics (Correio Brasiliense 2021).

    A third element is the emphasis on the pen the former president uses to write the note. Apparently, it seems to be the popular ‘Bic’ brand ballpoint pen, often referred to by the former captain as a symbol of his power: ‘Bolsonaro warned, “The Bic pen is mine”‘, regarding the decision to buy the Chinese vaccine Coronavac, produced by Sinovac Biotech (Nolasco 2020). In the image’s context, using the notable pen to draft a plea for absolution from verbal excesses underscores the inverse power usually signified by owning such a pen. The effect intensifies when the characters’ identities are established. Temer, a Brazilian Democratic Movement Party (PMDB) politician and unequivocal member of the centre-right power bloc called Centrão, guides the current ruler who uses power to align with the traditional political group and rectify previous exaggerations. For a president who aimed at disrupting conventional Brazilian politics, his decision-making power’s submission to the Centrão shows a contrary path.

    The colours of the suits are finally connected to the aspect of alliance convergence. There is a match in clothing. One character’s tie colour matches the other’s shirt, suggesting their complementarity. The cartoonist presumably aimed at highlighting the closeness between the two politicians, despite the ex-soldier’s numerous claims of being an outsider in Brasília and his government’s termination of the ‘Give and Take’ practices with Congress.

    About the situational context, the cartoon refers to an event from early September 2021. Owing to Bolsonaro’s constant attacks on the Supreme Federal Court (STF), particularly focusing on minister Alexandre de Moraes, Michel Temer was invited by the former president’s allies to defuse the situation. Political instability affected the financial market, and impeachment threats from Congress required the intervention of the PMDB politician to mitigate escalating tensions between powers. The meeting resulted in a note justifying the attacks as the ‘heat of the moment’, a term used in the cartoon, and re-establishing a fragile normality with the STF. Such a ‘note’ would be a key moment in the transition from rhetoric to action in the Bolsonaro government. If at the start of the term, the emphasis was on an autonomous stance towards traditional politicians, by mid-2021, the Bolsonaro administration had definitively forsaken its supposed political neutrality, declaring its active alignment with the so-called Centrão. This movement shows the tendency to use pragmatism in domestic and foreign policy to stay in power. Temer’s mediation, a traditional Brazilian political figure, symbolised the new phase. Laerte noticed this connection when depicting the presidential apologies due to the ‘inaccuracies’ in the UNGA speech.

    According to an iconological interpretation, pragmatism in foreign policy, contrary to what the populist discourse and previously mentioned fake news would suggest, can be demonstrated in three key instances of the Bolsonaro government: the relationship with China, promises made to Benjamin Netanyahu’s government in Israel, and Brazil’s withdrawal from the Paris Agreement at the multilateral level. With respect to these agenda, government action was confined to conservative discourse, influenced by the country’s tradition of external action and fear of reprisals.

    During his administration, Bolsonaro criticised China for several reasons: its communist governance, its perceived status as an enemy of the free West by former President Donald Trump, and being the origin of COVID-19. However, Rodrigo F. de Moraes verified that the majority of the former president’s online statements were moderate or positive towards the Chinese government. Mostly, criticism of China on Bolsonarist social media profiles came from presidential advisors and Brazilian far-right leaders. The reason for Bolsonaro’s restrained online criticism of the Asian country, despite his usual verbosity on social media, is due to governmental pragmatism and caution to avoid provoking retaliation from Brazil’s largest trade partner and major investor. Furthermore, agribusiness, a fundamental sector of Bolsonarism, exports a significant portion to China, reinforcing the caution of the head of government (Moraes 2022: 14-15).

    During the period Bolsonaro and Netanyahu were in power, cooperation between Brazil and Israel maintained historical standards, with some audacious promises unfulfilled by the Brazilian government. At the start of his term, the former president pledged to move the Brazilian embassy in Israel to Jerusalem, indicating recognition of Israeli sovereignty over the city sacred to Judaism, Islam, and Christianity. He declared, even before his presidency, his intention to close the Palestinian Authority embassy in Brasília, arguing it was not an autonomous country, and insinuating Palestinians were terrorists (Travassos 2021: 175; Casarões and Feldberg 2021: 80). Such measures aimed at pleasing the Trump government, enhance the partnership with Netanyahu’s conservative government, and serve internal pro-government sectors such as neo-Pentecostal churches and Jewish organisations critical of the PT administration. Despite its intentions, the Bolsonaro government neither relocated the Brazilian embassy from Tel Aviv nor closed the Palestinian representation in Brazil (Casarões and Feldberg 2021). The most daring achievement was merely opening a commercial office in Jerusalem (Travassos 2021; Moraes, 2022).

    If the affection for Trump, the Israeli government, the Brazilian Jewish community, and the neo-Pentecostal evangelical base led to more decisive stances from Bolsonaro, other pressures acted contrarily. By intensifying pro-Israel conservative government measures, the government encountered significant resistance from the Brazilian agro-export sector, which feared retaliation from Muslim Arab buyer countries. Following this path also damaged Brazil’s image with strategic partners, who were concerned about its assertive actions in a sensitive area of international geopolitics. Finally, following the end of Trump’s term, accusations of corruption, and Netanyahu’s electoral defeat, the balance tipped towards the pragmatism of the BFP, which restrained its changes.

    Finally, in December 2018, before assuming presidency, Bolsonaro threatened to withdraw Brazil from the Paris Agreement on Climate Change. In the same context, the country withdrew its candidacy to host the 25th United Nations Climate Convention Conference (COP 25), scheduled for 2019. In addition to emulating the United States, the measure aimed at assisting the more backward agribusiness sectors, which viewed intensive agricultural practices as the quickest and most affordable way to increase production. However, the Brazilian agricultural sector extends beyond these groups. Farmers committed to sustainability and adhering to environmental protection rules for export opposed the measure. The ruralist group, therefore, failed to reach consensus on supporting the government’s promise. This barrier was further compounded by international pressures for Amazon preservation, threats of regional internationalisation, and the potential suspension of foreign financial aid if Brazil abandoned practices tied to the Paris Agreement. In the end, the government remained a signatory to the environmental pact, but this did not prevent forest deforestation and the dismantling of environmental inspection institutions. Climate foreign policy pragmatism was accompanied by internal permissiveness.

    If pragmatism is evident in some of Bolsonaro’s foreign policy agenda, we should not exaggerate this behaviour. The Brazilian conservative administration implemented measures aligned with the international far-right movement, such as: joining the ‘International Anti-Abortion Alliance’, a multilateral forum of conservative governments guided by moral family and customs principles; weakening regional integration and withdrawing Brazil from key initiatives such as the Union of South American Nations (UNASUR) and the Community of Latin American and Caribbean States (CELAC), seen as ‘Bolivarianist’ tools of the vague concept of ‘globalism’ threatening national sovereignty; and withdrawing from the Global Compact for Safe, Orderly and Regular Migration, signalling alignment with Donald Trump’s administration, which had done the same. In addition to these acts, the former president’s radical stance and his advisors’ insults to leaders and criticism of foreign political and electoral processes greatly damaged Brazil’s image abroad. Pragmatism, therefore, proved to be a frequently chosen alternative in cases where the consequences threatened the continuity of the government’s foundations.

    Former president Bolsonaro and his closest advisors were not newcomers to the world of politics. Thus, in adverse or complex circumstances, he adopted pragmatism to remain in power. This trait of his behaviour was ironically depicted in the cartoon, contradicting the ex-soldier’s claims of opposing traditional political group agreements and diverging from his promises of a foreign policy aligned with the global alternative right movement.

    Conclusions

    In this study, we investigated the nature of Jair Bolsonaro’s government’s BFP through the political satire of cartoonist Laerte Coutinho. Using the iconographic-iconological method, we aimed to understand the meanings and situational context (iconography) and provide interpretations based on International Relations concepts (iconology) for the cartoons published by the cartoonist during the former Brazilian president’s speeches at the UNGA annual meetings in 2019, 2020 and 2021.

    In the first cartoon, Laerte uses the word ‘poop’ to summarise a speech, exposing populism and the recurrence of discursive eschatology. This contrasts with the speaker’s role as head of state in a formal, ceremonial moment. The iconographic analysis revealed elements in the cartoon emphasising institutional formality versus speech inadequacy, and the appropriateness of the eschatological synthesis in the situational context of previous presidential demonstrations. The iconological analysis revealed how the cartoon captures the Bolsonarist foreign policy’s ‘chaos strategy’, which is highly theatrical but lacks content.

    In the second charge, from 2020, smoke emerges from the UN emblem at Brazil’s location on the map, while Bolsonaro, also shrouded in smoke, asserts his government’s commitment to the Amazon. Laerte elucidates the dissonance between notorious facts and presidential discursive strategies. The iconographic analysis reveals elements that highlight the seriousness of the situation, and the stark disconnect between Bolsonaro’s rhetoric and the ongoing intense destruction of the Amazon and Pantanal. Additionally, they might also indirectly reference other forms of presidential denialism, such as the promotion of early treatment for COVID-19. The iconological analysis again reveals that the work focuses more on form than the objectives of the BFP. There is no clear reference to the content of BFP under Bolsonaro in the smoke represented in the image; instead, there is an emphasis on spreading false information, a tactic aimed at a loyal audience. The iconological analysis helps identify the artist’s sensitivity towards the president’s repeated denialism, using various platforms to keep a group engaged in an alternative reality. The use of Brazil’s international platforms to spread lies ends up serving to establish and strengthen the New Right’s transnational networks and provides opportunities for Bolsonaro to position himself as this group’s leader.

    The last cartoon, where a resigned yet reluctant Bolsonaro mimics Michel Temer’s sophisticated political speech, unveils the circumstantial alliances of political groups that, despite not being confused, see themselves as timely allies. The iconographic analysis reveals how these political characters practically converge and also highlights the personality traits and resourcefulness of each character. Although they are known to be distinct, they are instrumentally mobilised to facilitate the maintenance of a certain power project. In iconological terms, the cartoon captures a pragmatic trait of Bolsonaro’s political practice—one that makes him conform to behaviour more acceptable to the expected liturgy of his position to maintain power—a true pragmatism in potentially threatening situations, also evident in other situations. Although pragmatism is not a major consideration for Bolsonaro as a politician or his government’s foreign policy, the situations discussed reveal traces of political pragmatism that emerge under sufficiently adverse circumstances threatening the maintenance of his power.

    The three Laerte cartoons published in the context of Brazilian UNGA speeches between 2019 and 2021, analysed using iconographic and iconological methods, represent essential aspects of the modus operandi (Form) of Bolsonarist foreign policy. Finally, the analyses confirm the potential of artistic languages, particularly cartoons, as sources for study, dissemination, and assistance in teaching and learning processes related to Political Sciences and specifically International Relations.
