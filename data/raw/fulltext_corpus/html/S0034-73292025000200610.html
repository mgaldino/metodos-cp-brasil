Challenging Dollar Dominance? How Brazil Responds to RMB Internationalization

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This article explores the role of China’s currency, the renminbi (RMB), in Brazil, analyzing how the country positions itself in the shift toward alternative currencies to the U.S. dollar amidst changing global monetary power. It addresses three key questions: How widely is the RMB used in Brazil’s economy? What institutional measures support RMB adoption in Brazil? And what are the implications of this engagement for Brazil’s role in the international monetary and financial system? Findings indicate a modest but growing RMB integration in Brazil. Rather than embarking on a de-dollarization process, Brazil appears to be employing an incremental approach – one that leverages selective institutional measures to diversify its financial risks while preserving its strategic ties with the dominant global currency.

    Keywords
    De-dollarization; RMB Internationalization; Brazil-China Relations; International Monetary and Financial System; Economic Sovereignty; Developing and Emerging Economies

    Introduction

    The fallout from the 2022 Russia-Ukraine conflict has further intensified debates on de-dollarization, as Moscow’s exclusion from dollar reserves and the international payments system underscored, once again, the vulnerabilities of a dollar-centric international monetary and financial system (IMFS) (Eichengreen 2024). These events reignited discussions on alternatives to dollar dominance, which have been going on at least since the 2007-2009 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) (Torres Filho, 2019; Cohen 2019; Norrlof et al. 2020). In response to the dollar’s weaponization, nations worldwide have accelerated efforts to adopt alternative currencies in international trade, strengthen or establish independent payment systems, and even explore the creation of a new BRICS reference currency (Batista Júnior 2024). Against this backdrop, China’s ambitions to internationalize the RMB find fertile ground in the growing push, particularly by developing and emerging economies (DEEs)1, to reduce dollar dependence – while the United States seeks to preserve its currency’s centrality.

    These developments signal a current tension, and potential shift, in the IMFS, placing DEEs at the center of the escalating monetary rivalry between the United States and China. DEEs face structural constraints within the IMFS, which have been exacerbated by financial globalization (Tavares and Fiori 1997; Paula et al. 2017; Kaltenbrunner and Painceira 2018; Bonizzi et al. 2020; Carneiro and De Conti 2022; Alami et al. 2023; Jibril 2025). As Ocampo (2018) notes, their limited ability to generate international liquidity increases their reliance on foreign exchange reserves, reflecting heightened vulnerability to boom–bust financial cycles and balance of payments crises. Consequently, DEEs remain dependent on a flawed global financial safety net, lack a proper international debt resolution mechanism, besides being underrepresented in traditional global economic governance institutions – particularly in the International Monetary Fund (IMF) (Hooijmaaijers 2021). As a result, these countries face significant economic and political challenges within a hierarchical and asymmetrical IMFS, where their policy autonomy is constrained by the need to navigate spillover effects from core economies, most notably the United States (Paula et al. 2017). In this context, reducing dependence on the dollar would serve to mitigate these external vulnerabilities. At the same time, currency choice has increasingly taken on a geopolitical dimension, reflecting broader strategic alignments and power dynamics in the global economy.

    While recognizing that the success of the RMB’s internationalization depends on China’s domestic policies and structural constraints, this article emphasizes the willingness of foreign governments to integrate the Chinese currency into their domestic financial systems (Chey 2019, 2022; Chey and Hsu 2020; Pacheco Pardo 2018). In this sense, while China’s rise as a global economic power and its ambitions to promote its currency have catalyzed a rich body of literature on RMB’s international usage (Chin and Helleiner 2008; Chey 2013; Eichengreen 2015; McNally 2015) and its trade and investment influence in DEEs (Alden and Mendez 2023; Jenkins 2019; Menezes and Bragatti 2020; Xing and Vadell 2024), there remains a quite unexplored territory for analyzing how individual countries respond to the RMB’s ascent. This invites an investigation into how large DEEs, like Brazil, will adapt to and navigate a potential reconfiguration of the IMFS.

    Building on this empirical gap, this study explores the role of the RMB in Brazil, analyzing how the country positions itself in the shift toward alternative currencies to the dollar amidst changing global monetary power. It addresses three key questions: How widely is the RMB used in Brazil’s economy? What institutional measures support RMB adoption in Brazil? And what are the implications of this engagement for Brazil’s role in the IMFS?

    Our research design integrates qualitative and quantitative components in order to address the proposed questions. The qualitative dimension consists of constructing a chronology of key events (Armijo et al. 2025) from a Brazilian perspective, with particular emphasis on the policies and initiatives undertaken to reduce Brazil’s dependence on the U.S. dollar and to promote the incorporation of the RMB into the Brazilian economy. Special attention is devoted to Brazil’s alignment with BRICS initiatives aimed at reforming international monetary and payments arrangements to lessen reliance on the dollar. This stage involves a systematic examination of official developments – such as government agreements, policy measures, and diplomatic engagements – intended to advance RMB use in Brazil.

    The quantitative dimension draws on Liu and Papa’s (2022) framework for assessing de-dollarization in BRICS economies, adapted here to the Brazilian case and focused on the real economy.2 It employs descriptive statistics to examine three interrelated indicators: (i) the composition of international reserves as an expression of diversification away from the dollar; (ii) the currency predominance in trade invoicing as a measure of RMB incorporation into Brazil’s external transactions; and (iii) the evolution of payment infrastructures, particularly the adoption of alternative payment infrastructures to those traditionally dominated by the United States and other Western powers. Through this combined approach, the study seeks to capture both the institutional and the empirical dimensions of Brazil’s gradual shift in its external monetary practices.

    The findings of this research suggest that while Brazil is not actively championing the RMB as a global currency, its actions – such as including RMB into its foreign reserves and creating the institutional framework for invoicing and settling trade in RMB – demonstrate a pragmatic adaptation to shifting global dynamics. From this perspective, Brazil’s measures are better understood as incremental diversification rather than a deliberate de-dollarization strategy. The growing presence of the RMB in Brazil–China relations reflect an adjustment to China’s expanding influence, one that seeks to enhance economic cooperation without undermining Brazil’s autonomy or its ties with the United States. In this context, Brazil’s approach to the RMB embodies a delicate balancing act amid the China–U.S. rivalry3. Rather than signaling a decisive shift away from the dollar or the broader U.S.-led IMFS, these measures represent a strategic effort to facilitate trade with China while mitigating the risks of overreliance on the dollar. However, this positioning lacks a clear or cohesive long-term strategy, raising questions about Brazil’s ability to navigate its economic engagements with both major powers effectively.4

    This article is organized in six sections. Following this introduction, the next two sections present the theoretical framework to explain Chinese ambitions and limitations to internationalize its currency. The second section presents the currency internationalization literature, highlighting the role played by institutional and infrastructural processes in building the international appeal of a currency. The third section reviews the literature on the underlying power dynamics within the IMFS, where economic and political capabilities do not automatically translate into monetary power. The fourth section gives the historical background of Brazil and China shared desire to reduce their dependence on the dollar and China’s policies to internationalize the RMB. The fifth section evaluates Brazilian policies to reduce dollar dependency, and to what extent these measures have translated into an increased use of the RMB. The sixth section concludes the article.

    Reassessing currency internationalization dynamics

    In the absence of a global state with overarching monetary and financial authority, the traditional state monopoly over currency gives way to a competitive international arena where domestic currencies compete for influence (Cohen 1998; 2015). As a result, the IMFS is constituted by currencies that increasingly transcend their issuing state’s borders, establishing a hierarchy that reflects both their empirical performance as medium of exchange, units of account, and stores of value, and the institutional and political forces that underpin their global circulation. International monetary and financial relations thus lack a formal coercive authority, leaving currencies to engage in a hierarchical and asymmetrical dispute for influence, as “currency usage is by no means confined to the territorial limits of a state” (Cohen 1998, p. 1). Consequently, states become exposed to the process of currency internationalization – that is, when a currency circulates beyond its issuing state’s borders, it effectively invades another state’s monetary domain. To that extent, currency internationalization can be examined from a metric-based approach, quantifying a currency’s international use by assessing its performance across the three money functions, in the private and public spheres, as illustrated in Table 1.

    The determinants of a currency’s internationalization process remain a topic of debate (Helleiner 2008; Helleiner and Kirshner 2009). Academic literature points out that a currency needs to fulfil certain economic and political qualifications to be internationally competitive, qualifications which usually are met only by the most powerful states (see, for example, Cohen 2015, p. 10–3, Eichengreen et al. 2017, especially chapters 10–12). Economically, issuers of globally competitive currencies usually have a history of macroeconomic stability, deep and liquid financial markets, and significant trade and financial linkages, which create network externalities that enhance the currency’s global acceptance. Politically, strong creditor protections, including property rights and legal stability, are crucial (Walter 2006, p. 57), though democratic governance is not necessarily decisive (Eichengreen et al. 2017, p. 190–2). Additionally, the ability to project power externally – often tied to military strength – can reinforce a currency’s status. While economic and political pre-requisites are in all probability correct to explain in part the international appeal of domestic currencies, states actively shape and promote the international use of their currency, suggesting that strategic policy choices also influence a currency global standing (Strange 1971; Tavares 1985; Kirshner 1995; Tavares and Fiori 1997).

    Recent literature on currency internationalization has emphasized the importance of understanding how currency expansion relies on building strategic networks that support its use. McNally and Gruin (2017) argue that RMB internationalization is not just a quantitative phenomenon but a qualitative transformation of the IMFS, underscoring the need for analyses that place emphasis on the construction and evolution of institutional networks. Green and Gruin (2021) resort to the concept of “transnationalization” to examine how the RMB expands globally, focusing on the spatial distribution of financial institutional infrastructure that underpins its international reach. Ponsot and Berthou (2025) examine China’s strategic use of qualitative instruments to establish a global RMB network, analyzing its approach through the lenses of confidence, convenience, and control (“three C” framework).

    In addition, there is a growing argument that the limited internationalization of currencies like the RMB – which contrast with the significant economic weight of its issuer – signals the need to rethink how credit-debt relationships shape the process. As Lee and Lim (2024) highlight, understanding these relationships is crucial for grasping the dynamic nature of currency internationalization. This perspective draws from the credit theory of money, rooted in original institutional economics, which frames money primarily as a unit of account and a representation of debt, rather than just a medium of exchange. Building on foundational works by Goodhart (1998) and Rochon and Vernengo (2003), proponents of this view argue that the three classic functions of money are mutually reinforcing through institutional arrangements (see Berthonnet and Bracarense [2022], for an overview). For example, both foreign and domestic actors are actively engaged in the external process of RMB internationalization, demonstrated by the many offshore RMB centers and diverse currency swaps that China has established worldwide. This highlights the significant role of foreign actors, as foreign governments can incentivize their domestic private sectors to use the RMB for various business purposes, aligning with China’s policies and further enhancing the currency’s global stature (Lee and Lim, 2024).

    Together, these approaches suggest that understanding the process of currency internationalization goes beyond tracking economic indicators or transaction volumes. It demands attention to the institutional and infrastructural processes that create and sustain global monetary networks. In sum, the international expansion of a currency depends not only on the economic and political strength of its issuer but also on its ability to build and sustain strategic networks that enhance cross-border usability and trust. In this sense, the internationalization of the RMB cannot be fully understood without considering the institutional and network-building efforts that shape how third countries perceive and integrate these currencies into their domestic economic systems. This emphasis on the institutional and infrastructural foundations of currency internationalization connects to a broader discussion of how monetary power is exercised within the asymmetric structure of the IMFS, where the dollar’s dominance exemplifies the political and economic advantages conferred to the issuer of the leading currency, as will be discussed in the next section.

    Power dynamics within the IMFS

    The international use of a currency is never neutral, as it provides economic and political advantages to issuing states (Tavares 1985; Tavares and Fiori 1997; Helleiner and Kirshner 2009; Eichengreen 2011; 2024; Norrlof 2014). The international status of the dollar grants the United States an “exorbitant privilege,” a term coined by the French Minister of Finance Valéry Giscard d’Estaing in the 1960s. The privilege stems from the fact that, as the issuer of the world’s “top currency” (Cohen 2015), the United States does not face the same external adjustment pressures as other nations since its currency is always in high demand (Paula et al. 2017). As such, the country can finance its external deficits without facing the same adjustment pressures that confront other nations. This dominant position allows the U.S. to sustain persistent balance-of-payments deficits, lower borrowing costs, expand domestic financial market liquidity, and strengthen its capacity to exert global economic influence (Carneiro and De Conti 2022). For Cohen (2015), monetary power arises precisely from this interconnectedness – via balance-of-payments linkages and the existence of a monetary Pyramid. Its ultimate foundation lies in “a capacity to avoid the costs of payments adjustment – to maintain the state’s policy space, as free as possible from foreign constraint” (Cohen 2015, p. 48).

    Other scholars broaden the definition of currency/monetary power. Susan Strange (1971) conceives of currency power as a tool of economic diplomacy: states with strong currencies can use their monetary status to forge alliances, impose sanctions, and shape the global financial architecture. The internationalization of a currency grants a significant strategic advantage by enabling a state to influence the behavior of others – an advantage underpinned by worldwide confidence in the currency and by the issuer’s ability to sustain economic and political stability. Kirshner (1995, p. 8) identifies three main channels through which international monetary power is exercised: currency manipulation; the fostering and exploitation of monetary dependence; and the use of systemic disruption. The “weaponization” of the dollar encapsulates all three. By controlling access to the dollar and associated payment networks, the U.S. can coerce targeted states. Through widespread monetary dependence, it entrenches a sphere of influence that amplifies its leverage. And by disrupting systemic arrangements such as SWIFT, it can shape or destabilize entire subsystems of the international monetary order.

    The concentration of these benefits derived from the dollar’s status to the United States has been a growing source of geopolitical tensions. Particularly since the GFC, there has been a growing call for reform in the IMFS to better reflect the economic weight of DEEs, which account for nearly 60% of global GDP, measured by purchasing power parity (PPP), according to IMF data (2025, p. 107). DEEs are often left no choice but to accumulate dollar reserves as a safeguard against the volatility of the IMFS (De Paula, et al. 2017). This dependency restricts their ability to maneuver, leaving them with limited “policy space” – a term Gallagher (2015, p. 6-7) defines as the capacity for sovereign states to implement and coordinate their preferred policy measures.

    This resentment has reached new levels with the increasing weaponization of the dollar to achieve geopolitical goals. The United States has leveraged its currency power through financial sanctions that effectively cut off targeted entities from the dollar-based global financial network, isolating them economically (Torres Filho 2019; McDowell 2023). Over the years, the U.S. has increasingly weaponized these economic interdependencies, leveraging its control over critical “chokepoints” within the global financial infrastructure (Farrell and Newman 2019; Petry 2024). Recent examples include sanctions that disconnected Iran from the SWIFT banking system and, most notably, those imposed on Russia following its actions in Ukraine (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication 2022).

    The combination of (i) a longstanding dissatisfaction with the exorbitant privilege enjoyed by the United States; (ii) the growing asymmetry between the economic weight of DEEs and their limited policy space; and (iii) the weaponization of the dollar as a geopolitical tool has intensified global de-dollarization efforts, particularly among DEEs. However, this push for de-dollarization unfolds within the broader context of geopolitical rivalry between the United States and China, as China increasingly seeks to expand the international use of its currency.5 Moreover, among DEEs6, China is arguably the only issuer with the potential to offer a viable alternative to the dollar. In this sense, de-dollarization initiatives can be seen as an expression of the contemporary power struggle between “great powers” (Norloff 2023; Eichengreen 2024; Petry and Nölke 2024).

    In this context, the choice of international currencies is far from a purely technical matter and has increasingly taken on geopolitical dimensions (Cohen 2019). To DEEs like Brazil, the challenge lies in balancing economic objectives – particularly those related to financial stability and external vulnerability, which tend to be exacerbated by an overreliance on the dollar – with political and geopolitical considerations. In principle, Brazil can leverage its currency choices to support political objectives without necessarily committing to automatic alignments. The following section revisits the context of the initial debates on DEEs’ de-dollarization efforts, emphasizing the roles of China and Brazil, to then analyze how Brazil has responded to China’s RMB internationalization policies.

    Challenging asymmetries? Sino-Brazilian background towards de-dollarization within power dynamics

    The debate over reforming the IMFS gained momentum in the wake of the GFC, which temporarily disrupted dominant power structures (Armijo and Katada 2015; Gallagher 2015; Roberts et al. 2018). In this context, Brazil and China deepened their cooperation, particularly within the BRICS framework, a grouping whose identity is closely tied to its ambition to create alternative spaces beyond the influence of traditional powers, especially the United States (Stuenkel 2020). A key element of this effort has been the shared objective of reducing dependence on the dollar, which has shaped both the group’s narrative and policy initiatives aimed at increasing the use of local currencies (Liu and Papa 2022). Notably, the 2012 and 2013 summits led to proposals for the establishment of the New Development Bank (NDB) and the Contingent Reserve Arrangement (CRA), both of which were formally launched in 2014.7

    While academic and institutional forecasts in the late 2000s anticipated the rise of multiple DEEs currencies – including the Brazilian real – in global finance (Cohen 2009; Eichengreen 2011; International Monetary Fund 2011; Dailami et al. 2011), in practice only China’s RMB has shown potential for a greater internationalization. Remarkably, one of the BRICS key achievements in reforming the IMFS was the inclusion of the RMB in the IMF’s Special Drawing Rights (SDR) basket in late 2015 (effective October 2016) – the only currency issued by a DEE. This milestone underscored China’s unique and asymmetrical position relative to other underrepresented countries.

    As China began promoting the international use of its currency, for most DEEs – including Brazil – moving away from the dollar-centric IMFS meant incorporating other currencies (for a discussion on the case of Brazil, see Orsi [2019]). As previously discussed, the global appeal of a currency is linked to certain economic and political qualifications of its issuer – qualifications which are difficult to fulfill. Moreover, structural factors within the IMFS, particularly the embedded hegemony of the dollar, pose additional challenges to the rise of alternative currencies (Nölke 2024). In particular, as the dollar serves as the primary unit and benchmark of global wealth, even alternatives to the dollar often rely on it to gain credibility and attractiveness, including the RMB (Eichengreen et al. 2022).

    Yet, contrasting with historical examples where currency internationalization was predominantly driven by market forces (Eichengreen 2011), China’s approach has been characterized by a state-managed strategy with carefully crafted, long-term objectives. This politically motivated “supply-side strategy” focuses on government-led initiatives to build and sustain strategic networks that enhance the RMB’s cross-border usability and trust (Liao and McDowell 2015; Subacchi, 2016; Chey, 2022). Key initiatives include bilateral swap agreements with over 40 central banks8, offshore RMB hubs in financial centers like Hong Kong and London, and the introduction of the Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) in 2015, a global clearing and settlement network for RMB transactions designed as an alternative to SWIFT (Green and Gruin, 2021). These efforts serve to place the RMB as a credible alternative in trade settlement, reserve holdings, and debt issuance.

    Thus, while many DEEs have signaled a desire to shift away from a dollar-centric system9, only China has effectively mobilized its unique institutional capabilities and political incentives to establish a credible alternative. As the RMB internationalization process advances amid intensifying global geopolitical competition, China has strategically leveraged its geopolitical relationships to promote RMB usage in trade and investment. Initiatives such as the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI) and bilateral agreements have played a crucial role in encouraging RMB adoption among participating countries (McDowell, 2015; Zhang et al., 2017). Furthermore, China has actively facilitated RMB use in regions affected by U.S. and European sanctions, such as Russia, positioning its currency as an alternative to dollar-dominated systems. Within this context, China has also progressively sought to expand its monetary and financial influence in Brazil, as will be analyzed next.

    Empirical assessment: Brazil’s responses to RMB internationalization

    This section evaluates Brazil’s responses to RMB internationalization. It shows key measures taken by Brazil to reduce its dependence on the dollar and/or promote the integration of the RMB into the Brazilian economy. It also adapts Liu and Papa’s (2022) “pathways to de-dollarization” framework, focusing on three metrics: (i) the composition of Brazil’s foreign reserves, particularly the RMB’s share relative to the dollar; (ii) the prevalence of RMB invoicing in Brazil-China trade; and (iii) Brazil’s adoption of alternative payment systems, like China’s CIPS. These indicators contribute to understand whether Brazil leans toward building parallel structures (e.g., RMB-denominated trade corridors) or incrementally reforming dollar-centric systems (e.g., gradual reserve diversification). Finally, this section attempts to situate Brazil’s actions within its geopolitical balancing act between China and the U.S., shedding light on how the country has navigated the tensions between de-dollarization ambitions and structural constraints.

    In terms of Brazil’s international reserves composition and access to RMB, an important event took place in 2013, which was the celebration of a bilateral swap agreement (BSA) in local currencies between the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB) and the People’s Bank of China (PBoC), amounting to BRL 60 billion or CNY 190 billion (USD30 billion at the time) (Banco Central do Brasil 2013; “China and Brazil sign $30bn currency swap agreement.” 2013). Officially, the agreement was aimed at facilitating bilateral trade between the two countries, regardless of global liquidity conditions, while also serving to China’s push for a more international role for its currency. However, no currency operations were conducted under the BSA, and the agreement was not renewed after its three-year validity period (Klein and Martins 2019).

    In 2019, less than five years after officially integrating the IMF’s SDR basket, the RMB was included in Brazil’s international reserves, accounting for 1.10% of total reserves in 2019 (Banco Central do Brasil 2024). Officially, one of the objectives in the management of international reserves is to reduce Brazil’s exposure to exchange rate risk. Thus, the Central Bank of Brazil (BCB, in the Portuguese acronym) aims to build a diversified portfolio with a countercyclical profile. In 2021, the BCB increased the diversification in currency allocation, which translated in an increased contribution of the RMB to 5% of the portfolio, in addition to the inclusion of the Canadian dollar (CAD) and the Australian dollar (AUD), with shares of approximately 1% each. This diversification came at the expense of a reduction in the share of the dollar, which went from 89.93% in 2018 to 78.45% in 2024.     It should be noted that this change in the management of international reserves took place under the presidency of Roberto Campos Neto at the BCB, during the Jair Bolsonaro government (2019-2022), a government which was marked by unprecedented friction in diplomatic relations with China (Casarões and Farias 2022). That suggests the inclusion of the RMB in Brazil’s international reserves was not a political movement, but likely Brazil’s central bank reserve managers alignment with global trends of active portfolio diversification (Arslanalp et al. 2022). It is also observed that the amount allocated in RMB grew significantly between 2020 and 2021, from 1.2% to 4.9%, reaching 5.3% in 2024, the second largest allocation that year.

    Regarding trade invoicing and settlement, although China surpassed the United States as Brazil’s largest trading partner in 2009, most of their trade is still conducted in dollars. In 1997, Brazil’s trade flow with China (US$2.2 billion) was just one-tenth the value of its trade flow with the United States (US$22.9 billion). Over the next quarter-century, however, trade between Brazil and China expanded dramatically. By 2023, their bilateral trade flow reached US$157.5 billion – more than quadrupling since 2009 and now doubling the value of Brazil-U.S. trade (US$74.9 billion in 2023, down from US$88.7 billion in 2022). For comparison, trade between Brazil and the United States grew modestly over the same period, rising from US$35.6 billion in 2009 to US$74.9 billion in 2023 (Ministério do Desenvolvimento, Indústria, Comércio e Serviços 2024).

    As shown in Table 4, between 2019 and 2023, 95.38% of Brazil’s export earnings and 82.04% of import payments were conducted in dollars. The euro and Brazilian real followed at much lower levels (2.74% and 9.07% for exports and imports, respectively, in euros; 1.55% and 6.13% in reais). Notably, the Chinese renminbi accounted for only 0.35% of import payments, confirming that dollar-denominated transactions still underpin most trade with China (Ministério do Desenvolvimento, Indústria, Comércio e Serviços 2025).

    The Chinese renminbi’s presence in Brazilian trade remains extremely limited across specific sectors, confined almost entirely to imports and in negligible amounts. The currency is virtually absent in key industries such as agriculture and other critical product categories. Its minimal usage is restricted to the manufacturing sector, where its share remains marginal compared to dominant currencies like the dollar and euro.10 Even within manufacturing, the RMB’s role is insignificant, calling attention to its lack of penetration in Brazil’s trade sectors.

    The change of government in Brazil in January 2023 gave new impetus to Brazil’s bilateral relations with China. Under the Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva government (2003-2010 and 2023-present), Brazil took more concrete steps towards supporting the internationalization of the RMB. In February 2023, the PBoC and the BCB signed a Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) on establishing RMB clearing arrangements in Brazil. In April, during the President Lula’s official visit to China, it was announced the accession of the BOCOM BBM to the CIPS – making it the first bank to join CIPS in South America (“Brazil takes steps to transact in yuan as China ties grow.” 2023; “Brasil assina mais de 20 acordos de cooperação em missão empresarial em Pequim.” 2023). The CIPS is the Chinese alternative to the SWIFT, both clearing systems that facilitate transactions in local currencies. In addition, the Brazilian branch of the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China (ICBC) started to work as the RMB clearing house in Brazil (“Brasil assina mais de 20 acordos de cooperação em missão empresarial em Pequim.” 2023). Following the experience of other countries, after defining the clearing house (the ICBC, in the case of Brazil), the next step would be the creation of a “Renminbi Offshore Center”, a capital market structure that allows transactions of financial assets in RMB, and the execution of swap agreements between the PBoC and the BCB (Coelho 2023).

    As a result of this institutional building, in 2023 Brazil and China completed the first transaction in a closed circuit with local currencies, i.e., with transactions financed and settled in RMB and converted directly to reais, facilitated by the Bank of China (BOC), without any participation of the dollar (Greene 2023; Moliterno 2023). This milestone in the history of Sino-Brazilian trade comes in the wake of the memorandum of understanding to facilitate bilateral trade signed by the leaders of the two countries during President Lula’s visit to Beijing in April of the same year (Ministério das Relações Exteriores 2023). At that occasion, President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva explicitly criticized the dollar hegemony and called on BRICS nations to adopt their own currencies for cross-border trade, framing it as a tool to empower the Global South (Leahy 2023). In September 2023, a new memorandum was signed for cooperation on single windows in foreign trade, seeking to create interoperability between the Brazilian and Chinese systems in terms of tools and protocols (Ministério do Desenvolvimento, Indústria, Comércio e Serviços 2023).

    In 2024, two of Brazil’s top exporters, Vale (formerly Companhia Vale do Rio Doce) and Suzano Papel e Celulose, took concrete steps toward integrating the RMB into their operations. Vale, the largest producer of iron ore and nickel in the world, confirmed iron ore transactions with China conducted exclusively in RMB, bypassing the Brazilian real altogether. These transactions are tied to the company’s local subsidiary in China’s spot market, which primarily serves small and medium-sized steel producers. These mills, often managing low inventory levels, rely on the spot market to secure immediate supplies and avoid production interruptions. Similarly, Suzano Papel e Celulose, a major player in Brazil’s pulp and paper industry, affirmed to have initiated RMB transactions through small-scale pilot projects (Sá 2024).

    The adoption of the RMB by key Brazilian players has been possible due to the institutional framework established in Brazil to facilitate access to the currency and highlights its potential for expansion, given China’s pivotal role as the country’s largest trading partner. As David Cohen, treasurer of BoCom BBM (the Brazilian unit of the Bank of Communications of China), highlighted in a recent interview, many Brazilian exporters maintain substantial operations in China, including local offices and procurement systems (Sá 2024). Not only holding RMB reserves simplifies the settlement of obligations, particularly for imports of Chinese goods, like machinery, but also these transactions often face fewer bureaucratic steps and lower transaction costs, benefiting from China’s policies to promote its currency internationally. Moreover, Walter Schalka, CEO of Suzano, noted the rising importance of the Chinese currency, emphasizing that its adoption by smaller clients points to a larger trend: “the dollar will become less relevant” over time, reflecting a growing consensus among stakeholders in this transition (Sá 2024).

    Table 5 summarizes the advancement of the RMB in Brazil. It shows that, while there has been progress in the use of the RMB in Brazil, this remains very limited compared to the historical dominance of the dollar. The diversification of foreign reserves includes the RMB, but only in modest proportions. The bilateral swap agreements formalized between the BCB and the PBoC were not operationalized, and the agreement was not renewed after three years. In trade invoicing and settlement, although recent developments point to an increasing adoption of the RMB by key Brazilian companies, the Chinese currency plays only a minor role, with the dollar continuing to dominate transactions.

    There are, however, important institutional developments that signal possible changes in the future in the direction of a greater role for the RMB within Brazil. Yet Brazil’s approach remains measured (Financial Times 2024), reflecting what Guimarães (2020, p. 603) terms an “uneasy and ambivalent sense of well placement within Latin America and the West.” This ambivalence manifests in a strategy that prioritizes de-risking over outright displacement of the dollar, seeking to mitigate vulnerabilities without provoking systemic ruptures. In the context of U.S.-China rivalry, it follows the perspective that perceives it as a chance to expand its strategic autonomy, navigating between the two major powers while managing its historical dependence on the United States (Brun and Covarrubias, 2024; Stuenkel 2025). While the absence of a formalized roadmap for RMB adoption underscores caution, the convergence of political rhetoric, institutional partnerships, and corporate experimentation – such as Eldorado Brasil’s landmark RMB-denominated trade deals – positions Brazil as a strategic testing ground for China’s vision of a multipolar financial order (Fravel 2025). This duality reflects a pragmatic balancing act: leveraging RMB infrastructure like CIPS and ICBC’s clearing services to enhance trade efficiency, while avoiding direct confrontation with entrenched Western financial systems. Thus, Brazil’s strategy surfaces not as a rejection of dollar dominance, but as a calibrated effort to diversify risks and assert its role as a bridge between emerging and established economic powers.

    Final remarks

    This article analyzed Brazil’s engagement with the internationalization of the Chinese RMB. Our research suggests that while Brazil is not actively championing the RMB as a global currency, its actions – such as including RMB in its reserves and creating the institutional framework for invoicing and settling trade in RMB – reflect a pragmatic willingness to adapt to shifting global dynamics. Rather than embarking on a de-dollarization process, Brazil appears to be employing an incremental approach – one that leverages selective institutional measures to diversify its financial risks while preserving its strategic ties with the dominant global currency.

    From an institutional standpoint, Brazil has taken measured steps such as incorporating the RMB into its international reserves, experimenting with bilateral swap agreements, and integrating into alternative payment infrastructures like CIPS through key banking institutions. These initiatives, although modest in scale compared to the enduring prevalence of the dollar, are significant in that they represent initial efforts to build a complementary financial and payments network. This network aims to facilitate trade with China and mitigate the vulnerabilities of a dollar-centric system, all while contending with the inherent limitations imposed by Brazil’s position within a hierarchically structured IMFS.

    The implications of this engagement are multifaceted. On the one hand, these measures can serve to enhance Brazil’s resilience against external shocks and offer a pathway toward greater economic autonomy. On the other hand, they underline Brazil’s continuing reliance on established power structures within the IMFS. All in all, Brazil’s balancing act illustrates the complexities of navigating a global system marked by escalating geopolitical tensions, with possible repercussions in the international monetary and financial landscape.
