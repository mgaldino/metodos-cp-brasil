SPECIAL ISSUE: WHERE IS THE SEA IN IR? • Contexto int. 47 (2) • 2025 • https://doi.org/10.1590/S0102-8529.20244603e20230042 linkcopy

Ocean Governance, Maritime Security, and International Relations

Governança dos oceanos, segurança marítima e Relações Internacionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Taking as a starting point Philip Steinberg’s seminal insights on the social construction of the ocean, in this article we examine the maritime environment as a social space, seeking to connect contemporary debates on ocean governance, maritime security, and International Relations (IR). From this standpoint, we investigate how different narratives about the sea guide the way ocean governance has developed in the past and projects into the future. Furthermore, we argue that despite the lack of centrality of the sea in IR theorising, these different narratives and the way they guide ocean governance can be framed within the main theoretical approaches of the IR discipline. To advance this discussion, we draw on primary documentary sources, including international law and government and non-government reports, as well as on secondary bibliographic sources, in order to examine how narratives about the sea lead to alternative approaches to ocean governance. Based on this analysis, the article contributes not only to synthesise the debate on ocean governance, but also to connect this debate to the theoretical discussion in the IR field.

    Key words
    maritime security; ocean governance; International Relations; UNCLOS; maritime issues

    Resumo

    Partindo das ideias fundamentais de Philip Steinberg sobre a construção social do oceano, este artigo examina o ambiente marítimo como um espaço social, buscando conectar os debates contemporâneos sobre governança dos oceanos, segurança marítima e relações internacionais (RI). A partir dessa perspectiva, investigamos como diferentes narrativas sobre o mar orientam a forma como a governança oceânica se desenvolveu no passado e se projeta para o futuro. Além disso, argumentamos que, apesar da falta de centralidade do mar na teorização das RI, essas diferentes narrativas e a forma como orientam a governança dos oceanos podem ser enquadradas dentro das principais abordagens teóricas da disciplina de RI. Para avançar nessa discussão, utilizamos fontes documentais primárias, incluindo o direito internacional e relatórios governamentais e não governamentais, bem como fontes bibliográficas secundárias, a fim de examinar como as narrativas sobre o mar levam a abordagens alternativas de governança oceânica. Com base nessa análise, o artigo contribui não apenas para sintetizar o debate sobre a governança dos oceanos, mas também para conectá-lo à discussão teórica no campo das RI.

    Palavras-chave

    Introduction

    Although the oceans cover three-quarters of the planet’s surface, connecting hundreds of nations around the world, this space has been under-theorised in International Relations (IR). Indeed, historically speaking, the maritime domain has been generally conceived within western political thought and cartographic representations as an ‘empty’ space or a ‘void’; functioning as a mere surface of flows free from social relations, and destined for the circulation of goods, the exploitation of resources, and the military adventures of States (Steinberg 2001: 4). Even when classical strategists such as Alfred Thayer Mahan and Julian Stafford Corbett gave the ocean space centrality within their political thinking, this surface was seen as an instrument for the consolidation of land power and a route to attack land armies in wartime. Consequently, the maritime environment was usually an extrapolation of terrestrial interests and conflicts.

    Carl Schmitt’s insights regarding the ocean space go in the same direction. Although Schmitt’s political thinking put some emphasis on maritime space, especially in works such as Land and Sea (1997) and The Nomos of the Earth (2003), he made a clear division between land and sea, looking at the maritime space as a void that lacks a socio-political dimension. This means, as he emphasised in the opening paragraph of Land and Sea, that it is the land that offers the ‘firmly-grounded’ standpoint and base on which humankind ‘lives, moves and walks,’ determining its impressions and conditioning its point of view on the world (Schmitt 1997: 1). Thus, while on the one hand he considered the land as a space of law, unity, and order, on the other, he did not attribute these same qualities to the maritime space: ‘the sea knows no such apparent unity of space and law, of order and orientation’ (Schmitt 2003: 42). As Philip Steinberg and Kimberley Peters (2015: 249) noted, a vision like this turns the ocean into a space ‘ideologically and physically insignificant in reference to sociocultural and geopolitical concerns.’

    This does not mean that this vision regards the oceans as an ‘unimportant’ space. On the contrary, mercantile and industrial capitalist ideologies have constructed the sea for centuries as a useful surface for transport and flow of wealth, so that States’ projection of power at sea was considered relevant when necessary to keep this surface free of friction and obstacles to achieve those self-interested objectives (Steinberg 2001: 113-114). Despite this recognised relevance, it should be emphasised that this vision turns the sea into a space devoid of its intrinsic socio-political significance – i.e. the sea is defined in terms of land qualities and interests, idealised as an empty surface ‘outside society and the terrestrial places of progress, civilization, and development’ (Steinberg 2001: 114).

    However, the social and political significance of the ocean (or the sea, as an alternative term) has undergone great transformations in recent decades. The evolution of marine science and technology, the growing political perception of oceans as reservoirs of mineral wealth and sources of living resources available to States, the great global concern over the need to maintain the marine ecosystem under good natural conditions, as well as the articulation, in the early 1980s, of all these concerns in the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), have contributed to strengthen the idea of the ocean as a common and the need for its ecological preservation and sustainable use. They also led to the introduction of the concept of sovereignty and rights of sovereignty over certain maritime areas into the vocabulary of ocean governance. As a result, the maritime domain is increasingly seen as a space belonging to society at large, a potential arena to produce social change, and a space to be territorialised (Steinberg 2001: 6-7).

    Relying on a social constructivist perspective, we approach the maritime environment as a social space, seeking to connect contemporary debates on ocean governance, maritime security, and IR theories. Starting from Philip Steinberg’s (2001) seminal insight on the social construction of the ocean, we aim to show that there is always a process of formation – i.e. of social construction –, behind the images we have of the ocean space. This means that uses, regulations, norms, representations, and political narratives about the oceans are not fixed, but instead are in constant transformation according to the specific interests of global governance. From this perspective, the ocean space is no longer seen as a fixed and immutable category or a mere fact of nature. Instead, it is seen as a socially constructed space, which leads to multiple images in permanent transformation about the oceans.

    From this constructivist standpoint, we examine how different narratives about the maritime space guide the way ocean governance has evolved. With this objective in mind, the crucial starting question is: What does ocean governance mean? This is an evolving discussion on which analysts take different and nuanced positions. First of all, it is difficult to establish a convergent label for this topic – ocean governance, maritime governance, integrated ocean management, and international order at sea are just some of the labels found in the bibliography that are somehow related to the way the ocean space is managed (Haward and Vince 2008; Bekkevold and Till 2016; Lukaszuk 2018; Klein 2022). Secondly, scholars differ in their definitions, ranging from the more realist, strategic, and State-centric notion of order at sea to the multidimensional notion of governance developed in the last decade of the 20th century and the early decades of the 21st century.

    In an attempt to trace these conceptual developments, we begin with the period between the end of the 1950s and the early 1980s, when the United Nations (UN) convened a series of conferences that led to the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS 1982). Entering into force in 1994 after its implementing agreements, UNCLOS was an important step towards equating, from the standpoint of international law, a series of issues affecting the ocean space, such as maritime territorial demarcation; clear definition of the authorities responsible for the different areas; rights of navigation; conservation and utilisation of living resources; problems that affect maritime security such as piracy, smuggling, human trafficking, and other forms of crime at sea; predatory fishing; protection and preservation of the maritime environment; promotion of scientific research and marine technology; and so on (UNCLOS 1982). These provisions considerably expanded the scope of issues that became the subject of ocean politics and constituted important steps that paved the way for the emergence of the multidimensional notion of ocean governance.

    Together with the entry into force of UNCLOS, some events and contributions from the policy and academic fields have helped to strengthen and consolidate the notion of governance in contemporary ocean affairs. First, the end of the Cold War and the consequent reduction of pressures imposed by superpowers’ strategic competition and arms spending resulted in a decline of the military dimension of maritime security in the 1990s and 2000s, and pushed new issues into ocean politics, including non-military challenges to security, as well as developmental, social, and environmental concerns. Secondly, the UN Conference on Environment and Development, held in 1992 in the city of Rio de Janeiro (Rio-92), gave special emphasis to the ‘protection of the oceans, all kinds of seas, including enclosed and semi-enclosed seas, and coastal areas and the protection, rational use and development of their living resources’ (Agenda 21 1992, chapter 17). These developments contributed to the recognition of the ‘growing cross-disciplinary character of sea and ocean studies with ecology, security, economy and law’ (Lukaszuk 2018: 131). Thirdly, the emergence of the academic debate on globalisation and complex interdependence as a neoliberal alternative to the realist approach to International Relations contributed to emphasise aspects such as the multiplication of formal and informal ties between governmental and non-governmental elites and transnational organisations; the multiplication of items on the political agenda; and the reduction of the role of military force in resolving disputes in a context of interdependence, including in the ocean environment (Keohane and Nye 1997). Fourthly, the emergence of ‘informal lawmaking’ in ocean affairs – that is, the emergence of informal processes and the involvement of actors other than States in the production of policies and agreements to regulate maritime activities outside of formal, State-based and top-down lawmaking structures (Klein 2022) – expanded decision-making and deliberative processes on ocean politics far beyond traditional and formal structures of government.

    What these developments show is that the growing multidimensionality and complexity of decision-making processes, as well as the expansion of issues and actors that became part of the academic and political approach to ocean affairs, contributed to the consolidation of the concept of ocean governance in the last decade of the 20th century and the first decades of the 21st century. Instead of the traditional static and State-centric notion of order at sea – associated with a friction-free condition to be achieved by the self-interested action of States –, the notion of ocean governance is seen as a process – rather than a fixed state or stable condition – that encompasses the broader interests of the entire community engaged in ocean affairs. Thus, it involves not only its formal, official and top-down decision-making structures, but also the increasingly dynamic, multi-layered, fluid, pluralist, decentralised and informal processes of decision-making and regulation.

    Therefore, in light of the developments summarised above, and following Kimberley Peters (2020: 1), we outline a general definition of ocean governance as the complex ‘process of deciding, managing, controlling and organizing a set of activities, practices, people, resources and spaces’ that impacts the way the ocean is managed. This includes not only formal official institutions, agents, and practices conventionally associated with State executive structures, but also a broader range of non-governmental agents and practices that influence ‘the production of policy outcomes,’ including NGOs, private companies, pressure groups, social movements, scholars, environmentalists, etc. (Peters 2020: 1). How these main characteristics have evolved in the realm of ocean politics and the academic debate are examined in greater depth throughout this article.

    To advance this discussion, we draw on secondary bibliography and primary documentary sources, including international laws and governmental and non-governmental reports, in order to examine, using the method of historical description and analysis, the main developments observed in the governance of the oceans. Based on this panorama, this study tries to identify the different narratives (images) that guide ocean governance. As we seek to show, at least five main narratives can be identified: maritime security, maritime economics, maritime borders, maritime conservation, and maritime justice. These five narratives should not be seen as mutually exclusive, but rather as type-images, which eventually connect and intermingle. Furthermore, we argue that despite the lack of centrality of the sea in IR theorising, these different images and the way they guide ocean governance can be framed within the main theoretical debates of the IR discipline.

    The article proceeds in four main sections. Section 1 explores the bibliography on the social construction of the ocean, to define the theoretical framework that guides this study. Sections 2 and 3 examine developments in constructing the significance of the ocean space, seeking to show how UNCLOS’ provisions paved avenues for a multidimensional approach not only to maritime security, but also to other additional dimensions of contemporary ocean governance. Finally, Section 4 identifies how different narratives (images) of the maritime environment lead to different approaches to ocean governance, indicating how these different approaches can be seen through the lenses of IR theories.

    A social constructivist approach to the ocean space

    Traditionally, oceans are seen as spaces outside society, ‘an uninteresting abyss that separates the places that “matter”,’ that is, the continental land masses where the relevant physical, social, and political processes supposedly happen (Steinberg 1999: 367, 2001: 6). However, this perception of the ocean as an ‘uninteresting’ space for social and political processes has dramatically changed in recent decades, not only because of the oceans’ growing importance for development, but also because of their increasing vulnerability and susceptibility to challenges and conflicts that emerge from their use. As a result, the significance of ocean space has grown in complexity, becoming an expanded arena of problems and disputes, as well as of cooperation and transformation.

    Despite these developments, the ocean space and its problematisation remain a rare subject in the IR field. This lack contrasts with the more productive engagement of other areas and fields of study regarding the ocean, such as physical and political geography, biology, oceanography, international law, naval science, as well as governance and environmental studies. Even when considering these productive engagements, it is noteworthy that descriptive, legal, or military approaches to the ocean predominate, leaving a large gap to be filled in the discussion of how this space is socially constructed – i.e. how social and political discourses and practices construct representations that give meaning to this huge volume of water.

    Within this general panorama, the seminal work of the geographer Philip E. Steinberg stands out as a remarkable exception. Published in 2001, his book The Social Construction of the Ocean can be considered the first comprehensive work that deliberately assumed the objective of treating the ocean space from a constructivist point of view. According to Steinberg, the oceans, like land-spaces, are simultaneously ‘an arena wherein social conflicts occur and a space shaped by these conflicts,’ which means that, historically, ‘the social structures, individual behaviours, institutional arrangements, and natural features’ have intersected to mutually constitute each other (Steinberg 2001: 20). This is an explicit echo of the constructivist maxim that agents and structures are co-constitutive in the production and reproduction of reality.

    Steinberg draws his social constructivist approach from what he calls ‘territorial political economy perspective,’ borrowing from this tradition the idea that the organisation of society is a function of the material base and technologies developed for transforming nature so as to sustain social life (Steinberg 2001: 21). This means that nature-society relations – or, in other terms, material-social or economic-political relations – are co-constitutive in the processes that define the social construction of space. Steinberg also derives from this perspective the idea that the political-economic logic and structures of society prompt social agents to implement uses (defining the spatiality of the dominant political economy), regulations (defining territoriality), and representations (defining meanings through discourse) which, taken together, are operative in the social construction of spaces (Steinberg 2001: 21-38). Steinberg’s main argument is that social processes at sea occur similarly to these territorial processes, which means there is always a process of formation – i.e. of social construction –, behind the uses of the ocean and the way this space is managed.

    Despite this constructivist commitment, some authors note that the core of Steinberg’s book is closer to an international political economy analysis than to a social constructivist approach. As Mike Bell (2003: 218) and John Hannigan (2016: 32) observe, Steinberg’s book lacks a greater emphasis on discourse and representation that would be expected due to the ideational dimension usually attached to the term ‘constructivism.’ If it is worth recalling this criticism, it is also important to highlight other relevant aspects that do justice to Steinberg’s constructivist contributions to ocean studies.

    Firstly, Steinberg does not fail to emphasise discursive constructions (alongside uses and regulations) as a defining pillar of his approach. According to him, three kinds of discourse have informed constructions of space in modernity: the discourse of development (which constructs land space as a reservoir of economic resources and a space for growth and civilisation), the discourse of geopolitics (which constructs land space as a force-field within which States exercise their relative power over their competitors), and the discourse of laws (which define places, their hierarchical position, their scales and boundaries, property rights, and other legal aspects related to territorial control and order). For Steinberg, these three discourses serve to construct the land surface as a space within society (Steinberg 2001: 35), that is, a space where social and political relations that really ‘matter’ occur (Steinberg 1999: 367).

    While these discursive representations constructed the land surface as a space of society, Steinberg argues that they conversely served to define the water surface as a ‘void’ of society – i.e. as ‘a space devoid of potential for growth and civilisation’ (antithetical to the development discourse), a space ‘external to the territory of political society’ (antithetical to the geopolitical discourse), and as a space ‘immune to social control and order’ (antithetical to the legal discourse) (Steinberg 2001: 35). These three discourses, according to the author, have significantly reinforced how modern cartographic representations depict the ocean merely as ‘a space used by society’ rather than a ‘space of society’ (Steinberg 2001: 20, 35). These observations show that the way Steinberg approaches international political economy combines material and discursive factors, stressing their mutual constitution in the social construction of reality, which puts his work in line with how the so-called ‘conventional constructivists’1 make sense of the world.

    Secondly, Steinberg’s more recent contributions have gone beyond the international political economy framework and the conventional constructivist focus of his early work, becoming closer to a more critical-reflexive perspective of social constructivism.2 From these critical lenses, Steinberg (on his own or in collaboration with other authors) has approached the ocean from a more radical, critical and non-positivist perspective that emphasises the power and meaning of water, and the ways discourses construct different narratives about the ocean space (Steinberg 2018; Steinberg 2022; Steinberg and Peters 2015; Peters and Steinberg 2019). Kimberley Peters (2020), a frequent collaborator of Steinberg, has also contributed to this critical constructivist turn in ocean studies by calling for a radical engagement with how we understand ‘the ontologies (the regimes of what we believe exists) and geophilosophies (the geographically informed modes of thinking)’ that underpin ocean governance. According to Peters (2020: 2), the ontologies and dominant ways of thinking that drive ocean governance project onto the organisation of marine space a ‘static, one-dimensional and flat’ territorialising logic that ignores the particular characteristics of volume, mobility, fluidity, and wetness that are specific to the ocean space. The consequence of the dominant geo-approach to the water space is an unproductive effort to territorialise the sea (areas, sectors, borders, boundaries, limits) that reproduce land categories and prevent new modes of thinking – i.e. alternative ontologies and geophilosophies – that make room for more creative and original ways of managing the oceans.

    We will return to these considerations later in the context of the discussion on ocean governance. For now, the important point to highlight for the purposes of this study is that the social constructivist turn in ocean studies inaugurated by Steinberg provided scope for a more productive engagement with the different and competing narratives constructed about the ocean and their implications for how we position ourselves in relation to this space. Based on these insights, we try to identify, in the following sections, how different images impact the governance of the ocean space.

    UNCLOS, maritime security, and ocean governance

    UNCLOS’s preamble declares that State parties to the convention are ‘conscious that the problems of ocean space are closely interrelated and need to be considered as a whole,’ and recognises that a legal order for the seas will ‘facilitate international communication’ and ‘promote the peaceful uses of the seas and oceans, the equitable and efficient utilization of their resources, the conservation of their living resources, and the study, protection and preservation of the marine environment.’ Moreover, State parties to the convention manifest the belief ‘that the codification and progressive development of the law of the sea […] will contribute to the strengthening of peace, security, cooperation and friendly relations among all nations in conformity with the principles of justice and equal rights and will promote the economic and social advancement of all peoples of the world’ (UNCLOS 1982, Preamble).

    From the viewpoint of ocean governance, UNCLOS’s multidimensional legal framework presents immense challenges. If one considers, on the one hand, that the aim of UNCLOS’s provisions is to delineate a legal framework to resolve tensions over maritime disputes, promote peace and security in the oceans, and enable the sustainable use of marine resources, it is important to consider, on the other hand, that UNCLOS also has a strong moral and ethical dimension that is expressed through claims for justice, equal rights, and equitable international order that meet the ‘needs of mankind as a whole and, in particular, the special interests and needs of developing countries’ (UNCLOS 1982, Preamble).

    When considering this multidimensional approach of UNCLOS and the challenges involved in its implementation, ocean governance is faced with the task of developing policies that promote peaceful uses, security, development, and conservation of the marine environment. At the same time, it undertakes the challenge of guiding such policies through a normative commitment to inclusiveness, equity, justice, and sustainability, going beyond the State-centric and realist perspectives that traditionally dominated ocean affairs. According to the Independent World Commission on the Oceans (IWCO) – an independent global civil society group launched in 1995, under UN auspices, and integrated by intellectuals and scientists from several countries with the objective of fostering ‘critical reflection on the many aspects of the current situation of the oceans’ (IWCO 1998: 11) –, ocean governance should be organised around three main pillars: equity; sustainable development; and peace and security. Equity means greater sensitivity to the needs of individuals, social groups, and nations that are disadvantaged by geography, social and economic circumstances, and solidarity vis-à-vis vulnerable groups such as indigenous peoples, traditional fishing communities and coastal populations, as well as small island countries and landlocked and geographically disadvantaged nations. Sustainable development implies that benefits derived from the resources of the present should not hamper or prevent their ability to benefit future generations. Finally, peace and security express a concern with the several threats to the oceans, other than military ones, such as piracy; trafficking in drugs, weapons, and human beings; smuggling of goods; dumping of environmentally harmful and hazardous substances like toxic materials and nuclear wastes; maritime terrorism; and other illegal activities. To face these challenges, IWCO reports not only suggest that navies should be employed to enforce UNCLOS’s provisions regarding non-military threats affecting the ocean space, including ecological issues, but also to promote the implementation of peaceful conflict resolution mechanisms to address maritime territorial disputes; the establishment of nuclear-free zones; and the revision of the law of naval warfare (IWCO 1998: 17-19).

    Considering all these developments, which together depict a more complex, multidimensional, human and context-sensitive approach to the governance of the oceans, the 21st century has begun with countries considering revision of the concept of maritime security in their public policies, going beyond traditional concerns with naval power projection by States, especially in times of war. These policy developments, which have been pointed out by authors such as Till (2009), Germond (2015), Bueger (2015), and Bueger and Edmunds (2020), can be noticed, for instance, in documents from the United States produced for maritime purposes, such as The National Strategy for Maritime Security (USDHS 2005), or in documents from the European Union (Council of the European Union 2014; European Commission 2012, 2014).

    This change has also impacted the academic debate. As Bueger argues (2015: 161), since the end of the last century, maritime security has not been exclusively concerned with the security of maritime trade, and one could also add that this concept is now conceived far beyond its traditional strategic-military dimension. The image of the ocean as a rich reservoir of resources available for development (as a result of improvements in marine technology), and UNCLOS’s provisions on demarcation of maritime zones and peace and security concerns, have become integral parts of contemporary ocean politics. Due to these developments, the maritime security domain is nowadays not just a military arena, but includes, for example, constabulary practices (policing activities), leading to joint actions among navies, coast guards, and other security forces to face non-military threats in the ocean space. For example, piracy is today a salient issue in maritime security policy, including in countries with a small number of reported cases of this kind of crime. In some countries, smuggling and trafficking of human beings by sea can occur more frequently than piracy – although, in general, those illegal activities are not reported as a threat to maritime security in nations’ jurisdictional waters. Indeed, as Bueger (2020: 2) states, one observes a spillover of the role of transnational organised crime to illicit activities at sea, which he defines as ‘blue crimes.’

    The UN recognises the challenges posed by these new threats. In a document entitled UNCLOS at 30 (United Nations 2012: 4), piracy and armed robbery against ships are defined as maritime threats, as well as terrorist attacks against shipping and maritime installations, hijacking, smuggling of arms and hazardous wastes; smuggling of migrants and other human trafficking by sea; illicit traffic in drugs and psychotropic substances; illegal fishing, dumping of toxic wastes and other pollutants, and other violations of environmental laws. This document also considers the claims by States regarding ocean space and natural resources in the oceans. These often result in protracted maritime boundary disputes, leading to tensions between States (United Nations 2012: 5).

    Due to this wider range of issues and challenges, maritime security is no longer a concern restricted to the military field, as it was in the past; and a much wider range of actors and institutions, including non-governmental ones, have come to exert influence in policy formulation and decision-making processes. Within this scenario, maritime security has emerged as a relevant civilian issue, being incorporated in the public and private, national and international debates, creating a new context in which civilians compete with traditional strategic-military thinkers to influence decisions on maritime issues. As a result, in this 21st century, a more diverse group of agents, rather than a single exclusive group, contributes to propose and discuss approaches for ensuring the security of the ocean space, including navies; coast guards; organisations such as the IMO, UN, EU, and NATO; and non-governmental actors, such as academics and independent civil society groups, as is the case of the IWCO examined in this article. This generates many interpretations, narratives, and images of the ocean, which end up causing different constructions of maritime problems and multiple forms of actions or bureaucratic arrangements to respond to those problems.

    This expansion of the ocean governance framework has also been impacted by what Nathalie Klein (2022) calls ‘informal lawmaking’ – that is, collaborative processes conducted at the international level by actors other than States, operating beyond traditional top-down mechanisms of government, aimed at producing informal accords or expertise exchange that influence norm-setting or public policy on specific matters and problems. According to Klein, informal lawmaking processes have exerted a growing influence in ocean governance, ‘including in relation to maritime security and the management of ocean resources’ (Klein 2022: xiii). Unlike formal sources of law – as in the case of customary law or treaties such as UNCLOS –, informal lawmaking initiatives ‘are not binding as law’ and, in general, lack ‘formal mechanisms to review or monitor conduct under the agreement,’ which favours flexibility, discussion, and negotiation; information sharing; and the participation of stakeholders beyond official government agents (Klein 2022: 6). Despite these informal characteristics, they can influence regime formation, public policies, accords, and even produce a framework to involve States and government agents, as well as non-governmental actors, such as shipping companies and their vessels; seafarer unions; scientific bodies and conservation groups, in institutional or ad hoc arrangements to face specific challenges to maritime security or to manage ocean resources.

    With regard to maritime security, typical examples of this type of informal lawmaking can be observed in the context of the repression of piracy and armed robbery off the coast of Somalia. As Marie Jacobsson and Natalie Klein observe (2022: 44), the overall framework that provided responses to the outbreak of piracy cases off the coast of Somalia in 2008 involved not only a complex of formal binding laws (provided by UNCLOS and UN Security Council resolutions adopted under Chapter VII), but also informal lawmaking (informal agreements, arrangements, and processes). This included a broad range of stakeholders – e.g. international and regional organisations, individual States, and private stakeholders, such as the International Maritime Bureau and the shipping industry. The Contact Group on Piracy off the Coast of Somalia – an informal arrangement that sought to integrate and co-ordinate efforts of States –, intergovernmental organisations, the shipping industry, and individuals to face Somali piracy; and the Djibouti Code of Conduct (IMO 2009) – which sought to co-ordinate the regional efforts for ‘capacity-building in the Gulf of Aden and Western Indian Ocean to counter the threat of piracy’ – are examples of these kinds of informal arrangements (Jacobsson and Klein 2022: 45, 53).

    Despite the non-binding characteristic of the Djibouti Code of Conduct – it was negotiated under the auspices of the International Maritime Organisation (IMO) as an informal accord –, it also had concrete normative effects, making a significant contribution to construct a regional infrastructure, including a training centre in Djibouti and information-sharing centres, which facilitated the dissemination of warnings to international shipping companies and regional cooperation in the following areas: ‘investigation, arrest and prosecution’ of suspects of piracy; ‘interdiction and seizure of suspect ships’; ‘rescue of ships, persons and property subject to piracy and armed robbery’; and ‘conduct of shared operations’ not only among signatory regional States, but also with foreign navies operating in the region (Jacobsson and Klein 2022: 53).

    The case of Somali piracy is also emblematic to illustrate the expansion of the role of navies to perform non-traditional military functions. Indeed, as Bueger (2015: 159) argues, the transformations of the concept of ‘maritime security’ have gained salience in the 21st century as a consequence, among several factors, of the terrorist attacks of September 11th and the fear that terrorism would spread to the maritime space; and the rise of piracy attacks off the coast of Somalia (2008-2011). In this context, navies are increasingly being inserted in a framework of action that takes into account the so-called ‘new threats,’ usually related to non-state actors and non-military threats. As Oliveira (2018a) notes, the extraordinary mobilisation of navies from all over the world to fight Somali piracy – including those from States acting individually (Russia, India, China, Japan, Malaysia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, Kenya, South Africa, and Yemen), acting in coalitions (the US-led CFT-151), acting through alliances (NATO naval forces), and regional organisations (European Union naval forces) – exemplify this trend.

    The US Navy, which was already in the Gulf region leading an international naval coalition to combat terrorism since 2001 (CTF-150), divided this taskforce in 2009 to create a new coalition specifically focused on combating Somali piracy (CFT-151). The European Union (EU) saw Somali piracy as an opportunity to justify and put into practice its multinational naval structure under the Common Security and Defence Policy, then considered a priority by the EU presidency. ‘Operation EUNAFOR Atalanta,’ as this EU mission was called, remains to this day in the region, involved not only in anti-piracy operations, but also in an expanded mandate for the monitoring of illegal activities at sea (weapons and drug trafficking, illicit trade in charcoal, unregulated fishing), as well as actions that contributed to enforcing the arms embargo on Somalia. NATO saw anti-piracy efforts as an opportunity to expand its reach beyond transatlantic borders. Thus, for the first time in its history, the Atlantic Alliance deployed its naval forces in the Persian Gulf in 2008 with a dual mission: to visit various ports in the Gulf region (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, and the United Arab Emirates) and to carry out anti-piracy operations (escorting World Food Program ships and patrolling the waters adjacent to Somalia). A sequence of NATO missions – Allied Provider, Allied Protector, and Ocean Shield – kept its naval forces involved in anti-piracy operations in the region until 2016 (Oliveira 2013a, 2013b, 2018a, 2023).

    This involvement with anti-piracy operations off the Horn of Africa in late 2000s was crucial to NATO’s renewed attention to maritime security and the redefinition of its strategic concept. Indeed, the two decades after the end of Cold War were marked by the absence of any significant development in NATO’s doctrine and priorities regarding the maritime domain (Gade and Hilde 2016). However, in the late 2000s and early 2010s, largely motivated by Somali piracy and increased international attention on the vulnerabilities of strategic SLOCs, significant changes occurred in NATO’s strategic concept, leading to maritime security being included among the key roles for the Alliance’s naval forces (Gade and Hilde 2016: 122).

    The Somali piracy case provides, in short, a good illustration of several current developments in maritime security. The first important observation from this case is the fact that navies, despite their primary military focus, have been re-orientated to perform constabulary functions. The second important observation is the fact that maritime security has become an expanded blend of approaches, actors, and issues that merge domestic security, international security and national defence, private sectors and agents, as well as formal and informal lawmaking. This suggests an integrated and multidimensional approach to the ocean space and its problems, which significantly broadens the maritime security framework.

    Official statements from policymakers recognise some of these changes in maritime security. As Feldt, Roell and Thiele (2013: 2) point out, the security of the maritime domain has become a task that gathers several international entities from the public and private sectors with a common goal to achieve order at sea and maintain the free circulation of persons and goods. Tom Kelly (2014), former secretary of the US Department of Political-Military Affairs, argues that, nowadays, the concept of maritime security includes both public and private activities, sometimes with diametrically opposed interests. According to him, the maritime domain faces threats from nation-state actions, terrorism, unregulated fishing, environmental disruption, mass migration, and organised criminal activity – e.g. smuggling and piracy. Rahman (2009), when considering the policy implications of the concept of maritime security for New Zealand, considers that the practical policy and operational responses by States concerning maritime security should be based on an integrated and multidisciplinary perspective along the lines suggested by the IWCO Report (Rahman 2009: 30).

    The IWCO Report (1998), already mentioned at the beginning of this section, goes far beyond the usual official approaches to maritime issues by emphasising the need for a critical, collective, integrated and multidisciplinary perspective, centred on North-South dialogue, aimed at ‘making a critical evaluation of the state and trends of ocean affairs’ that results in ‘true international cooperation in the preservation of the oceans as an integrated ecosystem’ (IWCO 1998: 11). Among its various contributions, IWCO expressed many concerns regarding ocean governance and the question of equity and peace and security, highlighting the need for peaceful uses of the ocean space. Furthermore, according to the Report, the notion of ‘peaceful uses of the oceans’ not only incorporates the idea ‘that all peoples should benefit from their use,’ but also recognises ‘peace as being more than the absence of war,’ which extends the notion of peace ‘to include the idea of an equitable public order that governs all human activity’, and the idea that ‘the opposite of peace is not war but injustice’ (IWCO 1998: 35).

    What is worth noting from this notion of the peaceful uses of the oceans is that it relies on a critical agenda derived from peace studies, where the concept of peace is problematised beyond the traditional vision of peace as the mere absence of war – as predominates in strategic studies and conventional IR debates on security. From the alternative viewpoint of peace studies, peace should be multidimensionally defined, including a positive dimension that associates the concept of peace with the idea of social justice (Oliveira 2017: 149). Thus, the translation of this positive concept of peace into the ocean space presents normative and practical challenges to national governments, international organisations, and civil society that cannot be answered by conventional approaches to maritime security and should be redesigned in line with the expanding trend that has marked the contemporary ocean governance agenda.

    In this respect, the Somali piracy case is also instructive. Going beyond the predominantly military and legal focus of the fight against piracy, some authors have emphasised the need for a more positive, multidimensional and comprehensive perspective, concerned with transforming the social contradictions and unjust structures in the distribution of resources and power that lie at the deepest roots of piracy and other forms of crime at sea (Oliveira 2012, 2013a). From this viewpoint, more lasting and sustainable solutions to the problem of piracy cannot be achieved merely by repressing superficial manifestations of private violence at sea (although such measures are also necessary and relevant). In line with IWCO’s claims for a positive perspective of peace in the ocean space, the meaning of ‘peaceful uses of the ocean’ embodies a transformative concern for social justice and equity in the distribution of access to marine resources that depends on the concrete needs of different people and social groups, especially the most vulnerable ones.

    Such an assessment cannot be properly made by navies and great naval powers, which tend to filter such an assessment according to their own interests and objectives of defence. This is made at the expense of a genuine look at the concrete and micro-scale needs, which affect the everyday life of persons and groups that live on the margins of the dominant constructs of security. These new trends in approaching the peace and security dimension of ocean governance differ considerably from the classical image derived from strategic and realist approaches centred on warfare and the interests of great powers, as well as from the traditional idea of good order at sea. As Oliveira suggests, it is ‘time for a critical turn in the debate’ to enable questioning ‘the order at sea and the way such order organizes social and power relations in maritime contexts’ (Oliveira 2012: 55). This critical vision involves, for example, calling for more equitable access to the benefits of the oceans, exposing the ‘impact of maritime degradation on poverty and vice versa,’ claiming ‘the demilitarisation of the ocean,’ and investigating ‘how maritime problems affect the everyday lives of vulnerable people living in coastal communities dependent on fisheries or other maritime resources’ (Oliveira 2012: 57).

    Some authors add to this debate the idea that maritime security cannot be reduced to an objective definition, since it depends on the intersubjective perspectives of those who construct maritime issues as security problems (Pinto 2015: V; Oliveira 2018a, 2018b). From this perspective, maritime security can be described as socially constructed processes in the form of securitisation, as theorised by the so-called Copenhagen School (Wӕver 1995; Buzan, Wӕver and Wilde 1998). Thus, maritime security’s objects of reference are not restricted to States but can be extended to individuals and human communities; and maritime threats are not limited to the military sector but can be extended to political, economic, societal, and environmental sectors. These considerations suggest that maritime security is a social construct and depends on the way securitising actors (relevant agents speaking from positions of power) construct narratives about what is considered a threat and to what extent these narratives justify extraordinary responses. Oliveira (2018a, 2018b) relies on this securitisation framework to show how the social construction of Somali piracy as an international security problem was decisive for justifying the legal arrangement and the emergency and exceptional measures that shaped the international mobilisation against piracy from the mid-2000s onwards.

    Pinto (2015: 6) relies on Ken Booth’s critical security studies agenda (2005) by arguing that the critical reconceptualisation of security places people – rather than States – in the epicentre of security. Within this critical security framework, one should ask, as Rhonda Powell (2012: 5) suggests, the following critical questions when considering maritime security: (a) Security for whom (an agent)?; (b) Security of what (a value or interest)?; (c) Security against what (a threat or risk)?; and (d) Security by whom (a provider of protection)?

    Despite these critical engagements and the broad and complex developments in the political and academic agenda on maritime security and ocean governance, some scholars remain committed to the more strategic idea of ‘good order at sea.’ Geoffrey Till (2009), for instance, continues to use this notion as the organising concept to address the sources of ‘disorder’ to what he considers the four main attributes of the ocean: its resources; its utility as a means of transportation and trade; its importance as a means of exchanging information; and its uses as a source of power and dominion (Till 2009: 286). Although recognising the legal and practical developments that led to the shift of navies to constabulary functions and the complementarity between military and civilian efforts (to face the challenges at sea throughout the last decade of the 20th century and the early decades of the 21st century), he avoids an emphasis on the concepts of maritime security and ocean governance, remaining loyal to the traditional idea of ‘maintaining good order at sea.’ As Bekkevold and Till (2016) argue in the conclusion of their collaborative work, ‘international order at sea’ remains a relevant concept, for it ‘is the consequence of how countries and the international community at large make use of the oceans as a medium for dominion and strategic manoeuvre, as a stock resource, and as a medium of transportation in an orderly, legal, and sustainable manner’ (Bekkevold and Till 2016: 307). Despite the mention of legal and sustainable terms at the end of the statement, the fact is that the way these authors conceive the notion of order at sea privileges the ‘highly intertwined’ feature of this concept ‘with geo-political and geo-economic developments’ (Bekkevold and Till 2016: 307). As a result, even when considering aspects such as constabulary duties, non-traditional security challenges, and collective efforts to cooperate at sea, these aspects are filtered by and subordinated to the imperatives of strategic dominion, great power rivalry, and the access and denial to maritime spaces ‘for traditional warfighting as well as for the ability to undertake maritime security missions’ (Bekkevold and Till 2016: 308).

    Despite Bekkevold’s and Till’s considerations, we argue that the concept of ocean governance goes beyond the traditional notion of maintenance of good order at sea and encompasses, as noted in the several perspectives analysed above, a range of processes, dynamics, actors, and normative commitments that cannot be captured by the predominantly strategic-realist and state-centric framework that underlies order at sea discourses. Secondly, ocean governance is broader than maritime security and does not replace this last concept. Ocean governance encompasses maritime security along with a wider range of issues. Maritime security, from this perspective, is part of ocean governance, referring to the more security-orientated concerns that the concept of governance raises.

    Ocean governance beyond maritime security

    Beyond security concerns, there are challenges of a different character that matter for ocean governance today. As Anil Jai Singh (2022: 77) highlights, fish stocks around the world are being exhausted; global warming is being exacerbated by the melting of glaciers and the polar ice cap; ocean acidification is increasing; seabed mining effects on the fragile marine ecosystem are still not fully understood; the race for maritime dominance is leading to a contentious and conflictive maritime environment; climate change is causing a growing incidence of natural disasters, depriving many coastal communities of resources and livelihoods, as well as causing loss of life and property. These considerations indicate that ocean governance today faces a set of environmental challenges that increases the urgency of the concern for conservation and the responsible, balanced and just use of marine resources.

    Considering these other dimensions of ocean governance, which do not fit exactly in the maritime security framework, Natalie Klein and her collaborators note that, in addition to the security dimension of ocean governance, there is a range of informal lawmaking initiatives in progress committed to marine resources, research, and technology, as well as the marine environment, sea level rise, conservation measures, and sustainable development (Klein 2022). Nilüfer Oral and Tutku Bektaş (2022) argue, specifically concerning sea level rise, that although UNCLOS’s provisions define issues such as ‘baselines, maritime entitlements, and islands, it does not include climate change issues,’ which have profound impacts on those legal spatial demarcations. Thus, formal law is insufficient to address more recent concerns with global warming and its effects. Given this gap, informal lawmaking and the involvement of non-official agents in the production of ‘focused solutions’ to these pressing environmental problems are making important contributions to ocean governance (Oral and Bektaş 2022: 354).

    Ocean governance is therefore a broad and complex process which is influenced by multiple drivers and actors with different worldviews and goals (Haas et al. 2020: 253), and has emerged in the 21st century as an important part of the policy-oriented mainstream. Although it is not an easy task to summarise what is recognised by scholars and practitioners as a process of ocean governance, one may consider, following Degger et al. (2021), that ocean governance is ‘a multifaceted and complex process for marine environmental management, involving a wide range of stakeholders from numerous institutions and individuals with different interests, agendas, and sets of skills’ (Degger 2021: 1). Haas et al. (2020: 253) identify five drivers that can determine how ocean governance evolves, namely formal rules and institutions; evidence and knowledge-based decision-making; legitimacy of decision-making institutions; stakeholder engagement and participation; and empowerment of communities. Dionisio da Silva (2021) adds to this debate the complexity involved in most governance arrangements, by stressing that, in practice, governance is complicated by its fragmented frameworks: an ‘actor A analyses the situation collecting data, makes its agenda, and sets its skills; actor B does these steps on its own, and actor C does the same; and besides that, rarely, there is a similar methodology employed’ (Dionisio da Silva 2021: 2). Within this framework, levels of analysis can be local, regional, or global, the selection of stakeholders can be diverse, and the selected tools may be incompatible and difficult to integrate. Moreover, approaches and actors can be oriented towards safety or security perspectives to the detriment of other dimensions of ocean governance. This points to the need for action plans for integrated ocean governance, as one observer in the US National Maritime Intelligence-Integration Office (NMIIO) created to integrate data and generate knowledge about the maritime environment from multiple stakeholders (Dionisio da Silva 2021: 2). Moreover, the EU Integrated Maritime Policy (IMP) also describes ‘a holistic approach to EU sea-related policies’ aimed at coordinating ‘its wide range of interlinked activities related to oceans, seas and coasts’ (European Union 2007, 2024).

    Beyond these problem-solving and policy-oriented concerns, the debate on ocean governance has been impacted recently by a more radical critique that questions the ontologies of territories – zones, areas, sectors, borders, boundaries, limits – that generally guide the way policymakers and scholars approach the demarcation of spaces at sea and its implications for exploitation or conservation (Peters 2020; Steinberg and Peters 2015). Rather than the ‘flat’ ontology that drives ocean management – based on static, bordered, linear and horizontal qualities of spatiality derived from land – authors engaged in this critique argue for a ‘wet’ ontology that emphasises the qualities that give meaning to the experience of place intrinsic to the ocean environment, such as liquidity, fluidity, flows, motion, verticality, and volume (Steinberg and Peters 2015).

    This is not merely an abstract discussion, and the shift from a ‘flat’ to a ‘wet’ ontology has important implications for ocean governance and the politics of the oceans. As Philip Steinberg and Kimberley Peters (2015: 248) argue, ‘the plane geometry of points, lines, and areas’ that have long grounded geography provides a ‘poor foundation’ for theorising the different materiality of the ocean, and, consequently, for defining regimes of control or dispute settlement within this space. From these authors’ perspective, legal efforts to territorialise and demarcate areas in the ocean space – an environment that is not just a surface, but above all a depth; that is not linear and stable, and involves volume and movement – disregard the distinctive phenomenology of the oceans. The negligence of these qualities, according to Steinberg and Peters (2015: 253), has posed major problems for ocean governance, whose efforts to establish ‘marine zonation’ based on ‘a terrestrial ontology of bounded zones’ – from the 15th century Treaty of Tordesillas to contemporary attempts to control pollution from offshore oil wells or demarcate sustainable fishing areas – do not take into account the complexities of the marine environment, and for this reason have been unable to go beyond the production of ‘legal fictions.’

    The discussion of Bear and Eden (2008) on fishing certification areas is used by Steinberg and Peters (2015) to exemplify the kind of difficulty posed by the flat ontology underlying conservation policies: although fishing certification tries to define levels of legal authority over certain spaces by limiting them in terms of ‘straight lines and 90º angles’ (Bear and Eden 2008: 488), these bounded areas fail ‘to capture the mobility of either the water or the fish’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 253). The ‘Area’ – as UNCLOS named ‘the seabed and ocean floor and subsoil thereof, beyond the limits of national jurisdiction’ –, is considered to be the ‘common heritage of mankind’ (UNCLOS 1982). This is another illustration of the flat ontology that underpins ocean governance. As Peters (2020) notes, the word ‘area’ is typically a ‘two-dimensional measure of surface’, and in general designates a bounded flat zone applied to define a ‘particular place, piece of land or country’ (Peters 2020: 4). This land-based designation neglects the fact that the governance of the Area, whether for economic exploitation or conservation, takes place in a three-dimensional space. Mineral extraction, in particular, ‘requires movements through the water column, and exploitation may result in vertical, spherical plumes of material that disturb surrounding habitats’ (Peters 2020: 4).

    Given the predominantly static, two-dimensional, area-based understandings of the ocean, Steinberg and Peters defend new directions of the debate on ocean governance, which consider the ‘wet’ ontology that gives meaning to the materiality of the marine environment. As they argue, the great challenge is to understand that disputes over space and resources in the ocean cannot be ‘fought on a planar level,’ as if they were related to the ‘security of flat, surface-level sea territory.’ In the ocean space, ‘contestation has depth’ and ‘the source of conflict is ever moving and impacted by the movement surrounding it (be it fish, oil, silt, or water molecules themselves)’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 253).

    Narratives on the sea, ocean governance, and IR theories

    As Steinberg notes (2001: 209), the land is not the only space where socio-political relations and their contradictions emerge, claims for social change arise, and better social arrangements in the future are imagined. As observed in this article, the ocean is also one of those spaces and has its own ‘different politics’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 260, original emphasis). Therefore, strengthening the vision of the maritime space requires a complex and multidimensional look at its problems and challenges, which leads to an equally complex and pluralist conception of ocean governance that is sensitive to its socio-political dimension.

    Some authors dedicated to ocean studies have already offered insights into the narratives and discourses constructed about ocean spatiality (Steinberg 2001; Hannigan 2016). From the broad panorama outlined here, however, one can expand these authors’ observations by identifying at least five major narratives (or images) that differ in how the ocean is perceived. These narratives result from different constructions of the sea and compete in their vision on how this space should be collectively managed – i.e. how the governance of this space should be organised. We name these five main narratives according to the following labels: maritime security, maritime economics, maritime borders, maritime conservation, and maritime justice.

    Maritime security

    The first grand narrative – maritime security – constructs the ocean as a space to be protected against threats, risks, and sources of disorder. This narrative has several nuances and images. The first, more traditional image, is defined by ‘sovereignty games’ (Hannigan 2016: 38) and constructs the sea as an empty space to be ‘commanded’ by the most powerful States. This image is rooted in the history of colonial powers from the 15th to the early 20th centuries, and the geostrategic imagery of classic thinkers such as Alfred T. Mahan (2003) and Julian S. Corbett (2012), with the key concept of ‘command of the sea.’ This picture has geopolitical competition and warfighting capabilities as its core themes. Two main aspects marked the emergence of this narrative: firstly, the idea that a great naval power could ensure the ‘command’ of the sea, with 19th-century Great Britain as a typical case study; secondly, the idea that strategic control of sea lines of communication (SLOC) played a fundamental role in the wealth of nations. As Mahan’s work (2003) emblematically illustrated, the interests of European naval powers had evolved in favour of expanding their influence far beyond the coastal margins of the State, and this expansion, made possible by the possession of powerful warships and merchant fleets, was directly linked to the wealth of these nations. In short, this narrative tended to see the oceans as a medium for dominion, strategic competition, and accumulation of wealth. Contemporary naval strategic thinking naturalises this narrative by assuming that it objectively portrays the sea as a ‘force-field’ intended for the exercise of States’ relative power against their competitors (Steinberg 2001: 35) – and its main protagonists are States and their military apparatuses.

    Even when this image is softened by the notion of ‘good order at sea’ (Bekkevold and Till 2016) or ‘public order at sea’ (McDougal and Burke 1985), as noted in the previous sections, one should not disregard that this notion is ‘highly intertwined’ with geopolitical commitments and is filtered by the imperatives of strategic dominion, great power rivalry, and warfighting calculations and capabilities. From this perspective, the ‘order at sea’ that really matters and is interesting to achieve is that which meets and serves the interests of the most powerful States. Given all these characteristics – power and interest-based calculations, state-centrism, rationalism, competition, and domain and defence strategic objectives –, this ocean narrative clearly fits the IR realist paradigm.

    But maritime security narratives have evolved beyond this IR realist paradigm in the last three decades, now pointing to alternative images and different governance frameworks. As seen in the previous sections, maritime security has assumed a normative dimension – i.e. an ethical dimension committed to equity and social justice – derived from the UNCLOS preamble and strengthened in the IWCO Report. This normative orientation, which suggests a more critical, multidimensional, human, and context-sensitive approach, has guided the debate on ocean governance to a set of peace and security concerns closer to the agenda of peace studies and critical security studies. The debate on ocean governance, from this perspective, experiences a critical turn (Oliveira 2012) that claims a closer look at the most diverse sources of insecurity and micro-scale needs affecting the everyday life of people dependent on the sea, especially those who are left out of the dominant constructs of security. Thus, instead of defining the State as the preferential object of reference of security, this debate expands the levels of analysis, the range of actors and the issues that affect peace and security, drawing attention primarily to populations and vulnerabilities that are generally neglected in dominant security narratives. Given all these characteristics – a normative orientation towards equity, emancipation, and social justice –, this image and its implications for ocean governance clearly fit the Critical IR Theory approach.

    As also examined in the previous sections, maritime security has experienced a social constructivist turn that looks at the oceans through the lens of securitisation theory. From this standpoint, maritime security is socially constructed, which means that threats and risks, the objects of reference of these sources of insecurity, as well as the measures justified to handle the situations defined as maritime security problems, are not fixed and objectively defined, but are discursively constructed within political speech acts of relevant actors who generally speak from a position of power (Pinto 2015: V; Oliveira 2018a, 2018b). From this perspective, the expansion of the maritime security agenda to multiple topics, actors and policy solutions involves the way ocean politics articulates particular situations – such as piracy; terrorism; hijacking; smuggling of arms and hazardous wastes; smuggling of migrants and other human trafficking by sea; illicit traffic in drugs; illegal fishing; etc. – according to the emergency and exceptional logic of security. This approach to maritime security and its implications for ocean governance result from a combination of conventional IR constructivism and the post-structuralist theory that has influenced the linguistic turn in the IR field since the 1990s.

    Maritime economics

    The second main narrative – maritime economics – constructs the sea as a reservoir of resources and a route for trade. This image has as protagonists not only States, but also private companies and other non-governmental actors that benefit from the exploitation of living and non-living resources and trade. Underlying all the narrative developed in the previous sections, there are numerous aspects related to the economic dimension of maritime governance, from the more security-based concerns associated with the maintenance of good order at sea, especially on strategic routes for global maritime trade, to efforts to territorialise exclusive economic zones and the continental shelf, as well as the definition of the ‘Area’ and the legal and political instruments and authorities necessary to organise and control the exploitation of resources in this space – considered a ‘common heritage of mankind.’

    Robert Keohane’s and Joseph Nye’s complex interdependence theory is perhaps the most visible and direct effort within the IR field to consider the oceans and their governance regimes. The application of the complex interdependence theoretical model to the analysis of the ocean space, chosen by Keohane and Nye as a typical case study in their book Power and Interdependence (1997), tried to demonstrate how the intensification of global interdependence was crucial to the formation of the political agenda and the regime change in ocean governance that occurred in the second half of the 20th century. This narrative tends to focus on cooperation, multilateralism, transnational activities, international organisations, and interdependence, as well as the complex interactions among State and non-state actors, although this shift beyond national jurisdiction does not escape from a state-centric approach that maintains the primordial relevance of State institutions and interests in the organisation of ocean politics. Given these characteristics, this maritime economic image and its implications for ocean governance are closer to the IR liberal paradigm, particularly the complex interdependence approach developed within the IR institutional neoliberal framework.

    Maritime borders

    The third narrative – maritime borders – combines elements of IR realist and liberal paradigms and develops in permanent tension between a territorialisation impulse (trying to define borders, limits, sovereign areas, national exclusive rights of use, and exploitation) and a de-territorialisation impetus (claiming the freedom of navigation and the universal right of use and exploitation). This is an old dispute which dates back to the ‘battle of the books’ inaugurated by Hugo Grotius’s Mare Liberum, written in defence of the freedom of the seas, and a series of works by other authors who critically engaged with Grotius in defence of countries’ rights over the sea (Steinberg 2001: 89-98; Haward and Vince 2008: 10), as well as the efforts in the 17th and 18th centuries to establish the roots of the doctrine of freedom of the seas and the foundations of the principle and concept of territorial sea (Haward and Vince 2008: 10; Lukaszuk 2018: 129-130).

    In the second half of the 20th century, UNCLOS legal provisions tried to solve this tension. On the one hand, the Law of the Sea fixed the limits and rules applicable to territorial seas, contiguous zones, exclusive economic zones, and the continental shelf – in line with a territorialisation impulse that contributes to ensure the rights of States to exclusive use and exploitation or the sovereign exercise of defence and power over certain areas. On the other hand, the Law of the Sea ensured to all States the freedom of movement and the common exploitation of resources in the commons – a status that applies to the high seas both in the water column and on the seabed in the so-called ‘Area.’ Recently, the territorialisation trend has taken on a new impetus, linked to a narrative of conservation and sustainable use of the ocean, as observed, for instance, in marine spatial planning initiatives (Ntona and Schröder 2020), and in demarcation of marine protected areas (Segi 2014). This last territorialisation trend will be discussed in the context of maritime conservation narratives in the next sub-section.

    Going beyond realist and liberal views that provide the dominant framework for the maritime border narrative, some authors look at this narrative from a post-modern, non-positivist critical perspective, seeking to question the images and the linguistic constructions that give meaning to ocean spaces and to the initiatives to territorialise the sea. As examined at the end of the previous section, these authors question the ontologies of territories – zones, areas, sectors, borders, boundaries, limits – that guide the way policymakers and scholars approach the demarcation of spaces at sea (Bear and Eden 2008; Steinberg and Peters 2015; Peters 2020). According to this critique, land-based ways of naming and demarcating spaces at sea have reproduced ‘a logic of territory-making, governance and control’ (Peters 2020: 4) which disregards the ocean’s distinctive politics that ‘emerge from its materiality as a space of fluidity, volume, emergence, depth, and liquidity’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 260).

    Moreover, this radical critique emphasises that these acts of territorialisation of the sea are not strictly technical procedures, but are always political acts, immersed in power calculations (Peters 2020; Segi 2014). As Peters argues, these ‘modes of demarcating space do not “belong” at sea,’ but reproduce a ‘landed logics of territory-making, governance and control’ that has become so naturalised in ocean governance discourses and policies, which are ‘rarely questioned’ (Peters 2020: 4). From this critical viewpoint, efforts to territorialise the sea ‘are not politically innocent’ (Peters 2020: 7), and even when justified on the basis of the common good, their effects can be exclusionary and favour powerful actors and their particular interests (Segi 2014). Given these considerations on discourse, meaning, and power, this radical critique of the maritime border’s image of ocean governance is close to the post-positivist perspective adopted by IR postmodern and post-structuralist approaches.

    Maritime conservation

    The broad panorama reconstructed in this article also shows that ocean governance has been guided by an image built around ocean conservation. The UN Conference on Environment and Development (Rio-92) gave special emphasis to the protection of the oceans and their sustainable use (Agenda 21 1992, chapter 17), inspiring reports and academic studies to engage with maritime conservation. The IWCO, for instance, devotes a large part of its Report to drawing attention to the ‘rapid deterioration’ and the ‘unsustainable levels of exploitation of resources’ in the maritime environment, and to claiming the need for an ‘effective ocean governance’ that embodies ‘an unequivocal commitment to safeguarding the health of the oceans and the productivity of ocean ecosystems’ (IWCO 1998: 20-21). Natalie Klein’s (2022) collective work on informal lawmaking shows that there are numerous ongoing initiatives involved in the production of norms, regimes, and measures aimed at conserving the marine environment. According to Klein and her colleagues, informal lawmaking has helped to fill the gaps in formal law and develop a legal infrastructure on ocean conservation in areas as diverse as marine protected areas, fishery certification, marine plastic pollution, global warming and sea level rise, deep seabed mining, etc. (Klein 2022).

    Within this maritime conservation narrative, the focus is on building the idea that the ocean is a unique ecosystem that is being threatened (Hannigan 2016: 40), and, therefore, needs transformative governance that relies on new norms, institutions and structures for conservation. It is a narrative closer to conventional IR constructivism in that it attempts to strike a middle ground between instrumentalised use of the sea (with emphasis on rationality and objectivity) and critical reflection on the impacts of those uses (with stress on the power of ideas and reflexive interpretation). From this perspective, it is possible to regulate human activity through formal and informal efforts to develop ideas, norms, and regimes that advance the legal infrastructure of the Law of the Sea (Klein 2022) and, thereby, construct new visions that enable preservation of the oceans for ‘our future’ (IWCO 1998).

    Within this picture, there is also a more critical vision that questions the most recent nexus between territorialisation and conservation in ocean governance, as seen in spatial planning initiatives and marine protected area demarcations (Segi 2014; Peters 2020; Ntona and Schröder 2020). This critique seeks to show that conservationist NGOs’, policymakers’, and scientists’ commitments to these initiatives have proved problematic in certain circumstances due to a series of reasons. Shio Segi (2014), for instance, criticises the neoliberal hegemonic ideology of development that underlies some initiatives to demarcate marine protected areas. Rather than committing themselves to the development of local populations by resolving inequalities and empowering small fishing communities, such demarcation initiatives use other criteria to self-evaluate themselves as success stories. The cases of marine protected areas analysed by Segi (2014: 571-573) show that success is evaluated more by the satisfaction of the neoliberal expectations of diving tourism and recreational fishing than by the fulfilment of the needs of small-scale fishers and their poor communities. It is a kind of emancipatory-reflexive critique that fits Critical IR Theory. As seen in the previous section, other authors who engaged in this critique took a more radical approach, inspired by a post-positivist perspective that seeks to expose the discourse-power relationship that is behind the use of marine spatial planning devices and the territorialisation of spaces at sea under the justification of creating marine protected areas (Peters 2020; Ntona and Schröder 2020). This kind of critique on ocean governance and its initiatives of territorialising the sea is close to IR postmodern and post-structuralist approaches.

    Maritime justice

    Many aspects presented and discussed in this article are guided by a normative commitment to justice, equity, and empowerment of actors and social groups who depend on the sea but are left out of the dominant narratives. In this image of the ocean, the main emphasis is on the transformation of instrumental reason that has manoeuvred ocean governance to serve the interests of powerful actors. The focus of this narrative is on the inequality, injustices, marginalisation, silences, and vulnerability of people and social groups who depend on the sea in their everyday lives, but whose right to a better and just life is undermined by the logics of power and economic greed of dominant actors and institutions. This narrative is practically transversal to the previous ones. Since it shares much of the critical perspectives already explored in the context of the narratives on maritime security, maritime economics, maritime borders, and maritime conservation discussed above, the aspects involved in maritime justice will not be repeated here. The crucial point, for the purpose of this discussion, is that given its normative orientation towards equity and justice, and its commitment to transforming social, political, and economic discourses, practices and structures in favour of the most vulnerable people and social groups who depend on the sea for their livelihoods, this narrative is close to the reflexivist and emancipatory concerns introduced in the IR field by critical theory, feminism, and post-colonialism.

    Conclusion

    This article has examined the multiple concepts, narratives, agents, and problems that form the images of the maritime environment and its governance. Based on the social constructivist perspective on the ocean defined in the first section, our objective was to understand the way these images and problems impact what is meant by ‘ocean governance’ and the way its policy responses are imagined. Moreover, we aimed to examine the extent to which the images guiding ocean governance can be considered through the lens of IR theories. With this in mind, the article has contributed not only to synthesise the debate on ocean governance, but also to connect this debate to the theoretical discussion in the IR field.

    Based on this analysis, we can draw two main conclusions. Firstly, when looking at the maritime space as an arena of socially constructed relations with its own distinctive politics, rather than a static physical surface, there is no reason to uncritically accept fixed orders and structures and take models of maritime governance for granted. If dominant traditional images have constructed the oceans as ‘empty’ spaces, as a material surface free from social relations to be explored as a transportation space, source of wealth, and platform to attack the land in the military adventures of the great powers – and, as a consequence, an environment to be commanded, dominated, controlled and pacified through the military strength of the great blue water navies –, the alternative images guiding ocean governance in recent decades point to new visions. Within these new imaginaries, the peaceful use of the oceans, equity of access to their resources, and concerns about their uses in accordance with sustainable development open avenues for the construction of new orders of the sea that are more solidary, just, and socially committed. From this broader and multidimensional perspective, we identified five main narratives (images) that underpin ocean governance: maritime security, maritime economics, maritime borders, maritime conservation, and maritime justice.

    Secondly, we conclude that despite the absence of the sea in IR theorising, these five different narratives and their respective nuances, as well as the way they guide ocean governance, can be framed in the main theoretical debates of the IR discipline. By connecting these narratives with IR debates, the crucial point to note is that ocean governance has considerably broadened its range of concerns, shifting from a narrow focus traditionally based on instrumental, geostrategic, and state-centric security, and economic interests (closer to the orthodox realist and liberal IR paradigms). to more critical concerns (closer to a social constructivist perspective in its conventional or critical strands), which places the political and academic debate on ocean governance in line with the main post-positivist developments that expanded the IR field beyond its orthodox approaches.
