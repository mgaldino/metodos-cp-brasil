The Third Bank of the Sea: Maritime Traces of Constitutive Outside(r)s and International Ontopolitical Lines

A Terceira Margem do Mar: rastros marítimos de foras e forasteiros constitutivos e linhas ontopolíticas internacionais

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    In this article, we move into the water and engage with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s and international ontopolitical lines. Inspired by João Guimarães Rosa’s ‘The Third Bank of the River’ and by Jacques Derrida’s work, we offer a decolonial deconstructionist rethinking of the problem of the constitutive outside in relation to Atlantic traces of constitutive outsiders of the modern international world. Our main argument is that, negatively and ontopolitically constituted as constitutive outside(r)s of the modern international world, the outlawed pirate, the enslaved Black African and the savage Indian legitimized asymmetrical structures of relations, counterconcepts and grammars of violence. In other words, we argue that their individual bodies, histories and (counter-)archives provide Atlantic traces of a profoundly dehumanizing, abject, and racist form of world othering and ordering. In order to articulate its contribution, the article is structured in four sections. The first section engages with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s of the modern international world. The second section engages with the exceptional category of the outlawed pirate. The third section turns to the enslaved Black African and the Atlantic colour line. The fourth section questions the international ontopolitical lines and asymmetrical relations accompanying these exceptional categories of constitutive outside(r)s.

    Key words
    constitutive outside(r)s; Atlantic traces; international ontopolitical lines; decolonial deconstruction; outlawed pirate; enslaved Black African; modern international world

    Resumo

    Neste artigo, nós nos deslocamos em direção à água e (re)pensamos rastros de foras constitutivos e linhas ontopolíticas internacionais. Inspirados pela obra A terceira margem do rio, de João Guimarães Rosa, e pelo trabalho de Jacques Derrida, oferecemos uma releitura desconstrucionista decolonial do problema do fora constitutivo em relação a rastros atlânticos de forasteiros constitutivos do mundo internacional moderno. Nosso argumento principal é o de que, constituídos negativa e ontopoliticamente como fora e forasteiros constitutivos do mundo internacional moderno, o pirata fora-da-lei, o negro africano escravizado e o índio selvagem legitimaram estruturas de relações, contra-conceitos e gramáticas de violência assimétricas. Em outras palavras, argumentamos que seus corpos, histórias e (contra)arquivos individuais oferecem rastros atlânticos de uma forma profundamente desumanizadora, abjeta e racista de construção do outro e do mundo. Para articular sua contribuição, estruturamos o artigo em quatro seções. A primeira trata dos rastros marítimos de foras e forasteiros constitutivos do mundo internacional moderno. A segunda trata da categoria excepcional do pirata fora-da-lei. A terceira aborda o negro africano escravizado e a linha de cor do Atlântico. A quarta questiona as linhas ontopolíticas internacionais e as relações assimétricas que acompanham essas categorias excepcionais de fora e forasteiros constitutivos.

    Palavras-chave
    foras e forasteiros constitutivos; rastros atlânticos; linhas ontopolíticas internacionais; desconstrução decolonial; pirata fora-da-lei; negro africano escravizado; mundo internacional moderno

    Introduction

    In ‘A Terceira Margem do Rio,’ João Guimarães Rosa (1988) writes a story of estrangement and uncanniness. It is a life-long story narrated by a (once) boy who watches his father leaving their home to enter a canoe and go into the river, from where and when he would never return. As the (aging) boy tells the reader, his father never went anywhere. He had never canoed to the other bank of the river, nor to any other, including the (growing-old) narrator’s. The father never returned to his bank of the river. He just lived ‘the invention’ of living and being-with-in the canoe and the space-time of the river. Never going to an-other bank, nor leaving the river or the canoe, it was the uncanniness of this being-with-in the river of the father that appalled people. For no known reason, the father never returned to land. Thus, the becoming river of the father only left traces of the uncanny and the unknown; that is, traces of a constitutive outside(r) marked and marking an aporetic and elusive line between life and death, presence and absence. Never arriving, although having already left, the father left traces of a characterless line that ever since haunted those moving waters, but not as much as that ever-aging boy. The becoming river of the father was indissociable from the becoming (life and death) of the son. As a constitutive outsider, the externality of the father was constitutive of the son and his internality, subjectivity, being and becoming. It was outside(r), so not really and completely present in itself. But it was constitutive, thus not entirely other, external or absent. Somehow, aporetically and elusively, the outside(r) was (also) inside(r).

    This article moves into the water and engages with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s. Inspired by Jacques Derrida (2016) and his deconstruction of the metaphysics of presence, which involves problematizing the ‘ontological expectation’ of the ‘“is”’ (Butler 2016: x) while overturning and displacing logocentric dualistic constructions (Derrida 1981: 41-43), such as the binary oppositions of presence/absence, inside/outside, internality/externality, and identity/difference, we offer here a ‘decolonial deconstructionist’ (Yamato 2021; Chipato and Chandler 2024) rethinking of ‘the power of exteriority as constitutive of interiority’ (Derrida 2016: 341). In other words, we offer here a rethinking of the problem of the constitutive outside. As Jenny Edkins (1999: 15) has commented, ‘the constitutive outside’ in Derrida works as an aporetic supplement that is both ‘necessary’ and ‘too much.’ In this regard, borrowing the ‘dangerous supplement’ from Derrida (2016: 153), Peter Fitzpatrick (1991: 1) explains that the supplement not only complements and completes ‘that which is supplemented,’ but also persistently resists assimilation. Thus, as an ever-elusive constitutive exterior that ‘remains outside, challenging the completeness and the adequacy of that which is within,’ the supplement is always already ‘dangerous’ (Fitzpatrick 1991: 1-2). Moreover, as Edkins (1999: 15) suggests, the constitutive outside in Derrida may be read in articulation to Michel Foucault’s conception of the constitution of the subject and the social ‘through processes of exclusion.’ Indeed, as Fitzpatrick (2001, 2011) has shown, this articulation may be critically deployed to rethink the problem of grounding modern (inter)national law and society. Rereading Foucault’s ‘expansive idea of racism,’ he has suggested how a ‘negative universal reference’ has foundationally worked to (auto)authorize and (auto)legitimize the groundless grounds of modern (inter)national law and society, negatively enacting a cohering and grounding without the affirmation of ‘an essentially positive and ultimately delimited content’ (Fitzpatrick 2013: 47-48). From a different route, but again referring to a possible ‘comparison’ with Foucault, Edkins (1999: 71) borrows Henry Staten’s notion of the ‘constitutive outside’ to further comment on Derrida’s conception of the relation between inside, outside and the supplement. In her words, the constitutive outside (re)articulates ‘the outside as necessary for the constitution of the essence’ (Edkins 1999: 71). As Staten (1984: 16) puts it himself, Derrida ‘takes the outside to be necessary to the constitution of a phenomenon in its as-such, a condition of the possibility of the “inside.”’ The conception of constitution involved here implies that ‘X is constituted by non-X,’ where the former means ‘essence or self-identity’ while the latter means ‘that which functions as the “outside”, or limit, to the positive assertion of this self-identity’ (Staten 1984: 17). Correlatedly, as he reminds us, Derrida insistently denounced that ‘the characteristic move of metaphysics is to set up a binary opposition and then hierarchically subordinate one term of the opposition to the other’ (Staten 1984: 17): X/non-X.

    In a footnote of her ‘Can the Subaltern Speak?,’ Gayatri Spivak (2012: 85n54) argues that, in a certain way, ‘the critique of imperialism is deconstruction as such.’ She is suggesting how deconstruction involves an insistent critique of the general economy of violence that conditions the possibility (and impossibility) of the restricted epistemic violence of imperialism (Spivak 2012: 85). In this context, while commenting on some aspects of Derrida’s work that are of ‘long-term use to those outside the First World’ (Spivak 2012: 101), Spivak (2012:102) explains that the point of deconstruction’s critique of imperialism is ‘how to prevent the ethnocentric Subject from establishing itself [him or herself] by selectively defining an Other.’ In other words, deconstruction offers ‘a critique of European ethnocentrism in the constitution of the Other’ (Spivak 2012: 106). For her, then, the long-term use of deconstruction is due to its insistent critique of the logocentric ‘mechanics of the constitution of the Other’ (Spivak 2012: 109). In this article, we follow Spivak and Derrida, proposing a decolonial deconstructionist (re)engagement with the constitutive outside and the constitution of the other, that is, the outsider. Hence, our concern with both the constitutive outside and the constitutive outsider: constitutive outside(r)s. However, we do so in a particular way, within a specific (inter)disciplinary context, positioning our intervention in relation to what R. B. J. Walker (2010: 10; see also 2016a, 2016b, 2018) has named as the ‘modern international,’ that is, the particular formation of the modern world composed in principle of ‘four parts, with three zones of inclusion and exclusion’ (Walker 2010: 143). The four parts include the ‘modern individual subject,’ the ‘modern sovereign state,’ the ‘modern system/society of states,’ and ‘the spatiotemporal zone of absolute negation that came to be seen as the point of origin of all modern possibilities/impossibilities’ (Walker 2010: 143). The three zones of inclusion and exclusion, or ‘great fracture’ zones, are those mediating and (re)negotiating the (de)formation of those parts: one, in-between the modern individual and the modern state; the other, in-between the modern state and the modern system of states; and the last, in-between ‘those who are included in modernity and/or the modern state system, and those who are not’ (Walker 2010: 144). Most importantly for us here are that ‘spatiotemporal zone of absolute negation’ (fourth part) and its accompanying zone of great fracture. For both speak directly to the constitutive outside of the modern international world. As ‘a structure of inclusions and exclusions,’ and much like the modern subject and the sovereign state, ‘the modern international must itself have a constitutive outside’ (Walker 2010: 10). As Walker (2010: 23) suggests, this constitutive outside works as ‘a condition of the very possibility of having a modern political imagination,’ thus ‘enabling forms of political life organized as a spatial structure of insides and outsides.’ Moreover, if, on the one hand, the constitutive outside of the modern international may be conceived ontopolitically (Marchart 2007), as Walker (2010) often does, then, on the other hand, it may also be identified more specifically, in more historically instantiated, ontic (Marchart 2007) terms. As Walker (2010: 24) observes, the modern international’s ‘constitutive outsides’ (now in the plural) have included (at least) what moderns call ‘nature,’ ‘theology and/or metaphysics’ and ‘any other ways of being human except that affirmed by the potential autonomy of modern citizen-subjects.’ In this article, we are particularly interested in the last one, that is, in the instantiation of the constitutive outside through the construction of constitutive outsiders of the modern international world.

    However, before continuing, it seems important to ponder, with Walker (2010: 144, emphasis added), that the constitutive outside here is ‘[o]utside the insider’s outside’, that is, ‘it is an outside that is internal to a specific account of insides and outsides.’ In Kantian terms, this means that ‘the outsides of modernity are always doubled’ (Walker 2010: 145). In this article, then, we adopt deconstruction’s posture of immanent critique, moving from within the insider’s (the norm’s and/or centre’s) accounts of insides and outsides, while aiming to overturn and displace its conceptual order (Derrida 1981, 1988). For this purpose, we engage with traces of elusive lines marking inclusionary and exclusionary constructions of people and individuals whose (alleged) nature and existence have been instrumental in negatively constituting and legitimizing the modern (Christian-European-Civilized-Western) international world and its (auto)authorized telluric forms of life. More specifically, we follow the traces of two constitutive outsiders of the modern international, two constructions of (in)humanity, at the Atlantic Sea: the outlawed pirate and the enslaved black person. Each poses its own set of questions to common assumptions and narratives about the modern international political order and its sovereign subjectivities (of the national individual, the nation-state, and the system of nation-states), including the assumption that modern international forms of life and political association are (and must be) anchored and based on land. However, both are only partly understood when seen from a modern, telluric starting point. Marking with-in themselves not only separations, but also connections and articulations, these exceptional forms of life – the piratical and the enslaved – are indissociable from different maritime spaces. Consequently, they bring with-in themselves traces of otherness that became constitutive of being outside(r)s of modern international’s humanity, thus impersonating those elusive lines that, drawn and reinforced at the Atlantic Ocean,1 legitimized the formation of the modern international world through the discrimination between those who could (more or less (in)humanely) come and/or stay inside and those who had to be (more or less inhumanely) fought, proscribed, and/or abandoned outside.

    Thus, just like Alberto da Costa e Silva (2003) proposes, the Atlantic Sea is reimagined here as (if) a river that connects and articulates two banks – and not two shores independent from each other. The point we want to push forward here is that in thinking about the proximity of the shores, we are also instigated to think in terms of how the differentiation between these shores is elaborated. In so doing, those who are ignored are the ones on the third bank of this river: those who do not fit the patterns of belonging on either side, but whose existence, precisely for not fitting, is necessary for the definition of both sides and why they are so clear cut. For centuries, there were those outlawed, dehumanized and/or asymmetrically recognized, who were actively marginalized and forced to live (and die) beyond certain global lines drawn at the sea. As a constitutive outside space, the Atlantic has transformed the lives of these people whose bodies were (im)proper embodiments of those practices of differentiation. Like the canoe in Guimarães Rosa’s story, the vessels in which these people traversed the sea saw transformations in the peoples they were carrying: they definitely left to the journey, but have they arrived? The experience at sea created figures that needed this maritime space in order to be formed out of proscribed and de-humanized forms of life: outlawed pirates and enslaved black people. The dimension of the transition lies not only in the certainty that they left the ports, but in the understanding that the arrival saw different people than those who left. No longer people, rather pirates and slaves. Like the father in Guimarães Rosa’s story, who is transformed while being-with-in the river, these were people that never arrived at the other bank of the (maritime) river as they once were. However, Guimarães Rosa’s tale is not about separation or disconnection, but rather about hauntology, the constitutive presence of an absence, and the aporetic internality of an externality. In Carl Schmitt’s (in)famous terms, it is about the elusive line between a norm and an exception (Schmitt 2005). Supplementarily, in the traditions attached with the diasporic experience of those kidnapped from Africa, there is also the understanding of normalcy built upon the embodiment of a-normalcy that they represented. We want to provoke a reading of these two in tandem. To do so is to open even more the question of ‘what is at sea?’ by adding the question: which sea? Displacing Schmitt, however, the insistence in this article is that such an elusive line takes place and haunts the third bank of the river; or, indeed, the third bank of the sea.

    This article is structured in four sections. The first section engages with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s of the modern international world, dedicating particular attention to the way in which the sea is logocentrically (Derrida 2016, 1981) constructed in Carl Schmitt’s The Nomos of the Earth (2003) and Land and Sea (2015), while taking Paul Gilroy’s Black Atlantic (1995) and Tiffany Lethabo King’s The Black Shoals (2019) and ‘New World Grammars’ (2020), among (few) others, as Schmitt’s ‘counterpoints’ (Said 2011).2 In other words, Gilroy, King and others serve us here to otherwise (King, Navarro and Smith 2020b) Schmitt and his (hi)stories about the ‘law of peoples’3 and the nomos of the earth. So, if Schmitt’s international political thought, and most particularly his The Nomos of the Earth may be retrospectively considered as ‘a “missing classic”’ (Odysseos and Petito 2007: 2) of International Relations (IR), then Gilroy, King and others work throughout this article as otherwising counterpoints, always already problematizing and putting in question Schmitt’s (Eurocentric) narratives, assumptions, and (nostalgic) teleologies.4 Thus, navigating through the Schmittian world-historical meditation and its counterpoints, we aim to displace international political thought, IR, and their common (telluric) assumptions and starting points. In displacing (Schmitt’s) international political thought, the second section dedicates its focus to the exceptional category of the outlawed pirate, internationally proscribed as the enemy of the human race (hostis generis humani). As such, we suggest, the outlawed pirate ontopolitically worked as a constitutive outside(r) of the modern international order of sovereign territorial states, while legitimizing the outer limits of humanity and the human. Engaging with this line of the in/human race, in the third section, then, we turn to another embodied form of constitutive outside(r), engaging with traces of the enslaved black person and the Atlantic colour line. Kidnapped in Africa and forced to cross the Atlantic Sea, the enslaved black person was condemned to live (and die) in the third bank of this necropolitical sea, enacting that global line of race in the very skin of her/his own dehumanized body. The fourth section questions the international ontopolitical lines and asymmetrical relations accompanying these exceptional categories of constitutive outside(r)s. Although in different terms, the enslaved black person and the outlawed pirate negatively and ontopolitically worked as a form of constitutive outside(r) of the (post)colonial modern international world. Marked by international ontopolitical lines and exposed to international asymmetrical relations, we argue, these constitutive outside(r)s were indissociable from a racist and imperial form of world othering and ordering.

    Maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s

    In The Nomos of the Earth, Schmitt (2003: 351-352) argues that a new nomos of the earth emerged after the great discoveries of land and sea by Europeans in the 16th century. In his institutionalist legal thinking, nomos is a land-bound law through which order is immediately expressed: it is ‘the unity of a spatial order (Ordnung) and the localization/orientation (Ortung) of a particular community’ (Ojakangas 2006: 152-153). For him, the new nomos of the earth was now global, for the first time in history, and had a fundamental Eurocentric structure based on the distinction and equilibrium between land and sea. Indeed, it involved a dual balance: one, between land and sea; and other, between land powers (Schmitt 2003: 352-353). In Land and Sea, inspired by Ernst Kapp’s Hegelian ‘comprehensive thought-world,’ Schmitt retells a world (hi)story of human development and civilization, approvingly (re)turning to Kapp’s distinction of the three stages of development of ‘the empires of the water’ (Schmitt 2015: 20). For Schmitt, the fundamental elements (earth, water, air and fire) are collective signifiers which point to ‘different grand possibilities of human existence’ (Schmitt, 2015: 9-10), that is, to ‘wholly different worlds’ (Schmitt, 2015: 75). Referring most specifically to the worlds of water, Schmitt (2015: 20-21) follows Kapp’s distinction between the potamic, the thalassic, and the oceanic periods in world history. In the beginning, according to this (hi)story of the world, the potamic period was a period based on the river culture. The second, thalassic period of world history involved a culture of inland seas and of the sea basin of the Mediterranean. Finally, the last and ‘highest’ stage of world history was characterized by an oceanic culture which begun with the formidable achievements of the ‘discovery’ of America and the circumnavigation of the earth.

    In the Black Atlantic (1995), Paul Gilroy dismisses the claims of modernity articulated in land in Europe in order to argue that there is a different form of belonging that involves interconnected geographies thought more in terms of hybrid, diasporic movements, circuits and interconnections than in terms of ‘natural’ identities, fixities and stasis. Moreover, in counterpoint to Schmitt’s story, Gilroy (1995) does not engage with grand narratives or patterns of systematization of world (hi)story. In articulating the idea of counterculture, he proposes to identify a form of belonging that is not constrained in terms of sovereign lines, but rather ideas of belonging that are derivative of a process so vile and violent that defined the contours (the traces and the possibility of trajectories) of an ocean. In his account, each experience of the Middle Passage is, in itself, indescribable. Therefore, the possibility of articulating a cohesion out of this experience of necropower (Mbembe 2003) is perhaps only achievable if articulated through trauma. Consequently, personal experience speaks to the indiscernibility of a cohesive collective. A process that occurs together with the intense dynamic of othering percolated by a society still in debt with enslavement and the practices of racialization that insist on amalgamating such intense and discernible experiences of self in a series of collectivities that are understood through nationalism: Americans and British (specifically in the case of the book) that are African.

    For Schmitt (2003, 2015), the world (hi)story of the water civilization and development involved revolutionary transformations in knowledge and associated technological innovations, which conditioned the (im)possibilities of ventures and adventures taking place from rivers to inland seas to oceans to the circumnavigation of the globe. For instance, the perfection of the modern form of the compass was a crucial ‘nautical achievement’ (Schmitt 2015: 22), as the new ‘shipbuilding technology’ developed by the Dutch from the mid fifteenth century to the late sixteenth century (Schmitt 2015: 24). Mercator’s world map and projection, of course, were part and product of this new oceanic period of the (hi)story of the water world. As Schmitt proudly concludes, ‘[a]ll western and middle European peoples had their part in the collective achievement, which lay in the discovery of a new earth and which led to European rule over the world’ (Schmitt 2015: 33). This included particular individuals, such as Columbus, who greatly contributed to the ‘discovery’ of America with his ‘thinking’ and ‘the force of [his] knowledge’ (Schmitt 2015: 33). Crucially, for Schmitt, it is in this context, in the epoch of the ‘discovery’ of America and the circumnavigation of the earth from the late fifteenth century, that ‘the deepest and most consequential alteration in the whole of world history’ takes place, as ‘the first authentic spatial revolution in the full, earth- and world-encompassing sense of the term’ (Schmitt 2015: 55). According to him, not only the whole picture of our planet and the whole astronomical conception of the entire universe significantly changed, but it was the first time in world (hi)story that ‘the human held the whole terrestrial orb like a ball in its hand’ (Schmitt 2015: 55). The earth was now scientifically conceived as a sphere moving around the sun within an ever-expanding cosmos, which was to become associated with the revolutionary conception of ‘an infinite, empty space’ (Schmitt 2015: 55).

    Tiffany Lethabo King (2020: 77) begins her ‘New World Grammars’ reminding the event that took place on the night of June 24, 2015, at Christopher Columbus Park in Boston, USA, when ‘the statue of the itinerant sailor was doused in red paint.’ Columbus’ statue was covered in red, ‘simulating blood,’ and marked by a particular political, and protesting signature: ‘“BLACK-LIVES-MATTER”’ (King 2020: 77). As King (2020: 78) critically observes, the general reaction to and commentaries about the painting event ‘evinced a certain kind of disconnect and inability to make the relationship between Black death, Black rage, and Christopher Columbus.’ After all, did Columbus have anything to do with Black Africans in the USA? The general commonsense was that the ‘discoverer’ sailor had nothing to do with the matter of black lives in America. And for her, this inability to imagine, acknowledge and/or connect Columbus with the history of slavery and black lives in the Americas was ‘a form of “colonial unknowing”’ (King 2020: 78-79). Her critical point is that the privilege given to ‘settlers, settlement, and settler colonial relations’ has displaced and marginalized critical engagements with and problematizations of ‘genocide, slavery, and the violent project of making the human (humanism)’ (King 2020: 81). In other words, the focus given to ‘settlers and their relationship to land displaces the way the settler also becomes the conquistador/a (human) through Native genocide and Black dehumanization’ (King 2020: 82).

    In Gilroy (1995), the proposition of counterculture begs to argue that there is more to that scientific revolutionary transformation praised by Schmitt (2003, 2015) than technical achievements. For among other devasting effects, those technological advances came together with the racialization of a whole continent and the articulation of violent forms of hierarchy between different peoples and spaces in the world, as corroborated by King (2020) and Gómez (2008). The production of Christian-European modernity involved the creation of a non-modern Atlantic as its very condition of (im)possibility, that is, as modernity’s constitutive outside(r). In this regard, late historiographical works have finally acknowledged and (re)affirmed the ties that bounded wealth production and accumulation in Europe to African slavery in – and the slave trade to – the Americas. But scandalously, it was only in 1944, with Eric Williams’ Capitalism and Slavery, that this connection was first acknowledged.5 This acknowledgment was relevant because it allowed for a (re)reading of this historical process as a double-consciousness move: it was not that states were created before the production of the sea as a political space, but, instead, that they were contemporaneous processes. In other words, it was only that the privileged position of those making/writing ‘History’ in Europe had the imperial and (neo)colonial privilege of simply disregarding (or not even imagining, nor considering) the unspeakable experiences of those most violently and abjectly dehumanized by the middle passage.

    As Schmitt understands it, the ‘discovery’ of America by Columbus in 1492 and the circumnavigation of the globe from the late fifteenth century were part of a much wider and deepest spatial revolution, which involved ‘a change in the concepts of space encompassing all the levels and domains of human existence’ (Schmitt 2015: 57). Thanks to numerous, correlated scientific developments and breakthroughs, commonly associated with the proper names of Copernicus, Giordano Bruno, Galileo, Kepler, and Newton, among others, the ‘European humanity’ developed in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries an enormous temporal turn, imposing ‘upon all the domains of its creative spirit a new concept of space’ (Schmitt 2015: 57-58). It was expressed, for instance, within the Renaissance painting and its accompanying perspective which generated ‘an empty depth’ that enabled painters, now seeing otherwise, to paint humans and things ‘within a space’ (Schmitt 2015: 58). And according to Schmitt, this European spatial revolution had a profound significance in the macropolitical formation of the first global nomos of the earth:

    It is no overstatement to claim that all domains of life, all forms of existence, all kinds of human creative force, art, science, and technology partake in the new concept of space. The great changes in the geographic picture of the earth are now only a foregrounded aspect of the deep transformation, which is implied in a term as consequential as ‘spatial revolution’. That which is called the rational superiority of the European, that which has been termed the European spirit and ‘Occidental rationalism’, now advances irresistibly. It develops in the western and central European peoples, destroys the medieval forms of human community, builds new states, fleets, and armies, invents new machines, subjugates the non-European peoples, and places them before the dilemma of either adopting European civilization or of descending to the status of a mere colonial people. (Schmitt 2015: 59)

    In her groundbreaking work, The Black Shoals, King (2019: 25) proposes the shoal as an ‘expansive analytic of disruption and becoming’ that (re)articulates radical Black studies in order to disrupt Western humanist thought. She does it fiercely criticizing (white) settler colonialism, while defending ‘offshore formations,’ (re)articulations and otherwising relationalities between Black and Native studies. The conception of Black shoals, then, is thought as ‘a way of articulating a world-altering mode of existence’ (King 2019: 22). In other words, the project is ‘to reorder how we live with one another and the world,’ that is, ‘an otherwise world,’ as she puts it with Navarro and Smith (King, Navarro and Smith 2020b: 13) in their introductory chapter to the volume, Otherwise Worlds, they coedited (King, Navarro and Smith 2020a). King’s The Black Shoals (2019) is an extremely powerful – decolonial, disordering, and otherwise-ing – manifesto for such an otherwise relationality between Black and Native studies, which fiercely (re)positions itself against the (white) order and ordering of settler colonialism’s human, humanity, and humanist thought. Hence, it provides a radical critique to the naturalized imperial assumptions and presumptions articulated by the Christian-European humanism and Occidental rationalism nostalgically praised by Schmitt (2015, 2003).

    Humanism is commonly conceived as a philosophical worldview that displaces God and puts the human, that is, the rational, man with reason, at the centre of the world (King 2019: 15). As a deific substitute (Fitzpatrick 2001), the human, rational man is taken and naturalized as the (new) universal. King (2019: 16-17) severely criticizes this commonsensical and hegemonic narrative, strongly denouncing this form of ‘conquistador humanism’ and its accompanying ‘genocidal violence and brutal system of enslavement that relegated Niggers and Indios to the bottom ranks of the human order.’ For her, such a genocidal and brutal form of conquistador humanism not only ‘requires Black and Indigenous dehumanization,’ but survives to this day (King 2019: 16-17). Subverting the Schmittian narrative, King (2019: 16) offers a counter-narrative in which the Christian-European navigations of the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries were indissociable from the (re)construction of Africans and Amerindians ‘into the human others called Negroes and Indians.’ Quoting Sylvia Wynter’s work, King denounces that the idealized version of the human, the rational man, was being negatively invented and legitimized ‘through the construction of the sensuous and irrational Negro and Indian as “a category of otherness or of symbolic death”’ (Wynter 2006: 124 quoted in King 2019: 16). Displacing the 1492 origin story of Columbus’ ‘discovery’ of America, King (2019: 18) mobilizes Spillers’ and Wynter’s counter-archives of ‘Black conquest’, which, as an unexpected Black shoal formation, displaces ‘the traditional spatial and temporal frames’ conditioning the imagination and stories of conquest. Repositioning Blackness centre stage, these counter-archives spatially privilege ‘the shore of what is today’s Senegal,’ while temporally identifying 1441 with ‘the inaugural moments of conquest’ (King 2019: 18). In other words, ‘conquest begins before Columbus, with the landing of the Portuguese on the shores of western Africa’ (King 2019: 19). As she concludes about the counter-archive of Black conquest: ‘[t]he coast of West Africa works not so much to displace the horrors and legacies of Indigenous genocide in the Americas as to insert the invention of Blackness as a crucial line of demarcation for the thinking and writing of the human as Man’ (King 2019: 19). For King, then, the idealized rational Man constitutively demanded ‘the invention and negation of the Negro and Native in order to know her/his self’ (King 2020: 85). Conversely, the Negro and the Native worked as the conquistador rational Man’s constitutive outside(r)s.

    Hence, there seems to be an imperial centrism – that is, an ethnocentric logo-centrism (Derrida 2016) – characterizing Schmitt’s picture of the world and its accompanying politics of worldmaking. In Schmitt’s (hi)story, there seems to be a European, humanist, rationalist, optic- and land-centrism accompanying the new spatial imagination, drawing, circumnavigation, and appropriation of the world. Art being a historical index of spatial consciousness, the fact that the European Renaissance painters had their eyes altered and could now see otherwise, painting with perspective and generating ‘an empty depth,’ was a very meaningful signal of the deeper spatial revolution (Schmitt 2015: 58). Moreover, as Schmitt puts it in Land and Sea, and later develops at great length in his The Nomos of the Earth, all fundamental order is a spatial order and every great epoch has at its beginning ‘a great land-appropriation’ (Schmitt 2015: 59-60). In the particular context of (European) humanity’s spatial revolution, the epochal transformation involved an enormous world-political set of non-European land-appropriations. At first, in the fifteenth, sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the legitimation of such exceptional violence was enacted through the invocation of the mission to spread Christianity to non-Christian peoples, and later, in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, through ‘the mandate to spread European civilization to non-civilized peoples’ (Schmitt 2015: 61). Not surprisingly, then, Schmitt provocatively suggests that, instead of naming it ‘the Age of Discoveries,’ the world historical epoch of the circumnavigation of the globe should be more appropriately called ‘the Age of European land-appropriation’ (Schmitt 2015: 64). In his own (polemical) words, the Christian-Civilized-Europeans were ‘united in treating the non-European soil of the earth as colonial soil, i.e., as an object for their conquest and exploitation’ (Schmitt 2015: 64).

    In an effort to challenge the rationale of modernity, Gilroy’s counterculture questions origin’s stories, that is, narratives that assume an origin point to which everything is (genealogically and teleologically) related. Actually, Gilroy (1995: 16) explicitly says that the history of the Atlantic since the voyage of Columbus and his African pilot, Pedro Nino, ‘continually crisscrossed by the movement of black people - not only as commodities but engaged in various struggles towards emancipation, autonomy, and citizenship - provides a means to reexamine the problems of nationality, location, identity and historical memory.’ The way of (re)constituting this Atlantic is not by trying to counterfeit the wits and wills enacted in the constitutive violences that produced the Atlantic. The proposal of Gilroy’s countercultural approach to this space is to focus on the contacts that the Atlantic allowed for and the format privileged by it: cultural contact. As a consequence, politics and technological innovations developed new meanings: not for some abstraction of their objective meaning, but in the forms of the interpretations of their role in the world. For Gilroy, the signifier or metaphor that best symbolized and translated this countercultural counterpoint and counter-narrative was the ‘ship’:

    They [ships] were mobile elements that stood for the shifting spaces in between the fixed places that they connected. Accordingly they need to be thought of as cultural and political units rather than abstract embodiments of the triangular trade. They were something more – a means to conduct political dissent and possibly a distinct mode of cultural production. The ship provides a chance to explore the articulations between the discontinuous histories of England’s ports, its interfaces with the wider world. Ships also refer us back to the middle passage, to the half remembered micro-politics of the slave trade and its relationship to both industrialisation and modernisation. (Gilroy 1995: 16-17)

    The ship, here, works as a figure of speech capable of contributing to a defined and understandable space that we are used to frame in spatial terms. This is actually a more open and clear way of communicating than the reference of the state that is so pervasive, but that is also so elusive. Indeed, in addressing lived experiences, the ship, as a metaphor, addresses precisely the limits of the speech. In so doing, it challenges the assumptions and universals inscribed in and accounted for the discourses of (inter)national politics that include universalizing patterns, norms and subjects that never consider the experiences of those barely surviving with-in a slave-ship. In addressing the black lives not accounted for with-in the (inter)national politics of modern state sovereignty, Gilroy addresses the ‘politics of transfiguration’ in the following terms:

    This politics exists on a lower frequency where it is played, danced, and acted, as well as sung and sung about, because words, even words stretched by melisma and supplemented or mutated by the screams which still index the conspicuous power of the slave sublime, will never be enough to communicate its unsayable claims to truth. The willfully damaged signs which betray the resolutely utopian politics of transfiguration therefore partially transcend modernity, constructing both an imaginary anti-modern past and a postmodern yet-to-come. (Gilroy 1995, 37)

    As Didier Fassin and Veena Das (2021: 2, emphasis added) remind us, a word is always already ‘part of a political lexicon’. In Derrida’s words, every category or concept always already pertain to a conceptual order (Derrida 1988); and a signifier, to a language (Derrida 2016). Consequently, as King (2020) otherwising proposes, we must critically (re)turn to and (re)think world grammars. For her, for instance, it is through their ‘grammars of suffering’ that the Indian Native (Savage) and the Black African (Slave) share ‘urgent concerns,’ both being rendered ‘nonhuman flesh’ and thus existing ‘outside of the realm of the body and thus humanity’ (King 2020: 86). Inspired by the work of Frank B. Wilderson III (2010), King (2020: 86) comments on how the discourse of conquest enables the exceptional articulation and interlocution between the Savage’s genocidal grammar of suffering and the Slave’s grammar of suffering based on accumulation and fungibility. For King, following Wilderson III (2010), these grammars of suffering ‘find it difficult to assume narrative form within the lexicon made available by humanism,’ being ‘virtually impossible for the Native or the Black to speak’ through this colonial register of intelligibility, which is ‘predicated on their very death’ (King 2020: 86). In other words, with-in the (white humanist) conquistador’s language and grammar, paraphrasing Spivak (2012), both the Native and the Black cannot speak. Moreover, as King (2020: 89) concludes, ‘[t]he parasitic and genocidal violence required to unmake the Native and the Black in order for the white human to self-actualize is also hard to conceptualize within the discourse of an imported settler colonialism,’ as well as, we would add, within the kind of telluric, land-based international political thought articulated by a certain Schmitt (2003, 2007, 2015). Following Gilroy (1995) and King (2019, 2020), we move into the ocean, following maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s and striving to listen to a sea of silence6 haunting the third bank of the Atlantic River. In so doing, we aim to displace and otherwise (King, Navarro and Smith 2020b) IR and international political thought.

    Displacing (Schmitt’s) international political thought: hostis generis humani

    Schmitt (2003: 42) begins The Nomos of The Earth with a powerful line: ‘[i]n mythical language, the earth became known as the mother of law.’ According to his story, law and justice are bound to earth in three most fundamental ways. First, the fertile earth contains law and an inner measure of justice with-in herself. Second, the soil of the earth manifests law, as firm lines, definite divisions, and fixed boundaries, upon herself. Third, the solid ground of the earth sustains law and other human constructs, thus enabling the public manifestation of the order and orientation of human social life (Schmitt 2003: 42). In Heraclitus’ words, as mobilized by Schmitt (2003: 48), law is ‘nourished’ by earth. In logocentric, dichotomous terms (Derrida 2016, 1981), Schmitt contrasted these terrestrial corollaries to the sea. According to him, the sea knew ‘no such apparent unity of space and law, of order and orientation’ (Schmitt 2003: 42). Because of its (supposedly) intrinsic nature, the sea could not be engraved and delimitated by firm lines, and, hence, could not be bounded, divided, nor appropriated by mundane human labour and action. Elementally without ‘character,’7 the sea knew ‘no limits, no boundaries, no consecrated sites, no sacred orientations, no law, and no property’ (Schmitt 2003: 43). Thus, outside of the law and without character, the sea is constructed by Schmitt as the elementally different, asymmetrical counter-concept of that which mythically was the fundamental mother of law, the terrestrial earth. In its elemental nature, the sea could not be tamed, nor fixed, or anchored. In its characterlessness, and, hence, without arché, the sea was negatively constructed as the constitutive outside(r) of the modern international order of sovereign states (Walker 2010, 2016a, 2016b; Yamato 2019, 2020).

    However, this is not an uncontested construction of the sea. Philip Steinberg and Kimberley Peters (2015: 248-250), for instance, begin their exploration of oceanic thinking, fluid spaces, and wet ontologies with a critical reengagement with Schmitt’s dismissal of the sea in the very beginning of The Nomos of The Earth. For them, Schmitt’s dismissal is problematically articulated through a dichotomous ‘division of the planet’s surface between land and sea, in which the former is the privileged space of society,’ while the latter is perceived as ‘unnatural,’ ‘insubstantial’ and having ‘few (earthly) connections to humans and humanity’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 248-249). According to them, from Land and Sea (2015) to The Nomos of The Earth (2003), Schmitt has reduced the sea to ‘a space with neither a history nor a geography’ (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 249). For the dismissive Schmitt, waves were only waves, that is, they were immaterial nothings (Steinberg and Peters 2015: 249). In critically opposing Schmitt’s dismissal of the sea, Steinberg and Peters (2015: 247-248) turn to the sea’s ‘three-dimensional and turbulent materiality,’ in order to propose ‘a wet ontology’ that not only endorses ‘a world of flows, connections, liquidities, and becomings,’ but also fosters the imagination of ‘a world ever on the move.’ In so doing, Steinberg and Peters (2015: 260) question and displace Schmitt’s assumption about the unpolitical character of the sea, suggesting, alternatively, a wet ontology and ‘a different’ oceanic politics. In their more recent exploration of the ocean’s ‘excess,’ Peters and Steinberg (2019) suggest an oceanic (re)thinking that (re)articulates ‘a more-than-wet ontology.’ According to them, the sea and the ocean exceed their material liquidity, since their water evaporates, becoming air, and/or solidifies, becoming ice (Peters and Steinberg 2019). Interestingly, Peters and Steinberg (2019: 294-295) acknowledge that they have themselves reproduced the dichotomous imagination of land/sea, while criticizing Schmitt in their previous work (Steinberg and Peters 2015). Importantly, for them, the ocean in Schmitt ‘continues to be conceptualised as a closed-off space, a vast basin of salt water that, as a distinct geophysical entity, neither spills nor leaks into the starkly differentiated “landed” spaces to which it is set in strong opposition’ (Peters and Steinberg 2019: 295). As for others, Schmitt’s dichotomous framing of land/sea and its accompanying assumptions are highly problematic. Ontologically and phenomenologically, Peters and Steinberg (2019: 295) argue, land and sea not only meld in littoral spaces, but also ‘inshore, above ground, underground, in our senses, and as part of fantasy.’ Subverting Schmitt, the ocean (or hypersea) transforms and extends itself through a ‘more-than-wet’ ontology that is constitutive of ‘land, sea, and air, and the liminal spaces where they meet’; that is, the world is ‘an ocean in excess’ (Peters and Steinberg 2019: 297).

    But, of course, Peters and Steinberg’s ontological reimagination of the ocean and its more-than-wet excess is also not immune from critique. Although not entirely critical of their work, but also not simply opposing or dismissing Schmitt, Liam Campling and Alejandro Colás (2018, 2021), for instance, have offered an innovative and highly significant rereading of the ‘terraqueous predicament’ involving both global capitalism and the sea, in which they openly part ways with Peters and Steinberg’s work. On the one hand, positively acknowledging their innovative geographical work variously informed by new materialism, assemblage and actor-network theories, Campling and Colás (2021: 17; see, most especially, note 24, 326-327) recognize Peters and Steinberg’s contribution to those, like them, aiming to denaturalize the dichotomous separation between land and sea, while acknowledging their respective (telluric and maritime) ‘material particularities’. On the other hand, however, Campling and Colás (2021: 16-18; see, most especially, notes 24, 326-327, and 25, 327) part ways with Peters and Steinberg when ‘they ascribe to heterogeneous, non- and more-than-human ‘actants’ agentic powers’. In other words, despite agreeing that natural elements and forces are not merely passive objects of human activities, Campling and Colás (2021: 17) are quite sceptical of a (supposedly) post-humanist, de-politicized and de-historicized ‘“pure process” of de-territorialisation and re-territorialisation’ in which the potency of matter and natural forces would be unmediated by and through social power and ‘human practices and institutions of rule, knowledge, reproduction and exploitation’. So, Campling and Colás (2021: 17) not only recognize that all matter and natural forces (including the maritime and oceanic) matter, but, contrary to Steinberg and Peters, also insist that they are always already ‘enmeshed within enduring hierarchical relations where human agency – for good or ill – still claims the dominant role’.

    Informed by a Marxist, historical and geographical materialism, Campling and Colás (2021: 17) emphasize a metabolic relationship between society and nature, in which global capitalism and the sea are not only indissociable from each other but also inseparable from the (re)production of ‘terraqueous territorialities’. As they explain in a previous, homonymous work (Campling and Colás 2018: 777; see also endnote 1, 791), ‘terraqueous’ simply means that consisting of both land and water. More specifically, terraqueous describe a geo-physical condition of our planet earth, which ‘has been transformed and itself transforms certain organisations of space (i.e. territorialities) under capitalism’. According to them, capitalism has an ambiguous relationship with the sea, which metabolic and ambivalently (re)produces different terraqueous territorialities in different spatiotemporal contexts, but always involving a ‘distinctly capitalist articulation of sovereignty, territory and appropriation in the capture and coding of maritime space and how environmental conditions matter to these incursions of capital’ (Campling and Colás 2018: 777). Importantly for the purpose of our article, Campling and Colás (2018: 790) acknowledge a significant indebtedness to a certain ‘schema’ articulated by Schmitt in his The Nomos of the Earth. In concluding their article ‘Capitalism and the sea,’ they argue that Schmitt conceived world orders as being ‘sustained by the convergence of order and orientation in a sequence of political occupation, legal delimitation and economic appropriation’, a schema to which they gave a particular, ‘Marxist twist’, privileging ‘the interaction between sovereignty, territory and appropriation in the global ocean’ (Campling and Colás 2018: 790). So, terraqueous territorialities always emerge in correlation with a particular spatiotemporal interaction between the natural forces of the global ocean and the social forms of capitalism, as ‘both cause and effect of capital’s distinctive organisation of sovereignty, territory and appropriation at sea’ (Campling and Colás 2018: 790). Correlatedly, and equally telling, in their book Capitalism and the sea, Campling and Colás (2021: 70) (re)affirm that, despite its ‘mystical flights’ and ‘primordialism’, Schmitt’s conception of the nomos of the earth is a useful starting point for their ‘historical-geographical materialist account of capitalism and the sea in sustaining world orders’.8

    From a global historical perspective, Lauren Benton (2010) also criticizes the dualistic framing articulated by Schmitt in his The Nomos of The Earth. For her, however, the problem was not so much the land/sea dichotomy, but the sharp distinction between a European and an extra-European space, respectively imagined and contrasted as ‘a zone of law’ and ‘a zone of lawlessness’ (Benton 2010: 32, see also footnote 90). In her work on the multicentric and multilayered legal geographies of empire, Benton (2010: 282) problematizes Schmitt’s story of ‘the “bracketing” of a zone of war outside Europe as an essential element of the self-fashioning of Europe as a zone of peace and the seat of international norms,’ out of which a global system of nation-states would emerge in spatiotemporal contrast to imperial sovereignty and empires. But her critique of these dualistic framings in Schmitt, as in the case of Campling and Colás (2018, 2021), does not mean she did not consider other aspects of his work. Indeed, Benton (2010: 283) recognizes Schmitt’s engagement with sovereignty as ‘a more promising point of departure for the study of imperial sovereignty.’ As ‘an act simultaneously inside and outside the juridical order,’ the sovereign decision offers an aporetic legal anomaly that provides a promising point of departure for the study of what she has identified as ‘bare sovereignty’ (Benton 2010: 279-299). However, as Benton (2010: 284) critically observes, Schmitt did not have a particular interest in ‘the world outside Europe.’ Differentiating the rule from the rule of law, and critically opposing Agamben’s conception of the state of exception, Benton (2010: 289-290) argues that the latter had no externalities because it implied the circulation of law in all spaces of imperial sovereignty: ‘[l]ike the old joke about the mythic structural support for the world being turtles all the way down, the cultural context of norms is law all the way down.’ And, for Benton (2010: 292), ‘sovereignty implies the extension of law beyond the centre, not as a gradually dissipating force but as a set of relationships’ that, for instance, outsource and legitimize violence against ‘legal outsiders,’ such as enslaved and outlawed pirates. So, instead of assuming an Agambenian state of exception or a zone of lawlessness, Benton (2010: 298-299) suggests rethinking imperial sovereignty and the legal geographies of empire as multicentred and multilayered zones of legal variation and anomalous legal spaces, whose spatiotemporal reverberations find contemporary echoes, for instance, in Gaza and in Guantánamo Bay. In this sense, then, we suggest here, the enslaved Black African and the outlawed pirate enemy of humankind may be read as symptomatic traces of such imperial legal anomalies and legal outside(r)s.

    According to Schmitt, the ordering of the an-archical sea took place only with the emergence of the maritime nations and the great sea empires, and the(ir) accompanying ‘extensions of law to the space of the free sea’ (Schmitt 2003: 44). For Schmitt (2003: 44), such legal extensions, which he called ‘sea-appropriations,’ constituted ‘world-historical events of revolutionary significance.’ Correlatedly, the legal appropriation and ordering of the sea meant the outlawing of the pirate as an enemy of the human race (hostis generis humani), which also meant his becoming ‘ostracized and expelled, stripped of his rights, and made an outlaw by the rulers of the sea empires’ (Schmitt 2003: 44). And as Janice Thomson (1994: 140) explains in her international historical sociological study on sovereignty and the building of the modern state and its monopoly of extraterritorial violence, the international criminalization of the pirate involved the universalization of the metanorm against individual violence on the high seas, which meant that the areas of the earth not subject to sovereignty were then ‘converted from a state of nature into a realm of orderly interstate relations.’ But as Schmitt reminded, the world-historical events of ‘sea-appropriations became possible only at a later stage in the development of human means of power and human consciousness of space’ (Schmitt 2003: 44). Conversely, the great primeval acts in founding law – that is, appropriating land, founding cities, and establishing colonies – were ‘terrestrial orientations’ (Schmitt 2003: 44-45). Indeed, according to Schmitt (2003: 49), all pre-global orders were essentially terrestrial, and land-appropriation was the most fundamental source of all further law and ‘concrete order’ (Schmitt 2003: 48).9 Not surprisingly, then, as criticized by Steinberg and Peters (2015), in Schmitt’s international political thought, the world-historical grounding of concrete legal and political orders was elementally telluric.

    As Martti Koskenniemi (2004a) has reminded, Schmitt opposed both normativism and decisionism, conceptualizing law as a concrete order. In (re)constructing the (hi)story of the European order and its public law, which would have grounded the nomos of the earth from the late fifteenth to the late nineteenth century, Schmitt structurally (re)articulates his conceptions of the concrete order, the occupation of land, and statehood (Koskenniemi 2004a: 496). In other words, while land-appropriation is ‘the primeval act in founding law’ (Schmitt 2003: 45), the state is ‘the political form that regulated the occupation and administration of land inside and outside Europe’ (Koskenniemi 2004a: 496). Importantly, Schmitt not only insisted that the ‘concept of the state presupposes the concept of the political’ (Schmitt 2007: 19), but also that the political world is a ‘pluriverse’ (Schmitt 2007: 53). In Schmitt’s geopolitical ‘ontological framing’ (Chandler 2008: 47), the political world cannot be a universe. His conception of ‘the relations between law and politics at the international level’ (Chandler 2008: 45) (re)articulates the same Vattelian ontological framework favoured within Bull’s pluralist conception of modern international society (compare Schmitt 2003: 140–71; and Bull 1966). Indeed, Schmitt himself could well have written something along the lines of Bull’s affirmation that ‘[t]he society of states embraces all mankind and all the earth’ (Bull 2002: 62; see also Koskenniemi 2004a: 496).

    But, given the fundamental correlation between the conceptions of statehood, concrete order, and land occupation within Schmitt’s pluriversal political ontology, focusing on the terrestrial space of the earth and its telluric interstatist relations (including ‘war in form’) would be as if focusing on the Schmittian rule; and not on the – international political – exception (Yamato 2020: 12). So, taking Schmitt’s words on a philosophy of concrete life very seriously, the displacement of the political suggested here echoes his own political-theological insight that the ‘exception is more interesting than the rule’ (Schmitt 2005, 15). In so doing, we are aware that the sea played an important part in the historical geopolitical equilibrium since the Peace of Utrecht (Koskenniemi 2004a), being crucial to the management of inter-imperialist disputes and thus to ‘the bigger picture of global order between Great Powers’ (Chandler 2008: 46). Nonetheless, despite its historical, ontic significance, and those critiques from Benton (2010), Steinberg and Peters (2015) and Peters and Steinberg (2019), it is the sea’s – ontopolitical (Marchart 2007) – negativity that provokes us the most here. As Schmitt ontopolitically puts it, ‘[o]n the sea, there was no law’ (Schmitt 2003: 44). And as such an anomic, ontopolitical space, the sea counter-conceptually marked a constitutive outside of the modern international legal-political world articulated by Schmitt (Yamato 2020: 12–13; see also Yamato 2019; Walker 2010; 2016a; Lindahl 2013; Balibar 2016).10 Thus, as Yamato (2019: 224-225) has commented elsewhere, the sea played a pivotal role in negatively grounding Schmitt’s international ontopolitical imagination and story.

    It is important to remember at this juncture that the pirate, conceived and outlawed as the enemy of the human race, is an exceptional category of individual in the history and theory of international law and society,11 traditionally considered to be not only the first international outlaw but also the first international criminal to give rise to an – universal – jurisdiction (Yamato 2011, 2019, 2020; Heller-Roazen 2009; Simpson 2007; Policante 2015; Subramanian 2016). Most importantly, for the purposes of this article, is the counter-conceptual position of the pirate vis-à-vis the comprehensive spatiotemporal order of modern international law and society. In Schmitt’s own terms, it is the international counter-conceptual ‘exception’ that negatively (re)affirms the ‘rule’ (and rules) of the comprehensive unity of international law. Moreover, the pirate marked with-in itself – that is, as the international legal category of the outlaw enemy of humankind – not only the outer limits of the unity of international law, and humanity, but did so with-in a fundamental (and elemental) correlation and identification with the sea. This is structurally significant because, considering that until the late fifteenth century the Christian-European spatial order was essentially terrestrial in nature, thus lacking an oceanic culture and the global image of the earth as a whole, the sea represented the – ontic and ontological – ‘constitutive outside’ (Walker 2010: 10) of the comprehensive unity of that telluric pre-global spatial order (Schmitt 2003, 2007, 2015; see also Connery 2001). Correlatedly, as Yamato (2019: 225; see also 2020) has suggested elsewhere, the pirate would counter-conceptually exemplify the modern international’s constitutive outsider.

    As collective signifiers that marked two radically different worlds (Schmitt 2015: 75), land and sea also involved completely different concepts of enmity and war (Schmitt 2003: 353). On the one hand, at the basis of the Eurocentric telluric interstate order lied the symmetrical counter-conceptual enmity relations of the just enemy (justus hostis) and the associated duel-like conception of ‘war in form,’ which paradigmatically represented the detheologization of conflict and thus the rationalization, humanization, and bracketing of war (Schmitt 2003). Conceived by Schmitt as a conflict between ‘state-organized’ militaries, land war limited enmity to regular combatants and thus protected civilian non-combatants (Schmitt 2015: 74). The famous Schmittian conception of the political, based on the friend/enemy dualism, was anchored with-in this telluric imagination (Schmitt 2003, 2007, 2015). On the other hand, outside of the European terrestrial world, sea warfare was conceived as inherently having a propensity for absolute enmity and total war (Schmitt 1999; Minca and Rowan 2016: 161). In the European maritime world, both combatants and non-combatants could well be legitimately aimed at sea warfare (Schmitt 2015: 74). For Schmitt, as Mika Ojakangas (2006: 174) pointedly remarks, naval war was based on ‘the concept of hostis generis humani, the enemy of humankind,’ and, hence, identified with a conflict without those formal international rules responsible for the bracketing and humanization of war. The pirate was the exceptional example of such an absolute form of enmity and war (Schmitt 2003, 2011, 2015; Yamato 2019, 2020; Rech 2012; Heller-Roazen 2009, 2011). Hence, there was ‘an absolute dissymmetry’ (Ojakangas 2006: 179) between the concept of the enemy (justus hostis) and the pirate (hostis generis humani) within Schmitt’s conceptual order (Yamato 2019). Indissociable from the (high) sea, the pirate marked with-in itself the drawing of an international – a/symmetrical and un/political – line (Yamato 2019; Viriasova 2016).

    Traces of the enslaved black person and the Atlantic colour line

    Writing in the first quarter of the 21st century, from Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, a sovereign nation-state which was the last country to abolish slavery in the hemisphere, in 1888, we are certainly always already hauntologically conditioned and (in)formed by racist, necropolitical, colonial and capitalist tropes that internationally travelled by and continue to echo through the Black Atlantic. Moreover, not having being ourselves enslaved, and as non-African-descendant Brazilians (therefore not experiencing the same traces of 19th Century slavery) living and working in Rio, we do not intend nor pretend to speak (as if) we were able to occupy that space of unspeakable and enduring personal-familial-generational and structural experience of being a dehumanized and objectified subject – who was captured, kidnapped, traded, trafficked, forced to migrate, enslaved and forced to work (among many other abject forms of violence) – with-in the monstrous, necropolitical practices and institutions of the Atlantic slavery and slave trade. However, we understand that this is a sad Brazilian legacy that should be insistently problematized and discussed in society at large, so that we can engage with and try to grasp at least some understanding of its various meanings and effects and affects in Brazilian society. Our modest contribution to this discussion here is punctual: we aim to otherwise and bring some discomfort to an otherwise sanitizing, imperial discipline (David, Thakur and Vale 2020), which has been so successfully silent and silencing about slavery and the enslaved black person throughout its centenary existence and (re)articulations. So, writing from the former Brazilian Capital city, and the former extraterritorial Metropolis of the Portuguese colonial empire, we find ourselves in (an)other bank of the Atlantic river, believing to understand the magnitude of the debate, but forever challenged to (re)position ourselves before the echoes and traces of slavery and racism in Rio and Brazil, and the deafening silences and silencing with-in the discipline of IR. With that in mind, we want to acknowledge the countless traces of slavery always already conditioning our everyday lives, while critically engaging in this article with some traces of the enslaved black person and the ontopolitical line (forever) marking the Atlantic Sea with its racist colours.

    As Walker (2016a) has suggested, stories of the modern international world commonly mobilize two tropes. On the one hand, they often (re)articulate the spatial trope of friend/enemy; that is, the telluric, Schmittian trope of the political pluriverse and the detheologization of enmity and war. On the other hand, they also (re)affirm the temporal trope of civilized/uncivilized, assuming a teleological move which, inherent to a (supposedly) universal grammar of time, will lead to modern (Christian-European-Western) civilization. While the spatial trope speaks to those already internationally recognized as (minimally) civilized nations (Bull 2002; Schmitt 2003), the temporal trope speaks precisely to those entering this most select (and unequal) international society (Gong 1984; Keene 2002; Inayatullah and Blaney 2004). Thus, as Keene (2002) has critically observed, contemporary world order historically and structurally involves two patterns of global ordering: toleration and civilization. In this article, we do not disagree with Keene, nor with Walker, but, rather, supplement them (Derrida 2016; Yamato 2021). Displacing (Schmitt’s) international political thought, we engaged with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s. Internationally outlawed as the enemy of the human race, the pirate counter-conceptually marks with-in itself a third trope of the modern international world: the absolute trope of the enemy of the human race (Simpson 2007; Yamato 2019, 2020). Correlatedly, the pirate provides traces of an international politics of – in/humanizing – race and racism which also marked a fourth trope of the modern international world, that is, the trope of de-humanized forms of beings, which so violently and abjectly marked the racialized constructions of the savage Indian Native and the enslaved Black African (King 2019, 2020; Shilliam, 2020).

    More specifically, these are not only ontic, but also, and most foundationally, ontopolitical constructions (Marchart 2007) and relations (King, Navarro and Smith 2020), which, among other things, provide traces of the enslaved black person and the global colour line drawn in the Atlantic. Thus, we briefly (re)turn here to nineteenth century Atlantic slavery, accompanying Anievas, Manchanda and Shilliam’s (re)turning to Du Bois to critically rethink the problem of race and racism in the contemporary discipline of IR. As they put it:

    […] the utility of revisiting Du Bois’ work lies less in the claim that it provides an off-the-shelf theory of international relations and more in the fact that it signals a rich and venerable research agenda for the interrogation of international relations through an episteme that focuses upon the operation of race and racism. This episteme rests upon three key propositions. First, world order is constitutively – and not derivatively – structured, re-structured and contested along lines of race. Second, when world order is conceived in this way, the sites of analysis – geographical, social, economic and intellectual – shift; some sites might be unfamiliar to IR and common sights might be rendered unfamiliar. And third, as suggested by Du Bois, the proposition that the power that is exercised through the global colour line has woven through it modalities other than race, strictly speaking, especially those of capitalism, patriarchy and (inter-)state development and (neo-)imperial rule. (Anievas, Manchanda and Shilliam 2014: 7)

    Importantly, Du Bois’ work has contributed to a critical, post-colonial, and anti-racist episteme that radically reframes the discipline of IR through the prism of difference (Vitalis 2014; Inayatullah and Blaney 2004). In this article, we contribute to this critical reframing by (re)turning to the sea and engaging with maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s and discriminatory ‘lines of race’ – including those of the im/proper and in/human race(s). But, beyond the pirate (outlawed as the enemy of the human race), the lines of race accompanying the Atlantic slavery ontopolitically constituted a very peculiar form of de-humanization: the enslaved person was violently kidnaped, subjected and forced to cross the Atlantic Sea because of the – black – colour of her/his skin. According to Gómez (2008), this abject form of violence was cosmologically and macropolitically legitimized by a racist complex of assumptions that, for centuries (if not millennia), naturalized the (im)proper nature and place of peoples and things in the great chain of beings (and non-beings). In her de-humanized and animalized form of being, the enslaved black person was closer to property than nationality and citizenship.12 Thus, s/he – or, indeed, ‘it’ – had an exceptionally ambiguous character within the (post)colonial modern international world. S/he was not a national citizen, nor a foreigner, neither a criminal, nor an enemy. As such, the enslaved black person was forced to live (and die) in a shadowy third bank of the Atlantic River. Or, as put by King and her metaphor of the black shoal:

    In the fifteenth century, for those who did not enter (or leave from) the arched threshold of the Door of No Return, sandbanks and shoals were the last spots of sand that an African embarking the slave vessel stepped on before being carried into the hold. The shoal was also a place just off the coast of the archipelagos of the Caribbean and the ports of the British Carolinas where the enslaved Africans arrived in the New World and took their first wobbly steps on a small bar of sand, where they stumbled forward, slipped, or crashed and were made to stand before wading into a shorter stretch of water that would finally bring them to the shore—a place where an adult could hoist a child higher on a hip to get a better grasp before wading to shore; a place that caused unsteady sea legs to slip beneath themselves into a tumble and tangle of coffles and iron banging together. At the beginning or end of other planned voyages, the sandbars could also present another opportunity to kill the ship’s crew, seize the vessel, and head back to the sea in the other direction. (King 2019: 9)

    Thus, the shoal symbolizes in King’s account of (North-)American enslavement the transition between freedom and enslavement, just as much as it stands to a transition between land and sea. In this moment of transformation, she points even to the possibility of transforming enslavement into resistance, and even piracy (the possibility of seizing back the ship). At the maritime, Atlantic margins of the (white) European imperial order of nascent sovereign states, the bodies of pirates and slaves, as the shoal, could be transformed and re-signified while in-between the banks of that maritime river.

    Slavery that happened in modernity only makes sense when seen through the logic of difference. Be it the sign of sin, the condemned son, the ethnic other, the person that is not a citizen or – for that matter – a human being, the (i)legitimacy of slavery rests on the necessity of differentiation on these multiple grounds (Meltzer 1993; Davis 2006). The way of transforming this differentiation into a legal practice that results neither in annihilation nor in incorporation is the issue that allows for the understanding that though the enslaved black person was not seen as belonging to the modern international – for not fitting the prerequisites of modernity associated with national citizenship – s/he was an indispensable portion of the society for it was structured around her/his labour and the wealth it generated. The nineteenth century enslavement system is emblematic as for the meanings and dynamics of the inclusion and exclusion of the enslaved black person. Her/his labour exploitation, for instance, was core to a system in which wealth was created – and circulated – in plantation farms in Brazil. In this society, the enslaved black person was epitomized as exclusion (socially dead, to use the definition of Patterson (1982)), in a moment in time in which the liberal thinking and the idea of the self-made man is already so pervasive elsewhere in the (post)colonial modern international world (Tomich 2004).

    In this sense, then, the role of the enslaved black person is unique because the modern political space that has been constituted as a result of the use of her/his enslaved work was marked by a core differentiation, despite its attempts to emulate the modern European nation-state: the role the enslaved workforce played in the constitution of the independence (or freedom) of those spaces in relation to their colonial metropoles. The constitution of that freedom while maintaining the enslavement of the workforce is a core puzzle, one that does not correspond to the civilized (and civilizing) European model of state-building: the exclusion of difference through the practice of defining borders and categorically establishing those who belong and those who do not belong to the nation-state.13 The process of state-building that encompasses the enslaved black person is one that has to deal with the problem of difference which is internal to both the (post)colonial modern international world and its (post)colonial nation-states. Regarding the pervasiveness of the problem of difference in international society, Naeem Inayatullah and David Blaney suggest:

    The bounded political community constructs (and is constructed by) others both within and beyond its boundaries. The other lurks as a perpetual threat in the form of other states, foreign groups, imported goods, and alien ideas and as difference within, vitiating the presumed but rarely, if ever, achieved ‘sameness’. Internal others are managed or governed by some combination of hierarchy, eradication by assimilation or expulsion, and tolerance. External others are left to suffer or prosper according to their own means, interdicted at border crossings, balanced and deterred, or in appropriate cases, subjected to coercion or conquest. Our responses to others seem […] perpetually drawn toward Todorov’s equation: difference is translated as inferiority and thereby subjected to eradication. (Inayatullah and Blaney 2004: 39-40)

    By saying that the constitution of the (post)colonial nation-state in the Atlantic shores14 in which slavery played a central role has a close relation with the pervasive problem of difference, we are not so much concerned with the drawing of national borders as we are with slavery constituting a particular form of difference and enacting an exceptional form of differentiation. Inayatullah and Blaney (2004) are referring to an international society based on a traditional, modern European idea of the sovereign state, in which the nation and the state (supposedly) coincide. Our focus here is not on state building, but rather on the spacetime other than the territorial nation-state. In previous sections, we engaged with maritime traces of the enemy of the human race. In (re)turning to enslaved black person here, the stakes are other. Though present, fear, criminality and enmity are not the dominant criteria. The unaccounted phenomenon of slavery helps to (re)think hierarchical and a/symmetrical relations, since it epitomizes the difference between the process of European nation-state formation since early modernity, on the one hand, and the presence of enslaved black people within those (post)colonial states that would later challenge the national assumption about sameness, on the other hand. In such cases of economic dependency on the black enslaved labour, difference could not be tossed out of the state in its formation, for the state itself was dependent on that internal difference. The need for the enslaved black person’s labour did not allow for the eradication of her/his difference, even though it was ontopolitically translated into inferiority. Thus, accepting the idea of the eradication of difference as the sole form of state building is to legitimize the super-position of state and nation as two manifestations of the same corpus, and hence disregarding other forms of polity that incorporate inferiority in patterns of subservience under the legitimation of the state and the (idealized) sameness of its nation.

    It is important to reconstruct this antinomy of slavery and the modern state in order not only to locate the (im)proper spacetime of the enslaved black person in relation to the third bank of the Atlantic Sea, par excellence, but also to show how, differently from the more famous (or notorious) outlawed pirate, the enslaved black person has been systematically effaced from international political and legal narratives, despite the profundity of the material dependency of (post)colonial states (and their metropoles) upon his/her forced displacement and labour. Symptomatically, despite her/his enslaved labour being structurally indispensable to (post)colonial states and societies in the other bank of the Atlantic, the enslaved black person had no access to legal personhood or (much less) citizenship. Actively de-humanized and marginalized, the enslaved black person was included within the (post)colonial modern international world through an ontopolitical form of exclusion (King 2019, 2020; Agamben 1998; Marchart, 2007) which condemned her/him to live in the elusive, but not less violent and dehumanizing, third bank of the sea. In Achille Mbembe’s famous conceptual terms, the enslaved black person was structurally marked by a ‘necropolitics’ (Mbembe 2003). Indissociable from and ontopolitically marked by a global colour line drawn in the Atlantic Sea, the necropolitical body of the enslaved black person counter-conceptually – and (very) materially – defined the outer and inner limits of (white, European) humanity and the reach of (inter)national law. Perversely enacting the boundaries of the racist Atlantic world with-in her/his own skin, the enslaved black person continued to live (and die) in the third bank of that (post)colonial sea even if violently brought to and forced to work and produce wealth in (and from) the hinterlands of South America and Brazil.

    The enslaved black person exemplifies the violent transformation of human beings into the ‘perfect figure of a shadow’ (Mbembe 2003: 22). As Mbembe (2003: 21) conceives it, the slave condition involved a ‘triple loss’: the loss of a ‘home’; the loss of rights over his or her own ‘body’; and the loss of ‘political status’. The result was ‘absolute domination, natal alienation, and social death’ (Mbembe 2003: 21). In other words, the enslaved black person represented ‘death-in-life’ (Mbembe 2003: 21). The role played by the law (indissociable from the state, and thus from land) is inescapable: the social death as the consequence of the loss of rights. As a regulator of social life, imperial and (inter)national law enacted and legitimized the sovereign decision of not considering the lives of enslaved people as proper lives. They were actually considered property, akin to cattle. What is more pungent is that slavery in the Atlantic anticipated the state as an institution, which means that it was a practice prior to the existence of state law in these shores. Therefore, it was not state law that created slavery or slave trade, but rather inter-imperial and international (or interpolity) laws that first enabled them (Benton 2010), and then prohibited the latter (not necessarily the former). Thus, the line that marked the limits of the states and made the understanding of the pirate as an enemy of all, in the case of the enslaved Black African is blurred because the enslaved person actually traverses (in)to the sovereign state. S/he is the manifestation, on land, of a life transformed – as death-in-life – at sea.

    For Mbembe (2018), the systemic enslavement of the Black African in the Atlantic world constituted the first historical experience of necropolitics. King (2019, 2020), accompanied by Wynter and Spillers, would not disagree. However, the abject form of violence addressed by Mbembe should not be mistaken with exceptionality. The point was precisely the opposite: it was possible to normalize and naturalize this atrocious form of violence in the same way that we too often normalize and naturalize ‘war and peace,’ or ‘poverty and hunger.’ Necropolitics was indissociable from a structural form of racism (Mbembe 2003). Again, King (2019, 2020), Wynter and Spillers, would not disagree. Moreover, the shadowy presence of the enslaved black person was indispensable for the structural iterability of the (post)colonial modern international world and its asymmetric relations, including those of an expanding international political economy.15 The (systemic) thing about slavery was precisely that it included not only through a particular technology of exclusion, but also through the forced displacement and labour and the wealth that the enslaved black person had to produce once captured within the third bank of this racist Atlantic Sea (Mbembe 2018; King 2019, 2020; Campling and Colás 2018, 2021). And here it is important to highlight the transformative dimension of the sea to modern enslavement. In Africa, the person was (‘simply’) a person, that is, another human being, member of her/his local community. The global colour line inherent to the middle passage was precisely that which so violently transformed a human being into a de-humanized, forcedly displaced, and enslaved black person. The brutal change in spacetime and the violent crossing of the Atlantic Sea were indissociable from this particular form of racism, dehumanization, and necropolitics.

    Questioning asymmetrical relations and international ontopolitical lines

    In Schmitt’s international political (hi)story, the European geopolitical struggles over land- and sea-appropriations lead to the necessity of drawing the first ‘global lines’ (Schmitt 2003: 88), which served to divide, order, and give orientation to ‘the whole earth’ (Schmitt 2003: 86). They were both ontic and ontological – or ontopolitical (Marchart 2007) – lines. Ontically, there have been different historically instantiated global lines. Despite the grounding problem of sovereignty accompanying the drawings of these global lines, that is, the political-theological problem concerning the groundless grounds of the (auto-)authorization of the (supposedly) universal authority responsible for such a global form of drawing the line (Orford 2011; Fitzpatrick 2001, 2003, 2008, 2011; Derrida 2002a, 2002b; Walker 2010, 2016; Yamato 2021), the ontological or ontopolitical construction of global lines marked a foundational discrimination that has been determinant for the political and legal meaning of international phenomena. It marked the inner and outer limits of the globalizing (Christian-European) interimperial and interstate (or interpolity) law and order. Importantly, beyond this ontopolitical global line, there was ‘an “overseas” zone in which, for want of any legal limits to war, only the law of the stronger applied’ (Schmitt 2003: 93–94).16 As Yamato (2019: 225) has insisted, the crucial point here is that ‘[e]verything that occurred “beyond the line” remained outside the legal, moral, and political values’ which applied within the Christian-Eurocentric interstate order (Schmitt 2003: 93–94). Hence, war pursued beyond the line ‘against non-state, i.e. barbarian peoples or against pirates’ (Schmitt 2003: 167) meant an asymmetrical structuring of enmity relations outside of the humanizing formal containment of Christian-European public law. Counter-conceptually (Koselleck 2004), then, the criminal-pirate, the barbarian, and the wild-Indian marked this ontopolitical (outer) space of exception, inhumanity, and asymmetrical relations beyond that global(izing) line. Thus, beyond their ontic beings, these limit-categories of individuals worked as constitutive outsiders that – ontopolitically – marked (and guarded) the limits of the globalizing modern international world (Walker 2010; Prozorov 2014) and its accompanying legal-political conceptual order (Yamato 2019, 2020, 2021).

    Most importantly, for the purposes of this article, those global lines were ontopolitical lines drawn at the sea, that is, at the Atlantic Sea, demarcating the inside and the outside of a modern international ontological and political formation constructed by the family of Christian-European nations (Schmitt 2003) – polities and empires (Benton 2010). As such, those global lines constituted an a-legal (Lindhal 2013) ‘beyond’ in relation to which the public law of this (particular) family of nations, polities and empires (auto)affirmed itself, thus legitimizing asymmetrical relations of violence and ‘a hyper-imperialist appropriation’ of the world (Derrida 2002a: 66), without shame or (il)legality (Lindahl 2013).17 This is a story whose dark side involves the assimilation and extermination of many (other) worlds (Walker 2010; Cadena and Blaser 2018). Not surprisingly, then, the (ontic and ontological) formation of the modern international political world has been foundationally marked by exceptional, imperial, genocidal and dehumanizing traces (Walker 2016b; King 2019, 2020). In this article, we argue that certain limit-categories of individuals negatively enacted, legitimized and reinforced the (barely) sovereign (Benton 2010; Fitzpatrick 2001; Walker 2010) drawing of these international ontopolitical lines. Beyond their historical and ontic individualities and instantiations, the outlawed pirate and the enslaved Back African functioned as constitutive outside(r)s (Derrida 2016; Staten 1984; Edkins 1999, Fitzpatrick 1991, 2001, 2013; Walker 2010), whose (alleged) ontopolitical nature negatively legitimized the imperial formation of the modern international world and its sovereign subjectivities and abject forms of violence. As King (2019, 2020) suggests, the Indian Native could also be added to the list of constitutive outside(r)s.

    Indeed, in the very same passage in which Schmitt (2003: 142) acknowledges ‘war in form’ as ‘the strongest possible rationalization and humanization of war,’ he also acknowledges, comparatively, two other forms of war and enmity: one is that of religious and factional wars, which are, by nature, wars of annihilations wherein the enemy is treated as a criminal and a pirate; and the other is that of colonial wars pursued against ‘“wild” peoples’ (Schmitt 2003, 142). Considering Schmitt’s own contribution to the debate on total war, it is then possible to conceive here the conceptual correlations between different forms of war and different kinds of enemy: while the just enemy is inherent in ‘war in form,’ the criminal and pirate enemy is the (im)proper object of ‘wars of annihilations’, and the wild enemy is naturally fought in ‘colonial wars’. But here one must remember a footnote in The Concept of the Political in which Schmitt comments on the outlawing of war, approvingly referring to Pufendorf’s approval of Bacon’s consideration that some peoples – like ‘the Indians’ – are naturally proscribed because of their improper nature, habits, and/or behaviours (Schmitt 2007, 54n23). And as Schmitt (polemically) reminds therein, the proscription of the Indians resulted in their being – de facto – ‘exterminated’ (Schmitt 2007, 54n23). In these terms, then, the ‘criminal-pirate’ and the ‘wild-Indian’ were brought together through – international – legal-political forms of outlawing and dehumanization which legitimized discriminatory forms of violence and war (Yamato 2019). Although from a different perspective, King (2019, 2020), Spillers, and Wynter, would not disagree, but certainly insist on adding and denouncing the correlated dehumanization of the enslaved Black African. In Schmittian-Koselleckian language (Schmitt 1987; Koselleck 2004), then, while the (Christian-European) enemy pointed to an inherently symmetrical structure of counter-concepts (friend and enemy are brothers, after all), the outlawed pirate, the ‘wild’ Indian and the enslaved Black African involved a necessarily asymmetrical structure of relations (Yamato 2019; King 2019, 2020).

    For instance, if Francisco de Vitoria could be retrospectively read as a pioneering humanist-rationalist, who famously defended the Indians against the predatory Spanish colonization of the Americas, he could also be identified as part of a broader and ‘refined justification of imperial acquisition’ (Fitzpatrick 2011: 48). According to Fitzpatrick (2011: 49), although recognizing them as being ‘human’, Vitoria both included and excluded the Indians from the (supposedly) universal law common to all (Christian-European) nations. For Vitoria, the Indians were ‘undeserving of full inclusion because they were akin to madmen or children, cannibalistic, sexually perverted and culinarily outrageous’ (Fitzpatrick 2011: 49). Consequently, as the ‘barbarians (barbari)’ that they were, ‘they were considered well neigh impervious to a reforming natural reason’ (Fitzpatrick 2011: 49). In other words, as Antony Anghie (2004: 26) straightforwardly puts it, the Indians existed ‘within the Victorian framework only as violators of the law.’ Indeed, as Gómez (2008: 48–49) has importantly shown, the most learned minds in the Latin West, including Columbus, Las Casas, and Vitoria himself, were profoundly affected by a complex set of premises and assumptions that naturalized the ‘condition of slavery’ (Gómez 2008: 282) and provided an enduring legitimization of ‘Western colonialism’ (Gómez 2008: 288). Not surprisingly, the most fundamental ontopolitical-theological assumption about the world was that its centre was anchored in the terrestrial land of Christian-Europe nations, empires and civilization (Schmitt 2003). It was from this (provincial) centre that international ontopolitical lines were drawn, (auto)authorizing and legitimizing both ontic and ontopolitical asymmetrical relations and forms of violence, including the suppression of pirates, the genocide of Indian Natives, and the enslavement of Black Africans.

    Conclusion

    In discussing the writing of this text, we were caught in the conundrum: what is the third margin, the third bank of the sea? Is it the son that is left alone and never arrives in some (other) place, is it the father that developed an in-between life, is it the other bank that, actually, was never reached? Though never fully pacifying this discussion, we came to an agreement that the difficulty in understanding it is relatable with the experience of not fully grasping the meaning of this other ontopolitical point of departure that is centred not in land, but over water. As previously mentioned, for Schmitt, the land of the earth is the mother of law. Our goal here has been to displace this exemplary, telluric international political thought towards the sea, while privileging maritime traces of constitutive outside(r)s of the white, Christian-European modern international world of sovereign empires, nation-states and national citizens. In so doing, we also aimed to draw attention to asymmetrical relations and international ontopolitical lines accompanying those constitutive outside(r)s.

    Constructed as the enemy of the human race, the outlawed pirate counter-conceptually legitimized the outer limits of humanity and the modern international world, while negatively authorizing a universal form of jurisdiction in international law and society. Historically, the outlawed pirate marks the origin of contemporary international criminal law and the International Criminal Court (ICC). Ontopolitically, it works as a constitutive outside(r) of the modern international order of sovereign territorial states. Differently, the enslaved Black African inhabited a more ambiguous and shadowy place in the (post)colonial modern international world. S/he was not an enemy of the human race, nor an outlawed international criminal, much less a national citizen, a legally recognized foreigner, or a proper (civilized) human being. Neither s/he was an animal, or any other form of absolutely nonhuman being. S/he was not only a materialized form of ‘shadow’ (Mbembe 2003: 22) and ‘death-in-life’ (Mbembe 2003: 21), but also represented a very material form of labour and production of wealth. Moreover, s/he had a very colourful – black – skin. Marked by the global colour line drawn at the sea, s/he was kidnaped and forced to cross the racist Atlantic River. S/he was enslaved. Thus, condemned to live (and die) in the third bank of this necropolitical sea, the enslaved black person enacted that global line of race in the very skin of her/his own dehumanized body.

    Although in different terms, the enslaved Black African and the outlawed pirate, as the ‘wild’ Indian Native (King 2019, 2020), negatively and ontopolitically functioned as constitutive outside(r)s of the modern international political world. Hence, as negative limit-categories and conditions of possibility, the ‘pirate,’ the ‘Indian’ and the ‘Slave’ point not only to ontic, historical traces of abject forms of suppression, genocide and enslavement, but also to a much deeper, foundational, ontopolitical (Marchart 2007) form of constitution (Derrida 2016; Staten 1984; Fitzpatrick 1991, 2001; Edkins 1999) structuring and legitimizing asymmetrical structures of relations, counterconcepts and grammars of violence. In other words, their individual bodies, histories and (counter-)archives provide Atlantic traces of a profoundly dehumanizing, abject, and racist form of world othering and ordering.
