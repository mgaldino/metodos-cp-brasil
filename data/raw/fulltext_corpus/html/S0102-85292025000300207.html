Feminist Ideas and UN Incremental Institutional Change

Ideias feministas e mudança institucional endógena nas Nações Unidas

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    In recent decades, the increase in discussions on institutional change has relativised the assumption of institutional stability, linked to the effectiveness or durability of cultural and societal patterns. However, the main focus of these studies has been on the role of exogenous factors, neglecting slower processes independent of critical junctures. In dialogue with these efforts, this article seeks to answer the following questions:1) How can ideas incrementally impact an international organisation? 2) How endogenously do these ideas resonate in such a way as to bring about institutional change in terms of agenda, institutional design, and policy programmes? To this end, we propose an analytical framework based on the contributions of Mahone and Thelen (2010) and Campbell (2004). Applying the new framework, we analyse the impact and consolidation of feminist ideas on the United Nations, from a perspective of endogenous and gradual institutional change. We argue that feminist ideas have caused a broad and deep institutional change in the organisation with the insertion of the theme on the agenda, the creation of commissions, programmes, and new bodies, culminating in the creation of UN Women and the SDGs

    Key words
    gradual institutional change; incremental institutional change; ideas; feminism; United Nations

    Resumo

    Nas últimas décadas, o aumento das discussões sobre mudança institucional relativizou o pressuposto de estabilidade das instituições, tradicionalmente associada à eficácia ou durabilidade de padrões culturais e sociais. No entanto, o foco principal desses estudos tem recaído sobre o papel de fatores exógenos, negligenciando processos mais lentos e independentes de momentos críticos. Em diálogo com esses esforços, este artigo busca responder às seguintes questões: (1) de que modo as ideias podem impactar incrementalmente uma organização internacional? (2) de que forma essas ideias ressoam de maneira endógena a ponto de provocar mudanças institucionais em termos de agenda, desenho institucional e programas de políticas? Para tanto, propomos um quadro analítico baseado nas contribuições de Mahone e Thelen (2010) e Campbell (2004). Aplicando esse novo enquadramento, analisamos o impacto e a consolidação das ideias feministas nas Nações Unidas, sob uma perspectiva de mudança institucional endógena e gradual. Argumentamos que as ideias feministas promoveram uma mudança institucional ampla e profunda na organização, com a inserção do tema na agenda, a criação de comissões, programas e novos órgãos, culminando na criação da ONU Mulheres e dos ODS.

    Palavras-chave
    mudança institucional; mudança incremental endógena; ideias; feminismo; Nações Unidas

    Introduction

    In recent decades, studies on the possibilities of institutional change have challenged the assumption of the stability of institutions. For the rationalist strand, the creation of institutions would result from the interests of the actors in achieving their objectives more efficiently, generating predictability of the actors’ actions and mutual expectations linked to institutional effectiveness. For the sociological approach, the logic of appropriateness within political institutions, permeated by culture, norms, and understandings, would result in stability. Based on both logics, historical institutionalism, for a long time, also did not escape the assumption, connecting stability to path dependence (Hall and Taylor 2003; Mahoney and Thelen 2010).

    Stability was questioned based on the possibility of abrupt change, due to external variables, and critical circumstances such as major wars and economic crises. Historical Institutionalism is a precursor to this questioning, highlighting the asymmetries of power and their impact on stability as a vector of change (Hall and Taylor 2003; Mahoney and Thelen 2010).

    Later, this same current began to explore the possibility of incremental and endogenous changes, that is, arising from the institution itself, and not by an external factor. In a significant effort to deal with this theoretical absence, Mahoney and Thelen (2010) developed an analytical model that became the basis for studies in the area, pointing out variables such as political context and characteristics of the institution, different types of actors, and proposed changes. However, despite systematizing several variables, Mahoney and Thelen’s (2010) model received many criticisms due to the lack of clarity and coherence of the concepts, the static agency model proposed by the authors, and the lack of overall analytical power (Jeroen van der Heijden and Johanna Kuhlmann 2016). In dialogue with these authors, we add another criticism that directly addresses the agency power in the model the role of ideas in institutional change.

    In order to bring together the discussions on the role of ideas in the processes of change and Mahoney and Thelen’s (2010) theoretical model, we argue that the ideational categories proposed by Campbell (2004) can fill this gap, given its foundation on Historical and Sociological Institutionalism. As discussed below, Campbell (1998) distinguishes two conceptual distinctions to identify the types of ideas and their effects: ideas can be underlying assumptions or concepts and theories; they can also be cognitive or normative. From this initial distinction, four types of ideas can be identified: paradigms, public sentiments, programmes, and frames (Campbell 2004). We suggest that this overlapping of models can contribute to the greater comprehensiveness of the model proposed by Mahoney and Thelen (2010) and expand its analytical range.

    Based on these two theoretical models, which are further detailed below, we raise the following questions: 1) how can ideas incrementally impact an international organisation?; 2) how endogenously do these ideas resonate in such a way as to bring about institutional change in terms of agenda, institutional design, and policy programmes?

    We start from the assumption that change in institutions is the other side of stability. For an institution to maintain itself, it needs to adapt to new political and social contexts. To verify the applicability of the combination of the theoretical models listed, we conducted a case study on the impact of feminist ideas at the UN. We assume that the United Nations (UN), created after World War II as the main organisation for the maintenance of international peace and security, has not remained static, despite the assumption of stability. After all, the UN is not immune to new ideas, and reconfigurations in the asymmetry of power and interests of the actors. We aim to analyse the impact of feminist ideas and agenda at the UN from an ideational/historical institutionalist approach in a perspective of endogenous and gradual institutional change. We sustain that by adding ideas as a variable of analysis in the processes of incremental institutional change, we will be able to emphasise the importance of the agency of individuals and organised groups in these processes, highlighting the role of agents of change more incisively.

    Contradictorily, studies on international organisations, and specifically on the UN, have focused little on the processes of change in the organisation. Studies on the reform of the Security Council, for example, generally emphasize the non-reform of the body, disregarding the incremental changes that have occurred over the decades. However, to adapt to the new configurations of international power, the body has reformed itself informally and incrementally (Baccarini 2018). Indeed, international organisations should be seen as sites of fluid contestation, except over relatively short periods of time. They are open systems that create incentives for continuous contestation and are vulnerable to political and ideological changes (Beland and Orenstein 2013).

    The paper is divided into two main sections besides the introduction and the conclusions. First, we present the nature of the theoretical problem, contrasting institutionalist perspectives and pointing to the Mahoney and Thelen (2010) model, within the scope of historical institutionalism, as one of the first systematizations of what is determined as gradual/incremental institutional change. However, recognizing the flaws of this model, especially with respect to their lack of attention to the importance of ideas, we then propose an adaptation of Mahoney and Thelen’s (2010) model by adding Campbell’s (2004) typology of ideas. In the second section, based on the new theorical model, we expose how feminist ideas led a broad and deep institutional change in the UN over several decades with the gradual insertion of the theme in the organisation’s agenda.

    The nature of the theoretical problem

    This section aims to present the theoretical model for the analysis of incremental/gradual changes, primarily developed by Thelen and various collaborators, as well as the criticisms attributed to it by other researchers. Specifically, the limitation of Thelen’s model is anchored in the long convergence of historical institutionalism (HI) with Rational Choice institutionalism, and its predilection for analysing formal institutions from a historical perspective.

    According to Mahoney and Thelen (2010), historical institutionalists have so far emphasised institutional continuity rather than change, using the concept ‘path dependence’ as an explanation for the persistence of specific institutional patterns or outcomes, with increasing gains in power and correspondence to the interests of certain groups. However, historical institutionalists began to explore the possibility of incremental changes as vectors of institutional change.

    In this sense, institutional change can be seen through the reconfiguration of institutions in their legacy situation of concrete historical processes, since transformation can be identified with gradual modifications and incremental structural recombination’s (Thelen 1999). Thus, in an institution there are permanent reconsiderations about the allocation of resources, formation of new balances of power, and temporal dilemmas of interpretation and application of rules, making room for actors to implement previous existing rules in a new way, consistent with new scenarios.1

    Mahoney and Thelen’s (2010) analytical model represents a significant effort to fill this theoretical gap. It identifies the variables—actors, contexts, and processes—that influence and operate within an institutional structure, linking both exogenous and endogenous factors in the processes of institutional change. As for the endogenous aspect that promotes change, the authors consider institutions as living organisms where their compositions interact with each other in a logic of conservation, either by maintaining, improving or modifying them. In other words, the model notes that institutional variations are driven by action strategies linked to distinct change agents. These agents operate based on tensions and ongoing considerations of resources and their distributional impacts (Mahoney and Thelen 2010). These variables are systematised in the process where the characteristics of the political context and the institution delimit the type of institutional change and the type of dominant agent and the strategies of this agent to effect change.

    The model considers that there are different ways for institutional change to occur, these being through I displacement, II layering, III drift or IV conversion. Briefly and respectively: I is when there is the complete removal of existing rules and the introduction of new ones; II is when new rules are introduced in conjunction with/overlapping with existing ones; III is when the impact of existing rules is altered due to changes in the institutional environment; and IV is identified when existing rules have their impacts altered by being strategically reinterpreted.

    Linked to these types of change are the different actors driven by different purposes. To capture these intentions, the authors ask themselves if the actor seeks to preserve the existing institutional rules and/or abide by the institutional rules. ‘Insurrectionary’ actors, who aim to actively and visibly eliminate the institution or rule, would seek ‘displacement’ type changes. On the other hand, ‘subversive’ actors, who seek to displace the institution, without breaking the rules, but accommodating their interests, would act through ‘layering’. ‘Parasitic symbionts’, who exploit the institution for private interests, even depending on the existence and efficiency of the institution, would wait to act through ‘drift’. Finally, opportunist actors, who exploit any opportunity to achieve their goals, without necessarily actively seeking to preserve the institution, would act especially during ‘conversion’ possibilities (Mahoney and Thelen 2010).

    Thus, the model elaborates a set of propositions that link particular modes of incremental change to features of the institutional context and properties of the institutions themselves, which allow for specific types of change strategies and change agents (Mahoney and Thelen 2010). In addition, it draws our attention to the possibility of the need for institutional adaptation arising from various factors, such as: limitations in institutional design; the emergence of gaps between ideals and reality, and the need for politics of compromise; asymmetries and contestation of power; and changes in time/context, which allow for reinterpretations of rules (Conran and Thelen 2016).

    Change and stability are two sides of the same coin. Without change there would be no stability, because institutions necessarily need to deal with variables that require adaptation. Thus, both external shocks and internal processes of change should be taken into account. While displacement, layering and drift presuppose some degree of change due to an external shock and intentionality, conversion can be understood as a purely endogenous process, given the distributive nature of institutions (Conran and Thelen 2016).

    On the other hand, Bogaards (2022) discusses how feminist scholars have integrated gender into various institutionalist approaches, resulting in feminist historical institutionalism, feminist sociological institutionalism, feminist discursive institutionalism, and feminist rational choice institutionalism. It also explores the potential of developing a standalone feminist institutionalism, a fifth one.

    Bogaards (2022) highlights the strengths and limitations of these approaches, emphasizing the need for a coherent framework to unify the insights generated by feminist institutionalisms. He concludes that while feminist institutionalism (singular) has the potential to bring together these insights, the field remains fragmented due to the proliferation of different feminist institutionalist approaches.

    Despite the still incipient formation of a feminist institutionalism, the perspective is promising (Krook and Mackay 2011; Mackay and Chappell 2010). However, among the different versions, linked to the more traditional strands of institutionalism, feminist historical institutionalism is the most developed (Bogaards 2022). This is not surprising, given the greater attention paid by this institutionalism to issues of power and change (Fioretos, Falleti and Sheingate 2016). As the feminist perspectives presupposes the pursuit of social, political, and consequently institutional changes the historical institutionalist perspective is most permeable to change, and the analysis of power issues would generate greater proximity. Additionally, some authors engage directly with the model developed by Mahoney and Thelen (2010). Waylen (2014, 2017), for example, examines the four types of incremental change and their roles in fostering gender-equal institutions.2 Despite widely adopted by researchers, the model developed by Thelen (and Mahoney) faced criticisms regarding its empirical applicability due to the lack of clarity, coherence, and rigidity concerning the forms of change and agency of actors (Jeroen van der Heijden and Johanna Kuhlmann 2016).

    In a search of the Scopus database, 44 works (including articles, chapters, and books) were found that mention in their abstract one of the following combinations of terms: ‘institutional change’ AND ‘thelen’ (33 works); ‘gradual change’ AND ‘thelen’ (2 works); ‘incremental change’ AND ‘thelen’ (8 works); ‘institutional evolution’ AND ‘thelen’ (2 works). After excluding duplicate works, works before 2009 (as the first publication of the model was printed that year), and those not fitting the research objectives or due to the impossibility of finding the work, 31 works remained. The search is justified because, by looking directly at the abstracts and keywords of the works, we aim to engage directly with their impressions regarding Thelen's model.

    From those 31 works, 8 directly criticize the model or indicate the need for supplementation due to identified gaps. Among these, two propose the creation of new concepts, such as ‘institutional shrinkage’ (Monsalve 2022) and ‘reluctant agent’ (Lagassé 2017). However, as concluded in the article by Jeroen van der Heijden and Johanna Kuhlmann (2016), the proliferation and stretching of concepts result in a greater loss of the model’s explanatory power. On the other hand, other authors point out the need to incorporate the role of ideas (and ideology) as sources of change (Guiraudon and Martin 2012; Távora 2019).We consider this a limitation because, although Thelen’s model deals with incremental change, it does not consider the role of the ideas of the actors in the institution, and their political impact at the different decision-making levels. The problem becomes more evident when we try to apply the model to the study of international organisations. In order to overcome this limitation, we sought insights from Sociological Institutionalism (SI) and Ideational Institutionalism.

    For their part, SI link institutional stability to the stability of society itself, its culture, norms and understandings, factors that prevent (or make very difficult) change. Institutions are understood as practices established over time in a given society or patterns of behaviour. Furthermore, for SI supporters, institutions not only constrain the actions of social actors, but also underpin the constitution of their identities and interests (Hall and Taylor 2003; Giddens 2018; Zucker 1991; Berger and Luckman 2007; Martin and Simmons 1998; Duffield 2007).

    Due to their regulatory and constitutive nature, institutions in IS can also be considered norms of behaviour. March and Olsen (1983) were perhaps the authors who best developed this perspective, starting from the understanding that constitutive norms (or institutions) and practices prescribe appropriate behaviour for specific actors in specific situations, in what has come to be widely referred to as the ‘logic of appropriateness’.

    Because they operate on a cognitive level, sociological institutions are taken for granted, leading actors to reproduce their behaviour unreflectively, albeit within a logic of adequacy. Thus, only minimal variations would be observed over long periods of time, in a form of cultural path dependence. Institutional change is explained by the convergence of institutional practices that make organisations isomorphic, as a result of mimetic processes that reduce uncertainty about institutional designs; or by normative processes that take place in which organisational leaders have been professionalised to share similar views about what constitutes appropriate organisational practice; by direct or indirect coercive processes in which organisations adapt to pressure from other organisations around them to conform to institutionalised standards (Campbell 2004; Finnemore 1993; DiMaggio and Powell 1983).

    Then, although SI has elements to think in terms of norms and ideas, by it focus on its diffusion, it leaves formal institutional change through history aside. But just as in Historical Institutionalism, institutions are structures external to agents that constitute rules about how to act in the world that serve primarily as constraints - whether through rationalist incentives that structure action, historical paths that shape action, or cultural norms that frame action (Schmidt 2010).

    In subfields such as Comparative Politics, American Politics and Normative Political Theory, authors have started to include ideas as an independent or dependent variable in recent decades. This ‘ideational turn’ challenges traditional methods and approaches to political analysis, demanding methodological innovation, interdisciplinary collaboration (with sociology, psychology, history and philosophy) and advances in theoretical frameworks. Not least, the inclusion of ‘ideas’ as an analytical variable sheds light on power dynamics, ideological struggles, and the construction of political authority and increases our understanding of how beliefs, values and norms influence political processes and, consequently, how political and institutional change occurs (Checkel et al 2016).

    In International Relations, works based on the concept of ‘epistemic communities’ by Peter Haas (1992) and the book ‘Ideas and Foreign Policy’ by Goldstein and Keohane (1993) are the forerunners of discussions on the role of ideas in international political processes. Since then, constructivism has broadened the scope of these studies by turning to the study of norms, discourses, practices and narratives, and taking ideas as preferences (Checkel et al 2016). In common, all contributions highlight that ideas matter for political analysis and practice.

    A significant number of the authors in this debate on the role of norms and ideas have drawn on organisational institutionalism (Finnemore 1996). Nevertheless, the organisational isomorphism premise (Meyer and Rowan 1977; DiMaggio and Powell 1983) has conditioned studies on the international diffusion of norms, such as the work by Acharya (2004), Gilardi (2012), Finnemore and Sikkink (1998), Barnett and Finnemore (2012) and Finnemore (1993). However, as Gilardi (2012) points out, the focus of diffusion studies is on the process by which an idea or norm from one place impacts a political actor in another place. The focus is not on the medium and long-term effects of this diffusion, which is the formal change in the institutions affected by the diffusion of the norm. Even works such as Béland and Oreinstein (2013) on the ideational character of international organisations leave behind the process of institutional change resulting from an ideational change. In other words, they do not consider the historical character of formal institutional change, as highlighted by historical institutionalists.

    As pointed out by Perissinotto and Stumm (2017), the incorporation of ideas in institutionalist analysis allows us to apprehend important variables, particularly historical institutionalism. Ideational continuities cannot be considered independently from the institutions themselves. For instance, it is impossible to contemplate intellectual path dependence without acknowledging the process of institutionalizing ideas and the long-term effects that accompany it.

    Although some researchers have sought to remain within the traditional analytical framework of the new institutionalisms to explain the origins of institutional change from endogenous and incremental factors, other authors (of HI and especially of SI) have turned to the role of ideas and discourses. In any case, in seeking to explain the institutional change from ideas and discourses, authors of the three approaches began to reformulate the very foundations of their respective schools (Schmidt 2010; Perissinotto and Stumm 2017).

    Schmidt (2010), for example, argues that the role of ideas and discourses should be central to analyses of change. This centrality would lead to the emergence of a fourth institutionalism, ‘Discursive Institutionalism’, which considers both the substantive content of ideas and the interactive processes of communication and exchange through discourse. For the author, discourse is the mean by which ideas pass from individual thought to collective action. Thus, discursive capacities would be essential for explaining institutional change, as they refer to people’s ability to think outside institutions in a critical, communicative and deliberative way. So, after persuasion and a change of opinion about these institutions, it would be possible to act to change them.

    According to Blyth et al (2016), while authors of ideational institutionalism have sought to engage with the concepts of historical institutionalists, these are still orientated towards the institutionalism of the Rational School. Concepts such as critical juncture, path dependence and the very notion of power would benefit enormously from being rethought based on the impact of ideas on institutional change (Checkel et al 2016).

    Campbell’s (2004) categorization of ideas is anchored in historical and sociological institutionalism, without a specific focus on discourses, enabling an analysis in conjunction with the model proposed by Mahoney and Thelen (2010). Mahoney and Thelen’s (2010) model, however useful, is limited in not exploring the role of ideas as vectors of change. Including the role of various types of ideas and agents involved in this process can, to some extent, help address the criticism of the model regarding the rigidity of the agency (Jeroen van der Heijden and Johanna Kuhlmann 2016).

    Campbell (2004) starts from two conceptual distinctions to identify the types of ideas and their effects. First, ideas can be underlying assumptions or concepts and theories. Ideas as underlying assumptions are often taken for granted and operate in the background of people’s consciousness. In this sense, ideas are social institutions, which constrain and constitute actors. On the other hand, ideas can also be concepts and theories that operate in the foreground of a debate, when they are explicitly articulated by actors.

    Second, ideas can be cognitive or normative. At the cognitive level, ideas are descriptions and theoretical analyses that specify cause-and-effect relationships and are outcome-oriented. Normative ideas are values, attitudes, and identities and are not oriented towards specific results. From this initial distinction, four types of ideas can be identified: paradigms, public sentiments, programs, and frames (Campbell 2004).

    Ideas as programmes, or policy prescriptions, are cognitive concepts and theories that facilitate and enable action among elites by specifying how to solve problems and defining specific courses of action. Programmatic ideas are often key dependent variables for institutionalists, as they determine most directly how institutions change. Frames are normative concepts used by elites to legitimise policy change programmes to the general public and among themselves through processes such as transposition, selection, and bricolage. Frames usually appear in public pronouncements and other types of statements by policymakers and their advisors to gather public support for their proposals. Often, the symbols and concepts discursively mobilised are exactly the values and opinions reflected in public sentiment (Campbell 1998; Campbell 2004).

    Paradigms are underlying cognitive assumptions that constrain decision-making and institutional change by limiting the scope of alternatives available to policymaking elites. Unlike programmatic ideas, which are precise, concrete, and specific in defining a course of policy action, paradigms generally reside in the cognitive background as underlying theoretical and ontological assumptions. When new programmatic ideas fit into the dominant paradigm, they take on a natural and familiar character, facilitating change (Campbell 1998; Campbell 2004).

    Public sentiments are underlying normative assumptions that constrain decision-makers and institutional change by limiting the scope of programs that decision-makers perceive as acceptable and legitimate, both to constituents and themselves. Public sentiment involves public opinion, values, norms, identities, and other shared collective expectations, emphasizing the role of culture in delimiting appropriate understandings and actions. Since public sentiment covers a wide range of issues, they do not necessarily constitute a coherent and consistent set of positions (Campbell 1998; Campbell 2004).

    Campbell (2004), clarifying the interrelationship between the different types of ideas in his typology, points out that there is no pre-defined order from which a process of change starts. Virtually any of the idea types can trigger institutional change with greater or lesser speed, but it is important to note that only a change in one of the idea types is not enough for successful change. There is a need for the other types to be involved in the process.

    On the other hand, Campbell (2004) also discusses the agents of change in ideas. They are: decision makers, theorists, framers, constituents and brokers. Decision makers embrace programmes and incorporate them into decision-making, with decision-making elites being the closest bearers of ideas that affect decision-making and institutional change. Theorists, on the other hand, legitimise specific programmes from the paradigms they help to promulgate and sustain. Occasionally, these thinkers can alter paradigms causing enormous consequences for the programmes they suggest. However, for these programmes to be considered acceptable and convincing, they must fit the prevailing cognitive paradigm, norms, values and other public sentiments. Framers frame these ideas in a way that mobilises people, creatively shaping and blending different ideas through the process of bricolage. The targets, in turn, are the constituents, and can be the general public or decision-making elites.

    Finally, for ideas to affect decision-making and institution-building, they must be linked, connected or transported, which depends on brokers, actors who operate at the intersections of these ideational domains. At the international level, brokers generally involve epistemic communities, which develop new paradigms and transmit their programme implications to national decision-makers and citizens (Campell 2004).

In other words, change agents are not necessarily acting only within the institution but can be external to it.

    Starting from this adapted framework, in the next session we discuss the institutionalisation of feminist ideas at the UN. We assume that these ideas, initially exogenous to the organisation, were a theoretical development, based on feminist theorists and related epistemic communities, aimed at changing the paradigm known as ‘patriarchy’. Internationally organised feminist groups (and other supporting actors) then began to frame these ideas, acting as ‘framers’ and ‘brokers’, with the aim of convincing and altering public sentiment and pressing for institutional changes, including at the UN, at various levels.

    This is when a process of institutional change begins, via the agency of the actors, with the aim of altering frameworks and programmes at the UN in order to bring about an incremental change in the organisation’s rules and norms. The hope is that these programmes will also drive change in the organisation’s member states, unifying public sentiment on the issue.

    The UN institutional change: the impact of the feminist agenda

    The feminist movement emerged in the 18th century, developed, diversified and deepened over the centuries, along with the feminist agenda. This process is usually described as ‘waves of feminism’ being presented chronologically, despite strong criticism of this representation as a result of their interdependence (Biroli and Miguel 2014; Gray and Boddy 2010; Silva 2019).

    In general terms, between the 19th century and the 1920s we have the so-called ‘first wave’, and the emergence of liberal feminism. The main exponent, the suffragist movement, focused on political rights and education of women. In the following ‘waves’, we observe the beginning of the movement’s diversification and the emergence of several feminisms (liberal feminism, black feminism, identity, radical, social welfare, intersectional, postmodern academic, etc.). Connected to these various feminisms, the agenda also diversified to include: demands for civil rights; rights over sexuality and reproductive rights; discussions on the impacts of intersectionality, connecting race, gender and social issues etc. (Biroli and Miguel 2014; Gray and Boddy 2010; Silva 2019).

    Although we consider it important to pay attention to the construction of the feminist movement and agenda over the decades, our focus is less on this process itself and more on its impact within UN, which emerged with the end of World War II as the main international organisation. In this session, we will seek to identify the impact of feminist ideas on the process of institutional change at the UN, based on the concepts outlined in the theoretical model developed above. We seek to provide an overview of the process, which makes it impossible to focus on several key moments, leaving these analyses for future researches.

    1st moment –the institutionalisation of the UN, the definition of the problem (women matter), and first institutional effects

    The political context of the end of World War II provided a unique moment for the advancement of the feminist agenda. The feminist movements were fighting to maintain the few rights they had won and to expand their place in society after the of experiences and living outside the home while men were swallowed up by the war.

    The expectation that the new international system would be based on equal rights and duties for men and women encouraged the hope that the United Nations Organisation (UN) would include gender equality issues on the international agenda (UN, 1996). The prevalence of liberal values and the attached Declaration of Human Rights extended the demands of (liberal) feminists beyond their borders. The United Nations Charter itself explicitly includes in its preamble the equality of rights between men and women and, in Chapter IX, states the organisation’s commitment to promote ‘universal and effective respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms for all without distinction as to race, sex, language or religion’ (United Nations Charter 1945).

    Within the UN system, the Economic and Social Council (ECOSOC), responsible for promoting respect for human rights and fundamental freedoms, established the Commission on Human Rights in 1946 (ECOSOC, RES/5, 1946). Subsequently, a debate arose regarding the importance of a sub-commission, to deal exclusively with women’s issues, for fear that the discrimination suffered by women would be relegated to the background. Thus, the Sub-Commission on the Status of Women was created, and after a period of 4 months, was replaced by the Commission on the Status of Women (CSW) assigned with two basic functions:

    to make recommendations and reports to the Economic and Social Council on the promotion of women’s rights in the political, economic, social and educational fields. The Commission shall also make recommendations to the Council on urgent problems requiring immediate attention in the field of women’s rights. And the Commission may submit proposals to the Council regarding its terms of reference (ECOSOC RES/2/11 1946)

    The process of creating the CSW, the collection of data, and the very orientations given by the UN correspond to the first incremental changes in the organisation, based on the impact of feminist ideas, as a result of liberal norms in general. Until the early 1970s, the focus of discussions on women’s rights was on their political rights, the main focus of liberal feminism.

    The entry of new nations into the UN as a result of the decolonisation processes brought about a series of changes. In 1963, 50 of the 112 UN member states were African or Asian (BOSCO 2009). The external shock resulting from decolonization directly impacts the incipient institution that dealt with women’s rights, the CSW.

    (...) As initial actions on women’s issues were guided mainly by the concerns of women in Western Europe and the Americas, some delegations were concerned that the problems of women in developing countries and rural areas were neglected. As a result, UN efforts for the advancement of women increasingly focused on women’s role in development, both as beneficiaries and as agents of change (UN 1996: 26.).

    In the face of this new reality, the UN oriented its technical assistance programs to support women in peripheral countries. It was long believed that with the advance of industrialization and the growth of the economies, women would have greater access to the labour market, have better opportunities, and accelerate the achievement of gender equality. However, as the 1960s unfolded, women were much more affected by poverty and restrictions on their rights (UN 1996).

    Until the mid-1970s, the CSW was the only United Nations body dedicated to discussing women’s issues exclusively (Charlesworth and Chinkin 2013). Despite the importance of its creation, the ideas promoted by the new institution did not achieve the necessary vocalization.

    Up until this point, feminist ideas, exogenous to the organisation, had not yet gained enough traction to have an impact on the organisation’s direction. This situation began to change with resolution 3520, of the UN General Assembly. Published on 15 December 1975, the resolution established the United Nations Decade for Women: Equality, Development and Peace, between the years 1976 and 1985. To help implement the programmes planned for the Decade, the UN also approved a Voluntary Fund, which would later become the United Nations Development Fund for Women (UNIFEM). In 1976, ECOSOC approved the creation of the International Research and Training Institute for the Advancement of Women upon the recommendation of the First World Conference on Women, held the previous year in Mexico City (INSTRAW) (UN, A/RES/3520 1975). That marked the beginning of a deeper institutionalisation of feminist ideas.

    2nd moment - consolidation of the feminist agenda at the UN and deeper institutional effects

    It was not until the Women’s World Conferences that we see the strength of this framer and subversive actor. At this moment, the feminist movement acts, institutionalised strongly in non-governmental organisations and also vocalised through government support from more progressive governments. search for complementation, through layering, of the norms and rules created for human, political, and social rights.

    To this end, the characteristics of the political context and the characteristics of the institution led to a greater systematization of these issues at the UN. During the meeting held in Mexico City in 1975, a World Plan of Action was adopted, a document which, for the first time in history, dealt exclusively with problems faced by women, discussing the difficulties encountered, by women’s (lack of) participation in political life to issues involving food and access to education (Chen 2010).

    In the following years, the generalization of the document was the target of various criticisms and suffered pressure for the agendas to be more debated and actions better designed. Irene Tinker and Jane Jaquette (1987) argue that although the decade was, in theory, about women, the politics in the world conferences that took place during this period revolved around the question of whether the focus on women would be maintained, or whether women would be immersed in the many discussions involving the new world economic order, Zionism, and apartheid, as had happened in the first conference.

    The United Nations General Assembly (UNGA) then determined as a sub-theme for the following conference: employment, health, and education, understanding that women could not achieve equality with men if they did not have equal opportunities for access to education and professional life, as well as a healthy environment that would allow them to enjoy these opportunities (General Assembly, RES/33/185 1979).

    The UNGA resolution marks the most forceful institutional entry of issues related to women’s civil and social rights; agendas raised in various feminist theoretical perspectives. This period provided a structure and a timeline for the development of institutions within and outside the UN aimed at the advancement of women. At the end of the decade, in addition to INSTRAW, the Voluntary Fund was also established within the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP) to carry out the Plan of Action of the conferences, adding two new institutions focused on gender equality issues (Charlesworth and Chinkin 2013).

    However, despite the attempt to direct the discussions, the problem faced at the First Conference worsened at the Second World Conference on Women, held in the city of Copenhagen in 1980. The discussion of broader issues related to the new economic dynamics, adversity in Palestine, and political segregation in South Africa, again diverted the focus of the discussion of the Plan of Action and women’s struggles for equality (Chen, 2010). The recommendation was to hold a third meeting, at the end of the Decade, so that progress during these years could be assessed and debated, to set the goals for the final 15 years of the century (UN 1996; UN, A/CONF.94/35 1980).

    The Third World Conference on Women took place in Nairobi in 1985, marking the end of the United Nations Decade for Women.3 Tinker and Jaquette (1987) draw attention to the strengthening at this meeting of the clash between the perspectives of women from Northern countries and women from Southern countries. The Northern women’s discourses defended the separation of women’s issues from global issues, problematizing the time dedicated to discussing political issues that were debated in other bodies, in meetings dedicated to debating women’s agendas.

    In contrast to this vision were the Southern women. Since 1975, Southern women have occupied more space and assumed the leadership of the movement (Chen 2010). They defended the non-separation of agendas and understand that women’s poverty should be seen as a consequence of the new world economic order. Racism, for example, could not be treated as an internal issue for each people, since it was a legacy of colonialism that affected the economic and power relations between countries and had a direct influence on the lives of women in these countries.

    So, there is a clash in the construction of the new paradigm: on the one hand, the ideas advocated by feminists from the Global North and their focus on political rights; on the other, feminists from the Global South and their search for a strengthening of social rights. This clash would be configured as a competition for the development and definition of specific programmes.

    Meanwhile, the non-governmental groups that increased exponentially in numbers during the decade were learning and teaching more women around the world to lobby their governments for change in national laws, access to education, health care, and the labour market (Tinker and Jaquette 1987).

    In 1995, 10 years after the Nairobi meeting, the Fourth World Conference on Women took place in Beijing.4 Despite the expectations at the end of the decade, data made available by the UN during the meeting showed that most of the objectives were not met and the strategies were not very efficient. The women of the world continued to face great obstacles in the struggle for rights and empowerment (UN 1995).

    At the end of the conference, a new Platform for Action was adopted, establishing 12 critical areas of focus.5 The Beijing Declaration recommends the Secretary-General create a high-level position in his cabinet, an advisory office to deal specifically with gender issues and the implementation of the Plan of Action. In 1996, the Office of Special Adviser on Gender Issues (OSAGI) was established.

    The creation of INSTRAW, OSAGI, UNIFEM and the Conferences themselves helped to better frame the ideas of the feminist movement for the international organisation. Symbols and concepts previously confined to feminist movements and often poorly perceived by society are now openly discussed at the UN, expanding its reach. This deepens the legitimization of political solutions for the public by policymakers.

    3rd moment - consolidation of institutional change: the creation of UN Women

    The Beijing Platform for Action emerges as a first and more robust attempt of a Program, or ‘ideas as elite policy prescriptions that help policymakers chart a clear and specific course for policy action’ (CAMPBELL 2004). However, there is a recognition of the need for even greater institutional, political and financial depth. Aruna Rão, the director and

    founder of the NGO Gender at Work, argued in 2006 for the establishment of a lead institution with access to the highest levels, field presence, autonomy and adequate resources, focused on gender issues. Otherwise, the agenda would be, at the same time, everybody’s responsibility and nobody’s (Rão 2007).

    The creation of UN Women6 in 2010 was part of a broader UN reform agenda aimed at streamlining functions and enhancing the UN’s impact on gender equality. For years, civil society organizations and feminist movements pressured the UN and its member states to improve the institutional structure dedicated to gender equality. They submitted proposals and documents, such as those from the Center for Women’s Global Leadership and the Women’s Environment and Development Organization, outlining the need for a well-funded, independent entity with normative, operational, and oversight capacities (Charlesworth and Chinkin, 2013).

    In 2007, feminist organisations launched the Gender Equality Architecture Reform (GEAR) campaign to promote the creation of a new gender entity within the UN. The campaign involved extensive consultations with women worldwide and mobilized support for the establishment of UN Women (Charlesworth and Chinkin 2013).

    Feminist movements and NGOs actively participated in UN consultations to discuss options for reforming the gender architecture. They provided feedback and suggestions that helped shape UN Women’s structure and mandate. They also mobilized global support and raised awareness about the importance of a strong and effective entity to promote gender equality, organizing events, campaigns, and conferences to keep the issue on the international agenda (Charlesworth and Chinkin 2013).

    After the creation of UN Women, civil society and feminist movements continued to play a vital role in monitoring its activities and holding the entity accountable for its commitments. They maintain an ongoing dialogue with UN Women to ensure that its actions are effective and aligned with the needs of women worldwide. Reflecting the constituent process, UN Women collaborates with various entities such as civil society groups, businesses, and governments, which assist in meeting local demands and disseminating the outputs promoted by the institution (Charlesworth and Chinkin 2013).

    The UN Women also became an important actor in the fight for women’s rights. As an organisation, it hosts several programmes focused on gender equality and women’s rights. As an institutional actor, it operates within the UN, in close contact with feminist NGOs from around the world, promoting the feminist agenda within the UN system. It thus became one of the main engines of the institutional change of the international organisation for feminist issues. The importance of the feminist movement and non-governmental organisations remains high even after the creation of UN Women. The vast majority of UN Women’s projects are carried out in partnership with these NGOs, constituting a fundamental arm for the achievement of its objectives. Since its creation, UN Women has published an annual report on its activities around the world.7

    UN Women uphold a standard institutional policy focused on technical and normative support and training for NGOs, International Organisations, States, and civil society. It coordinates the agenda inside and outside the United Nations, supervising and encouraging programs and policies focused on the theme. Moreover, the agency is one of the main bodies responsible for academic production, data, and statistics in its field.

    About the UN Women and the historical process described, we can point to the absence of a specific critical juncture, but rather to an endogenous process of institutional change in the UN about the search for equality and women’s rights that has begun practically since its creation. The greatest external impact observed was precisely the decolonisation movement of African and Asian states, which boosted the paradigm of Southern feminists in the organisation.

    The feminist movement, decades old and representing the various existing feminisms, emerges as the main subversive actor. Through NGOs and more progressive governments, the feminist movement brings to the UN the idea that women should have the same rights as men. Over the decades, institutional change has taken place through the creation of commissions and bodies through layering, that is, the superimposition of rules and regulations, which do not deny the human, political and social rights defended by the organisation, but emphasise the feminist focus.

    Finally, the creation of UN Women crowns this process with a new actor, subversive or insurrectionary in itself, which will act in an ambivalent way, as a coordinator of programmes and projects for States, defending an expanded paradigm in defence of women’s political, civil and social rights, and as another strategic institutional actor in the fight for women’s rights in the UN System and the international system as a whole.

    Beyond UN Women, the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) and Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) also present themselves as reinforcers, able to underpin institutional change through ideas from deepening programmes.

    In September 2000, at the Millennium Summit held in New York, the Millennium Development Goals were adopted, using 2015 as the deadline for achieving them and using 1990 as the benchmark. The MDGs focused on a North-South aid agenda, primarily for developing countries, with goals like universal primary education and were driven by development ministers (technocrats) and agencies to justify aid in the post-Cold War era. The MDGs have been criticised for being overly simplistic, technocratic and not aligned with human rights standards. They focused narrowly on poverty reduction, with 8 goals and 21 targets. They simplified development to meeting basic needs, ignoring structural issues like inequality, governance, and economic growth. (Fukuda-Parr 2016).

    The Platform for Action adopted in Beijing was reduced to Goal 3 of the MDGs, with a single target: to achieve parity between men and women in primary and secondary education. According to Fukuda-Parr (2016), stakeholders with local and diffuse interests and political dynamics, including demands for women’s reproductive rights, were completely frustrated. The MDGs led to a detachment between the international agenda and its goals with national and global debates on development, including the debates at the UN conferences of the 1990s (Fukuda-Par 2016).

    Twenty years after the Rio Earth Summit, the United Nations Conference on Sustainable Development (Rio+20) was held in Rio de Janeiro, with extensive stakeholder consultations. The discussion during the conference centred on two main themes: (1) how to build a green economy to achieve sustainable development and lift people out of poverty and (2) how to improve international coordination on sustainable development (Gupta 2017). The Conference defined a set of Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) capable of covering all three dimensions of sustainable development and their interconnections. Five pillars were defined: leaving no one behind; sustainable development at the centre; transforming economies for jobs and inclusive growth; building peace and effective, open and accountable institutions for all; and forging a new global partnership (Browne 2017).

    The working group responsible for defining the post-2015 agenda was organised into a High-Level Task Force chaired by the Prime Minister of the United Kingdom and the Presidents of Liberia and Indonesia (Fukuda-Par 2016). These negotiations would be guided by multilateralism and the participation of social actors in order to establish global sustainable development goals to be approved by the UNGA. To this end, a working group would be set up with the participation of representatives appointed by the member states of the five regional groups of the United Nations (Arajärvi 2018).

    The formulation of the SDGs was consciously set up as a process of political negotiations between states. Although the UN Secretary-General initially appointed a High-Level Task Force of Eminent Persons to make proposals, it was the intergovernmental body - the UN General Assembly’s Open Working Group - that formulated the SDGs, through a process of intense diplomatic negotiations and open debates with various stakeholders over the course of three years. The negotiation process also structured dialogue with the so-called ‘major groups’, convened to represent the interests of specific social groups, including women. The process of drawing up international development priorities involved an unprecedented level of participation by governments, civil society groups, academics, business groups and UN agencies, in intense debates at meetings around the world and on the internet (Fukuda-Par 2016).

    The SDGs include 17 goals and 169 targets and were developed through a participatory process involving governments, civil society, academics, and businesses. They emphasize the need for structural reforms in the global economy and address power structures that perpetuate poverty and inequality. Gender equality and women’s empowerment are treated as multi-dimensional processes, with targets related to gender-based violence, unpaid care work, and sexual and reproductive health. While in the MDGs, gender equality was limited to a single target under Goal 3, focusing on education parity, at the SDGs, gender equality is a standalone goal (Goal 5) with multiple targets addressing violence, unpaid care, reproductive rights, and economic empowerment. Gender issues are also integrated into other goals, reflecting a more holistic approach (Fukuda-Parr 2016).

    However, despite the institutional advancement of the agenda, once again the political and institutional context reaffirmed itself as defining variables. The economic crisis that began in 2008, as well as the various political crises and the devaluation of the human rights agenda in general with the crisis of liberal democracy, impacted the advancement of the agenda, which is reflected in the scarce resources directed to the body.

    Put briefly, feminist theorists, organised feminist groups and NGOs have systematically sought to develop, spread and frame a new paradigm, acting as subversive theorists, brokers and framers. Allied to the political context and the favourable institutional context, these actors influenced states and UN bodies (UNGA, ECOSOC) and managed to get feminist ideas to impact the organisation. In turn, the organisation responded with a better institutional framework, with the creation of various bodies and committees focused on the agenda.

    Institutional change is leveraged by the Beijing Platform, the subsequent creation of UN Women and, finally, the SDGs. A new set of norms and rules complements the rules created until then, which were centred on human rights in general. In turn, programmes become the focus of UN Women (which also contributes to the change in political sentiment and helps frame the feminist agenda).

    And this is the moment in which we find ourselves in terms of institutional change due to the influence of feminist ideas at the UN. In a political and institutional context that is no longer so favourable, in which there is still a long way to go for these ideas to become a consolidated paradigm in all regions of the world, with the capacity to impact and re-frame the political sentiment, the frame and the programmes aimed at gender issues at the UN.

    Conclusion

    In recent decades, institutionalist authors have been examining the possibilities of institutional change. The theoretical assumption of institutional stability as a provider of predictability delimited the theoretical advances in this perspective. From the moment the possibility of change began to be considered, the first wave of studies turned to the abrupt changes that occurred after major external shocks.

    This article discusses the limits on Mahoney and Thelen's model (2010) (based on historical institutionalism) and proposed a new analytical framework taking into account the role of ideas on the process of incremental change. To test the new framework, we analysed the impact of feminist ideas at the UN, historically outlining the advancement of the feminist agenda in the organisation, depending on the political and institutional context. It was noted that the change came in a crescendum, first from the commissions and institutional bodies, through the Women’s Decade and several Conferences, until the creation of UN Women and elaboration of SDGs. In addition, the active role of feminist organisations was highlighted as subversive actors, which over the decades have acted to change the rules. Feminist theories (feminisms), based on the framing carried out by organised feminist groups and other actors managed to bring some of feminists ideas into the UN. This observation highlights the argument of Keck and Sikking (1998) about international advocacy coalitions networks, advancing a particular theme in international agenda. But as noted, feminist theorists and organisations are only one type of actor and cannot explain solely institutional change in the UN. We must pay attention to state actors and decision makers inside the organisation as well.

    Finally, we observed the emergence of political programmes following institutional decisions by member states and the organisation’s decision-making bodies. The gradual modification processes depend on the institutional design in question, the political context, and the actors involved in this scenario, driven by transformative ideas. How an institution is structured depends on how variables operate on this structure, whether endogenous or exogenous factors. However, the design of these programmes does not necessarily result in their implementation, especially regarding the domestic institutions of the states. Therefore, in addition to the agency of the actors analysed, other institutional mechanisms, such as path dependence, diffusion and bricolage, complement the analysis of the process cycle.
