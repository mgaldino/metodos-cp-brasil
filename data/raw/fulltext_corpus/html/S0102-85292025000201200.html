Where is the Sea in International Relations?

Onde está o mar nas Relações Internacionais? O oceano como fronteira teórica e política

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    Resumo

    O oceano representa um paradoxo profundo na disciplina de Relações Internacionais (RI). Apesar de cobrir 71% da superfície da Terra, ele continua sendo notavelmente marginalizado no discurso disciplinar. Nossa provocação central – por que e como esse esquecimento ocorre – revela vieses epistemológicos fundamentais em um campo historicamente ancorado em lógicas terrestres, soberania estatal e noções de fronteira (Schmitt, 2003). A natureza fluida e ilimitada do oceano (Steinberg e Peters, 2015) desafia tais categorizações rígidas, ainda que sua importância política, econômica e cultural seja inegável. Esse descaso suscita perguntas mais profundas: a disciplina contemporânea de RI funciona como uma Pangeia disciplinar – um supercontinente teórico que obscurece imaginários políticos mais fluidos? Com base nas obras de Carvalho e Leira (2023) e de Philip Steinberg e Kimberly Peters (2015), este volume desafia coletivamente a miopia terrestre, oferecendo estruturas alternativas que recentram os espaços e imaginários marítimos na política global, a partir da perspectiva do Sul Global (Hau‘ofa, 2008).

    Palavras-chave
    Oceanos; Teórico; Político; Fronteira; Espaço; Mar; Fronteira

    Introduction

    This special issue is propelled by a fundamental question: ‘Why and How We Forget the Ocean?’ This inquiry demands critical reflection on how the discipline of International Relations (IR) has historically overlooked the oceanic dimension and the political consequences of this omission. Suppose we conceive of IR as an epistemologically Pangaea-centred framework on terrestrial spaces and state sovereignty. In that case, we must interrogate whether this framework imposes conceptual limitations that constrain more imaginative approaches to politics and governance. This collection proposes a paradigm shift, examining international relations through a maritime lens while exploring its theoretical implications and political, economic, and social ramifications.

    The ocean represents a profound paradox in the discipline of IR. Although it covers 71% of the Earth’s surface, it is still remarkably marginalised in disciplinary discourse. Our central provocation – why and how this oblivion occurs – reveals fundamental epistemological biases in a field historically anchored in terrestrial logics, state sovereignty and notions of borders (Schmitt 2003). The fluid, unbounded nature of the ocean (Steinberg, Peters 2015) defies such rigid categorisations, yet its undeniable political, economic, and cultural importance remains. This neglect prompts deeper questions: Does contemporary IR function as a disciplinary Pangaea – a theoretical supercontinent that obscures more fluid political imaginaries? Following the works of Carvalho and Leira (2023), and Philipe Steinberg and Kimberly Peters (2015), this volume collectively challenge this terrestrial myopia, offering alternative frameworks that re-centre maritime spaces and imaginaries in global politics from the perspective of the Global South (Hau‘ofa 2008).

    This special edition is divided into three parts. The first part addresses the marginalisation of the ocean in IR, a topic explored by Bruno Sowden-Carvalho, Marcelo Valença, and Francisco Eduardo Lemos de Matos. As Sowden-Carvalho and Valença argue in Addressing ‘Maritime Aphasia’ in International Relations, IR is structurally predisposed to silence the sea through its terrestrial biases, resulting in what they term ‘maritime aphasia’ – an epistemological and conceptual condition that renders the political centrality of the ocean incomprehensible in dominant paradigms. This silence reflects the discipline’s attachment to Eurocentric and sedentary logics of sovereignty and territoriality, in which the sea appears only as a passive extension of the land-based order. Building on this critique, Francisco Eduardo Lemos de Matos interrogates the land-sea dichotomy in Carl Schmitt on the Move, highlighting how Schmittian spatial thought reinforces the political subordination of the maritime within a tripartite logic of appropriation, division, and exploitation. Henrique Campos de Oliveira’s work, in his turn, argues that the discipline of IR avoids maritime space because the Modern State, focused on security and sedentarism, represses nomadism. The ocean’s exclusion reflects the discipline’s aversion to fluidity and movement.

    The second section broadens this interrogation by addressing the complex entanglements between the Ocean and Ontological Security (Mattos; Cavalcanti), the cultural and symbolic representations of the sea across different communities (Mattos; Guerra; and Nogueira, Lopes, Ferreira & Freitas), and the tension between nomadism and sedentarism in the political formation of the Modern State (Oliveira). In this context, the Ocean is reimagined not merely as a void or conduit, but as a site of memory, displacement, and affective attachment. Roberto Vilchez Yamato and Gustavo Alvim de Góes Bezerra contribute to this reconfiguration in The Third Bank of the Sea: Maritime Traces of Constitutive Outside(r)s, where they draw on the metaphor of the ‘third riverbank’ to rethink the Atlantic as modernity’s constitutive outside—a space marked by slavery, piracy, and unregulated mobility. Their analysis reveals how oceanic space destabilises core binaries of IR thought, such as friend/enemy and order/anarchy, by foregrounding the sea as a terrain of alterity and resistance.

    The last section features contributions from Gilberto de Oliveira, Daniele Dionísio da Silva, and Thauan Santos, who examine the governance of the oceans as both a regulatory mechanism and a platform for political discourse. Dionísio da Silva and Oliveira illuminate how legal frameworks, surveillance systems, and governance architectures not only shape maritime order but also reinforce geopolitical hierarchies and epistemic exclusions, while simultaneously becoming focal points for resistance, counter-mapping, and critical appropriation. Building on these insights, Thauan Santos’ essay, Sailing on Waves Beyond National Sovereign Land Borders, critiques the dominant dichotomy between land and sea that serves as the foundation for global politics. He further develops the concept of International Ocean Relations (IOR), which aims to integrate issues related to the blue economy, climate change, and transnational security. This framework repositions the ocean as a politically generative space that necessitates renewed theoretical and conceptual vocabularies within the discipline of IR.

    The marginalisation of the Ocean is neither accidental nor incidental but rather rooted in IR’s ontological foundations. As Sowden-Carvalho and Valença demonstrate in Addressing ‘Maritime Aphasia’ in International Relations, the discipline suffers from a conceptual limitation where the sea is acknowledged yet silenced by a terrestrial paradigm, denying its ontological constitutive role in global politics. This ‘maritime aphasia’ – the inability to articulate the ocean’s political significance – stems from Eurocentric disciplinary origins privileging sedentary, state-centric models. It also exposes how IR recognises yet interprets the ocean exclusively through terrestrial concepts, thereby denying it an autonomous ontology. This conceptual invisibility compromises not only maritime political understanding but also constrains theoretical and political imagination.

    Similarly, Francisco Eduardo Lemos de Matos’ Carl Schmitt on the Move: Spatial Politics and the (Political) Sacrifice of the Sea offers a critique of Schmitt’s land–sea dichotomy, demonstrating how his tripartite framework – appropriation, division, and exploitation – places maritime spaces in a subordinate position, thereby reinforcing a territorialised conception of sovereignty. These analyses underscore how IR’s theoretical apparatus remains ill-equipped to engage with oceanic fluidity, demanding radical reconsideration of spatial politics. The hegemony of terrestrial approaches in IR originates from the primacy of state territoriality and Cartesian political spatiality, as exemplified in Lemos de Matos’ Schmittian analysis. Schmitt’s paradigm privileges land as the locus of sovereignty while diminishing the sea’s role in the political constitution. This marginalisation reveals both Eurocentric bias and reductionist understandings of land-sea interactions, as demonstrated through critiques employing fluid sovereignty concepts.

    Henrique Campos de Oliveira’s Fluid Boundaries: Reassessing Maritime Spaces and Nomadic Waves in International Relations Theory offers philosophical, psychoanalytic, and anthropological perspectives on why the IR discipline struggles to engage with maritime spaces. The hypothesis posits that the Modern State’s drive towards security and sedentarism relies on the repression of nomadic impulses. This suggests that the nomadism/sedentarism binary positions sedentarism as the desired state of being, dismissing nomadism as an anomaly. The ocean’s exclusion from IR signifies not merely an empirical gap but a conceptual one, reflecting deeper disciplinary anxieties about fluidity, movement, and alterity in global political history.

    In the second section, the authors illustrate the political implications of this theoretical oversight for populations that have been historically affected by colonial violence. Beatriz Rodrigues Bessa Mattos’ Between Nuclear Tests and Rising Sea Levels illustrates how colonial and Cold War legacies violently disrupted Indigenous oceanic worldviews, particularly in the Marshall Islands. Deterministic narratives framing island states as ‘tiny and vulnerable’ erase expansive maritime identities, reducing oceans to mere resources or strategic zones rather than lived spaces. The Pacific islands develop an understanding of Ontological Security, which is connected to a specific territorial imagination intrinsically embedded in water, as proven by the idea of ‘Sea of Islands’ (Hau’ofa 2008).

    Meanwhile, Flavia Guerra’s Thinking Ontological (In)Security with Water delves into Ocean Studies and Blue Humanities to expand Ontological Security Studies (OSS). Guerra argues that IR’s territorial ontology and epistemology reproduces rigid land-sea, inside-outside divisions, neglecting how “bodies of water” intermingle with dry and solid bodies, forming human-ocean fluid arrangements or ‘wet assemblages’ that produce a complex security/insecurity dynamic, and different modes of belonging. Thinking Ontological Security through wet ontology and assemblages allows for the analysis of migrant’s resistance narratives in which water is rather an ally than an obstacle.

    Silvia Garcia Nogueira, Betina Lopes, Ângelo Ferreira and Samuel de Sousa Freitas’ What is the Place of Mar de Timor in Timorese Geopolitics, Culture and Education? examines the Timor Sea’s centrality in national identity construction and geopolitics, demonstrating how maritime border disputes encompass not only geopolitical considerations but also cultural and educational dimensions. The ocean’s cultural and pedagogical aspects challenge state-centric narratives. Nogueira et al. explore how Timor-Leste’s maritime identity informs nation-building, from border disputes to curriculum reform. Here, the sea functions not just as a geopolitical arena but as a cultural archive and pedagogical tool, aligning with Blue Humanities efforts to reconceive oceans as spaces of memory, resistance, and epistemological renewal.

    These interventions prompt us to consider: What alternative political imaginaries arise when we truly regard the ocean? This volume collectively asserts that the sea is not an empty space but rather a dynamic landscape of resistance, governance, and epistemological transformation. Consequently, it demands a fundamental reconfiguration of IR.

    This resonates with Roberto Vilchez Yamato and Gustavo Alvim de Góes Bezerra’s The Third Bank of the Sea, which reimagines the Atlantic as modernity’s ‘constitutive outside’ – a space of piracy, enslavement, and unregulated transit defying friend/enemy binaries. These contributions collectively destabilise IR’s foundational assumptions, revealing how oceans force reckoning with the discipline’s suppressed Others. Yamato and Bezerra revisit Schmitt through The Third Bank of the Sea: Maritime Traces of Constitutive Outside(r)s, reconceptualising the ocean as a space of exclusion and resistance. Employing the ‘third riverbank’ metaphor, they suggest that the sea constitutes not merely a flow and domination space, but also an alternative existence and a means of dissent.

    Daniele Dionisio da Silva and Gilberto Carvalho Oliveira’s Ocean Governance, Maritime Security, and International Relations analyses the intersections of maritime security and governance, illustrating how competing narratives shape ocean governance. Despite IR’s marginalisation of maritime spaces, they remain central to the formulation of international security policy and regulation. The ocean emerges as a contested terrain for governance and security. Da Silva and Oliveira examine how competing narratives shape maritime regulation, ranging from piracy to environmental protection, demonstrating that ocean governance requires interdisciplinary engagement with law, ecology, and political theory, thereby resisting singular IR paradigms.

    Thauan Santos’ Sailing on Waves Beyond National Sovereign Land Borders critiques the global policy’s land-sea dichotomy, advocating for ‘International Ocean Relations’ (IOR) that integrates the blue economy, climate change, and transnational security. These chapters illustrate oceans as active global political participants demanding new theoretical vocabularies. The volume’s structure reflects this problematic’s multiple dimensions. Santos’ Sailing on Waves investigates the emergence of international ocean relations and the blue economy as a new global paradigm, arguing that maritime incorporation into contemporary political agendas challenges terrestrial sovereignty boundaries and requires reconfiguration of the IR analytical category.

    Conclusion: Toward a Maritime Turn in IR?

    This collection invites critical reflection on the disciplinary limits of IR and the imperative to incorporate oceans as constitutive elements of global politics. By rescuing maritime spaces from conceptual oblivion, the contributors challenge epistemological boundaries while pioneering novel approaches to twenty-first-century international relations. The ocean, far from being a passive backdrop or mere resource frontier, emerges as a dynamic space of governance, resistance, and epistemological rupture—one that demands radical rethinking of IR’s terrestrial foundations.

    The contributions collectively expose the consequences of IR’s ‘maritime aphasia’ (Sowden-Carvalho & Valença), revealing how the discipline’s Eurocentric, state-centric frameworks have systematically obscured the ocean’s political, economic, and cultural significance. From the violent erasure of Indigenous maritime worldviews (Mattos) to the subversion of territorial sovereignty by migrant ‘wet assemblages’ (Guerra), the volume demonstrates that the sea is not an empty void but a contested terrain where alternative political imaginaries take shape (Matos). The ocean’s fluidity resists rigid categorisations, forcing IR to confront its suppressed Others—whether in the form of piracy, unregulated transit (Yamato & Bezerra), or the transnational challenges of climate change and blue economy governance (Santos).

    This volume not only incorporates the ocean into the field of international relations but also questions the foundations of this discipline. By examining the sea as a theoretical, political, and cultural force, the authors argue in favour of a maritime turning point. This paradigmatic reorientation shifts the focus from land-centred logics to a fluid and relational understanding of global politics. Such a reorientation requires an interdisciplinary engagement that draws on the insights of blue humanities, critical geography, postcolonial studies, and legal theory to reconceptualise sovereignty, security, and governance beyond land-based frameworks. The fundamental question, then, is not merely why we forget the ocean, but what IR becomes when we remember it. If the discipline continues to operate as a “disciplinary Pangaea,” it risks irrelevance in a world where rising sea levels, maritime disputes, and oceanic mobilities redefine political space. A maritime turn compels IR to embrace uncertainty, movement, and hybridity—concepts that its sedentary epistemology has long marginalised. The ocean, long relegated to the periphery, must now be recognised as central to the futures of international theory and practice.
