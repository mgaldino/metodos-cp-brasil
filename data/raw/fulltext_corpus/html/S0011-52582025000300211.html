Inside the Fog: Assessing Foreign Policy Expertise in the Brazilian Federal Senate*

Por Dentro da Névoa: Avaliando a Expertise em Política Externa no Senado Federal Brasileiro

Dans la Brume: Évaluation de l’Expertise en Politique Étrangère au Sénat fédéral Brésilien

Por Dentro de la Niebla: Evaluando la Expertise en Política Exterior en el Senado Federal Brasileño

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    This study aims to explore the contributions of the Brazilian Senate members within the Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE), focusing on key elements for assessing expertise. Analyzing 21,994 interventions from 2013 to 2023, obtained via the specialized R language package senatebR, we employ Natural Language Processing techniques to extract metrics across four dimensions: Lexical Complexity, Syntactic Structure, Style, and Conceptual Depth. Using principal component analysis (PCA), we develop a specialization index and conduct a K-means cluster analysis, revealing three groups: Prolix, Expert, and Concise. Some senators, referred to as “Prolix”, employ complex language with varied sentence structures, while others, labeled as “Experts”, exhibit sophisticated syntax and rich vocabulary. Additionally, a group labeled “Concise” prefers simpler language with fewer sentence variations. This research identifies distinct communication styles within the committees, shedding light on how senators convey their positions and engage in deliberations.

    senate; committees; expertise; natural language processing; expertise index

    Introduction

    “If reports and CVs were sufficient, we could excuse the senators and even ambassadors from this hearing. However, I believe the hearing serves the purpose of allowing us to assess the Ambassador’s intentions and their stance on issues that hold significance for us as senators” (Abreu, 20201). These remarks were made by Senator Kátia Abreu during Fabio Mendes Marzano’s confirmation hearing for the position of Brazil’s permanent delegate to the United Nations in Geneva. Following these comments, she further stated, “…therefore, I want to emphasize that, without the existence of a hearing, the approval of ambassadors would not be possible. While a CV holds significance, it doesn’t encompass everything.” (Abreu, 2020).

    During the hearing, the Senator expressed dissatisfaction as she felt the candidate did not adequately address her question about the Southern Common Market (MERCOSUR) - European Union agreement. Marzano’s deviation from the subject displeased the Senator2, leading her to exclaim, “I am sure Itamaraty is turning into a house of horrors, where ambassadors can’t open their mouths and give their opinions” (Abreu, 2020). Senator Kátia Abreu’s case illustrates two crucial aspects for this research: firstly, her background as former Minister of Agriculture and former president of the Brazilian National Confederation of Agriculture and Livestock (CNA) highlights her keen interest in international trade, particularly concerning the potential MERCOSUR-European Union agreement. Secondly, the Senator explicitly emphasized the commission’s role in exercising horizontal control over the Executive, even in matters related to foreign policy.

    Regarding this last element, during the plenary vote on Marzano’s confirmation, Senator Major Olímpio revisited the episode with Kátia Abreu from the pulpit of the upper house and urged his colleagues to vote against the nomination: “I implore the senators, on behalf of the Senate’s dignity, not to endorse this nomination. If the Senate approves this individual, we are negating our fundamental existence and disrespecting each and every one of us. Let us collectively vote against it for the sake of the entire Senate” (Olimpio, 20213).

    While the call for a vote against the nomination might seem unusual initially, Senator Olímpio concluded his speech by strongly expressing his differences with Foreign Minister Ernesto Araújo. In his words, “…to come here and express what was said in such a grotesque manner, claiming to be on the Chancellor’s team – to hell with the Chancellor! Let there be respect for the senators!” (Olimpio, 2021). Towards the session’s end, the Senate rejected Ambassador Fabio Mendes Marzano’s nomination to serve as Brazil’s permanent delegate in Geneva with a vote of thirty-seven against nine. These brief narratives highlight the significance of considering the Senate’s involvement in foreign policy matters. The cases herein mentioned clearly assert the legislative’s upper house political oversight at various stages of a confirmation process.

    It is noteworthy that within the subfield of Foreign Policy Analysis, the legislative branch has received limited attention, often due to assumptions of delegation or abdication (Lima; Santos, 2001; Duval, 2022). These perceptions are frequently based on the alleged lack of expertise among Senate members (Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2014) or the perceived absence of electoral returns (Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2014). The assessment of electoral impact, or lack thereof, recalls Ulysses Guimarães’ famous words: “Itamaraty4 only takes (or gives) votes in Burundi” (Guimarães as cited in Lopes & Faria, 2014), which have since come under scrutiny.

    This assessment is particularly evident in the gradual inclusion of international issues in electoral discussions (Faria, 2008; Lopes & Faria, 2014; Contrera, Hebling, 2017; Belém Lopes; Carvalho; Santos, 2022). Specific concerns such as perceptions of Chinese presence in some areas of the country further intensify the discussion (Campello & Urdinez, 2021; Belém Lopes; Carvalho; Santos, 2022). In light of the challenge within existing literature and while asserting the legislative’s disinterest or lack of experience in foreign policy matters, this study aims to unravel the intricacies of senators’ contributions within the upper house’s Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE).

    Therefore, this study tackles several interconnected challenges: the insufficient attention provided in legislative studies to the role of the Senate, the neglect of its involvement in foreign policy investigations, and the relatively modest emphasis on the contributions of committees to legislative dynamics (Santos, Canello, 2016; Silva, Spohr, 2016; Rubiatti, 2020; Santos, Lopes, 2022). In light of these challenges, our objective is to align the agendas of legislative studies, which predominantly focus on the Senate, with an investigation into committee systems and their intersections with foreign policy studies.

    The central research question is: what elements can be identified to measure expertise within the CRE)? To address this, the study analyzes Shorthand Notes extracted from 444 committee meetings held between 2013 and 2023. These notes were collected using an R language package (senatebR).

    This article employs a mixed-methods design. First, we use Natural Language Processing techniques to extract text metrics on expertise based on four distinct dimensions: 1) Lexical Complexity and Specialized Vocabulary, 2) Syntactic Structure and Textual Cohesion, 3) Style and Expressiveness, and 4) Conceptual and Argumentative Depth. We developed a composite specialization index based on these metrics through Principal Component Analysis (PCA). Subsequently, we identified three distinct groups using cluster analysis based on the conjunction of metrics. Finally, with the results obtained from a K-Means Cluster Analysis, we conducted a set of case studies for qualitative validation of the quantitative findings (Goertz; Mahoney, 2012; Goertz, 2020).

    We have organized this article into three sections. We begin by diving into the debate surrounding the legislative’s role in foreign policy, exploring concepts such as Abdication, Delegation, and Balance. We then shift our focus to the theoretical discourse on the role of the commission system, identifying the three main theoretical traditions in the field and emphasizing their informational perspective. We proceed to detailing the origin of the data, and the metrics used to compose the expertise indicator. This article then briefly discusses the role of the dimensionality reduction technique, introducing and contextualizing cluster analysis.

    The data and methods section outlines the criteria used to select our cases, setting the stage for the next section that delves into the results and discussion. We detail significant insights from the analyzed meetings, the findings from our PCA and Cluster Analysis. We then delve into the case studies. Finally, we present our concluding remarks and outline the emerging research agenda resulting from this article’s findings.

    Legislative and Foreign Policy: Abdication, Delegation or Balance

    “In short, the foreign policy decision-making process is characterized by the abdication of authority from Congress to the Executive5” (Lima; Santos, 2001: 132). Delegation refers to the transfer of authority from one entity (in this case, Congress) to another (typically the executive branch), allowing the latter to make decisions and implement policies on behalf of the former (McCubbins and Schwartz, 1984). Abdication, on the other hand, implies a voluntary relinquishment of authority by legislators (Lima; Santos, 2001), often due to perceived deficiencies in expertise or electoral incentives (Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2004).

    The literature on abdication contends that senators, confronted with the intricacies linked to a lack of technical knowledge, voluntarily abstain from engaging in foreign policy affairs (Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2014). However, a contrasting research agenda indicates that there are not only institutional mechanisms for legislative involvement, but also empirical evidence of active engagement (Schmitt, 2011; Anastasia et al., 2012; Diniz, 2016; Silva, 2016; Santiago, 2019; Diniz, Ribeiro, 2023).

    McCubbins and Schwartz (1984) posit that what might seem like an omission is, in fact, a rational preference for a specific form of oversight. According to these authors, the legislative employs an oversight policy designed to detect and address violations of the executive branch in pursuing its goals. They distinguish between two types of monitoring: the first, termed “fire-alarm oversight”, and the second, referred to as “police-patrol oversight”.

    The initial approach is decentralized and involves a system of rules, procedures, and informal practices empowering individual citizens and organized interest groups to scrutinize executive decisions. Upon identifying a violation, these actors can seek solutions or appeal to agencies, courts, and Congress to address the perceived wrongdoing. In this context, rather than actively seeking out issues, the legislative metaphorically “places fire alarms on street corners, distributes firefighters throughout the neighborhood and sometimes mobilizes its own hose and ladder in response to an alarm” (McCubbins and Schwartz, 1984).

    Conversely, the term “police patrol” refers to monitoring carried out under the legislature’s own authority through a centralized, active, and direct approach to examine executive activities. The objective is to detect and prevent violations of legislative objectives, thereby discouraging such transgressions. This patrol can take various forms, including i) research involving document analysis, ii) requests for scientific studies, iii) field observation, and iv) hearings designed not only to question authorities’ actions but also to listen to citizens potentially affected by the violations.

    The skepticism surrounding the legislature’s involvement in foreign policy issues emanates from four aspects: 1) the debate on the concept of insulation and the central role exercised by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs (Nunes, 1997); 2) the belief that international issues lack the electoral traction to influence votes significantly; and 3) the limited constitutional space granted to the legislature to engage with this matter (Lima & Santos, 2001; Alcântara, 2001; Neves, 2003; Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2004); 4) the demand for expertise arising from the intricate nature of international issues, potentially distancing members of parliament from this policy domain (Lima & Santos, 2001; Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2014)

    Nevertheless, several studies have shifted perspectives, introducing greater nuance to these points and prompting the research community to reassess the role of members of parliament in foreign policy matters. Regarding the first aspect, concepts such as “pluralization” (Silva; Spécie; Vitale, 2010; Farias, Ramanzini Júnior, 2015), “detachment” (Faria, 2012), and “presidentialization” (Cason, Power, 2009) have emerged as efforts to divert attention from Itamaraty in the foreign policy system (Silva et al., 2010; Schmitt, 2011; Faria et al., 2015; Almeida, 1992; Onuki; Oliveira, 2010).

    On the second, the use of international issues to garner political support, and ultimately, electoral gains – has been increasingly evident, as evidenced by recent empirical findings (Campello et al., 2021). Regarding the third element, mapping exercises – such as those conducted by Anastasia et al. (2012) and Santos et al. (2023) – have identified institutional instruments and potential arenas for legislative action, offering deeper insights into the parliamentary role in foreign policy. Finally, this article contributes to this ongoing debate by challenging the fourth aspect: the assumption that members of parliament are disengaged from international issues due to a lack of expertise.

    Legislative Studies: The Informational Role of Committees

    From the outset, it is essential to consider the pivotal role of the legislative committees in the initial analysis of bills related to their respective thematic areas. These committees undertake key functions, including structuring scheduling issues and setting processing timelines. According to the Internal Rules of the Federal Senate and Federal Constitution, the CRE) is tasked with (FC, Article 49, I; FC, Article 49, II; Article 49, III; FC, Article 52, IV; IR Article 103):

    1. Providing opinions on international acts and relations,

    2. Offering pronouncements on foreign trade,

    3. Nominating individuals to lead permanent diplomatic missions in foreign governments and international organizations,

    4. Initiating requests for votes of censure, applause, or similar actions if they pertain to international public events or acts,

    5. Authorizing the passage of foreign forces through national territory,

    6. Granting authorization for the President or Vice President to leave the country, and

    7. Addressing related matters.

    In the realm of legislative studies, it is possible to categorize the research agenda concerning committees into three main schools: distributive (Shepsle & Weingast, 1994; Shepsle, 1995), partisan (McCubbins & Kiewiet, 1991; Cox and McCubbins, 1993), and informational (Fenno, 1971; Krehbiel, 1991). Literature acknowledging the partisan nature of legislative studies examines the role political parties play, such as in appointing members to committees.

    This school underscores the influence of political parties over their representatives, often exercised through the agenda-setting authority vested in caucus leaders. Proponents of the party model emphasize the central role of parties in shaping the parliamentary agenda and the legislative process. These entities have the capacity to regulate members’ actions, create incentives for cooperation, and, through party discipline, foster a stable environment for legislative decision-making (Santos; Almeida, 2005).

    The distributist perspective focuses on the electoral advantages that committee membership can yield for members of parliament. Representatives seek committee positions to enhance their capacity to channel resources to their constituencies (Mayhew, 1974), thereby strengthening their electoral standing. Unlike the party-centric approach, distributists focus on individual members of parliament within this competitive arena. These legislators engage in strategic calculations, continually assessing the electoral benefits of their actions, particularly within the committees. Their interests direct their actions and drive them to pursue cooperative arrangements, often through coordination with other stakeholders in a process responsible for generating a “market” of votes, a practice known as logrolling (Santos; Almeida, 2005; Santos, Canello, 2016).

    Finally, this research is grounded in the informational perspective, which emphasizes the role of committees in the development of parliament members’ expertise. Frequent involvement in these spaces allow members to acquire and refine the skills necessary to address the committee’s specialized issues. This development of skills, in turn, enhances their effectiveness in pursuing political objectives. In a decision-making environment that hinges on qualified assessments of highly technical and specialized issues, accumulated knowledge and expertise become invaluable resources (Gilligan, Krehbiel, 1987; 1989; Austen-Smith, Riker, 1987; Krehbiel, 1991; Santos; Almeida, 2005; Santos e Almeida 2011; Santos, Canello, 2016).

    Under the assumption of specialization, political parties would have little incentive to promote high turnover within committees, instead favoring the principle of seniority. In a context, where “seniority is rank”, those with status of “deans” stand to gain from their accumulated expertise. Over time, information initially held as private becomes a collective good through public hearings, discussions, and deliberations, potentially perpetuating the cycle of specialization to which committee “newcomers” are exposed (Austen-Smith e Riker, 1987; Gilligan e Krehbiel, 1987; 1989; Santos; Almeida, 2005; Santos, Canello, 2016).

    The distinction between the informational and the distributive perspectives lies in the argument that the former posits that committees are not primarily engaged in logrolling but rather in cultivating expertise and disseminating information (Santos; Almeida, 2005). Thus, as stated by Shepsle and Weingast (1994, pp. 159-160), “committees may be powerful in a legislature not (only) because they monopolize agenda power but (also) because they monopolize information and expertise”. In an environment where political decisions carry inherent uncertainty, access to high-quality information and the ability to understand the relationship between choices and outcomes provide significant advantage for members of parliament (Austen-Smith e Riker, 1987; Gilligan e Krehbiel, 1987; 1989; Santos; Almeida, 2005; Santos, Canello, 2016).

    Given these insights, committees inherently aim to advance distributive policies, bolster political party influence within the legislative arena, or foster expertise. That said, we maintain our position that the legislative holds the capacity to exert influence on foreign policy, in this case, through interventions in the CRE, by leveraging its expertise. Building on this theoretical debate, we present the following argument:

    Argument: Leveraging the informational capacity of committees allows for the identification of expertise among the senators who serve on them.

    In this context, “Expertise” refers to the specialized knowledge, skills, and experience in a given domain. Within the CRE, expertise encompasses a deep understanding of foreign policy, including international relations, diplomacy, national security, and military affairs. Additionally, it involves the adept capacity to analyze and effectively communicate solutions to the challenges facing the nation in these spheres.

    Informed Decision-Making: Why Measure Legislative Expertise?

    The intricate nature of discussions within the CRE necessitates a thorough and all-encompassing approach to assessing the expertise of participating senators. The formulation of specific metrics is crucial to capture the subtleties inherent in parliamentary discourse, surpassing mere word counts and delving into crucial aspects of verbal expression (Benoit et al., 2019).

    Measuring legislative expertise holds paramount importance in contemporary governance and policy-making landscapes. At its core, expertise among legislators serves as a cornerstone for effective decision-making and policy formulation. A knowledgeable and skilled legislative body is better equipped to navigate intricate policy challenges, understand complex issues, and develop well-informed solutions. Moreover, expertise fosters robust debate, critical analysis, and the creation of legislation that addresses the multifaceted needs of society (Lupia, Mccubbins, 1994; Callander, 2008; Hamm et at, 2011; Makse, 2017; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019).

    Furthermore, legislative expertise is vital for accountability in democratic systems. Citizens entrust representatives with decision-making responsibilities that impact their lives, and expertise ensures that these decisions are grounded in sound knowledge and evidence. When legislators possess specialized expertise they are better positioned to justify policy choices, engage in meaningful dialogue with constituents, and be held accountable for their decisions (Lupia, Mccubbins, 1994; Callander, 2008; Hamm et al., 2011; Makse, 2017).

    The quality of legislation directly correlates to lawmakers’ expertise. Those with specialized knowledge can draft more precise, effective, and comprehensive laws that address the complexities of modern society. Moreover, expertise enables them to critically evaluate legislative proposed, identify potential pitfalls or unintended consequences, as well as propose amendments or alternatives that enhance the overall quality of the legislative process (Lupia, Mccubbins, 1994; Callander, 2008; HAMM et at, 2011; Makse, 2017; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019).

    Ultimately, measuring legislative expertise is not just about assessing individual capabilities but about fostering a culture of informed decision-making, public trust, and effective representation. By ensuring that legislators possess the necessary knowledge and skills, societies can uphold the principles of democratic governance, promote effective policy outcomes, and better serve citizens (Lupia, Mccubbins, 1994; Callander, 2008; Hamm et at, 2011; Makse, 2017; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019).

    Developing a measure of expertise through natural language processing (NLP) based on legislators’ interventions is crucial for several reasons. Firstly, it allows for the objective assessment of legislators’ capabilities, moving beyond subjective impressions or traditional metrics such as seniority or political experience (Oliveira, 2014). By analyzing speech content, NLP can assess the depth of knowledge, the clarity of arguments, and the effectiveness of communication (Jackson, Moulinier, 2007; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019; Benoit et al., 2019).

    Secondly, a measure of NLP-derived metrics enables comparative analysis across legislators, committees, or legislative sessions, helping identify patterns, trends, and disparities in expertise that can inform decision-making processes and resource allocation within legislative bodies (Jackson, Moulinier, 2007; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019; Benoit et al., 2019; Grimmer et al., 2022).

    Thirdly, NLP-based measures of expertise offer a scalable and efficient approach to assessing large volumes of legislative data. As legislative proceedings become increasingly digitized and the number of online repositories grow, manual analysis of interventions becomes impractical. NLP algorithms can process vast amounts of text data quickly and accurately, extracting relevant information and generating meaningful insights efficiently and accurately (Jackson, Moulinier, 2007; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019; Benoit et al., 2019; Grimmer et al., 2022).

    Moreover, developing a measure of expertise through NLP fosters transparency and accountability in the legislative process. It enables citizens, policymakers, and other stakeholders to monitor their representatives’ contributions, evaluate their effectiveness, and hold them accountable for their actions.

    Leveraging NLP for measuring expertise in legislators’ interventions offers a data-driven approach to enhancing legislative performance, fostering informed decision-making, and strengthening democratic governance. It empowers stakeholders with valuable insights into the competence and effectiveness of their elected representatives, ultimately contributing to the advancement of democratic principles and the improvement of legislative outcomes (Jackson, Moulinier, 2007; Fernandes, Goplerud, 2019; Benoit et al., 2019; Grimmer et al., 2022).

    Research Design

    Data

    To facilitate this research project, the data were obtained using a specialized R language package named “senatebR” (Santos, 2024). This comprehensive package served as the backbone for consolidating diverse data collection protocols essential for the investigation. The dataset encompasses a wide array of crucial information pertaining to the parliamentary landscape and activities. For this article, we mobilize a subset of this data relating to:

    1. Committee Meeting Details: Comprehensive information concerning committee meetings, encompassing the topics discussed, decisions made, and other pertinent details;

    2. Shorthand Notes: A repository of shorthand notes from each meeting, capturing nuanced aspects and contributing to a richer dataset;

    To extract data from Senate committee meetings, we used the function `info_data_reuniao_comissao()`. This function retrieves information on Senate committee meetings based on specified committee codes and a defined date range. For our study, we focused on the CRE committee, which is defined by the code 54. The date range of interest spans from January 1, 2013, to December 1, 2023. To implement this, we define the objects `start_date <- “2013-01-01”` and `end_date <- “2023-12-01”`. Using these parameters, we execute the following command: `df_meetings <- info_data_reuniao_comissao(54, start_date, end_date)`.

    After collecting data on the committee meetings, we extracted shorthand notes from the database using the function `extrair_notas_taquigraficas(codigos)`. This function accesses the Senate’s shorthand notes page and retrieves the necessary content. It requires the meeting codes, which were stored in the vector `codigos <- df_meetings$codigos`. Hence, the shorthand notes extraction was performed with the following command: `df_conteudos <- extrair_notas_taquigraficas(codigos)`.

    The resulting dataset includes speeches delivered within CRE from 2013 to 2023, providing a comprehensive exploration of the parliamentary landscape over this period. It Notably our analysis is conducted at the level of 21,994 interventions per political actor, including senators per meeting.

    The utilization of the senatebR package underscore our commitment to methodological rigor, enabling a nuanced and thorough investigation into the intricate dynamics of parliamentary activities. This innovative approach not only enhances the reliability of our findings but also sets a precedent for future political research. Additionally, it strengthens our conditions to provide empirical contributions for foreign policy debates at the policy-making level.

    Assessing the complexity of interventions: Natural Language Processing

    It is worth noting that exploring innovative approaches to measuring expertise is particularly productive, especially given the intricate nature of foreign policy texts. This challenge – assessing complexity – has been addressed in prior research, as documented by Benoit et al. (2019). In our study, we have developed a composite expertise index that spans four distinct dimensions of discourse. This index aims to provide a comprehensive assessment of the proficiency and depth of senators’ interventions during parliamentary discussions. The four dimensions are: 1) Lexical Complexity and Specialized Vocabulary; 2) Syntactic Structure and Textual Cohesion; 3) Style and Expressiveness; 4) Conceptual and Argumentative Depth.

    The first, “Lexical Complexity and Specialized Vocabulary” (1), evaluates senators’ proficiency in using technical terminology and sophisticated language. The second dimension, “Syntactic Structure and Textual Cohesion” (2), assesses the diversity of sentence structures and the logical flow of ideas, ensuring effective communication. The third dimension, “Style and Expressiveness” (3), examines senators’ individual communication styles by analyzing pronoun and modal verb ratios, providing insights into their expressive preferences. The fourth and final dimension, “Conceptual and Argumentative Depth” (4), measures the richness of ideas using metrics such as Word Entropy and Adverb Frequency, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the depth and complexity of senators’ arguments. By combining these four dimensions, this composite index serves as a holistic measure of expertise, delivering nuanced insights into the multifaceted nature of parliamentary discourse.     Discussion: What are the Implications of Measuring Expertise?

    Theoretical Implications of Expertise: Legislative Participation and Foreign Policy

    The use of the legislative expertise index provides an empirical foundation for theories on the delegation of authority and legislative participation in foreign policy. Traditionally, literature on delegation of authority (Oliveira, 2003; Oliveira, 2014) has emphasized the transfer of responsibilities from members of parliament to the Executive, often predicated on the confidence in the Executive’s expertise in international relations. This work highlights that expertise exists within the legislature, albeit unevenly distributed. By employing this index, future research can more precisely investigate how and why certain members of parliament exert greater influence over various aspects of foreign policy.

    Given that some members of parliament possess the expertise to engage substantively in foreign policy issues – and considering the existence of institutional mechanisms for legislative oversight (Anastasia, Mendonça, & Almeida, 2012; Spohr & Silva, 2018; Spohr, 2019; Santos & Belém Lopes, 2022), this literature allows for a more nuanced analysis of the Senate’s influence on foreign policy. Legislators with greater specialization can provide in-depth analyses during debates, influence policymaking by questioning executive decisions, proposing alternative policies, and shaping legislative agendas. Those with greater expertise can take on a leading role in setting the foreign policy agenda and ensuring effective government oversight actions in international affairs, thereby enhancing the legislature’s ability to exercise effective foreign policy oversight.

    The ability to quantify and analyze legislative expertise enriches academic understanding of the legislature’s role in international politics while also providing critical insights for improving democratic practices by strengthening informed and deliberative processes.

    Institutional Aspects and Expertise: an Inferential Research Agenda

    One of the main motivations for investing in research on legislative expertise stems from the perception that low legislative interest and participation in foreign policy is due to a lack of knowledge among members of parliament. However, as previously noted, expertise is not entirely absent and unevenly distributed. The framework developed in this article enables the identification of varying levels of expertise among senators and other political actors. This framework allows for the identification of distinct levels of expertise and their association with institutional elements (Santos; Almeida, 2005, Centeno, 2022).

    Factors such as (i) the selection of members of parliament for committee positions; (ii) the tenure in committee positions; and (iii) re-election prospects, which emerge as components in discussions of expertise. The assignment of members of parliament to committees can directly influence their ability to develop specialized knowledge on foreign policy issues. Longevity and continuity in office, for example, are crucial for legislators to accumulate experience and establish influential networks, enhancing their effectiveness in committees (Pereira & Müller, 2000; Müller, 2005; Rocha, & Barbosa, 2008; Müller, 2011; Centeno, 2022)

    The selection of Senate committee members is influenced by both party criteria and the experience and interest of the legislators. Political parties play a significant role in these appointments, often prioritizing political loyalty and the expertise of candidates. Selection based on pre-existing expertise allows committees to start with a high level of specialized knowledge, essential for effective oversight and deliberation on complex foreign policy issues. Members’ prior experience in specific areas can accelerate the development of technical knowledge and argumentative skills within committees (Santos, 2001; Rocha, Santos & Almeida, 2005; Santos & Canello, 2016; Lemos & Power, 2013; Centeno, 2022).

    The length of senators’ terms, which in Brazil is eight years, provides a significant opportunity to develop expertise. This extended period allows members to gain a comprehensive understanding of the issues addressed by the committee. Additionally, prolonged time in office facilitates to gain and refine knowledge, enabling legislators to closely follow developments in foreign policy over several years. However, the effectiveness of this continuity depends on the stability of committee appointments. Frequent changes in committee composition can disrupt the accumulation of knowledge and hinder the development of expertise (Figueiredo, Limongi, & Valente, 1999; Centeno, 2022).

    The possibility of re-election is another factor contributing to the formation of legislative expertise. Members who anticipate long-term political careers are more likely to invest in acquiring specialized knowledge, recognizing its potential impact on future mandates. Re-election enables the continuity and deepening of work initiated in previous terms, facilitating the development of well-informed and grounded policies (Centeno, 2022).

    The next stage of this research involves overcoming measurement challenges related to expertise and conducting inferential analyses to explore the relationship between expertise and legislative participation. This approach addresses the criticism of the relationship between expertise and low legislative participation, and establishes a research agenda that empirically examines the institutional effects on legislative expertise in foreign policy, offering substantial contributions to both the academic and practical fields.

    Final remarks

    The primary goal of this first initiative is to showcase the feasibility of leveraging NLP to extract evidence from qualitative data. We aim to illustrate how such efforts can provide novel insights into the legislature’s role in foreign policy. In this endeavor, we construct a composite expertise index using PCA. Subsequently, employing cluster analysis, we discern three distinct groups by leveraging the combined metrics within the developed index.

    The first group, labeled “Prolixers”, is characterized by a high average density of complex words and a relatively high sentence variety, reflecting the use of language rich in complex terms and a diverse sentence structure. The second, named “Experts”, demonstrates an average sentence complexity and high word entropy, indicating interventions with a more complex syntactic structure and greater vocabulary diversity. Lastly, “Concise” shows a moderate density of words, a relatively smaller variety of sentences, and a tendency towards more direct and simple communication.

    Cluster analysis provided supplementary categories for the trends identified by PCA. By categorizing individuals according to similarities that may not have been fully captured by principal components alone, this methodology offered a more nuanced perspective on the intricacies inherent in the discourses.

    By identifying clusters, it is possible to hypothesize about potential political or contextual influences that contribute to the variations in the senators’ speeches, and consequently to the differences in expertise. The qualitative interpretation of the formed clusters aimed to uncover insights into how different groups of senators express themselves in their interventions in committees. This analysis involved evaluations of topics, rhetorical styles, and distinct linguistic preferences.

    In light of the results presented here and the argument that the legislative branch plays a significant role in foreign policy, it is evident that senators possess expertise comparable to other foreign policy actors, despite the distinctions between them identified by our grouping.

    We acknowledge that interpreting the classification as “experts” based on the high levels of PC1 relies on the specific characteristics of original variables contributing to this principal component and on the contextual considerations surrounding these results. Since PC1 is a linear combination of variables that captures patterns of variation in the original data, inferring expertise from these patterns requires a nuanced approach.

    While indicators such as textual characteristics may be associated with a more elaborate, complex, and diverse discourse, expertise in a political context is not solely defined by linguistic features. Rather, it encompasses a profound understanding and knowledge of specific domains, along with the ability to articulate this expertise effectively. Therefore, the next crucial step in enhancing the external validity of the measure would involve incorporating external metrics of knowledge and performance in specific areas into the index.

    Our forthcoming research endeavors will involve refining the measure to explore how levels of expertise may vary depending on the types of meetings and the topics under discussion. Despite this challenge, we remain committed to seeking insights into the determinants of expertise.

    table_chartTable 1   : Metrics
    Dimension Metric Description Level of analysis Expertise measurement
    1 Density of Words Measures the proportion of words with more than 6 characters, pointing to the presence of complex terms. Lexical Captures presence of complex terms.
    2 Sentence Variety Calculates the ratio of unique sentences to total words, revealing the diversity of sentence structures. Syntactic Indicates the ability to convey information in various sentence formats.
    2 Average Sentence Computes the average number of words per sentence, providing an indication of sentence structure complexity. Syntactic Indicates the syntactic complexity and structure of sentences used.
    3 Modal Verb Ratio Calculates the ratio of modal verbs to total words, identifying the use of tentative or expressive language. Lexical/Syntactic Reflects the expression of possibility, necessity, or intention in the discourse.
    3 Passive Verb Ratio Measures the ratio of passive verbs to total words, indicating the use of passive voice constructions. Syntactic Reflects a deliberate choice of sentence structure and style in communication.
    4 Word Entropy Assesses the entropy6 of word distribution, indicating the diversity of vocabulary usage. Lexical Reveals the richness and variety of language employed.
