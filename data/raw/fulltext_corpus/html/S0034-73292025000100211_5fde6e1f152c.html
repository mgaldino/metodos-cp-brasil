Before the Alarm Sounds: How Parliaments Manage Silence, Salience, and Control in Foreign Affairs

AuthorshipSCIMAGO INSTITUTIONS RANKINGS

    Abstract

    What if the alarm never goes off? This article rethinks legislative oversight in foreign policy through the lens of proactive calibration, a model that transcends classic police patrol and fire alarm paradigms. Drawing on over 70,777 legislative records from the Brazilian Senate (1985–2025), we uncover patterns of institutional vigilance in which parliaments internally generate salience and exert control. In this framework, requests are not mere reactions, but deliberate signals of political concern and symbolic assertion. Unlike the fire alarm model, where external actors trigger institutional response, here the legislature itself becomes the initiator of the alarm, reframing visibility and activating scrutiny on its own terms. Silence, therefore, is not passivity but strategic latency; activation is not always coercive, but expressive and anticipatory. Our findings challenge dominant assumptions about legislative irrelevance in foreign affairs and propose a novel theory of how parliaments manufacture visibility, manage discretion, and exert control.

    Keywords:
    Legislature; Foreign Policy; Police Patrol; Fire Alarm; Proactive Calibration

    Introduction

    On June 16, 2015, during a Senate confirmation hearing, Senator Ronaldo Caiado threatened to obstruct proceedings unless the government responded to congressional demands over Venezuela. His warning, “If there’s one thing I know, it’s the rules. And another thing I know is how to obstruct a session” (Agência Senado, 2015), captures a broader pattern: legislative oversight in foreign policy often emerges not as constant vigilance, but as selective assertion under executive dominance and low transparency (Ribeiro et al. 2021).

    Parliamentary silence should not be conflated with passivity. Rather, it may embody strategic latency, a mode of anticipatory monitoring and rhetorical signaling that prepares the ground for later oversight. This article develops a conceptual framework to analyze how legislatures manage silence, salience, and control, based on 70,777 Senate records (1985–2025) and targeted case studies.

    We revise classic models of oversight, “police patrol” and “fire alarm” (McCubbins and Schwartz 1984), by showing that parliaments can also manufacture visibility from within. In this perspective, oversight is calibrated: silence reflects readiness, salience signals concern, and control imposes costs and constraints rather than outright vetoes.

    Our contribution is twofold. Empirically, we document how the Brazilian Senate, often seen as marginal in foreign affairs, uses petitions, hearings, and procedural tools to signal vigilance and contest executive discretion. Theoretically, we recast legislative oversight as proactive calibration, a model that captures the graded, strategic nature of parliamentary engagement in high-discretion policy arenas.

    The Brazilian Legislature and Foreign Policy: Apathy, Delegation, and Control

    The foundational literature on Brazilian foreign policy has provided a compelling and historically grounded account of congressional delegation, particularly in trade and institutional diplomacy. Pioneering works by Lima and Santos (2001) and Oliveira (2003) argued that legislative abdication was not a symptom of dysfunction, but a strategic response to post-authoritarian imperatives including technocratic centralization, capacity asymmetries, and developmentalist pressures.

    Indeed, as Onuki and Oliveira (2006) have warned, prolonged marginalization of the legislature in international affairs (particularly in regional integration) weakens its role as a normative bridge between domestic preferences and diplomatic outcomes. Moreover, as Feliú Ribeiro (2014) demonstrates, parliamentary behavior in foreign policy often reflects coalitional dynamics and internal party alignment, rather than policy autonomy. Yet this should not be mistaken for institutional irrelevance.

    If anything, it signals a shift toward indirect and strategic forms of oversight, a repertoire that includes committee activation, agenda signaling, rhetorical dissent, and symbolic positioning. A growing body of empirical research now shows that Brazilian legislators, especially in the Senate, do want to, do know to, and do act in foreign policy, particularly when reputational incentives align and windows of opportunity emerge (Anastasia et al. 2012; Spohr and Silva 2018; Santos and Lopes 2022; Santos 2025a).

    The idea that Congress lacks institutional tools has been empirically nuanced. As documented by literature, the Senate wields a set of oversight mechanisms, including formal summons, agenda manipulation, and symbolic resolutions, that constrain executive discretion even in the absence of veto power (Spohr and Silva 2018, Santos and Lopes 2022).

    Silence as Strategic Latency: Threats, Timing, and Oversight Readiness

    The episode involving Senator Ronaldo Caiado’s 2015 procedural threat illustrates more than legislative brinkmanship; it signals a deeper institutional logic. His vow to obstruct proceedings was not immediately followed by action, nor did it result in a formal legislative response. Instead, it marked a moment of strategic silence, a pause loaded with procedural intent, political calculation, and latent pressure on the Executive. Nothing was “done,” and yet much was signaled (Vasco 2015).

    This kind of silence is analytically elusive. It does not leave a clear footprint in legislative outputs or formal decisions, thereby challenging common approaches to legislative studies that treat visible actions (bills, hearings, roll calls) as the only valid evidence of political behavior (Mayhew 1974).

    We propose the concept of strategic latency to describe this posture. Unlike structural marginalization or procedural inertia, strategic latency reflects a mode of readiness. Legislators monitor, anticipate, and prepare responses before any formal oversight is initiated, enhancing what we call proactive capacity: the ability to respond quickly and credibly once a political or reputational threshold is crossed (Mahoney and Thelen 2010).

    To avoid conceptual overreach, we differentiate strategic latency from two other forms of inaction: (i) inertia due to resource constraints or institutional weakening (Krehbiel 1992), and (ii) deliberate disengagement based on partisan alignment or deference to executive prerogative (Howell and Pevehouse 2007).

    Salience as Symbolic Activation: When Legislatures Sound Their Own Alarms

    Salience represents a threshold condition in the oversight continuum, the point at which strategic latency transitions into visible engagement. Unlike traditional “fire alarm” models, in which legislatures respond reactively to external signals raised by interest groups, the media, or constituents, our framework emphasizes that parliaments can also generate salience endogenously (McCubbins and Schwartz 1984).

    This understanding builds on but also revises strands of the literature. Classical studies of legislative oversight tended to equate salience with external pressure (Mayhew, 1974), while more recent accounts have highlighted the expressive and symbolic dimensions of parliamentary activity (Anastasia et al. 2012; Spohr and Silva 2018; Santos and Lopes 2022; Santos 2025).

    Empirically, this shift is captured not by the final outcome of oversight, for instance, whether a public hearing was successfully convened, but by the very act of proposing such instruments. As Raunio and Wagner (2017) note, even failed or rejected initiatives can signal parliamentary resolve and shape broader reputational dynamics. Thus, a denied ministerial summons or an unfulfilled hearing request may still constitute a symbolic escalation: an institutional signal that elevates an issue into the political arena regardless of procedural success.

    This mechanism also resonates with broader theories of agenda-setting and institutional signaling. Jones and Baumgartner (1993) show how institutions manufacture attention by selectively amplifying issues, while Hall and Taylor’s (1996) insights on historical institutionalism underscore how symbolic signals structure political expectations even absent material enforcement.

    Control as Procedural Friction: Oversight Mechanisms of Activation

    At the point where issue salience is converted into legislative action, a range of oversight mechanisms comes into play. These mechanisms can be informal or symbolic, yet capable of exerting real influence over the formulation and implementation of foreign policy. Rather than reshaping outcomes directly, they recalibrate the policymaking process, creating points of tension, delay, and negotiation.

    Among the most common instruments are public hearings, which both enable scrutiny and serve as a means of signaling political concern (Figueiredo and Melo 2022). Budgetary control allows the legislature to attach conditions or restrictions to executive spending, thereby exerting indirect pressure on foreign policy choices (Pereira and Mueller 2004). Invitations and summons compel engagement from ministers, diplomats, or other executive actors, serving as formal avenues for accountability (Macedo, 2018).

    Symbolic acts, such as motions, statements, and resolutions, function to frame international events and assert normative positions from within the legislative branch. Finally, procedural obstruction, through delays or legislative maneuvers, is deployed strategically to extract concessions or register dissent (Cox and Morgenstern 2001; Raile et al. 2011).

    Proactive Calibration: Between Police Patrol and Fire Alarm Paradigm of Oversight

    Legislative oversight in foreign policy unfolds under conditions of high asymmetry: executive dominance, informational opacity, and episodic public salience (Jones and Baumgartner 1993). In this context, parliamentary inaction should not be equated with irrelevance. Silence may serve strategic purposes: to preserve credibility, avoid polarization, or signal principled distance (Lindsay 1994; Glennon 2014; Höhmann and Sieberer 2020).

    The classical literature distinguishes two dominant models of oversight. The police patrol model assumes proactive and systematic monitoring, with legislatures relying on committees, audits, and institutionalized routines (McCubbins and Schwartz 1984). The fire alarm model, by contrast, emphasizes external triggers, citizens, interest groups, or media, that alert legislators to executive deviations.     However, both models fall short in capturing oversight in high-discretion arenas such as foreign policy, where visibility is intermittent and formal veto powers are weak (Lindsay 1994; Martin 2000; Raunio and Wagner 2017). In these contexts, the boundary between inaction and action is blurred, and oversight often unfolds less as constant monitoring or crisis response and more as a calibration of institutional presence.

    Recent work on symbolic politics and institutional adaptation (Mahoney and Thelen 2010; Maricut-Akbik 2021) points toward a third mode of oversight: proactive calibration. In this view, oversight is neither routinized nor purely reactive. Rather, legislatures manage thresholds of engagement internally, weighing reputational risks, diplomatic stakes, and thematic salience.

    Proactive calibration refers to a mode of institutional reasoning through which legislatures govern their oversight posture in high-discretion policy arenas. It does not denote a single action, but a strategic logic that informs how, when, and with what intensity oversight instruments, symbolic, procedural, or coercive, are deployed. It captures the sequencing, timing, and reputational calculus behind these actions, especially where formal veto power is limited but symbolic authority remains consequential (Raunio 2015; Spohr 2019).

    What distinguishes proactive calibration from routine or reactive oversight is its anticipatory orientation: a legislature’s ability to remain vigilant, to selectively raise salience, and to exert reputational pressure without necessarily escalating to confrontation. A symbolic gesture or procedural request becomes an instance of proactive calibration not by its form, but by its embeddedness in a strategy of visibility management.

    In this framework, silence, salience, and control are not discrete categories but interconnected phases in a continuum of calibrated engagement. Proactive calibration thus provides the connective tissue that links latent oversight with moments of activation. It is best understood as a spectrum of institutional postures, ranging from latency to symbolic signaling to visible confrontation, modulated in intensity and timing according to strategic needs.

    These mechanisms are structured by three contextual variables widely noted in comparative legislative studies. First, political interest, or the extent to which parties and leaders gain from activation (Mayhew 1974; Cox and McCubbins 2005). Second, thematic sensitivity, capturing when issues carry reputational or ideological stakes (Lindsay 1994). Third, institutional cost, encompassing the resources, risks, and intra-coalition frictions needed to mobilize oversight tools (Tsebelis 2002). Together, these factors shape whether oversight remains latent, becomes symbolically activated, or escalates into visible control.

It captures oversight not as a binary of action versus inaction, but as a spectrum of institutional postures, ranging from silence to salience to symbolic activation, managed internally by legislatures in ways that maximize strategic and reputational payoff.

    Data and Methods

    This study operationalizes the concept of proactive calibration by leveraging legislative outputs authored by Brazilian senators (1985–2025). This dataset provides a structured means of detecting when and how the legislature monitors, reacts to, or refrains from intervening in foreign policy decisions.

    Legislative Data Collection and Corpus Construction

    To empirically examine how the Brazilian Senate exercises oversight over foreign policy, we constructed a structured legislative dataset covering the period between January 1985 and December 2025. Data collection was conducted using the R package senatebR (Santos 2025b), which interfaces directly with the Brazilian Federal Senate’s Open Data API.

    a) Data Collection Procedure

    Using the function obter_dados_senadores_legislatura(), we retrieved the full list of senators who served between the 47th and 56th legislatures (corresponding to 1985–2025). This list provided the unique identification codes for each parliamentarian, which were then used to query the Senate’s API via the custom function coletar_producao_legislativa ().

    df_senadores_legislatura <- obter_dados_senadores_legislatura(47, 56)

    ids <- df_senadores_legislatura$IdentificacaoParlamentar.CodigoParlamentar

    dados <- coletar_producao_legislativa(ids)

    This function iteratively downloads all legislative items authored by each senator, including: 1) Motions (Requerimentos), 2) Bills (Projetos de Lei), 3) Resolutions (Resoluções), 4) Formal communications and other procedural proposals.

    b) Temporal Filtering and Dataset Scope

    To ensure analytical coherence, we filtered the resulting dataset to include only legislative items formally presented between January 1, 1985, and December 31, 2025, using the field dataApresentacao.

    dados_filtrados <- dados %>%

    mutate(dataApresentacao = as.Date(dataApresentacao)) %>%

    filter(dataApresentacao >= as.Date(“1985-01-01”) & dataApresentacao <= as.Date(“2025-12-31”)) %>%

    distinct(id, .keep_all = TRUE)

    The resulting corpus comprises 70,777 unique legislative entries, each containing: a) Identification metadata (bill ID, type, chamber of origin); b) Temporal and procedural data (submission dates, legislative status); c) Author metadata (senator name, party, state); d) Content fields (titles, keywords, stated objectives).

    c) Analytical Use of the Dataset

    This dataset serves as the empirical backbone of our analysis. It is used to: 1) classify types of oversight behavior; 2) Identify patterns of i) strategic latency, ii) symbolic activation, and iii) procedural friction. This transparent, replicable data pipeline allows for robust tracking of how the Brazilian Senate engages foreign policy issues through a broader lens than formal vetoes, incorporating symbolic discourse and procedural tools as key dimensions of legislative oversight.

    Proactive Calibration: Typology of Proactive Calibration

    Based on the tipoConteudo variable, we identified 31 substantive categories that reflect the diverse mechanisms through which the Brazilian Federal Senate exercises oversight and control. This reclassification replaces the official metadata taxonomy, which in many cases was redundant, vague, or overly generic.

    To move beyond descriptive categories, these 31 actions were organized along two analytical axes. The first axis concerns Oversight Calibration, which captures the orientation of the control mechanism: 1) Latent Monitoring, referring to anticipatory or indirect scrutiny; 2) Procedural Calibration, shaping institutional conditions ex ante; 3) Symbolic Performance, encompassing expressive or legitimating gestures; 4) Strategic Activation, subdivided into request (initiating visibility) and execution (deploying oversight tools); 5) Institutional Obstruction, the tactical blocking or reversal of executive initiatives.

    This classification logic is crucial for our research design. It allows us to operationalize salience not as its external effect, media coverage, public mobilization, or executive reaction, but as a legislative signaling act. What matters analytically is the decision to initiate visibility, even if no external uptake follows. In this sense, the typology captures intentionality in legislative behavior: whether through latent monitoring, symbolic performance, or institutional friction, the key lies not in outcomes alone, but in how legislatures strategically calibrate their engagement.

    Addressing the Inference from Salience to Latency

    A central methodological challenge is inferring strategic latency, since silence leaves few formal traces. To avoid treating all inaction as oversight, we adopt a triangulation rule: a period of silence is coded as latency only when at least two of the following are present—(A) institutional records (e.g., archived motions, prior requests, committee mentions); (B) rhetorical behavior (media statements, speeches, social media posts by legislators); or (C) contextual triggers (diplomatic crises, electoral timing, reputational risk). This framework differentiates anticipatory oversight from structural passivity or partisan disengagement. Salience, in turn, is measured not by external uptake but by intentional signaling acts (e.g., filing requests, proposing hearings), even if outcomes are limited.

    Case Selection: Analytical Strategy

    Case studies were selected to illustrate theoretical mechanisms rather than statistical representativeness. Three criteria guided the choice: (i) institutional relevance (CRE arena); (ii) modal diversity (each case exemplifies latency, symbolic activation, or procedural friction); and (iii) substantive significance (high-stakes debates with reputational impact). Accordingly, we analyze: an exploratory hearing on Alcântara (2017), a confrontational convocation during the vaccine diplomacy crisis (2021), and symbolic critique of Venezuela policy (2024). Together, they highlight how oversight moves conditionally from silence to activation, offering process-oriented insights that complement the aggregate patterns.

    The temporal distribution of oversight modes reveals a dynamic yet patterned repertoire, consistent with the logic of proactive calibration. Latent monitoring emerged already in the late 1980s and remains a background constant, evidencing that silence does not mean absence, but rather sustained vigilance.     From the early 2010s onward, procedural calibration expands steadily, peaking around 2020–2021. This indicates that rule-setting and agenda-structuring became critical mechanisms in times of heightened polarization. Similarly, strategic activation (request) remains relatively subdued until the late 2010s but rises abruptly after 2018, reaching its highest intensity during 2019–2021. This trend underscores the legislature’s growing tendency to generate salience internally, particularly through hearing and summons requests, before translating them into executed actions.

    Case Studies: Tracing Activation from Silence to Control

    This section puts our conceptual model into empirical motion. Having theorized legislative oversight in foreign affairs as a non-linear process, moving from strategic latency (silence), through symbolic or procedural salience, to visible forms of control, we now turn to a series of case studies that illustrate this trajectory in action.

    Oversight through Hearings: Defense and Diplomacy in the Legislative Arena (2017)

    a. Background and Latency Conditions

    In the early months of 2017, Brazil was navigating a politically sensitive moment. The Temer administration, seeking to reorient Brazil’s foreign and defense policies, was accelerating negotiations with the United States regarding the use of the Alcântara Launch Center, a move with significant implications for national sovereignty and technological autonomy (Fleck 2017; Andrade et al. 2018; Bojikian et al. 2022).

    However, congressional sensitivity to Alcântara predated these negotiations. Already in 2016, the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE) had called for an audit by the Federal Court of Accounts (TCU) of the failed Brazil–Ukraine agreement, which had consumed approximately R$1 billion without producing operational results due to the absence of a safeguards arrangement with the United States (Agência Senado, 2016).

    Senators Ricardo Ferraço (Sem Partido-ES), Cristovam Buarque (PDT-DF), and Edison Lobão (PMDB-MA) explicitly linked the audit demand to the need to reassess Alcântara’s strategic use and re-open negotiations for international cooperation (Agência Senado, 20162). This prior episode of scrutiny underscores that legislative attentiveness was not episodic or reactive. Instead, it illustrates a longer trajectory of strategic latency, in which senators monitored, debated, and signaled concern over Alcântara even before concrete negotiations with Washington were reactivated.

    Public statements by Foreign Minister José Serra in 2017 reinforced this shift: “We are taking the initiative to reopen negotiations around several agreements and treaties that did not materialize. One of them refers to the Alcântara base. The issue was much debated in the past and, now, we will seek a partnership” (Serra, quoted in O Globo, 20173). Such remarks formalized the executive’s intent to pursue new agreements, but within Congress, oversight pressure was already incubating through procedural signals and reputational concerns.

    At this stage, no formal legislative action had yet been taken, but the institutional environment was charged with vigilance. Committee debates, rhetorical interventions, and the legacy of prior oversight attempts foreshadowed the oversight activity that would follow, illustrating what we term strategic latency: a posture of anticipatory monitoring in which silence is not inaction, but preparation.

    b. Salience Triggers and Symbolic Activation

    Between late May and early June, a series of hearing requests were submitted to the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE). Senator Lindbergh Farias (PT-RJ) filed RRE 14/2017, requesting the presence of Minister Raul Jungmann to clarify the terms of Brazil–U.S. defense cooperation. Shortly thereafter, Senator Vanessa Grazziotin (PCdoB-AM) introduced RRE 16/2017, which initially called for a formal summons of the minister regarding the deployment of the Armed Forces for internal security, but was later amended to also include explanations about the Alcântara negotiations (Senado 2017a, Senado 2017b, Senado 2017c)

    Senator Romero Jucá4 (MDB-RR), a government leader, filed RRE 17/2017, reiterating the demand for clarifications on bilateral defense cooperation and explicitly linking the issue to concerns over sovereignty (Senado 2017c). The simultaneity and convergence of these initiatives indicate that the CRE’s attention to Alcântara was not the product of a single partisan maneuver, but rather reflected multi-party concern.

    While the proposals differed in tone and emphasis, ranging from critical scrutiny by opposition figures (PT, PCdoB) to cautious signaling from within the governing coalition (MDB), their overlap reveals a broader institutional sensitivity to the issue. As the CRE president remarked during deliberations: “It is more appropriate to use ‘invitation’, since Minister Raul Jungmann already has approved requests to appear before this Committee as an invitee. A new summons would be redundant; perhaps ‘invitation’ would be more adequate.” (CRE, Senate Records, 2017d).

    Although the invitation was not coercive, its symbolic value was high. The very act of requesting the hearing served as a formal register of salience, a shift from quiet monitoring to visible signaling. The framing of the hearing emphasized transparency, technological risk, and sovereignty.

    c. Oversight Activation and Procedural Friction

    On May 31 and again on June 29, the CRE transitioned from signaling to activation. In the first session, Minister Jungmann was questioned about Brazil’s defense negotiations. In the second, General Eduardo Villas Bôas joined the table, and the scope expanded to include issues such as internal security (GLO operations), peacekeeping missions (Haiti/MINUSTAH), and regional crises (notably Venezuela).

    Although these hearings did not halt or reverse executive decisions, they generated procedural friction: introducing delay, compelling justification, and raising the political cost of unilateral decision-making. These actions were strategically non-confrontational but normatively forceful, allowing senators to extract information, assert institutional presence, and shape the public framing of defense policy.

    The initiative, led by Senator Romero Jucá (MDB-RR), was rooted in concerns over sovereignty, technological control, and transparency. In his opening remarks, Jucá stated: “This Committee is not against cooperation, but it will not accept blind endorsement. We want to know what is being negotiated, what is being transferred, and what Brazil gains5 (p.7)” .

    The friction was particularly evident in the questions posed by Senators Jorge Viana (PT-AC) and Ricardo Ferraço (PSDB-ES), who scrutinized not only policies but the ideological orientation and long-term vision of Brazil’s defense strategy.

    Minister Jungmann responded by emphasizing economic potential and sovereignty safeguards, arguing: “Brazil will not relinquish control over Alcântara. We are seeking a model that brings investment while preserving national interest6” . The choice to invite rather than summon reflected a strategy of institutional restraint, privileging dialogue while reinforcing legislative relevance.

    Invitation as Confrontational Accountability: The 2021 Vaccine Diplomacy Crisis

    a. Background and Latency Conditions

    In early 2021, Brazil faced an acute public health crisis. The COVID-19 vaccination campaign was paralyzed by supply shortages, logistical delays, and deteriorating diplomatic relations, especially with China, a major supplier of vaccines and inputs. President Bolsonaro publicly discredited the Chinese vaccine, declaring: “I don’t believe the Chinese vaccine conveys safety, given its origin. From China, we will not buy” (Coletta 2020a; 2020b). This stance politicized vaccine diplomacy, transforming what might have been a pragmatic partnership into a polarized dispute over sovereignty, legitimacy, and Brazil’s international alignments.

    Yet the seeds of confrontation had been planted earlier. In April 2020, Foreign Minister Ernesto Araújo published the essay “Chegou o Comunavírus”, framing the pandemic as an opportunity for communist and globalist forces to undermine national sovereignty (Araújo 2020). His discourse placed Brazilian foreign policy on an ideological collision course, amplifying domestic and international tensions well before the vaccine shortage became acute.

    The Senate began to register discomfort by the end of that year. In December 2020, it blocked the nomination of Fábio Mendes Marzano (Agência Senado 2020) to the Brazilian mission at the UN in Geneva, a move widely interpreted as a message to Araújo and a rejection of his “anti-China” posture and Olavista agenda (Agência Senado 2020; Machado & Della Coletta 2020a; 2020b). Senators from across party lines emphasized that relations with China should “walk well,” not be jeopardized by ideological provocation.

    Taken together, these episodes reveal that strategic latency was already in motion: the legislature had begun to monitor, contest, and position itself against Araújo’s confrontational diplomacy, even before the escalation of the vaccine crisis. Figures such as Senator Kátia Abreu (PDT-TO), chair of the Committee on Foreign Relations (CRE), voiced concern early on. What began as rhetorical discomfort and procedural signaling gradually evolved into overt activation once vaccine diplomacy turned into a matter of domestic survival.

    b. Salience Triggers and Symbolic Activation

    By the second half of 2020, the Senate Committee on Foreign Relations (CRE) had moved from latent monitoring to formal signaling. A sequence of requests submitted in September illustrates how legislators sought to elevate foreign policy disputes into matters of institutional oversight.

    On September 21, Senator Jaques Wagner (PT-BA) filed REQ 17/2020, demanding the convocation of Foreign Minister Ernesto Araújo to explain the visit of U.S. Secretary of State Mike Pompeo to Roraima, Brazil’s support for the American candidate at the Inter-American Development Bank, and broader orientations of Brazilian foreign policy.

    In parallel, other senators pursued complementary strategies. On September 28, Senator Kátia Abreu (PP-TO), together with Senator Nelsinho Trad (PSD-MS), submitted REQ 25/2020, requesting detailed information on the costs of embassies and consulates, as well as trade balance data. This initiative reframed oversight not as purely ideological but also as administrative and financial accountability.

    That same day, opposition senator Randolfe Rodrigues (REDE-AP) advanced two further measures: REQ 23/2020, a motion of censure against Araújo, and REQ 26/2020, demanding disclosure of preparatory documents for Mike Pompeo’s visit.

    On March 28 (2021), Ernesto Araújo tweeted about a private lunch with senator Kátia Abreu, claiming she had suggested that “if you make a gesture on 5G, you will be the king of the Senate.” Araújo framed this as evidence of political bargaining, insisting that he had refused to comply (Araújo 2021). Abreu immediately issued a public rebuttal, accusing Araújo of distorting a three-hour institutional meeting into “a tweet that lacks truth.”

    She went further, reframing the episode as proof that the minister was unfit to lead Brazilian diplomacy: “If a Foreign Minister treats the president of the Senate Foreign Relations Committee with such belligerence, he is definitively outside any possibility of leading Brazil’s diplomacy. We must free Brazilian diplomacy from this marginal deviation.” 7.

    This exchange illustrates how Abreu acted as a policy entrepreneur (Kingdon 1995): transforming a personal attack into an institutional argument for change. By elevating Araújo’s conduct into a broader issue of reputational damage to Brazilian diplomacy, she shifted the debate from partisan disagreement to institutional accountability.

    The dispute escalated beyond the committee level when Senate President Rodrigo Pacheco publicly defended Abreu. Responding to Araújo’s tweets, he declared: “The minister’s attempt to disqualify the competent Senator Katia Abreu affects the entire Federal Senate—precisely at a moment when we are seeking to unite, to pacify relations among the branches of government. This constant disaggregation is a great disservice to the country.” (Pacheco 2021).

    By framing Araújo’s remarks as an attack on the Senate as an institution, Pacheco amplified the reputational stakes of the dispute. What had begun as a personalized exchange between minister and committee chair became a confrontation between the Executive and the Legislative, raising the cost of continued silence and accelerating the move from strategic latency toward overt activation.

    c. Oversight Activation and Procedural Friction

    In March 2021, amid mounting domestic and international scrutiny of Brazil’s COVID-19 response, the Senate’s Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE) summoned the Minister of Foreign Affairs, Ernesto Araújo, for a public hearing. Held on March 188, the session was a direct response to a full-scale diplomatic crisis involving vaccine procurement delays, frayed relations with China, and the perceived ideological hardening of Brazilian diplomacy.

    Senators delivered bipartisan and incisive criticism. Senator Fabiano Contarato9 (Rede-ES), who had authored the request, accused the Foreign Ministry of sabotaging Brazil’s vaccine diplomacy: “Your Excellency has guided Brazilian diplomacy with unfounded attacks on China and the World Health Organization. This undermines our capacity to access vaccines and supplies. This is not foreign policy; it is negligence with human life10” (p. 12). His remarks reframed foreign policy as inseparable from public health and citizen welfare.

    Senator Kátia Abreu (PDT-TO), then chair of the CRE, delivered some of the most pointed criticism. She described the Foreign Ministry’s stance as “disastrous” and asked bluntly: “Do you believe you still have the conditions to lead the Ministry of Foreign Affairs at this critical moment in our history?11” (p. 42). Her intervention highlighted the widening gap between Brazil’s diplomatic rhetoric and the practical demands of pandemic governance.

    The hearing also included calls for Araújo’s resignation. Senator Tasso Jereissati (PSDB-CE) stated: “The only dignified solution would be your resignation. You have lost the conditions to lead Brazil’s foreign policy12” (p. 56). Such remarks reinforced the CRE’s role not merely as an information-gathering forum, but as a platform for political censure and institutional accountability.

    Araújo’s responses were largely defensive and evasive. At one point, he insisted: “The Foreign Ministry has been working intensely to secure inputs. There is no sabotage⊠only diplomatic effort, even in the face of distorted narratives13” (p. 28). The tone of the session oscillated between scrutiny and confrontation, with senators invoking their constitutional mandate to reassert legislative oversight.

    This episode exemplifies a high-intensity mode of legislative activation, where summoning becomes a tool not just of inquiry, but of direct political contestation. The CRE was transformed into a stage for domestic and international signaling, revealing the costs of unilateralism and ideological rigidity in foreign affairs.

    Contrasting sharply with previous, more dialogical formats, the 2021 hearing represents the upper threshold of legislative control in foreign policy. It illustrates how the legislature, though often latent, can emerge as a decisive actor when institutional thresholds of risk, negligence, or irresponsibility are perceived to have been crossed.

    Symbolic Oversight and Preventive Critique14: The 2024 Venezuela Convocation15

    a. Background and Latency Conditions

    Foreign policy toward Venezuela has long been a sensitive issue in Brazil, intersecting questions of sovereignty, democracy, and regional stability. The CRE has a history of engagement on the matter. As early as 201416, the Senate’s Committee on Foreign Relations and National Defense (CRE) hosted opposition leader María Corina Machado in a highly contested session, framing Venezuela’s internal crisis as a regional concern with direct implications for Brazilian diplomacy (Agência Senado 2014).

    Nearly a decade later, in 2023, Senators Sergio Moro (União-PR) and Esperidião Amin (PP-SC) revived this tradition by inviting Machado to testify before Senate committees, underscoring continuity in the legislature’s concern with Venezuela’s democratic erosion (Agência Senado 2023a; 2023b). Interestingly, this invitation was not processed through the CRE, traditionally responsible for foreign affairs, but rather through the Committee on Public Security (CSP), highlighting how concerns over Venezuela’s political trajectory transcended diplomatic channels and were reframed in terms of regional stability and domestic security17 (Senado, 2023c).

    By mid-2024, as Venezuela approached presidential elections under Nicolás Maduro, Brazil’s Executive adopted a cautious, conciliatory posture, prioritizing mediation over public condemnation. Within the legislature, opposition voices began to frame Brazil’s silence as complicity, emphasizing reputational risks and the erosion of democratic credibility.

    Senator Ciro Nogueira (PP-PI), for instance, accused the government of double standards: “Lula, your comrade Maduro vetoes a political opponent and your government remains silent, does not condemn? Here, you hold rallies in the palace talking about ‘unshakable democracy.’ But in Venezuela, unshakable is Maduro’s dictatorship, and unshakable is the PT government’s support for the dictator there. Unshakable is the PT’s contradiction18” (Nogueira 2024).

    b. Salience Triggers and Symbolic Activation

    The tipping point came with the submission of formal requests in the CRE. Senator Tereza Cristina filed a motion to summon Amorim, framing the issue not only as a diplomatic stance but as an institutional question of authority19. In parallel, Senator Ciro Nogueira (PP-PI) presented REQ 15/2024, demanding explanations from Foreign Minister Mauro Vieira regarding Brazil’s silence in the face of alleged Venezuelan electoral fraud (Senado Federal 2024a).

    In March, the committee had already approved REQ 5/2024, proposed by Esperidião Amin, Sergio Moro, and Hamilton Mourão, inviting opposition leaders María Corina Machado and Edmundo González to present their perspectives on the Venezuelan elections (Senado Federal 2024b). Together, these initiatives transformed diffuse unease into symbolic activation, making Venezuela a visible item on the Senate’s agenda and signaling to domestic and international audiences that legislative oversight was in motion.

    In her case, Senator Tereza Cristina justified the request by raising concerns over Brazil’s perceived support for what she labeled “undemocratic elections.” She stated: “Brazil is exposing itself unnecessarily by supporting elections that lack endorsement from international organizations. We cannot use diplomacy to legitimize dictatorships20.” Her remarks framed the hearing as an act of symbolic resistance to an alleged shift in Brazil’s foreign policy orientation.

    c. Oversight Activation and Procedural Friction

    On August 15, 2024, the CRE convened Celso Amorim. The hearing was not designed to alter Brazilian policy directly, but to publicly contest its legitimacy. Senator Tereza Cristina accused the government of “legitimizing dictatorships,” arguing that silence in the face of fraud equaled complicity.

    Amorim, in response, defended the government’s position by emphasizing dialogue and sovereignty. He noted:“Brazil’s role is to foster dialogue, not to impose solutions. Legitimacy does not derive from external certification, but from the sovereign will of the people21”. His intervention echoed a broader doctrine of non-interference, positioning Brazil as a facilitator rather than arbiter in regional disputes.

    The hearing featured sharp interrogations about institutional authority and foreign policy conduct. Senator Esperidião Amin (PP-SC) voiced procedural concerns: “This Committee needs to know whether there is a new directive in Brazilian foreign policy. The silence of Itamaraty and the central role of a special advisor generate institutional insecurity.”22 Here, the scrutiny transcended policy substance and touched on the legitimacy of the actors executing diplomacy.

    Senator Mara Gabrilli (PSD-SP) added a reputational dimension, warning: “We are discussing a sensitive topic with direct implications for Brazil’s international image. Transparency and alignment with democratic principles are essential.”

    This case exemplifies the legislature’s use of oversight not in reaction to failure, but as a proactive mechanism of symbolic contestation and reputational defense. The convocation served to demarcate institutional boundaries, question executive centralization, and project an alternative narrative to Brazil’s diplomatic stance on Venezuela. Unlike hearings centered on factual clarification, this session functioned as a performative assertion of the Senate’s normative role in shaping and contesting foreign policy direction.

    Conclusion

    This article reconceptualizes legislative oversight in foreign policy as proactive calibration, a graded process through which parliaments manage silence (latency), salience (activation), and control (friction). Drawing on 70,777 Senate records and case studies on Alcântara (2017), vaccine diplomacy (2021), and Venezuela (2024), we show that Brazilian oversight is neither constant “police patrol” nor purely reactive “fire alarm.” Instead, legislatures manufacture visibility internally, relying on petitions, hearings, and summonses to signal concern and impose reputational costs.

    Our findings demonstrate three key payoffs. First, oversight in the Senate’s Committee on Foreign Relations is narrow but durable, with hearings and requests serving as observable proxies of vigilance. Second, procedural friction is the dominant pathway of constraint: rarely overturning policy, but regularly recalibrating executive discretion by forcing delay, justification, and exposure. Third, salience is generated endogenously—requests themselves are signals, regardless of outcome.

    Theoretically, this perspective clarifies the ambiguous boundary between symbolic gestures and substantive control. When embedded in sequencing strategies, symbolic action shapes expectations, raises costs, and repositions the legislature within high-discretion arenas. Methodologically, our triangulated inference rule offers a way to render legislative silence analytically visible.

    Ultimately, even “before the alarm sounds,” oversight is underway. By making latency legible, salience measurable, and control gradational, proactive calibration explains how legislatures with limited formal powers still shape outcomes by shaping visibility, turning quiet vigilance into consequential constraint.

    Strategic Latency Inaction rooted in background monitoring and anticipatory capacity. Evidenced by symbolic framing, prior attentiveness, or readiness to activate.

    table_chartTable 3   Modes of Proactive Calibration in Legislative Oversight1
    Conceptual Stage Calibration Mode Definition Typical Instruments Aggregated Categories Oversight Function Theoretical Anchoring
    Strategic Latency Latent Monitoring Background attentiveness without initiating formal action. Petitions, solemn sessions, voting statements Informational Control; Technical Oversight; Administrative Acts Maintains institutional readiness, symbolic alertness, and reputational sensitivity. Arnold (1990); Lindsay (1994); Santos e Lopes (2022)
    Procedural Calibration Ex ante institutional structuring without immediate oversight activation. Treaty approvals, nomination votes, committee appointments Procedural Control; Appeals and Reviews Shapes the conditions for future oversight without breaking silence. Tsebelis (2002); Mattos (2018); Malamud and Gurgel (2017)
    Symbolic Activation Symbolic Performance Expressive acts that raise visibility without legal or procedural consequence. Commemorative motions Symbolic Control; Nominations and Approvals; Missions and Delegations; Parliamentary Leaves Signals reputational concern, manages attention, frames discourse. Edelman (1988); Raunio (2015); Spohr (2019)
    Strategic Activation (request stage) Formal requests for oversight tools signaling issue salience. Proposed summons, hearing requests, motions Hearings and Inquiries Constitutes the moment legislators “sound the alarm” themselves, creating visibility before formal action. McCubbins and Schwartz (1984); Strom (2000); Baumgartner and Jones (1993)
    Procedural Friction (Visible Intervention) Strategic Activation (execution stage) Full deployment of oversight tools in response to political/reputational triggers. Approved hearings, ministerial summons, PFCs Normative Production; Budgetary Control; Institutional Creation Institutional enforcement of visibility and constraint on executive discretion. Strom (2000); Spohr (2019); Santos and Lopes (2022)
    Institutional Obstruction Tactical use of procedural mechanisms to delay, block, or renegotiate policy. Agenda withdrawals, appeals, filibusters Institutional Obstruction; Ethics and Conduct Generates friction, imposes reputational and institutional costs on the executive, strengthens bargaining power. McCubbins and Schwartz (1984); Tsebelis (2002); Santos and Lopes (2022)
